You close on a brand-new house and the solar array is already bolted to the roof. Nobody in your household ever met the installer, signed a contract, or wrote a check to one. The builder handled all of it months before you showed up. So when tax season arrives, a fair question lands on the kitchen table: who gets the 30% federal credit for those panels, you or the builder, and for which year?

The short answer resolves most of the confusion. On a typical new-construction home where the builder includes an owned solar system in the sale price, the homebuyer claims the Residential Clean Energy Credit, and claims it for the tax year the home is placed in service, which for a new build normally means the year you take occupancy. The builder does not claim it, even though the builder is the one who actually purchased the panels. That reversal catches people off guard, so it is worth walking through why the rule lands where it does, what document you need to make it stick, and the one common arrangement that quietly takes the credit off the table entirely.

## Who claims the credit, and why it lands on the buyer

The credit follows ownership and use of the home, not the person who physically arranged the installation. A homebuilder installs solar as an improvement to a property it intends to sell, not to a home anyone at the company lives in. That distinction matters, because the Residential Clean Energy Credit is a residential credit: it attaches to the taxpayer who owns the qualifying property and uses the home as a residence. The builder is neither of those things by the time you have keys. When you purchase the finished house, you are treated as having purchased the solar system as part of that home, and the IRS guidance for the credit addresses this situation directly. A taxpayer who buys a newly constructed home with solar can claim the credit based on the portion of the home's purchase price that is properly allocable to the solar property.

That last phrase, *allocable to the solar property*, is the part that requires a document rather than a guess. You cannot apply 30% to the whole price of the house. You apply it only to the cost of the solar equipment and its installation, which the builder should be able to itemize from its own records. Before you close, or as part of closing, ask for a written statement or a line on the paperwork that breaks out the solar system's cost. A builder that installs solar routinely will usually have this ready; one that installs it rarely may need a nudge. Without that figure you have no defensible basis for the credit, and "I estimated it from a brochure" is not a position anyone wants to defend if a return is ever questioned. The number needs a paper trail back to what the builder spent on the array.

This owner-claims-it logic is not special to new construction. It is the same principle that governs the credit in general, and the broader mechanics of who qualifies and what expenses count are laid out in our [explainer on the solar tax credit](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/). New construction simply adds a wrinkle that trips people up: the party who bought the panels and the party who claims the credit are two different people, connected only by the sale of the house. Once you see that the credit rides with ownership of the residence rather than with the invoice, the outcome stops feeling strange.

There is one arrangement where this whole chain breaks, and it is common enough that you should check for it before assuming a credit is waiting. New-construction solar is not always sold to you as owned equipment. Some builders partner with a third party that keeps ownership of the panels and signs the homeowner to a lease or a power purchase agreement. Under that structure, the system belongs to the leasing company, not to you, and the party that owns it is the one entitled to the credit. You receive whatever the lease promises, often a fixed or discounted rate for the electricity the panels produce, but you do not get the 30% credit, because you never purchased the equipment. A leased array and an owned array can look identical on the roof and produce very different tax outcomes. So before you pencil a credit into your plans, confirm two things with the builder in writing: that the system conveys to you as owned property with the sale, and what dollar figure they are assigning to it. Those two answers decide whether there is a credit for you to claim at all.

## The tax year hinges on when you move in

The credit is claimed for the tax year in which the system is *placed in service*, and for a new home that phrase has a specific meaning worth getting right. Placed in service is not the year the panels were manufactured, delivered to the site, or mounted on the framing. It is the point at which the home is ready and available for use as your residence, which in practice means when you close and can occupy it. The construction calendar and the installation date are irrelevant to the timing; your occupancy is the trigger.

That distinction carries a real consequence whenever a build straddles a calendar year, which happens constantly. Picture an array installed in October, on a house that is not finished, inspected, and closed until the following January. The panels have been sitting on the roof for months, but the system was not placed in service for you until you closed and moved in, so the credit belongs to the new tax year, not the one in which the crew did the work. The reverse holds too: a home that installs solar in December but closes and is occupied that same December places the system in service in the earlier year. Because the closing date is what anchors everything, keep your closing documents somewhere you can find them at tax time. The settlement statement establishes when the residence became yours, and that date is the one your filing turns on.

A second timing detail matters as much as the year: whether you can actually use the full credit when the year arrives. The Residential Clean Energy Credit is nonrefundable. It offsets federal income tax you owe, and it cannot turn into a refund beyond your liability. If the credit for a sizable new-construction system is larger than the tax you owe in the year you move in, the current rules generally let you carry the unused portion forward to reduce tax in future years rather than losing it. Whether you use the whole amount in year one, or spread it across a couple of returns, depends entirely on your own tax picture, and the mechanics of that situation are worth understanding before you count on the money. We cover it in [what happens when you have little or no tax liability](/blog/solar-tax-credit-tax-liability/), which is exactly the scenario that surprises buyers who assumed a big credit automatically means a big check. It does not; it means a big offset against tax owed, applied over as many years as it takes to absorb it.

One more edge case belongs here, because buyers ask it constantly. If you buy a home that already had solar installed by a previous owner, and the system was placed in service under that earlier owner, there is no fresh credit for you. The credit went to whoever owned the system when it first went into service. Buying a resale home with existing, already-operating panels does not regenerate the credit for the new owner. New construction is different precisely because the system is placed in service for the first time when you, the first occupant, take possession. That first-in-service moment is what makes the credit available to a new-build buyer and unavailable to someone buying a house where the panels have been running for years.

## Pinning down the allocated cost, and sanity-checking it

With the who and the when settled, the whole claim comes down to a single number: the cost properly allocable to the solar property. Suppose the builder's cost allocation puts the solar system at $18,000 of the home's price. The credit is 30% of that eligible amount, roughly $5,400, claimed on your federal return for the year you move in and reported on IRS Form 5695. The form itself is not unusually complicated, and the step-by-step of filling it out is covered in our [walkthrough of filing Form 5695](/blog/irs-form-5695-solar/). The only input unique to new construction is where your "cost" comes from: it is the builder's documented allocation, not an installer's invoice made out to you. Everything else on the form works the same way it would for a homeowner who hired a contractor directly.

Because that allocation is the entire basis for the credit, it is worth a sanity check rather than blind acceptance. Builders occasionally assign a solar figure that is generous relative to what a comparable system would actually cost to install, and a number that sits well above typical installed pricing is a reason to ask questions before you rely on it for a tax filing. You can gauge whether the builder's figure is in a believable range by running the system's size and equipment through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) and comparing the result to the allocation. If the two are in the same neighborhood, the allocation is credible. If the builder's number is far higher than a normal installed price for that size of array, that gap deserves an explanation, because you are the one whose return carries the figure.

A related question comes up when a builder markets the credit as something they will "pass through" to you. There is no formal pass-through of the Residential Clean Energy Credit from a builder to a buyer; it is not a mechanism that exists in the tax code. What actually happens is simpler and entirely in your hands. You own the newly purchased system, so you claim the credit directly on your own return, based on the allocable cost the builder documented. If a builder is folding an expected credit into their pricing or using it as a selling point, that is a business decision on their side of the table. It has no effect on how your claim works: your credit still runs through your own Form 5695, computed from the solar cost allocated to you, in the year your home is placed in service. Keep the allocation statement and the closing documents together, treat the 30% as a credit against tax you owe rather than guaranteed cash, and the new-construction wrinkle turns out to be far less mysterious than that fully assembled array on the roof first suggests.
