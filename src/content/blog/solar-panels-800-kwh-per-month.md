Eight hundred kilowatt-hours a month is a comfortably modest level of electricity use — below the national average, the kind of number you'd see from a smaller household, an efficient home, or a place without electric heat and central air running constantly. If that's what your bills show, the practical question is how much solar it takes to cover it, and the honest answer is a range rather than a single figure: something in the neighborhood of a 6 to 8 kilowatt system, which works out to roughly fifteen to twenty panels with today's typical modules. The spread in that range isn't vagueness for its own sake. It reflects the two things that genuinely move the answer — how much sun your location gets and how powerful the panels you install are — and understanding how those two inputs interact is what lets you turn "800 kWh a month" into a system size you can actually trust.

## From monthly usage to system size

The first move is to convert the monthly number into an annual one, because solar production is best reasoned about across a full year. Eight hundred kilowatt-hours a month is 9,600 kilowatt-hours a year. That yearly figure matters because your usage and your production both swing hard with the seasons — you'll use more in the months you're cooling or heating, and the panels will produce far more in June than in December — and a system sized to cover your annual total lets the surplus of the sunny months offset the shortfall of the dark ones, assuming your utility's billing lets you carry credits across the year. So the real target is a system that generates around 9,600 kWh annually, and everything from here is about figuring out how many kilowatts of panels it takes to produce that where you live.

The bridge from kilowatts of panels to kilowatt-hours of production is the sun. Every kilowatt of solar you install produces a certain number of kilowatt-hours per year, and that number depends overwhelmingly on your local sunshine — specifically your average daily peak sun hours, which is the way solar folks compress a location's whole solar resource into one usable figure. A rough but reliable way to estimate annual production per kilowatt is to multiply your daily peak sun hours by 365 days and then by a system derate factor of about 0.8, which accounts for the real-world losses that a lab-rated panel never shows: heat, wiring resistance, inverter conversion, dust, and imperfect angles. Skip that derate and you'll oversell the system to yourself; it's the single most commonly omitted step in back-of-envelope solar math.

Run that calculation across the range of American climates and the reason for the 6-to-8 kW spread becomes concrete. Assume a sunny Southwestern location with about 5.5 peak sun hours a day: 5.5 × 365 × 0.8 gives roughly 1,600 kWh per kilowatt per year, so covering 9,600 kWh takes about 6 kilowatts of panels. Now assume a cloudier Northern or coastal location with about 4.0 peak sun hours: 4.0 × 365 × 0.8 is closer to 1,170 kWh per kilowatt per year, and covering the same 9,600 kWh now takes roughly 8.2 kilowatts. Same house, same 800 kWh a month, and the system that covers it is a third larger in the cloudier spot purely because each panel there does less work. This is why a national "you need X panels" answer is close to useless — the sun does the heavy lifting, and the sun is local. A [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) that lets you plug in your actual location will pin your number down far more precisely than any table, because it's using your real sun-hour figure instead of a continental average. The general logic of matching system size to your consumption is laid out in our guide to [what size solar system you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/), and 800 kWh a month is simply one point on that curve.

## Panel count, wattage, and roof space

Kilowatts describe the system; panels are what actually go on the roof, and translating one into the other hinges on panel wattage — a number that has climbed steadily over the years. A modern residential panel commonly falls somewhere around 400 watts, though many current modules run higher, into the 430-to-450-watt range, and older or budget panels can sit lower. That per-panel wattage is what turns a kilowatt figure into a panel count, and it's why the same system size can mean different numbers of panels. Take the 6-kilowatt system for the sunny location: at 400 watts a panel, that's 15 panels (6,000 ÷ 400); at 440 watts, it's closer to 14. Take the 8-kilowatt system for the cloudier location: at 400 watts, that's 20 panels; at 440 watts, about 18. Across the whole realistic range of climates and panel wattages, covering 800 kWh a month lands you somewhere around fourteen to twenty panels — which is where that "fifteen to twenty" rule of thumb comes from, and why it's a rule of thumb rather than a precise count. Higher-wattage panels get you the same production from fewer of them, which matters most when roof space is tight. The way panel ratings actually work, and why a higher-wattage panel isn't automatically "better," is worth understanding from our explainer on [solar panel wattage](/blog/solar-panel-wattage-explained/) before you let a fewer-panels pitch drive the decision on its own.

Roof space is the reality check that turns these numbers into something buildable. A typical residential panel occupies roughly 18 to 22 square feet, so a system of fifteen to twenty panels needs somewhere in the range of 300 to 450 square feet of usable, well-oriented roof — and "usable" is the operative word. Not every square foot of your roof counts. You need panels on the sections that face the right direction (south is ideal in the U.S., with east and west facings producing respectably but less), you need to keep clear of shading from trees, chimneys, and neighboring structures, and you need to leave the setbacks that fire codes require around roof edges and ridgelines. A large, simple, south-facing roof plane will swallow a 6-to-8 kilowatt system with room to spare. A cut-up roof with multiple dormers, several orientations, and a shaded corner may struggle to fit twenty panels in the good spots, which can push you toward higher-wattage modules to hit the target in less space, or toward accepting a slightly smaller system than a full offset would require. This is exactly the kind of constraint that a desk estimate misses and a real site assessment catches, and it's why panel count is a starting point rather than a guarantee.

The other adjustment that quietly changes your panel count is how much of your usage you actually intend to offset — and whether that usage is about to change. Many homeowners deliberately size to cover somewhat less than their full consumption, because the last increment of offset often produces surplus that gets exported at a lower value than the retail power it's replacing, softening its payback. Others deliberately size larger because they know a big new load is coming: an EV in the driveway, a heat pump replacing a gas furnace, or a pool. If your 800 kWh a month is going to become 1,100 next year when the electric car arrives, sizing to today's number leaves you short, and adding panels later is usually more expensive per watt than including them now. So the count that's right for you isn't just the one that matches last year's bills — it's the one that matches the usage you expect to have over the twenty-plus years the panels will run.

## Why 800 might not be your real number

It's worth pressing on the premise a little, because "800 kWh a month" is often an average that hides a more complicated truth. Two households can both average 800 and need noticeably different systems. One might use a steady 800 give or take every month — an efficient home with consistent, year-round patterns — which is close to the ideal case for sizing, because production and usage stay in reasonable balance across the seasons. The other might average 800 while actually swinging from 400 in the mild months to 1,300 in the depths of summer air conditioning or winter electric heat. That second household's average is the same, but its system has to grapple with a huge seasonal peak, and whether the array can bank enough spring and fall surplus to cover those peak months depends entirely on the local net-metering or billing rules. An annual average is the right starting point, but the shape of your usage across the year determines how well a system built to that average actually serves you. Pulling twelve months of bills and looking at the swing, not just the average, is the step that separates a system that zeroes out your bill from one that leaves a stubborn summer balance.

Those "what ifs" are where the real sizing decision lives. The headline answer for 800 kilowatt-hours a month — roughly a 6-to-8 kilowatt system, roughly fourteen to twenty panels — is a solid orientation, the number to keep in your head when a quote arrives so you can tell at a glance whether it's in a sane range. But the number that ends up on your roof should come from your sun, your panels, your roof, and the usage you're actually going to have, not from a national average that happens to land near yours. If a quote for an 800-kWh household comes in far outside that 6-to-8 kilowatt band, that's not necessarily wrong — but it's the moment to ask exactly which of those local factors is pushing it there, and whether the answer makes sense for your roof.
