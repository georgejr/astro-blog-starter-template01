A home battery and the grid do the same job for your solar system: they both soak up surplus power at midday and give it back after sunset. The battery does it in your garage; the grid does it through net metering. So the real question a battery poses isn't "storage or no storage" — it's "should I store my surplus in a box I bought, or in the grid I'm already connected to?" And the answer flips entirely depending on how your utility credits the power you send back.

Under one compensation structure the grid is a nearly perfect, free battery, and buying a physical one to do the same thing is hard to justify on economics alone. Under another, the grid pays you pennies for surplus it later sells back to you at full price — and suddenly keeping your own energy makes obvious sense. Everything below is about telling those two situations apart, and about running the numbers for your own roof rather than a neighbor's.

## The grid as a battery: full retail net metering

Classic net metering treats an exported kilowatt-hour and an imported one as equal. Send 1 kWh to the grid at noon, pull 1 kWh back at 8 p.m., and the meter nets them to zero. Older mechanical meters made this literal — they spun backward while you exported — and the modern digital version keeps the same accounting even though nothing physically reverses. In effect the grid stores your surplus at a one-to-one ratio, charges you nothing to do it, never loses a fraction of a kilowatt-hour to conversion, and never wears out. No battery ever built can match those terms.

Set that against what a physical battery actually is. It costs thousands of dollars installed. It loses a slice of energy every time it charges and discharges — call it around 10% on a good lithium system. Its usable capacity shrinks over the years as the cells age, and most warranties acknowledge as much by guaranteeing only a percentage of the original capacity after a decade. So when full retail net metering is on the table and your only goal is a smaller electric bill, a battery is competing against a free, lossless, effectively infinite store that will still be there in fifteen years. It loses that contest almost every time, and no amount of clever sizing changes the underlying arithmetic.

It helps to put a number on how little room a battery has under these terms. Assume you pay a retail rate of $0.17 per kilowatt-hour and, on a typical day, your panels send 10 kWh to the grid that you later pull back after dark. Under one-to-one net metering, those exports have already erased 10 kWh of imports — the grid gave you full value for every one. A battery inserted into that same day doesn't create new savings; it just intercepts energy the grid would have credited you for anyway, and it hands back only about 9 of every 10 kWh it stored. You've spent thousands of dollars to slightly underperform a service you were getting for free. The only way that math turns positive is if the credits themselves are worth less than retail — which is precisely the situation the next section describes.

That's exactly why, under full net metering, batteries get bought for reasons that have nothing to do with the energy math. Backup power is the big one. When the grid goes down, a standard grid-tied array shuts off for safety, and without storage your panels sit idle in a blackout while your neighbors' lights are out too. A battery keeps the refrigerator, the well pump, and a few chosen circuits alive through an outage. In places where the power fails often — wildfire shutoffs, ice storms, an aging local grid — that resilience is worth paying for on its own terms, and so is the plain preference for energy independence. Those are legitimate reasons to own a battery. They're just not the bill-savings case, and it helps to be honest with yourself about which motivation is actually driving the purchase, because backup and savings lead to very different sizing choices and very different expectations about payback.

Net metering also isn't a single uniform rule even where it survives. Some utilities net your production against consumption every monthly billing cycle; others track a running balance and settle up once a year at an annual true-up, when any leftover credit is either paid out at a lower rate or simply forfeited. Those details rarely change the headline conclusion — under genuine retail net metering the grid is still the better deal — but they do shape how an oversized system behaves, and they're the kind of thing worth reading straight off your own utility's tariff rather than assuming. A homeowner whose surplus gets trued up to nearly nothing each year is in a different position from one whose credits roll indefinitely.

There's one more wrinkle worth holding onto: full retail net metering is not a permanent fixture. It's a policy, and policies get revised. Many states have already moved newer solar customers onto less generous structures, sometimes with grandfathering that protects existing systems for a set number of years and sometimes without. If you're weighing a battery today under favorable net metering, it's worth knowing whether your credits are locked in and for how long, because a rule change mid-ownership can reshuffle the whole calculation. What a battery does to the payback figure specifically — under today's rules and under a less friendly future one — is worked through in [adding a battery and your solar payback](/blog/solar-battery-payback-impact/).

## When exporting pays less than importing

The picture inverts the moment your utility credits exports below the retail rate. That's the direction the industry has been moving for years — the net-billing and inflow-outflow structures steadily replacing classic net metering pay you one price for the power you send out and charge you a higher one for the power you bring back. The instant those two numbers diverge, a spread opens between what you're paid for surplus and what you pay to buy power in the evening, and that spread is exactly what a battery captures.

Follow a single kilowatt-hour through the day. Your panels produce a surplus kWh at midday, when the house isn't using much. You have two choices for it. Export it, and you earn the export credit — whatever your utility has decided a sent kilowatt-hour is worth. Or store it in the battery and pull it back out that evening instead of buying a kilowatt-hour at the retail rate. Storing it is worth the retail rate you avoid paying; exporting it is worth only the export credit. Subtract the smaller number from the larger, shave off the battery's round-trip loss, and what remains is the value the battery earns on that one cycle. Repeat it daily and the cycles add up into the payback.

To make the spread concrete, it helps to run the same surplus kilowatt-hour through three common compensation regimes side by side. The figures below use labeled assumptions: a $0.17 retail rate, and a battery that returns about 90% of what it stores after conversion and standby losses.

| Compensation structure | Value of exporting 1 kWh | Value of storing then using it | Battery advantage |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Full retail net metering | $0.17 credit | ~$0.153 (after storage loss) | None — export wins |
| Net billing at $0.08 export | $0.08 credit | ~$0.153 | ~$0.073 per kWh |
| Minimal export credit ($0.03) | $0.03 credit | ~$0.153 | ~$0.123 per kWh |

Reading across the rows tells the whole story. Under full retail net metering, the small round-trip loss means storing a kilowatt-hour actually leaves you slightly worse off than exporting it — the grid gave you the full $0.17, and your battery can only hand back about $0.153 of it. Drop the export credit to $0.08 and the calculus reverses hard: storing is still worth roughly $0.153, exporting is worth $0.08, and the battery pockets about $0.073 every cycle. Push the export credit down to a token $0.03 — increasingly common under the newest tariffs — and the battery earns something like $0.123 per stored kilowatt-hour. The battery's economic case is, at bottom, a bet that this spread is wide and will stay wide across the fifteen-plus years you own the thing.

That mechanism has a name: self-consumption, meaning the share of your own generation you use yourself rather than sending to the grid. A battery is the tool that maximizes it. Without storage, your self-consumption is capped at whatever the house happens to be drawing while the sun is up — the refrigerator, a handful of standby loads, maybe the air conditioning on a hot afternoon. On a mild, low-occupancy day that can be less than half of what the panels produce; the rest exports whether you want it to or not, and under a low export credit it leaves at the cheap rate. A battery lets you keep that midday surplus and spend it during the evening peak, when the house lights up, the oven comes on, and — under time-of-use pricing — the rate you'd otherwise pay is at its highest. That's the compounding effect: you're not merely avoiding an ordinary retail import, you're avoiding the most expensive import of the day.

How much a battery can actually shift depends on three things worth being clear-eyed about. The size of your daily surplus sets the ceiling: a system sized tightly to your usage exports little and leaves the battery not much to capture, while a system that overproduces at midday hands it a full tank to work with. Round-trip efficiency then takes its cut — every battery loses energy converting from AC to DC and back and holding charge, commonly around 10%, which is the reason stored energy never quite equals a full-retail export. And usable capacity paired with depth of discharge sets the practical limit: a battery you can only cycle partway holds less useful energy than its nameplate suggests, capping how much of your evening it can genuinely cover. Get those right and the spread does real work; get them wrong — a tiny surplus, a deeply derated pack — and even a wide spread produces disappointing cycles. The specific numbers your utility uses to set that spread are the subject of [net billing vs. net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/) and its published [export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/), and they're the first thing to look up before modeling anything.

Notice how sensitive the payback is to that per-cycle number. A battery that earns $0.07 on each of, say, 300 full cycles a year is bringing in on the order of $20 of value per usable kilowatt-hour of capacity annually — enough to matter over a warranty life, but slow. Widen the spread to $0.12 and the same battery earns closer to $35 per kWh of capacity per year, and the payback compresses accordingly. Neither figure is a promise; both assume the battery actually finds a full surplus to store most days, which is where seasonality bites. In summer a well-sized array may hand the battery a complete charge every afternoon. In midwinter, shorter days and a snow-dusted roof can leave it half-empty, and a battery sitting at partial charge earns nothing on the cycles it never completes. There's a sizing judgment hidden in all of this: because the value lives in the spread and in cycles completed, the most economically efficient battery is often not the biggest one you can buy but the one matched to the surplus you reliably produce and the evening load you reliably need to cover. That's the opposite of the intuition many buyers start with.

## Deciding for your own bill

The decision reduces to two questions, asked in order. Start with the one that settles most cases on its own: does your utility still offer full retail net metering, or has it moved to a lower export credit? If it's genuine one-to-one net metering, the grid is already the best battery you'll ever own for pure bill savings, and a physical battery has to justify itself on backup, resilience, and independence rather than on the energy arithmetic. There's no shame in buying one for those reasons — just budget for it as insurance and comfort, not as a bill-reduction investment, because under these terms it won't behave like one. If your credits are grandfathered, find out for how long, and plan to revisit the question near the end of that window.

If exports pay less than retail — the net-billing world — the spread between your import and export rates is the yardstick, and the wider it is, the stronger the storage case. Pull your own retail rate and your utility's export credit off a recent bill and off the tariff, and the gap between them tells you roughly what each stored kilowatt-hour is worth before losses. A dime or more of spread is a meaningfully different proposition from two or three cents, and it's the single number that most determines whether storage pencils out.

Then comes the second question, which is about your house rather than your utility: how much surplus do you actually generate to store, and how much of your usage lands after dark when the battery would discharge? A battery only earns its keep on energy it can both capture and later displace. A household that's home all day and burns most of its generation as it's produced has little surplus to bank and a weaker case; one that sits empty from nine to five and comes alive in the evening is close to the ideal storage profile, exporting cheaply all afternoon and buying expensively all night unless a battery bridges the gap. Your roof's production shape, your occupancy pattern, and your rate plan together decide how many full cycles a battery would realistically complete in a year — and cycles completed, not capacity installed, are what pay for it.

Put the two questions together with rough numbers and the shape of the answer appears quickly. Assume net billing with a $0.17 retail rate and a $0.05 export credit — a $0.12 spread — and a battery that reliably banks and returns about 8 kWh a night after losses. That's roughly $0.96 of avoided cost a day, or a few hundred dollars a year, before you count any backup value. Whether that pays back a five-figure battery in a reasonable window depends on the exact install price, incentives, and how many nights the battery actually fills. One thing that genuinely helps the math: the same 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit that applies to panels also applies to a qualifying home battery, which trims the effective cost you're trying to pay back — worth folding in, and worth confirming against current IRS guidance or with a tax professional. The arithmetic itself isn't hard; it just has to use your inputs, because swapping the $0.05 export credit for a $0.14 one, or the 8 kWh for 3, changes the conclusion entirely.

One more input resists a clean number: the value of backup itself. If your grid is reliable and outages are rare, backup is worth little in dollars and the decision rests on the spread. If you lose power several times a year, or a medical device or a home business makes an outage genuinely costly, backup can be the deciding factor even when the energy arithmetic is thin. That value is real but personal, and it belongs in the decision as its own line rather than smuggled into the savings estimate, where it quietly inflates a payback the cycles alone don't support.
