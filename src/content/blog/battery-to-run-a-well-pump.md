For a household on a private well, a power outage isn't just about lights and a warm refrigerator — it's about water. No grid, no pump, no water to the taps, the toilets, or the animals. That makes a well pump one of the first loads people want their home battery to cover, and it turns out to be one of the trickier ones to size for. The difficulty isn't the amount of energy a well pump uses over a day; that's usually modest, because the pump only runs in short bursts to refill a pressure tank. The difficulty is the violent gulp of power the pump demands in the instant it starts. A well pump can draw several times its normal running wattage for a fraction of a second at startup, and if your battery's inverter can't deliver that surge, the pump won't start at all — no matter how many kilowatt-hours are sitting in the battery. Getting water during an outage is therefore mostly a question of surge capability first and stored energy second.

## Why the startup surge, not the running watts, is the real hurdle

A well pump is driven by an electric motor, and induction motors have a characteristic that resistive loads like a toaster or a heater don't: they pull a large inrush of current the moment they energize, before the motor is spinning and while it's fighting inertia and the pressure of the water column above it. This inrush — sometimes quoted on a motor's data plate as locked-rotor amps, or LRA — commonly runs somewhere in the range of three to eight times the pump's steady running draw, and it lasts only a fraction of a second before settling. A pump that hums along at, say, 1,000 watts once it's running might momentarily demand 3,000 to 6,000 watts or more as it kicks on. That spike is invisible on your electric bill, because the utility grid is effectively an infinite source that shrugs off a brief surge without noticing. A battery inverter is not infinite. It has a continuous power rating — the wattage it can supply indefinitely — and a separate, higher surge rating it can sustain only for a short moment. Whether your pump starts on battery power comes down to whether that surge rating comfortably exceeds the pump's inrush.

This is the exact same physics that governs whether a battery can start an air conditioner's compressor, another motor-driven load with a nasty startup spike, and the parallel is close enough that the reasoning in [can a home battery run your air conditioner](/blog/can-battery-run-air-conditioner/) transfers almost directly. In both cases the running wattage is the easy number that lulls people into undersizing, and the surge is the hidden number that actually decides it. The failure mode is specific and frustrating: you have a fully charged battery, the pump tries to start, the inverter can't muster the inrush, its overload protection trips, and the pump stalls. Some inverters recover and retry; some shut down and need a reset. Either way, you're standing at a dry tap with plenty of stored energy you can't use for the one thing you needed it for.

Before you can plan around the surge, you need to know your pump's actual numbers, and they're usually closer than you think. Most residential well pumps are rated in horsepower — a half, three-quarters, one, or one-and-a-half horsepower being the common sizes — and that rating is stamped on the pump or its pressure-switch documentation along with the running amps and, often, the locked-rotor amps. As a rough translation, one horsepower is about 746 watts of mechanical output, but the electrical input while running is higher because the motor isn't perfectly efficient, so a one-horsepower pump commonly draws somewhere around 1,000 to 1,500 running watts. The inrush is what you multiply from there. If the data plate lists locked-rotor amps, multiply that by your voltage to get the peak surge wattage the inverter must supply for that split second; if it doesn't, assume the inrush lands several times the running draw and size for the high end. The single most useful thing you can do before shopping for a battery is to go read those numbers off your own pump, because a system sized for a generic "well pump" is a system sized for a guess, and the gap between a three-quarter-horsepower pump and a one-and-a-half-horsepower one is the difference between a modest inverter and a substantial one.

The good news is that the surge problem is solvable, and there's more than one way to solve it. The most direct is to choose a battery-and-inverter system whose surge rating clearly clears your pump's inrush with margin to spare — you never want to size the inverter to just barely handle the spike, because a tired motor, a low battery, or a cold morning can push the inrush higher than the data plate suggests. The second is to reduce the inrush itself with a soft starter, a small device wired at the pump that ramps the motor up over a second or two instead of slamming it to full power, cutting the peak demand substantially. Soft starters are common on the air-conditioning side for exactly this reason and work on well pump motors too, and they can be the difference between a modestly-sized battery being adequate or hopeless. The third, for those designing a new system, is to favor a pump and pressure setup that starts less often and more gently. The point is that surge is a design constraint you plan around, not a wall you hit by accident — provided you know your pump's numbers before you size the battery.

## Sizing for the water you actually need, once the surge is handled

Once you're confident the inverter can start the pump, the second question is how much stored energy you need to keep water flowing for the length of an outage — and here the numbers turn friendly. A well pump doesn't run continuously; it runs in short cycles to refill a pressure tank, then shuts off until the tank draws down again. Across a day, the total run time might add up to well under an hour for an ordinary household, which means the energy consumed is far smaller than the scary surge number would lead you to expect. What matters for sizing is average daily energy, and that's a product of the pump's running wattage and how long it actually runs, not how hard it spikes at startup.

Work through a labeled example to see the scale. Assume a pump that draws 1,000 watts while running and, across a normal day of household water use — showers, dishes, laundry, flushing, maybe some outdoor use — accumulates about 45 minutes of total run time. That's 1,000 watts for 0.75 hours, or roughly 0.75 kWh per day. Even a modest home battery holding, say, 10 kWh of usable capacity could in principle cover close to two weeks of pumping on that basis alone. Of course you're never running the pump in isolation during an outage — you're also keeping the refrigerator cold, running some lights, charging phones, maybe a well's water is feeding a heating system. But the pump itself is a surprisingly small slice of the daily energy budget. The discipline of adding up each essential load's real daily kilowatt-hours, rather than its nameplate wattage, is the same method laid out in [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), and the pump is a textbook case of a load whose fearsome peak masks a gentle daily total.

This is why the honest way to size a battery for a well is to treat the two constraints separately and let each set its own requirement. The surge constraint sizes the inverter: it must start the pump, full stop, ideally with a soft starter easing the demand. The energy constraint sizes the battery's capacity: it must hold enough kilowatt-hours to cover the pump's daily pumping plus your other essential loads for however many days of autonomy you want. In most real households the inverter's surge capability is the binding constraint — you'll have plenty of stored energy for water long before you run out — which flips the usual intuition that a bigger battery is the answer. A bigger battery doesn't help if the inverter can't start the pump, and a beefier inverter doesn't help if there's no energy left to run it. You need both adequate, and for a well the surge side is the one people underestimate. Running your specific pump wattage, run time, and the rest of your outage loads through the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) is the cleanest way to see which constraint binds first for you, and to stop guessing about how many days of water a given battery actually buys.

There's one more practical wrinkle that separates "the battery can run the pump" from "the household actually stays supplied through a long outage." If the outage runs for days, the battery has to be recharged, which in a real backup scenario means solar on the roof putting energy back during daylight. A battery paired with solar can cycle indefinitely through an extended outage as long as daily production keeps pace with daily consumption — and pumping water is a small enough load that solar usually keeps well ahead of it. A battery without a recharge source is on a countdown from the moment the grid drops, and the pump's daily draw, small as it is, adds to everything else emptying the tank. How long any battery actually lasts under a given load, and how solar recharging changes that arithmetic from a countdown into a sustainable cycle, is worked through in [how long can a home battery actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/). For a well specifically, the reassuring conclusion is that water is one of the cheaper things to keep running once you've cleared the surge hurdle — the pump sips energy, and a solar-charged battery of reasonable size can keep the taps flowing through outages that would leave a generator-owner hauling fuel. The whole game is making sure the inverter can deliver that first violent gulp of power, every time the pump kicks on, for as long as the lights are out.
