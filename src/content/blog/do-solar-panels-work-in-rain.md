There's a stubborn assumption that solar panels are fair-weather machines — that they switch on when the sun is out and switch off the moment a storm rolls in. It's an understandable picture and it's wrong. Panels don't run on sunshine in the sense of needing a clear blue sky; they run on light, and light still reaches the ground when it's raining. A photovoltaic cell doesn't care whether the photons hitting it arrived in a direct beam or bounced around inside a rain cloud first. It converts what it gets. So the real answer to whether panels work in the rain is yes, they keep producing — just less, sometimes a lot less, and the exact amount depends on how heavy the cloud cover is rather than on the rain itself. Worth adding, because it surprises people: a good rain often leaves your system producing *more* in the days that follow, for a reason that has nothing to do with the weather and everything to do with what the water washed off the glass.

## Diffuse light is still light, and cells still convert it

To understand why a panel keeps working under a dark sky, it helps to separate two kinds of sunlight. Direct light is the beam you can trace straight back to the sun — the light that casts sharp shadows on a clear day. Diffuse light is everything that's been scattered on the way down: sunlight bounced and softened by clouds, water vapor, and particles in the air until it arrives from all directions at once, casting no crisp shadow. On a bright day, most of the energy hitting your roof is direct. On a rainy day, the thick cloud deck scatters the incoming sunlight so thoroughly that almost all of what reaches the panel is diffuse. The total amount of energy is lower, because clouds reflect a chunk of it back to space, but it's not zero and it's not close to zero. Overcast, rainy conditions typically deliver something in the range of 5 to 20% of what the same panel would produce under full sun, with heavy, dark storm cells at the low end and light rain under a thinner deck toward the high end. A photovoltaic cell converts that diffuse light with the same physics it uses on a sunny day — fewer photons in, fewer electrons out, but the machine is running the whole time.

This is exactly the same mechanism that keeps panels producing on any overcast day, rain or no rain, which is why the rainy-day question and the cloudy-day question have the same underlying answer. The extended version of that story — how modern panels handle low-light conditions and why "no direct sun" doesn't mean "no power" — is worked through in [the cloudy-day myth](/blog/do-solar-panels-work-on-cloudy-days/). The rain adds one wrinkle worth naming: heavy rain usually rides along with the darkest, densest clouds, so the days with the most water falling tend also to be the days with the least light getting through. It isn't the raindrops on the glass blocking meaningful production — a wet panel and a dry panel under the same sky produce nearly the same. It's the storm system overhead. When people say their system "barely made anything during that downpour," what actually suppressed output was the black cloud deck, not the water sheeting down the modules.

It's also worth clearing up a common confusion baked into the phrase "do solar panels charge when raining." Panels themselves don't store anything; they generate electricity in the moment. If your system feeds the grid, whatever it makes during the rain flows out to offset your usage or export, same as always, just at a lower rate. If you have a battery, the panels will keep sending power to it during the rain — trickling it in more slowly than on a sunny afternoon, but charging nonetheless as long as any light is reaching the array. The idea that a rainy day means a dead system, a dark battery, and no production at all is simply not how the hardware behaves. It behaves like a machine running at low throttle: quieter, slower, but on. What one panel actually produces across a full day under varying conditions, and how the good hours and the poor hours average out, is spelled out in [what one solar panel really produces in a day](/blog/solar-panel-output-per-day/), which is the right frame for understanding that a few dim hours don't sink the daily total the way they feel like they should.

## What the rainy days actually cost you over a year

The instinct after a gloomy, drizzly week is to worry that rain is quietly wrecking your solar economics. It's a fair worry and the reassuring part is in how the numbers accumulate. A solar system isn't judged by its worst hour or its worst day; it's judged by what it produces across a full year, and the annual total already has rainy days baked into it. When an installer estimates your production, they're not assuming 365 days of unbroken sunshine — they're using long-run local weather data that includes the region's typical share of rainy, cloudy, and clear days. The peak-sun-hours figure for your area is essentially an average that has already accounted for the gloom. So a rainy week doesn't represent production you were promised and didn't get; it represents production that was expected and priced in from the start. A run of unusually clear weather the following month tends to pull the average back toward the estimate. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) uses that same kind of location-based averaging, which is why its output estimate is more trustworthy than eyeballing a few bad days and extrapolating.

The magnitude matters too, and it's smaller than the emotional weight of a dark sky suggests. Consider a rough, clearly-assumed example. Say a system produces around 40 kWh on a bright day and, on a heavy rainy day, drops to 10% of that — 4 kWh. That's a big proportional hit for that day. But now zoom out: if a region gets, say, 30 genuinely heavy-rain days in a year, the total production lost relative to those days being sunny is on the order of a thousand-odd kilowatt-hours out of an annual production that might run 12,000 to 14,000 kWh for a typical residential system. It's a real subtraction, but it's a single-digit percentage of the year, not a system-defining one. And most rainy days aren't the pitch-black downpour variety; plenty are light rain under a moderate deck, producing well above that 10% floor. The reason this feels worse than it is comes down to timing and attention: rainy days are the ones you notice, because you're inside watching the rain and thinking about your panels, whereas the surplus of a brilliant clear day passes without a second thought. Averaged across the year, the sunny days quietly do the heavy lifting and the rainy ones take a modest, expected bite.

Geography changes the size of that bite, and it's honest to say so rather than pretend rain is irrelevant everywhere. A home in the Pacific Northwest, with its long gray winters, will see rain and heavy cloud suppress a larger share of the year than a home in the desert Southwest that goes months between storms. That doesn't make solar pointless in rainy climates — plenty of cloudy regions have solar economics that work fine, propped up by higher electricity rates or good incentives — but it does mean the annual production estimate for a Seattle roof will be lower than for a Phoenix roof of identical size, precisely because the weather data feeding that estimate already reflects the difference. The mistake is treating a rainy stretch as evidence the estimate was wrong. In almost every case, the estimate anticipated exactly that stretch; it just spread its effect across twelve months instead of letting you feel it all in one soggy week.

## The overlooked upside: rain is free panel cleaning

Here's the part that flips the whole premise. For most rooftop solar owners, rain isn't just a tolerable interruption — it's a net benefit to the system's year-round performance, because it keeps the glass clean. Panels sit out in the open collecting whatever the air deposits on them: dust, pollen, bird droppings, soot, the fine grime that settles on any horizontal-ish surface over weeks. That film doesn't have to be thick to matter; a layer of dust you'd barely notice can shave a few percent off output by scattering light before it reaches the cells. A steady rain rinses most of that away for free, and a system that gets regular rainfall largely maintains itself. This is the central reason the question of whether panels need active cleaning has such a location-dependent answer — the wetter your climate, the more the sky handles it for you, a trade-off examined in [do solar panels need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/). In many regions the honest recommendation is to let the rain do the work and only intervene when something specific, like a heavy pollen season or a bird-heavy roofline, defeats it.

So a rainy day carries a small, expected cost during the rain and a lingering benefit after it. Production dips while the storm sits overhead, then the sky clears and the panels are producing on clean glass that's shedding light more efficiently than the dusty surface it was a day earlier. In dry climates, where weeks can pass without rain, that cleaning effect is actually one of the arguments for occasional manual washing — you don't get the free rinse often enough to keep the grime from accumulating. In wet climates it's the opposite: rain is frequent enough that manual cleaning is rarely worth the cost or the risk of getting on the roof. Either way, the framing of rain as pure enemy of solar gets it backward. The water on the glass isn't the problem; the dark cloud that brought it is the temporary drag, and it lifts. What's left behind is a cleaner array.

None of this is to wave away the reality that a genuinely cloudy, rainy climate produces less solar energy per year than a sunny one — it does, and that shows up honestly in production estimates and payback math. But "less" is the operative word, not "none." Panels in the rain keep converting the diffuse light that filters through, batteries keep taking a slow charge, grid-tied systems keep offsetting the bill at a reduced rate, and the annual numbers already assume all of it. The picture of solar as a delicate technology that quits at the first raindrop belongs to the same bin as the belief that panels are useless under any cloud. Both mistake a reduction for a shutdown. The machine on your roof is a low-light generator that happens to do best in bright sun — and one that, conveniently, gets washed off every time the weather turns.
