The panels are on the roof, the inverter is on the wall, and the wiring is run. It would be easy to assume the job is done — but a solar system that's *built* is not yet a solar system that's *proven*. Commissioning is the deliberate gap between those two states: the structured round of tests and verifications that confirms the array is wired correctly, performs to spec, and is safe to switch on for good. It's the difference between "the parts are installed" and "the machine works," and skipping it properly is how latent faults survive to become expensive surprises years later.

## What commissioning actually means

Commissioning is the formal hand-off from construction to operation. A technician works through a checklist that verifies every part of the system does what the design says it should, documents the results, and only then declares the system fit to run. It's a distinct step from the municipal inspection and the utility's approval — those are outside parties signing off on code compliance and grid connection, covered in [solar permits and inspections](/blog/solar-permits-and-inspections/) — whereas commissioning is the installer proving to themselves, and to you, that the thing they built genuinely functions.

The reason it matters is that many installation faults are invisible until tested. A reversed connector, an under-torqued lug, a string wired a panel short — none of these announce themselves. The system might even appear to produce power while quietly underperforming or harboring a hazard. Commissioning is the stage designed to catch exactly those silent problems before they're buried under months of normal operation.

## The electrical checks

The first half of commissioning is a sequence of electrical measurements, done before and during the system's first energization. A technician confirms polarity, so that positive and negative conductors land where the design intends — a reversed string can damage equipment or refuse to produce. They verify each string's open-circuit voltage against the expected figure for the panel count and conditions; a reading that's off points to a wiring error or a missing module. Insulation resistance testing checks that current isn't leaking to ground where it shouldn't, which would signal damaged wiring or moisture intrusion.

Mechanical checks ride alongside the electrical ones. Torque on the electrical terminations gets confirmed to specification, because a loose lug is a classic cause of heat and eventual failure. Grounding and bonding are verified so the whole metallic structure has a safe path to earth. None of this is glamorous, but it's the layer where careful workmanship separates itself from the merely adequate, and it's the layer a homeowner can't see once the covers are back on.

## Firing up the inverter and verifying production

With the wiring proven safe, the inverter is energized and configured. It gets set for the local grid parameters and, where required, the anti-islanding and rapid-shutdown behavior that let it interact safely with the utility. Then comes the part that feels like the payoff: watching real power flow. The technician confirms the inverter reports production consistent with the array size and the current sunlight — a 7 kW system on a clear midday should be producing in the neighborhood the design predicts, and a figure far below that is a flag worth chasing before anyone leaves.

This is also where the monitoring system comes online. The commissioning technician confirms each panel or string is reporting, establishes the baseline that future performance gets measured against, and makes sure you can actually see the data. Learning to read that baseline is worth the effort, because it's the tool you'll use to catch problems for the next two decades — the subject of [what your solar monitoring app is really telling you](/blog/solar-monitoring-systems/). A quick sanity check against what your system *should* make on a good day, using the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), gives you an independent yardstick for that first production reading.

## The paperwork you should walk away with

Commissioning produces a paper trail, and collecting it is part of your job as the owner. Ask for and keep the commissioning report or test results, which record the measured voltages, insulation readings, and torque confirmations — your evidence that the system was verified, not just assembled. Gather the equipment documentation and, critically, the warranty registrations for the panels, inverter, and any battery, since some warranties depend on being registered promptly.

Round out the file with the final as-built system specifications, the monitoring account credentials and login, copies of the permit sign-offs, and the utility's permission-to-operate notice — the document that formally allows the system to run and export. That last one matters because a system can be fully commissioned and still not legally cleared to operate until the utility says so; where that step sits in the overall sequence is mapped in [the solar installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/). A complete folder now saves real friction later, whether you're filing a warranty claim, diagnosing a fault, or handing the system to a future buyer.

## How to tell it was actually done

Commissioning is easy to shortchange, because the homeowner rarely watches it and the system will appear to run either way. A system that was energized but never properly tested can produce power while hiding an under-torqued lug that will overheat, a string wired one panel short that quietly costs you production, or a grounding gap that's a latent safety issue. Nothing on the surface distinguishes it from a system that was verified end to end — until a fault surfaces months or years later.

The homeowner's protection is documentation. A crew that ran a real commissioning process can produce the test results; a crew that skipped it cannot. So the single most useful thing you can do is ask for the commissioning report and actually look at it — the measured string voltages, the insulation-resistance readings, the torque confirmations. You don't need to interpret every figure, but their presence is evidence the work happened, and their absence is a flag worth raising before you release a final payment.

Two more low-effort checks close the loop. Compare that first-day production reading against what the system should make in clear conditions, so you leave with a documented baseline rather than a vague memory. And confirm the monitoring is live and reporting per panel or string before the crew departs, because a monitoring system that isn't actually watching is one you'll discover only when you needed it to have caught something. Both take minutes and turn a leap of faith into a verified hand-off.

## Common questions

### Is commissioning the same as the city inspection?
No. The inspection is a government official verifying code compliance, and permission to operate is the utility clearing you for grid connection. Commissioning is the installer's own testing that the system performs and is safe — separate from both, though they cluster near the end of a project.

### Can I do anything useful during commissioning?
Mostly you observe and collect. Ask the technician to walk you through the monitoring app, show you the first production reading against expectations, and confirm which documents you're receiving. Getting the walkthrough while an expert is standing there beats figuring it out alone later.

### How long does it take?
For a typical residential system it's usually a matter of hours, not days, and often happens the same day the install wraps. A larger or more complex system, or one with a battery, takes longer because there are more subsystems to test and configure.
