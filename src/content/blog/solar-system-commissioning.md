The panels are on the roof, the inverter is on the wall, and the wiring is run. It would be easy to assume the job is done — but a solar system that's *built* is not yet a solar system that's *proven*. Commissioning is the deliberate gap between those two states: the structured round of tests and verifications that confirms the array is wired correctly, performs to spec, and is safe to switch on for good. It's the difference between "the parts are installed" and "the machine works," and shortchanging it is how latent faults survive the handoff to become expensive surprises years down the line, long after the crew has moved on.

## What commissioning is, and the electrical checks it runs

Commissioning is the formal hand-off from construction to operation. A technician works through a checklist that verifies every part of the system does what the design says it should, documents the results, and only then declares the system fit to run. It's a distinct step from the municipal inspection and the utility's approval — those are outside parties signing off on code compliance and grid connection, the sequence covered in [solar permits and inspections](/blog/solar-permits-and-inspections/) — whereas commissioning is the installer proving to themselves, and to you, that the thing they built genuinely functions. The inspector confirms it's legal; commissioning confirms it works. Both happen near the end of a project, but they answer different questions and neither substitutes for the other.

The reason commissioning matters is that many installation faults are simply invisible until something tests for them. A reversed connector, an under-torqued lug, a string wired a panel short, a grounding conductor that was never properly landed — none of these announce themselves. The system might even appear to produce power while quietly underperforming or harboring a hazard that won't surface for months. A string missing one panel still generates; it just generates less than it should, and without a baseline you'd never know. A loose electrical connection still passes current; it also heats up over time until it fails. Commissioning is the stage designed to catch exactly those silent problems before they get buried under months of normal operation and become far harder to trace back to their cause.

The first half of the process is a sequence of electrical measurements, done before and during the system's first energization. A technician confirms polarity, so that positive and negative conductors land where the design intends — a reversed string can damage equipment or simply refuse to produce. They verify each string's open-circuit voltage against the expected figure for the panel count and the conditions at the time of the test; a reading that's meaningfully off points to a wiring error, a missing module, or a bad connection somewhere in the string. Insulation resistance testing checks that current isn't leaking to ground where it shouldn't be, which would signal damaged wire insulation or moisture that has found its way somewhere it doesn't belong. Each of these is a specific, measurable check with a right answer, and each catches a class of fault that would otherwise stay hidden.

Mechanical checks ride alongside the electrical ones, and they're just as consequential even though they're less glamorous. Torque on the electrical terminations gets confirmed to specification, because a loose lug is a classic cause of heat buildup and eventual failure — one of the most common roots of problems that surface years later. Grounding and bonding are verified so the entire metallic structure of the array has a safe, continuous path to earth, which is what protects both the equipment and anyone who might touch it during a fault. None of this is the exciting part of going solar, and the homeowner rarely watches it happen, but it's precisely the layer where careful workmanship separates itself from the merely adequate. It's also the layer you can't inspect yourself once the covers are back on and the conduit is buttoned up, which is why the documentation of these checks matters so much later.

Each of these checks is made with a specific instrument rather than by eye, which is part of what makes the resulting numbers trustworthy. Open-circuit voltage and polarity are read with a meter across the string; insulation resistance comes from a dedicated tester that applies a high voltage and measures how much, if any, current leaks where it shouldn't; torque is set and confirmed with a calibrated torque wrench or driver rather than a guess about how tight is tight enough. A thorough technician may also capture a current-voltage curve for each string, a quick trace that reveals whether the panels are performing as expected across their full operating range and can expose a subtle problem — a mismatched module, a partial shading issue, a marginal connection — that a single voltage reading would miss. The homeowner doesn't need to operate any of this, but knowing that real measurements from real instruments underlie the commissioning report is what makes it more than a checkbox: each figure is evidence that a specific thing was tested and found right, not merely assumed.

A system with a battery adds its own commissioning steps on top of the array's, because storage brings subsystems that also have to be proven rather than just installed. The battery's own management system gets checked, its communication with the inverter is confirmed, and its behavior during a simulated grid outage is often tested so everyone knows the backup will actually engage when the grid drops. That's genuinely important to verify up front, because the whole point of a backup battery is the day the power fails, and discovering then that it never transferred correctly is the worst possible time to learn it. If your system includes storage, expect commissioning to take longer and to include a deliberate test of the transition to backup, and ask to see that it was done.

## Firing up the inverter and verifying production

With the wiring proven safe, the inverter is energized and configured for the job it will do for the next couple of decades. It gets set for the local grid parameters — the voltage and frequency limits it must respect to interact safely with the utility — and, where required, the anti-islanding behavior that shuts it down if the grid goes dead and the rapid-shutdown function that de-energizes the roof on command. These settings aren't cosmetic; they're what allow the inverter to be a cooperative citizen on the grid rather than a hazard during an outage or an emergency. Getting them right is part of what commissioning confirms before anyone signs off.

Then comes the part that actually feels like the payoff after weeks of paperwork and construction: watching real power flow. The technician confirms the inverter reports production consistent with the array size and the current sunlight. A 7 kW system on a clear midday should be producing somewhere in the neighborhood the design predicts, and a figure far below that is a flag worth chasing down before anyone leaves the property. This is the first honest test of whether all the upstream work — the panels, the strings, the wiring, the inverter configuration — actually adds up to a functioning generator. A number in the right range is reassuring; a number well short of expectations is exactly the kind of thing commissioning exists to catch while the crew and their ladders are still on site.

One caution keeps that first-day reading honest: a single production number means little without the conditions attached to it. An inverter reporting 5 kW tells you nothing until you know what the array should be making at that moment given the sunlight, the season, the time of day, and the temperature. Panels also produce somewhat less when they're hot, so a strong midday reading on a scorching afternoon can look lower than the same array would show on a cool, clear spring day, and that's normal rather than a fault. A good technician frames the reading against those conditions — "on a clear midday like this, in this season, at this temperature, we'd expect roughly this, and we're seeing that" — rather than quoting a bare number. What you're really confirming isn't a specific wattage but that the system is producing in the right neighborhood for the conditions in front of you, which is why the reading is a sanity check rather than a precise acceptance test.

This is also where the monitoring system comes online, and it's worth paying attention as it does. The commissioning technician confirms that each panel or string is reporting, establishes the baseline that all future performance will be measured against, and makes sure you can actually see the data through whatever app or portal the system uses. That baseline is not a one-day curiosity — it's the reference point you'll quietly rely on for the next twenty-plus years to notice when something has drifted. Learning to read it is genuinely worth the effort, because it's the tool that will tell you a panel has failed or a string has dropped offline long before you'd notice it in a bill, a skill unpacked in [what your solar monitoring app is really telling you](/blog/solar-monitoring-systems/). To give yourself an independent yardstick for that first production reading rather than taking the installer's word for what's "normal," it helps to have a rough sense of what your system *should* make on a good day; running your size and location through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you a back-of-envelope figure to sanity-check the number the inverter is showing.

For a typical residential system, the whole commissioning process is usually a matter of hours rather than days, and it often happens the same day the physical install wraps up. A larger or more complex system — one with many strings, a battery, or a complicated roof — takes longer simply because there are more subsystems to test and configure, and a system with storage adds its own round of checks for the battery and its interaction with the inverter. Either way, it's a discrete, bounded step, not an open-ended one, and a crew that treats it as a real procedure rather than a formality will move through it methodically rather than rushing to close out the job.

## The paperwork, and how to tell it was actually done

Commissioning produces a paper trail, and collecting it is genuinely part of your job as the owner rather than an optional nicety. Ask for and keep the commissioning report or test results, which record the measured string voltages, the insulation-resistance readings, and the torque confirmations — your evidence that the system was verified rather than merely assembled. Gather the equipment documentation and, critically, the warranty registrations for the panels, the inverter, and any battery, since some warranties depend on being registered promptly and can be weakened or lost if that step is skipped. Round out the file with the final as-built system specifications, the monitoring account credentials and login, copies of the permit sign-offs, and the utility's permission-to-operate notice — the document that formally allows the system to run and export power to the grid. That last one matters because a system can be fully commissioned and still not be legally cleared to operate until the utility says so; where that approval sits in the overall order of events is mapped in [the solar installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/). A complete folder assembled now saves real friction later, whether you're filing a warranty claim, diagnosing a fault, or handing the whole system over to a future buyer who wants proof it was done properly.

That documentation is also your only real protection against a commissioning process that was shortchanged, because commissioning is easy to skip and nearly impossible to spot after the fact from the outside. A system that was energized but never properly tested will appear to run exactly like one that was verified end to end. It can produce power while hiding an under-torqued lug that will overheat, a string wired one panel short that quietly costs you production every day, or a grounding gap that sits there as a latent safety issue. Nothing on the surface distinguishes the two until a fault finally surfaces months or years later, at which point the trail back to the original mistake has gone cold. The homeowner's defense is documentation: a crew that ran a genuine commissioning process can produce the test results on request, and a crew that skipped it cannot. So the single most useful thing you can do is ask for the commissioning report and actually look at it — the measured string voltages, the insulation-resistance readings, the torque confirmations. You don't need to interpret every figure to benefit; their presence is evidence the work happened, and their absence is a flag worth raising before you release a final payment.

If the documentation isn't forthcoming, treat that as leverage rather than an inconvenience. The moment before you release final payment is the moment you have the most influence, and a crew that genuinely commissioned the system can produce the report without much trouble. Asking for it politely but firmly, and holding the last payment until it arrives, is a reasonable and common thing to do — not an accusation, just a buyer confirming they received what they paid for. A reputable installer expects this and has the paperwork ready; resistance to a straightforward request for test results is itself a useful signal about how the rest of the work was likely handled.

Two more low-effort checks close the loop and turn a leap of faith into a verified hand-off. Compare that first-day production reading against what the system should make in clear conditions, so you leave the day with a documented baseline rather than a vague memory of the inverter "looking fine." And confirm the monitoring is actually live and reporting per panel or string before the crew departs, because a monitoring system that isn't really watching is one you'll discover only at the worst possible moment — when you needed it to have caught something and it wasn't recording. Both checks take minutes. If you can be present for any part of the process, this is the part to attend: ask the technician to walk you through the monitoring app, show you the first production reading against expectations, and confirm which documents you're receiving. Getting that walkthrough while an expert is standing next to you, with the system live in front of both of you, beats piecing it together alone weeks later when a question comes up and the crew is three jobs away. Commissioning done right is the moment your solar array stops being a construction project and becomes a proven, documented machine you can trust to run itself for decades.
