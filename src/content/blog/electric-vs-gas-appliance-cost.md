Ask whether gas or electric appliances are cheaper to run and you'll get a confident answer from almost anyone — and half of them will be wrong for your house. The honest answer is that it depends on two things that vary enormously by household: the price you pay for each fuel, and how efficiently the appliance turns that fuel into the heat or work you actually want. Natural gas is cheap per unit of energy in most of the country, which tempts people to declare gas the winner and stop thinking. But some electric appliances are so much more efficient than their gas cousins that they win anyway, and gas isn't even available in every home. The only reliable way to know is to put both on the same footing and run the arithmetic on your own rates.

That comparison is easier than it sounds once you standardize the units. The trick is to convert everything to a cost per unit of useful output, accounting for efficiency, rather than comparing the raw price of gas against the raw price of electricity — a comparison that's meaningless because the two are sold in different units and used at different efficiencies.

## Putting both fuels on the same yardstick

Electricity is billed in kilowatt-hours. Natural gas is billed in therms (or sometimes CCF, hundreds of cubic feet, which is close to one therm). To compare them you need a common unit of energy, and the useful bridge is that one therm contains about 29.3 kWh worth of energy. That single conversion factor lets you translate a gas price into the same terms as an electricity price and see which fuel actually costs more per unit of energy delivered.

Work an example with clearly labeled assumptions. Suppose your electricity costs $0.17 per kWh all-in and natural gas costs $1.30 per therm all-in, both of which you should replace with the real numbers off your own bills including all delivery and fixed charges. On a pure energy basis, that therm of gas at $1.30 buys 29.3 kWh of energy, which works out to about $0.044 per kWh-equivalent — roughly a quarter of what the same energy costs as electricity. On raw energy price alone, gas looks like a runaway winner, nearly four to one. If efficiency were equal, the decision would end there. But efficiency is rarely equal, and that's where the interesting reversals happen.

The efficiency gap cuts both ways depending on the appliance. A gas appliance loses energy up the flue — combustion is never complete, and hot exhaust carries usable heat outside. A traditional gas furnace or water heater might deliver 80 to 95 percent of the fuel's energy as useful heat, the rest going out the vent. An electric resistance appliance — a baseboard heater, a standard electric water heater, an electric oven's heating element — is essentially 100 percent efficient at the point of use: every kilowatt-hour becomes heat in your home. So for resistance-based heating, you compare the gas price adjusted for maybe 85 percent efficiency against the electric price at nearly 100 percent. Using our example numbers, gas heat costs about $0.044 ÷ 0.85 ≈ $0.052 per useful kWh, versus $0.17 for electric resistance. Gas still wins heating decisively — often three to one — which is why gas furnaces and gas water heaters remain cheaper to operate than their plain electric-resistance equivalents in most markets. The appliances that dominate a home's energy use are exactly these big heating loads, as our breakdown of [what uses the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) shows, so getting their fuel choice right moves real money.

Then comes the twist that upends the whole comparison: the heat pump. A heat pump doesn't create heat by burning fuel or by running current through a resistance coil — it moves existing heat from one place to another, and moving heat takes far less energy than making it. A modern heat pump delivers 3 to 4 units of heat energy for every 1 unit of electricity it consumes, a ratio expressed as its coefficient of performance. That efficiency multiplier changes everything. At a COP of 3.5, our $0.17/kWh electricity delivers useful heat at about $0.17 ÷ 3.5 ≈ $0.049 per useful kWh — essentially tied with the $0.052 gas figure, using cheap gas. Bump the heat pump's efficiency, or start from a market with pricier gas or cheaper electricity, and the heat pump pulls ahead. This is the crux of the modern electrification argument: heat pumps and heat-pump water heaters compete with gas on running cost despite electricity's higher per-unit price, precisely because they sidestep the create-versus-move penalty. You can put your own local gas and electric prices into a heat pump comparison with the [heat pump cost calculator](/heat-pump-calculator/) and see which side of the line your household falls on, because at typical prices it's genuinely close and small local differences decide it.

## Working the numbers for the appliances people actually ask about

The dryer is the most common gas-versus-electric question because most homes have a laundry decision to make. A standard electric resistance dryer pulls a lot of power — often around 3 kW while running — and a typical load might use 3 to 4 kWh. At $0.17/kWh that's roughly $0.50 to $0.70 per load. A gas dryer still uses some electricity to turn the drum and run controls, but generates its heat from gas, using perhaps a third of a therm per load; at $1.30/therm that's around $0.43 plus a few cents of electricity, landing modestly below the electric dryer's running cost. So a gas dryer is usually a bit cheaper to run, but the gap per load is small — pennies to a couple of quarters — and it takes a lot of laundry to repay the gas dryer's higher purchase price and the cost of a gas hookup and vent if you don't already have them. This is the pattern for many appliance comparisons: gas wins the running cost, but by a margin small enough that installation cost, convenience, and whether you have a gas line at all often decide it. A heat-pump dryer, the newest option, uses far less electricity than resistance — often half — and can beat both on running cost while needing no vent, though it costs more up front and dries more slowly.

The stove and oven comparison is where running cost matters least, because cooking is a small slice of any home's energy use — you run the oven for an hour, not all day. The gas-versus-electric-versus-induction debate for cooking is really about performance and preference, not operating cost: the dollar difference over a year of cooking is typically small change next to heating, water heating, and cooling. Induction, the high-efficiency electric option, transfers heat directly to the pan and wastes little, making it competitive on running cost and superior on responsiveness, but nobody's payback math hinges on the cooktop. Spend your fuel-comparison energy on the big loads and let the cooktop be a preference decision.

Water heating is where the comparison gets consequential, because it's often the second-largest energy load in a home after heating and cooling, and our look at [water heaters as a major household load](/blog/water-heater-electricity-usage/) shows just how much a family's daily hot water adds up. A gas water heater with cheap gas beats a standard electric resistance water heater on running cost by a wide margin, mirroring the furnace math above. But a heat-pump water heater flips it: pulling heat from the surrounding air, it uses roughly a third of the electricity of a resistance model, which drops its effective running cost to gas-competitive or better in many markets. For a household weighing a water-heater replacement, the real contest is often gas versus heat-pump water heater, not gas versus plain electric — and the heat-pump version is frequently the cheapest to run over its life even where gas is inexpensive, especially if it can claim available efficiency incentives.

Running cost is only half of any honest appliance comparison, and leaning on it alone leads people astray. The other half is the cost of getting the appliance in place. A gas appliance needs a gas line, a vent or flue, and sometimes a meter upgrade, none of which is free if your home isn't already set up for it — running a new gas line to a laundry room or kitchen can cost more than years of the running-cost savings it was supposed to deliver. An electric appliance needs an adequate circuit, and a high-draw one like an electric dryer, a heat-pump water heater, or an induction range may require a dedicated 240-volt circuit and, in older homes, panel capacity that isn't there. So the fuel that's cheaper to run can easily be the more expensive choice once installation is counted, especially for a one-off replacement rather than a whole-house decision. The right comparison weighs the running-cost difference over the appliance's realistic lifespan against the up-front cost of each path, and for appliances used lightly — a stove, a rarely run dryer — the running-cost gap is often too small to ever repay a costly hookup.

There's also a direction-of-travel worth factoring in for anyone making a long-lived decision. Electric appliances get cheaper to run when you add solar, because self-generated power undercuts grid rates, and they get cheaper still as more of a home's energy comes from panels rather than the meter. A gas appliance's running cost, by contrast, tracks the price of natural gas, which you have no way to generate yourself. For a household that has solar or expects to add it, that tilts long-term appliance decisions toward electric — particularly the efficient heat-pump versions — because the fuel they burn is one you can partly make on your own roof. This doesn't override today's prices, but it's a reason not to lock in a gas appliance purely on this year's cheap-gas math if you're planning to electrify and generate.

The method matters more than any single verdict, because your answer will differ from your neighbor's two states away. Pull your electric rate and your gas rate off real bills, all-in with fixed charges included. Convert gas to a per-kWh-equivalent using the 29.3 factor. Adjust each appliance for its real efficiency — near 100 percent for resistance electric, 80 to 95 percent for gas combustion, 300 to 400 percent for a heat pump, and account for the fact that a gas appliance you never buy has no fuel cost at all if your home isn't already plumbed for gas. Then compare cost per useful unit, not cost per raw unit. Do that and the confident blanket answers dissolve into a clear, house-specific number. To make the electricity side concrete for your own rates and usage, the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) turns kilowatt-hours into dollars at your local price, and pairing it with the therm conversion above gives you both halves of an honest comparison. The general shape that emerges for most US homes: gas beats plain electric resistance on running cost, heat pumps close or erase that gap by moving heat instead of making it, and for a household adding solar the electric side gets cheaper still, because self-generated power undercuts both fuels at the meter.
