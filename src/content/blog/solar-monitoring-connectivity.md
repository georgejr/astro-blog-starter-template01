A solar array that's producing perfectly can still leave you blind if the little box that reports its numbers can't reach the internet. Most homeowners discover this the first time they open the monitoring app after a few weeks and find a flat line where their production should be — not because the panels stopped working, but because the gateway that phones home lost its Wi-Fi connection and no one noticed. The system kept generating and offsetting the bill the whole time; the data just stopped flowing. That gap matters more than it seems, because monitoring is how you catch a genuine fault early, and a monitor that's offline half the time is a smoke detector with a dead battery. Getting the connection solid and keeping it that way is one of those small, unglamorous jobs that pays off quietly for the entire life of the system, and it's worth understanding why these gateways drop offline before you can reliably keep yours online.

## Why the monitoring keeps dropping in the first place

The root of most connectivity trouble is physical: the device that needs the network signal is usually installed in the worst possible place to get one. Inverters and their monitoring gateways live where the electrical equipment lives — a garage, a basement, a utility closet, or an exterior wall — and those spots tend to sit far from the home's Wi-Fi router, often behind thick walls, concrete, or the metal of an electrical panel and the inverter's own enclosure. Wi-Fi signal weakens with distance and every obstruction it passes through, and a metal-cased device in a corner of the garage two rooms and a foundation wall away from the router may be clinging to a signal that's marginal on a good day and gone on a bad one. A connection that tests fine when the installer sets it up on a clear afternoon can prove flaky over months as conditions shift, which is why "it worked at commissioning" is no guarantee it'll stay working.

The radio band adds a wrinkle that trips up a lot of otherwise tech-savvy owners. Many inverter monitoring gateways connect only on the 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi band, not the faster 5 GHz band, because 2.4 GHz travels farther and penetrates walls better — a sensible choice for a device buried in a garage. The complication arrives with modern routers, many of which broadcast both bands under a single combined network name and steer devices between them automatically. That convenience for phones and laptops can confuse a 2.4-GHz-only gateway, which may fail to connect or keep dropping as the router tries to move it to a band it can't use. The fix is often to expose a separate 2.4 GHz network the gateway can lock onto, but you have to know the device needs it before the symptom — a monitor that won't hold a connection — starts to make sense.

The other common cause of a suddenly dead monitor has nothing to do with signal strength and everything to do with change. The gateway stores your Wi-Fi network name and password, and it reconnects to that specific network. The day you replace your router, change the Wi-Fi password, or switch internet providers and get new equipment, the credentials the gateway memorized stop working, and it silently drops offline until someone re-enters the new details. Homeowners rarely connect the dots — the router swap and the monitoring going dark can be weeks apart in their memory — so a monitor that's been offline "for a while" is very often a monitor that was never reconfigured after a network change. Any time your home network changes, the solar gateway is one more device that needs its settings updated, and remembering that in the moment saves a puzzled troubleshooting session later. ## Wiring beats radio when you can manage it

When Wi-Fi to the inverter is unreliable, the most durable fix is to stop relying on radio at all. Most monitoring gateways have an Ethernet port, and a hardwired connection is immune to the distance, obstruction, and band-steering problems that plague Wi-Fi. If the inverter is anywhere near a spot you can run a cable from — a router in an adjacent room, a network switch in the basement, a wall jack — a straight Ethernet run is the gold-standard solution, set-and-forget, with no signal to fade and nothing to reconfigure when you change your Wi-Fi password. The obstacle is usually just the physical cable path: getting a wire from the router to the garage or utility area can mean drilling, fishing through walls, or running cable along a baseboard. When the run is feasible, though, it's almost always worth the one-time effort, because it eliminates the entire category of wireless dropouts for good.

When you can't pull a cable cleanly, several intermediate options bridge the gap without a full wired run. Powerline adapters send network data over your home's existing electrical wiring: you plug one adapter in near the router and connect it to the router, plug a second in near the inverter, and run a short Ethernet cable from that second adapter to the gateway. Performance varies with the age and layout of the home's wiring, but for the modest data a monitor sends it's often more than enough, and it turns an impossible cable run into two power outlets. A mesh Wi-Fi system solves the problem from the wireless side by placing an additional access point closer to the inverter — a mesh node in the garage or a nearby room can deliver a strong 2.4 GHz signal to a gateway that the main router could never reach, effectively moving the network to the device rather than fighting the distance. Where none of these work, some gateways support a cellular option, connecting over a mobile network instead of your home internet, which sidesteps household network changes entirely at the cost of a small ongoing fee and dependence on cell coverage at the house.

Choosing among these comes down to what your home makes practical. If a clean Ethernet path exists, take it — nothing else is as reliable. If the wiring is old but the outlets are convenient, powerline adapters are the low-effort middle ground. If your home already runs on mesh Wi-Fi, adding a node near the inverter is often the simplest fix of all and improves coverage for everything else in that part of the house too. Cellular is the fallback for detached garages, outbuildings, or homes where the internet itself is unreliable. The one approach worth abandoning early is fighting a marginal Wi-Fi signal indefinitely — nudging the router, re-entering the password, resetting the gateway every few weeks — when a wired or bridged connection would end the problem permanently. The detail you get out of a solid connection depends partly on your monitoring hardware; our comparison of [panel-level versus system-level monitoring](/blog/panel-level-vs-system-monitoring/) explains why some systems report far more granular data than others, which is worth knowing before you decide how much effort a rock-solid link is worth.

## Keeping it online, and why a gap matters

Even a well-connected monitor will occasionally go dark, and knowing the routine recovery steps saves you a service call for something you can fix in minutes. After a power outage, the whole chain has to come back in order — the internet has to restore, the router has to reboot and re-establish the network, and only then can the gateway reconnect — so a monitor that's offline the morning after a storm has often just not finished that sequence, and giving it time or power-cycling the gateway and router together usually brings it back. If it stays offline, walk the chain from the house outward: confirm the internet itself is up, confirm the router is broadcasting the network the gateway expects, confirm nothing about the network name or password has changed, and only then suspect the gateway or the inverter. Most "the monitoring is broken" complaints resolve somewhere in that sequence without any hardware being at fault.

It's worth separating two very different situations that look identical in the app, because they call for opposite responses. When the network connection drops, the monitor goes offline but the inverter keeps running and the panels keep producing — you've lost the data, not the electricity, and your bill savings are unaffected while you sort out the connection. When the inverter itself faults or shuts down, production genuinely stops, and that's a real problem costing you money every sunny hour it persists. The trouble is that a monitor which can't connect can't tell you which situation you're in, because a system that's truly down and a system that's merely unreported both show the same flat line. That ambiguity is exactly why keeping the connection healthy matters: an offline monitor doesn't just hide missing data, it hides the difference between "fine but unreported" and "actually broken," and the longer the gap runs, the longer a genuine fault could sit unnoticed. Our guide to the [warning signs in your monitoring data](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) covers how to read production numbers for early signs of trouble — but every one of those signals depends on the data actually reaching you.

The payoff for all this is early detection, which is the entire reason monitoring exists. A system that suddenly produces far less than it should — because a string has faulted, a panel has failed, or shading has crept in as a tree grew — announces itself in the monitoring data long before you'd notice it in a slightly higher electric bill months later. Catching that early can mean a warranty claim honored, a fault repaired in a week instead of a season, and a lot of lost production recovered. To make use of that early warning, though, you have to know what the system should be producing in the first place, so a drop stands out as a drop. Running your system's size and location through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you a baseline expectation for daily and seasonal output, and once you know roughly what a healthy month looks like, a reliably connected monitor turns that expectation into an ongoing check. A monitor that's online is a monitor doing its job — quietly confirming, every day, that the array on your roof is earning what it's supposed to, and flagging it the moment it isn't.
