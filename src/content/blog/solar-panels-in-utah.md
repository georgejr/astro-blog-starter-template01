Utah gets a lot right for solar: high elevation, thin dry air, and long stretches of clear sky along the Wasatch Front and across the southern half of the state. A roof near Salt Lake City or St. George sees strong year-round irradiance, and the productive equivalent of five-plus full-sun hours a day is a solid head start over cloudier, dimmer markets. The catch in Utah is not the sun. It's what happens to the electricity you send back to the grid.

## The export credit is the whole story

Most Utah homes are served by Rocky Mountain Power, and the state has moved away from old-style retail net metering toward an export credit set well below the retail price of electricity. In plain terms: the power you pull from the grid costs the full retail rate, but the surplus you push back earns only a fraction of that.

That single gap reorders how a Utah system should be designed. Under full retail net metering, exporting is nearly as good as using power at home, so bigger arrays that dump surplus onto the grid still pay. When exports are worth less than retail, every kilowatt-hour you consume the moment you generate it is worth more than one you export. The mechanism behind that difference is worth understanding on its own terms — [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/) walks through why the two structures produce very different bills from the same panels.

I'm deliberately not quoting a cents-per-kilowatt-hour export figure here. The rate has been revised through regulatory proceedings and can change again; what stays stable is the structure — a below-retail export credit that rewards self-consumption. Confirm the current rate with Rocky Mountain Power before you size anything, and treat any installer who waves off the export rate as a rounding error with suspicion. A broader primer on how utilities value your surplus lives in [solar export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/).

## Why self-consumption becomes the lever

When exports pay less than retail, the goal shifts from "build the biggest array the roof allows" to "cover the load you actually use during the day." A kilowatt-hour your air conditioner draws straight from the panels at 3 p.m. offsets electricity at the full retail rate. The same kilowatt-hour sent to the grid earns only the lower export credit.

Two practical moves follow from that:

- **Right-size rather than oversize.** An array roughly matched to your annual usage tends to return more per dollar than one built to export large surpluses. A quick footprint and output check with the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) gives you a defensible starting point before a sales quote anchors you higher.
- **Shift heavy loads into daylight.** Running the dishwasher, pre-cooling the house, or charging an EV midday converts would-be exports into full-retail self-consumption. In a state this sunny, there's plenty of midday generation to soak up.

## Where a battery changes the arithmetic

Utah is one of the clearer cases where storage earns a second look, precisely because of the export gap. When afternoon surplus is worth only the export credit but evening grid power costs full retail, storing that surplus to use after sunset captures the spread. The wider the gap between retail and export rates, the more the math tilts toward a battery.

That doesn't make storage automatic. A battery is a large added cost, and the spread has to be big enough, and your evening usage high enough, to justify it. Model the solar-only case against the solar-plus-battery case in the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) rather than taking a salesperson's word that storage "pays for itself" — in Utah it sometimes helps the case, but the numbers decide.

### Heat and dust, the desert's quiet taxes

Utah's summers, especially in the south, run hot, and panels lose a little efficiency as they heat up — the nameplate rating oversells a 100°F afternoon. The state's dry climate also means long rain-free stretches where windblown dust builds a film that shaves a low-single-digit percentage off output between cleanings. Neither is a crisis, and both are why an honest production estimate for a Utah roof bakes in a modest derate rather than assuming factory-clean panels under lab conditions.

## How Utah compares

If you've read up on neighboring desert markets, the pattern will feel familiar. [Nevada solar](/blog/solar-panels-in-nevada/) runs on the same logic — excellent sun, an export structure worth less than retail — and the design lessons carry across state lines: size to your real usage, keep as much generation at home as you can, and run storage as a deliberate calculation rather than a default add-on.

Utah's sun is a genuine advantage that a dim-market homeowner would envy. The below-retail export credit just means the smartest Utah systems are the ones sized and operated to consume their own power, not the ones built biggest.
