Utah has almost everything a solar shopper could want in a climate. High elevation, thin dry air, and long stretches of clear sky along the Wasatch Front and across the southern half of the state mean a roof near Salt Lake City or St. George soaks up strong irradiance for most of the year. The productive equivalent of five-plus full-sun hours a day is a real head start over cloudier, dimmer markets back east. So the catch, when Utah homeowners run into it, tends to surprise them, because it has nothing to do with the sun. It's about what happens to the electricity you send back to the grid, and it quietly reshapes how a smart Utah system should be designed.

## The export credit is the whole story

Most Utah homes are served by Rocky Mountain Power, and the state has moved away from old-style retail net metering toward an export credit set well below the retail price of electricity. Stated plainly: the power you pull from the grid costs the full retail rate, but the surplus you push back earns only a fraction of that. Under the retail net metering that dominated solar's first two decades, exporting was nearly as valuable as using power at home — a kilowatt-hour sent to the grid at noon rolled back your meter at the same rate you'd later pay to draw one at night, so building a big array that dumped its midday surplus onto the grid still paid off. When exports are worth a fraction of retail, that equivalence breaks, and every design assumption built on it needs revisiting.

That single gap reorders the economics. A kilowatt-hour your home consumes the instant the panels make it is worth the full retail rate you'd otherwise have paid. The same kilowatt-hour exported earns only the lower credit. The difference between those two numbers is no longer a rounding error — it's the hinge the whole system turns on. If you've read about how utilities are shifting away from one-for-one crediting, the mechanism will be familiar; [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/) walks through why two structures that look similar on a brochure produce very different bills from the same panels, and it's worth understanding before you evaluate any Utah quote.

I'm deliberately not quoting a cents-per-kilowatt-hour export figure here, and you should be wary of anyone who does. The rate has been revised through regulatory proceedings and can change again; what stays stable is the structure — a below-retail export credit that rewards self-consumption. Confirm the current rate directly with Rocky Mountain Power before you size anything, and treat any installer who waves off the export rate as a minor detail with real suspicion, because in a below-retail market it's one of the biggest levers on your payback. A broader primer on how utilities value the surplus you send them lives in [solar export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/), and the general principles there map cleanly onto Utah's specific structure.

Utah's own history makes the shift easy to underappreciate. Homeowners who went solar under the older, more generous crediting still talk about their systems in terms that no longer apply to a new installation, and their advice — build big, export freely, watch the meter spin backward — was sound under the rules they signed up under and misleading under today's. A neighbor's glowing account of a system installed years ago isn't a reliable guide to what a new array will earn, because the crediting structure underneath it may have changed entirely in the interim. This is worth keeping in mind when you gather informal advice, because the most enthusiastic solar owners on your street are often the ones whose economics were locked in under terms a new buyer can't get. What they experienced was real; it's just not the deal on offer now, and sizing your own system to their memory rather than to the current export credit is a common and expensive mistake.

There's a temptation to read all this as bad news, and it isn't, quite. A below-retail export credit lowers the ceiling on what a Utah system can earn compared with the golden days of full retail net metering, but Utah's sun is strong enough that a well-designed system still pencils out for many homes. The point isn't that solar stopped working in Utah — it's that the design that maximizes value changed. The array that would have been optimal under retail net metering is often the wrong array now, and a homeowner who doesn't know the rules changed can easily buy the old, oversized design at today's export rates and wonder why the savings underwhelm.

## Why self-consumption becomes the lever

When exports pay less than retail, the goal shifts from "build the biggest array the roof allows" to "cover the load you actually use during the day." Picture a July afternoon with the air conditioner running hard. A kilowatt-hour the compressor draws straight from the panels at 3 p.m. offsets electricity you'd otherwise buy at the full retail rate — that's the most valuable thing your system can do. The same kilowatt-hour, produced while nobody's home and pushed to the grid, earns only the lower export credit. Two identical units of electricity, very different value, decided entirely by whether your house happened to be using power at the moment the panel made it.

Two practical moves follow from that, and both push in the direction of matching production to consumption rather than maximizing raw output. The first is to right-size rather than oversize. An array roughly matched to your annual usage tends to return more per dollar than one deliberately built to export large surpluses, because those surpluses are worth so much less than the retail power a right-sized system displaces. A quick footprint-and-output check with the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) gives you a defensible starting point before a sales quote anchors you higher, and if a proposal comes in noticeably larger than your usage justifies, that gap is your cue to ask what's actually driving it. The second move is to shift heavy loads into daylight. Running the dishwasher at midday instead of after dinner, pre-cooling the house before the afternoon peak, or scheduling an EV to charge while the sun is high all convert would-be exports into full-retail self-consumption. In a state this sunny there's plenty of midday generation to soak up, and a household that leans into daytime usage captures far more of its own production at full value.

Heat and dust deserve a mention here too, because both quietly trim the output an honest estimate should account for. Utah's summers, especially in the south, run hot, and panels lose a little efficiency as they warm — the nameplate rating flatters a 100-degree afternoon, when a module is running well above its lab-test temperature and shedding a slice of rated output. The dry climate that delivers all that clear sky also means long rain-free stretches where windblown dust builds a film on the glass that shaves a low-single-digit percentage off production between cleanings. Neither is a crisis. Both are simply why a credible production estimate for a Utah roof bakes in a modest derate rather than assuming factory-clean panels performing at lab conditions, and why the occasional rinse during a dry summer earns back a bit of lost output.

## Where a battery changes the arithmetic

Utah is one of the clearer cases in the country where storage earns a genuine second look, and the reason is precisely the export gap. When your afternoon surplus is worth only the low export credit but the grid power you buy after sunset costs full retail, a battery lets you capture that spread — store the cheap-to-you surplus during the day, discharge it against expensive evening usage instead of exporting it for pennies and buying it back for dollars. The wider the gap between the retail rate and the export credit, the more the math tilts toward adding storage, and Utah's gap is wide enough that the question is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing.

That doesn't make storage automatic, and it's worth being honest that a battery is a large added cost that has to earn its place. The spread between retail and export rates has to be big enough, and your evening usage high enough, for the daily arbitrage to pay back the hardware over its life. A household that's away all evening and uses little power after dark captures far less of that spread than one that runs heavy loads at night, and the same battery pays back very differently in those two homes. The way to settle it isn't a salesperson's assurance that storage "pays for itself" — that phrase should always prompt a follow-up question — but a side-by-side comparison of the solar-only case against the solar-plus-battery case using your real usage pattern. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you model both and see whether the spread and your evening load actually justify the cost, and in Utah the answer is genuinely "sometimes," which is more than can be said in states where exports still credit at retail.

If you've read up on neighboring desert markets, none of this will feel foreign. [Nevada solar](/blog/solar-panels-in-nevada/) runs on the same underlying logic — excellent sun paired with an export structure worth less than retail — and the design lessons carry straight across the state line: size to your real usage, keep as much generation at home as you can, and treat storage as a deliberate calculation rather than a default add-on a salesperson bundles in. Utah's sun is a genuine advantage that a homeowner in a gray, cloudy market would trade for in a heartbeat. The below-retail export credit simply means the smartest Utah systems are the ones sized and operated to consume their own power, not the ones built the biggest the roof can hold.
