Look at almost any inverter spec sheet and you'll find two efficiency numbers that don't match. One might read "peak efficiency 98.5%," the other "CEC weighted efficiency 97.5%." A single percentage point apart doesn't sound like much, and a salesperson will happily quote you the larger one, but the gap between those two figures is the difference between a best-case laboratory moment and the efficiency your inverter will actually average across a full year of real, uneven sunlight. Knowing which number to trust — and, more usefully, why they differ at all — is what separates reading a spec sheet from being marketed to by one.

The [inverter's job](/blog/what-does-a-solar-inverter-do/) is to convert the DC electricity your panels produce into the AC electricity your home and the grid can use, and no conversion is perfectly lossless. A small fraction of the energy passing through becomes heat inside the inverter's electronics rather than reaching your loads. Efficiency is simply the share that survives the conversion intact. The catch, and the reason two numbers exist in the first place, is that this share isn't a single fixed value stamped into the hardware. It changes constantly, minute by minute, depending on how hard the inverter is working at that instant — and any honest attempt to describe the inverter with one number has to grapple with a value that's moving all day long.

## Why a single efficiency number can't describe an inverter

An inverter's efficiency depends heavily on its load — how much power is flowing through it at a given moment relative to its maximum rated capacity. Plot efficiency against load and you don't get a flat line; you get a curve with a distinct shape, and that shape is the whole reason a lone number is misleading. At very light load, in the dim minutes just after sunrise or under heavy cloud, efficiency is genuinely poor. The inverter has its own baseline power draw just to keep its electronics awake and its grid-monitoring active, and when only a trickle of energy is passing through, that fixed overhead eats a large share of it. As production climbs through the morning, efficiency rises quickly and then flattens into a broad plateau, usually somewhere between about 30% and 70% of rated load. That plateau is where a grid-tied inverter spends the overwhelming majority of a normal day, cruising along at high efficiency while the sun does its work. Near full load, efficiency often dips a touch again, because the internal components run hottest and resistive losses grow when the inverter is pushing near its ceiling.

The peak efficiency figure is drawn from the very top of that plateau — the single highest point on the entire curve, achieved at one specific load and one specific input voltage under favorable conditions the manufacturer selected. It's a real, measured value. It is not a lie. But quoting only that peak is like describing a car by its top speed: technically accurate, occasionally reached, and nearly useless for predicting the miles you actually drive. Your inverter will touch its peak efficiency for brief stretches on good days and spend the rest of its life at every other point on the curve, including the unflattering low-load edges of every single morning and evening. A number that captures only the best instant tells you almost nothing about the aggregate energy the inverter will actually deliver over a year, which is the only quantity that shows up in your production and your savings. This is precisely why a second, more representative number had to be invented — because the peak figure, left on its own, systematically overstates real-world performance in a way that happens to favor whoever is selling the inverter.

## What CEC weighting does, and why it's the honest number

The CEC weighted efficiency figure — named for the California Energy Commission testing protocol that defined it — exists to fix exactly the problem the peak number creates. Instead of reporting the single best point on the curve, it measures efficiency at several load levels spread across that curve and blends them into one weighted average, with the weights deliberately chosen to reflect how much time a real grid-tied inverter actually spends at each load. In practice the protocol samples efficiency at load points such as 10%, 20%, 30%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of rated output, then weights the mid-range points most heavily, because that mid-range is where the inverter genuinely operates most of the time. The result is a number that intentionally folds in the less flattering parts of the curve: the light-load inefficiency at the edges of every day and the slight droop near full power. That's why the CEC weighted efficiency always comes in at or below the peak figure — typically by a few tenths of a percent up to about a full percent. A European equivalent called "Euro efficiency" does the same thing with weightings tuned to cloudier northern climates that spend more time at lower loads, so on some spec sheets you'll see that number instead, or listed right alongside the CEC one.

For estimating what an inverter will do on your roof over a year, the CEC weighted figure is the honest predictor, and it should be the number you compare and the number you feed into any production estimate. It already accounts for the reality that your system operates across the full span of loads as the sun climbs and sinks, not just at the flattering peak. If you're comparing two inverters, lining up their CEC weighted efficiencies is a genuine apples-to-apples comparison; lining up their peak figures can quietly mislead you, because two inverters with identical peaks can have differently shaped curves and therefore different weighted averages — one might hold its efficiency well at low load while the other sags, and the peak number hides that entirely. The practical size of the difference is modest but real and worth putting in dollars. A one-percent efficiency gap on a system producing, say, 12,000 kWh a year is roughly 120 kWh annually. That's not enormous in any single year, but it compounds across the two-decade-plus life of the system and costs you nothing extra to get right at the point of choosing, which makes it a free improvement rather than a trade-off.

There's a further subtlety worth knowing, because it reinforces why the honest number is a weighted, condition-aware one rather than a lone best case. Efficiency also depends on the DC input voltage the panels feed the inverter, so some manufacturers report CEC weighted efficiency at three separate voltages — the low, nominal, and high ends of the inverter's operating window — and the figures differ slightly between them. If your array's string voltage tends to sit toward one end of that window, as it will in very cold weather when panel voltage rises or very hot weather when it falls, then the efficiency measured near that voltage is the more relevant one for your actual conditions. It's a small effect, rarely decisive on its own, but it's one more reminder that a single confident percentage rarely tells the whole truth about a device whose performance genuinely varies with load, temperature, and voltage all at once. Weighted efficiency is also just one input among several that determine how much energy the inverter harvests: its behavior under partial shade, the quality of its [maximum power point tracking](/blog/mppt-explained/) that squeezes energy from panels operating at different points, and how it's sized relative to the array all matter too. On sizing in particular, the [DC-to-AC ratio](/blog/inverter-sizing-dc-ac-ratio/) interacts directly with the efficiency curve, because an inverter loaded closer to its sweet spot spends more of its hours in the high-efficiency plateau and less at the inefficient low-load edges.

## Reading it on a real spec sheet

When a spec sheet lists both numbers, the simplest working rule is to treat the peak figure as marketing and the CEC weighted figure as engineering. Use the weighted number to compare products against one another and to feed any production estimate you're building. And if a listing shows only a peak efficiency and conspicuously omits the weighted one, treat that omission as a small signal in itself — the honest, informative, harder-to-flatter number is precisely the one that's missing, and its absence usually isn't an accident on a document written to sell.

None of this should drive the entire inverter decision, and it's worth keeping the fraction of a percent in perspective. Reliability, the length and terms of the warranty, the quality of the monitoring, how gracefully the unit handles shade, and how the architecture — string, microinverter, or optimizer — fits your particular roof usually matter more to your long-run satisfaction than a sliver of conversion efficiency. But once you've narrowed the field to two solid, comparable candidates and you're trying to break the tie, the CEC weighted figure is the one that reflects real days full of changing light rather than a single ideal instant staged in a lab.
