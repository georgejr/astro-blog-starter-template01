The Great Lakes hand the region around them a distinctive kind of weather, and that weather is the single most important thing to get right when sizing a solar system in Michigan, Wisconsin, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois, or the lake-shadowed corners of Minnesota, Pennsylvania, and western New York. The lakes are enormous reservoirs of moisture, and as cold air sweeps across their relatively warm water in the colder months, it picks up that moisture and dumps it downwind as thick cloud cover and heavy snow. Whole stretches of the region — the west side of Michigan, the snowbelt east of Lake Erie and Lake Ontario, the counties downwind of Lake Michigan — spend long runs of the winter under gray skies. For solar, this isn't a footnote. It directly shapes how much power an array makes, when it makes it, and therefore how many panels you need to cover your usage. A system sized on a generic national assumption will disappoint here; a system sized on the region's actual, cloudier reality won't. The difference between those two outcomes is almost entirely a matter of modeling the weather honestly before you buy.

## Lake-effect weather is a production variable, not just a nuisance

Lake-effect cloud cover is the defining feature of Great Lakes solar, and it's worth understanding as a production input rather than merely bad weather to grumble about. Solar panels make power from the sunlight that reaches them, and persistent overcast reduces that sunlight for stretches at a time. The region's winter skies are frequently gray for days on end downwind of the lakes, and while panels do still produce something under clouds — diffuse daylight isn't zero — they produce far less than under clear sky. This suppresses winter output specifically, and it does so unevenly across the region: a home in a heavy lake-effect zone sees more cloud than one farther from the water or on the lee side of the prevailing pattern. The practical consequence is that the region's annual sunlight, and therefore its solar production, skews hard toward the long, clearer days of late spring through summer and away from a dim, cloudy winter. Any honest sizing has to start from that seasonal reality rather than an even spread across the year.

Because the cloud cover varies so much within the region, the only reliable way to estimate production is to model it with local data rather than a rule of thumb. Solar production modeling uses historical weather and irradiance records for a specific location to estimate how much a given system will actually make there, month by month, and in the Great Lakes those local records are what capture the lake-effect clouds a national average would smooth away. As a rough regional assumption, much of the Great Lakes area sees something in the range of 3.5 to 4.5 daily sun-hours of usable production on an annual average — meaningfully less than the sun-drenched Southwest, and your specific figure depends on exactly where you are relative to the lakes. That number is the heart of the sizing math, and it's why plugging your address into a production model matters more here than in a climatically uniform region. Running your location and roof through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you an estimate built on your local sun rather than a generic one, and getting that input right is the foundation everything else rests on. Our guide to [solar in cold climates](/blog/solar-panels-in-cold-climates/) covers the broader physics of northern solar, which applies across the region even as the lake-effect clouds add their own local twist.

The reason to treat this with care rather than optimism is that an over-optimistic production estimate cascades into an undersized system. If a homeowner assumes their panels will produce like panels in a sunnier state, they'll conclude they need fewer of them to cover their usage — and then live with a system that offsets less of their bill than they were promised, because the winter clouds took a bigger bite than the estimate allowed. The error isn't in the panels; it's in the input assumption about how much sun the region actually delivers. Modeling the lake-effect climate honestly at the design stage is what prevents that gap between expected and actual savings, and it's the single most valuable thing a Great Lakes homeowner can insist on before signing: a production estimate built on local irradiance data, with the cloudy winter properly accounted for.

## Sizing when each panel makes less

Lower average production has a direct, arithmetic consequence for system size: to offset a given amount of electricity usage, you need more panel capacity in a cloudier climate than in a sunny one, because each panel makes fewer kilowatt-hours over the year. This isn't a flaw to be discouraged by — it's simply the sizing reality, and it's entirely manageable once you plan for it. A household that would need, say, a certain number of panels to cover its usage in Arizona needs somewhat more of the same panels to cover the same usage in Michigan, because the Michigan panels are working under thinner annual sun. The right way to approach it is to start from your actual electricity usage — your annual kilowatt-hours from your bills — and size the array to produce that much under the region's real production figures, not under a national average. Our [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) calculator does exactly this translation, taking your usage and turning it into a panel count based on production assumptions you can set for your climate.

There's a design judgment embedded in this about how much to size for. In a cloudy region, a homeowner aiming to offset most or all of their annual usage will build a somewhat larger array than the raw usage number might suggest at first glance, and that's the correct response to lower per-panel output rather than an extravagance. The counterweight is roof space and budget: more panels cost more and need more room, so at some point the marginal panel added to chase the last slice of a cloudy winter's usage stops making economic sense. The balance most Great Lakes homeowners strike is to size for a strong annual offset knowing that the summer surplus will carry a lot of the load, rather than trying to brute-force winter production that the clouds will suppress no matter how many panels you add. This is where local modeling pays off twice: it tells you both how many panels cover your usage and where the point of diminishing returns sits for your specific site.

Snow losses belong in this sizing conversation too, because they're part of why annual production runs lower than a clear-sky estimate would suggest. When snow accumulates on panels, it blocks the light and output drops toward zero until the snow clears — and in heavy lake-effect snow country, that can happen repeatedly through the winter. A realistic annual production estimate for the region should already fold in some allowance for snow-covered days, so a homeowner isn't blindsided when a snowy stretch produces almost nothing. The good news is that these losses are concentrated in the lowest-production months anyway — a week of snow-covered panels in January costs you far less than a week of downtime in June would — so their impact on the annual total, while real, is smaller than it feels when you're looking at a blanketed roof. The design should account for them in the estimate; it shouldn't panic about them.

## Snow, winter, and the annual balance

Snow on Great Lakes panels behaves better than newcomers expect, and understanding why takes some of the dread out of the region's winters. Panels are smooth glass, they're usually tilted, and they warm slightly when they're generating, all of which encourage snow to slide off rather than cling — often faster than snow clears from the surrounding roof. A steeper roof pitch sheds snow more readily than a shallow one, which is a genuine design consideration in heavy-snow country and one worth weighing at the design stage. Our dedicated look at [snow on solar panels](/blog/snow-on-solar-panels/) walks through when snow clears on its own, when it lingers, and whether removing it is ever worth the effort and risk — the short version being that for most homeowners, letting it slide is the safe and sensible default, since climbing onto a snowy roof to clear panels is more dangerous than the lost production is valuable.

The cold that comes with the snow is quietly on your side, which is one of the pleasant surprises of northern solar. Solar panels are electronics, and like most electronics they operate more efficiently at lower temperatures — a panel in cold air produces a bit more power from the same sunlight than the same panel baking in summer heat. On the clear, cold days that punctuate a Great Lakes winter, panels can perform quite well precisely because the air is cold, partially offsetting the shorter days. This is the counterintuitive fact that reframes the whole region: the cold isn't the enemy of solar that people assume, and a clear January day with snow on the ground and cold air over the panels can be a genuinely productive one. What hurts winter production is the clouds and the snow cover, not the temperature itself, and separating those two effects makes the region's winters far less discouraging than they look from under a gray sky.

The way all of this resolves into a working system is through the annual balance and the way the region's net metering rules let you use it. Because production skews so heavily toward the sunny months, a Great Lakes system typically overproduces in summer and underproduces in winter, and the arrangement that makes this work is banking the summer surplus as credit to draw down through the lean winter — the mechanics of which depend on your utility's specific net metering or export policy, set at the state and utility level rather than nationally. When those rules credit your summer surplus at a fair rate, the annual math works out well even though winter production is thin, because the year as a whole is what counts. This is why our state-specific guides matter for the details; our look at [solar in Illinois](/blog/solar-panels-in-illinois/), for instance, examines how one Great Lakes state's unusually strong program design changes the economics, and the lesson generalizes: the region's cloudy climate sets the production, but the state's policy sets how much that production is worth. Get the production modeled honestly for your lake-effect location, size the array to your real usage against that honest number, plan for snow to slide and cold to help, and lean on the summer surplus to carry the gray winter — that sequence is what makes solar in the Great Lakes region work as well as it genuinely can.
