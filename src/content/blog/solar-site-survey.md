A few weeks after you sign, someone shows up with a ladder, a tablet, and a laser measure, and spends an hour or two walking your roof, opening your electrical panel, and poking around your attic with a flashlight. This is the site survey, and it's the moment your solar project stops being a satellite estimate and becomes an engineered plan for your actual house. The salesperson's proposal was built from aerial imagery and a year of utility bills — fast, cheap, and good enough to get a number in front of you. The survey exists to check whether that proposal survives contact with reality. Sometimes it doesn't, and that's not a failure of the process; it's precisely the reason the process exists.

Most homeowners treat the survey as a formality, a box to tick on the way to installation day. It's better understood as the point where the real design is finally settled. The panel count, the exact placement on the roof, the electrical work required, and occasionally the price can all move based on what the surveyor finds, because they're now measuring things no aerial photo could ever show — the true condition of your rafters, the spare capacity in your service panel, the shadow a neighbor's oak actually throws across your south slope at four in the afternoon in December.

## Why the survey comes after you've already signed

The order can feel backwards, and a lot of buyers quietly wonder about it: you agree to a price, and *then* someone comes out to verify the house. But the initial quote is preliminary on purpose, and understanding why makes the sequence sensible rather than suspicious. Installers generate early proposals from satellite and aerial data, public roof measurements, and your electricity usage history, because that approach is fast and costs almost nothing, and it gets you a realistic ballpark before anyone burns a half-day driving out and climbing on your roof. Sending a trained surveyor to every homeowner who requests a rough quote would be wildly inefficient, so the industry front-loads the cheap desktop work and saves the expensive hands-on measurement for the projects that are actually moving forward. The survey is where they finally spend that time and labor to confirm every assumption the early estimate quietly rested on.

That structure is also why a reputable contract is written so the design can be adjusted after the survey, and why any material change to scope or price should come back to you for explicit approval before work proceeds. The survey isn't there to spring surprises on you or to manufacture reasons to charge more. It's there to replace educated guesses with real measurements, so the system that ultimately gets built is one your roof and electrical service can genuinely support rather than one that looked plausible in an aerial photo. A well-run survey protects you as much as the installer, because the alternative — discovering a problem after equipment is already bolted to the roof — is far worse and far more expensive for everyone involved. If a company resists putting the post-survey adjustment terms in writing, or treats the survey as a rubber stamp rather than a real engineering check, that reluctance itself tells you something about how they operate.

## What the surveyor is actually checking

The visit covers three broad areas, and each one can feed back into the design in ways the desktop estimate couldn't anticipate. The roof itself comes first. The surveyor measures it precisely, confirms its orientation and pitch with real instruments rather than inferring them from imagery, and inspects both its condition and its underlying construction. They check the roofing material and its remaining life, locate the rafters and note their spacing — which determines where mounting hardware can actually anchor — and assess whether the structure can carry the added load. A roof near the end of its serviceable life is a genuine problem at this stage, because nobody wants to install panels onto shingles that will need replacing in three or four years, which would mean paying to remove and reinstall the whole array later. They'll also catalog the vents, chimneys, skylights, plumbing stacks, and other obstructions that eat into usable area and dictate where panels can and can't go, details that a top-down satellite view routinely flattens or misses entirely.

The electrical system is where surveys most often change things, and it's the part homeowners least anticipate. The surveyor examines your main service panel closely — its amperage rating, the busbar rating, and how much spare capacity it has to accept solar backfeed safely without exceeding code limits. Older homes with 100-amp panels, or panels already crowded with breakers and running near their busbar limit, sometimes can't accommodate a new solar circuit without a main panel upgrade, and that's a real, sometimes four-figure cost that no satellite estimate could possibly foresee. The surveyor also locates the utility meter, checks the condition and rating of the service wiring, and works out the cleanest, code-compliant route to run conduit from the array down to the panel — a run that can turn out longer or more awkward than the estimate assumed, especially on homes with finished interiors or complex layouts. Shading and obstructions round out the visit, and this is one area where being physically on the roof beats any desktop tool. Even good aerial data struggles to judge shade accurately, because it can't easily model how a tree line or a neighboring rooftop throws shadows across the day and across the seasons. The surveyor assesses trees, adjacent buildings, and rooftop features that cast shadows, often using a tool that models the sun's path from each individual roof plane through the year. This on-site read is far more reliable than a desktop guess, and it ties directly into the dedicated [shading analysis](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/) that determines how much any given roof section will realistically produce once real shadows are accounted for.

## Why your quote can move afterward — and how to be ready

Given everything the survey uncovers, a handful of changes are common, and knowing them in advance keeps you from being caught off guard. The design might shift panels off a roof plane that turns out to be more shaded than the imagery suggested, which changes the layout or the total count. The system size might be trimmed if the usable roof area proves smaller than estimated, or if the electrical service caps how much capacity can be interconnected. And costs can rise if the survey reveals a needed main panel upgrade, roof reinforcement, or a longer and more difficult conduit run than the original quote assumed. None of that automatically means you're being upsold. A well-run survey can just as easily confirm the original plan exactly, or even find room to improve it — a better roof plane, a cleaner wiring route, a slightly larger system the service can handle. The point is that the post-survey design is the accurate one, so when a revised proposal lands, it deserves the same careful, line-by-line reading you gave the first: check what changed, why it changed, and what each change costs, the same discipline you'd bring to [comparing quotes item by item](/blog/compare-solar-quotes/). If the price moves, that movement should be explained and approved, never slipped in silently between signing and installation.

Occasionally the survey turns up something that halts the plan rather than merely tweaking it, and it's genuinely better to learn that now, on paper, than after hardware is on the roof. A roof with only a few years of life left may need replacing before any panels go on, since installing onto a roof you'll soon tear off means paying to remove and reinstall the array — a cost that can quietly dwarf the savings of skipping the re-roof. An electrical service that can't safely accept the system may require an upgrade before anything else proceeds. Neither situation is common, but both are exactly the kind of expensive surprise the survey is designed to catch early, while changing course still costs a phone call rather than a crane. You can make the whole visit faster and more accurate with a little preparation: clear access to your electrical panel and to the attic or crawlspace so the surveyor can inspect rafters and the underside of the roof, and have a recent electric bill or two on hand in case they want to confirm your usage. If you have specific concerns — a particular tree, a roof section, where the equipment will be mounted, or whether a room's ceiling can be accessed — raise them during the visit, because this is the ideal moment, with a trained person physically standing at your house looking at the actual conditions. Expect to be home for at least part of it, since access to the panel, attic, and interior almost always requires someone to let the surveyor in, and expect the visit to run roughly one to two hours for a typical home, longer for a complex roof or a hard-to-reach electrical setup. It helps, too, to walk in knowing roughly what system size you're expecting, so any change stands out clearly against your baseline. Running your usage through a [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) or a [how many panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) estimate beforehand gives you exactly that reference point. Once the survey is done and the design is genuinely final, the project moves on into permitting and scheduling — the next stretch of the [installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/) — with the reassurance that the plan being permitted is the one your house can actually carry.
