When a home has no gas line — no gas furnace, no gas water heater, no gas range — every bit of energy it consumes shows up on a single bill, measured in kilowatt-hours. That's tidy in one sense: there's only one utility to deal with and one meter to read. But it also means the electricity number for an all-electric house is much larger than for an otherwise identical home that burns gas for its heaviest jobs, because the loads that used to hide on the gas bill now run on electrons. Anyone going gas-free, or buying a home that already is, needs a realistic picture of that total, because it changes everything downstream — the size of the bill, the size of a solar system to offset it, and how sharply usage swings from summer to winter.

## Where the kilowatt-hours go when nothing burns gas

Every home, gas or electric, has a baseline of electricity use that has nothing to do with heating or cooking: the refrigerator running around the clock, lights, televisions and computers, the washer and dryer, phone chargers, the internet router, and the small always-on loads scattered through the house. For a typical American home, that baseline plus air conditioning adds up to something in the neighborhood of 10,000 to 11,000 kilowatt-hours a year — the commonly cited figure for average household electricity use, though it varies widely by home size, climate, and habits. A gas home covers its heat, hot water, and cooking separately, on the gas bill, so its electricity use stays roughly in that baseline range. The all-electric home starts from the same baseline and then piles the big thermal loads on top of it, and those loads are where the real kilowatt-hours live.

Three jobs do most of the adding. Space heating is by far the largest, and how it's done matters enormously. A home heated with old-fashioned electric resistance — baseboard heaters, electric furnaces, the coils in a resistance system — turns electricity into heat at a one-to-one ratio and can consume staggering amounts of power in a cold winter, which is why [electric heat drives such a sharp winter usage spike](/blog/electric-heating-usage/). A home heated with a modern heat pump does the same job far more efficiently, moving heat rather than generating it and delivering several units of heat for each unit of electricity, so its heating load can be a fraction of what resistance heat would draw. The gap between these two is the single biggest variable in an all-electric home's total usage. Two identical houses in the same cold climate can have wildly different electric bills depending on whether the heat comes from a heat pump or from resistance coils, and it's the first thing to establish when you're estimating usage.

Water heating is the second big addition, and it follows the same logic. An electric resistance water heater — the common tank kind with heating elements — uses meaningful power to keep a tank hot and to reheat after big draws, often landing among the larger single loads in the house. A heat pump water heater does the same work using the heat-pump principle and can cut that consumption substantially, which is why the choice between the two is worth understanding before you assume a number. Cooking is the third, and though it feels dramatic when you switch from a gas range to an electric or induction cooktop, it's actually the smallest of the three additions for most households — cooking is intermittent and brief compared to the constant demands of heating a house or a tank of water. An induction range is efficient and responsive, but even a resistance electric oven and stove, run normally, add far less to the annual total than space heating does. The mental model to carry is that space heating dominates, water heating is a solid second, and cooking is a modest third — and that the efficiency of your heating and water-heating equipment swings the total more than anything else.

There's a fourth category that isn't new to an all-electric home but interacts with the others, and that's cooling. Air conditioning runs on electricity in every home, gas or electric, so it isn't something going gas-free adds — but in a hot climate it can rival heating as the biggest single driver of the annual total, and it stacks its own summer peak on top of whatever the rest of the house draws. The reason this matters for an all-electric home specifically is that heat pumps do double duty: the same heat-pump equipment that heats the house in winter cools it in summer, so a single efficient system can be the workhorse for both peaks. That's part of why heat pumps are so central to the all-electric picture — they're not just an efficient way to heat, they consolidate two of the year's biggest loads into one relatively efficient machine. A household in a climate with both cold winters and hot summers, then, sees its usage curve pushed up at both ends of the year, with milder shoulder seasons in between, and the shape of that double-peaked curve is worth picturing before you assume a flat monthly average describes your bills.

## Putting a real monthly and annual number on it

Translating all of that into a usable figure means starting from the baseline and adding the thermal loads, with honest ranges rather than false precision, because climate and equipment move the answer so much. Take a moderate-climate, all-electric home using efficient heat pumps for space and water heating. Its baseline plus cooling might run near the 10,000-to-11,000 kWh national-average range, and efficient heat-pump heating and water heating in a mild winter might add a few thousand more, landing the total somewhere around 13,000 to 16,000 kWh a year — averaging out to roughly 1,100 to 1,300 kWh a month, but with the months anything but even. Now take a cold-climate home, especially one still using electric resistance heat: winter heating alone can add many thousands of kilowatt-hours, and the annual total can climb well past 18,000 or even 24,000 kWh, with midwinter months spiking far above the summer floor. If you had to name a single rough midpoint for a whole-house all-electric total, something near 1,500 kWh a month — roughly 18,000 a year — sits in the middle of that spread for many homes, but treat that number as a starting stake in the ground, not a target, because the range around it is enormous. Treat these as illustrative brackets built on labeled assumptions, not promises about your house; the point is the wide spread and what drives it.

The seasonal shape is as important as the annual total, and it's the thing that most distinguishes an all-electric home from a gas one. A gas home's electricity use is relatively flat across the year, maybe bumping up in summer for air conditioning. An all-electric home's usage curve has a dramatic winter peak, because the biggest new load — space heating — is concentrated in the cold months. A home that hums along at 800 kWh a month in spring and fall might draw 2,000 kWh or more in January if it's heating with electricity in a cold climate. That's not a billing error; it's the furnace being an electric appliance now. Averaging it to a single monthly figure hides the reality that your bills will be modest in the shoulder seasons and heavy in winter, and possibly heavy again in summer if you run a lot of cooling. Understanding that curve is the difference between being surprised by a huge January bill and expecting it.

The most reliable way to get your own number is to work from your actual situation rather than averages, because the ranges above are so wide that a generic figure isn't much use for planning. If you already live in the home, your past twelve months of bills are the truth — read across a full year to capture the winter peak and summer floor, not just one convenient month. If you're planning a conversion from gas, you have to estimate the new loads you're adding: figure the efficiency of the heat pump or resistance heat you'll install, your climate's heating demand, and your household's hot-water and cooking habits, then add them to your current electric baseline. An [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) that lets you enter usage and your rate turns those kilowatt-hours into dollars so you can see what the all-electric total actually costs each month, which is often the number people really care about. And because the running cost of an all-electric home depends on both the kilowatt-hours and your local electricity rate, it's worth reading how those pieces combine in [what it costs to run an all-electric home each month](/blog/all-electric-home-running-cost/) before drawing conclusions about whether going gas-free saves money — the answer depends heavily on your rates and equipment.

## Why the total matters when you add solar

For anyone pairing an all-electric home with solar, the elevated usage total is the central design fact, and getting it right up front prevents the most common sizing mistake. Solar systems are sized to offset a home's electricity use, so a home using 16,000 or 20,000 kWh a year needs a substantially larger array than one using the 10,000-kWh national average. Size the system to a gas home's baseline and you'll fall well short of offsetting an all-electric load; the panels will cover the summer months and leave a large winter shortfall. This is why establishing your real all-electric usage — including the winter peak — has to come before sizing the array, and why the honest input to a [solar sizing tool](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) is your true annual kWh, not a rule-of-thumb average that assumes gas heat.

The seasonal mismatch makes it more interesting still. An all-electric home's usage peaks in winter, exactly when solar production is at its lowest — short days, low sun, and in many places snow on the panels. Solar production peaks in summer, when an all-electric home's usage may be lower (unless heavy air conditioning fills the gap). That timing mismatch means an array sized to cover your annual total will typically overproduce in summer and underproduce in winter, and how that shakes out on your bill depends entirely on your utility's net-metering or export rules — whether summer surplus banks against winter draw at full value or is credited at a lower export rate. In a full-retail net-metering market, an all-electric home can size solar to its annual total and let summer overproduction carry the winter deficit. Where exports are credited below retail, the winter shortfall costs more than the summer surplus earns, and the offset math gets harder. None of this makes solar a poor fit for an all-electric home — often the opposite, since a bigger electric bill means bigger potential savings — but it does mean the sizing has to start from an accurate, seasonally aware picture of a usage total that's simply larger and spikier than a gas home's. Get that number right, and everything downstream — the bill, the array, the payback — is built on solid ground rather than on an average that was never meant to describe a house with no gas line.
