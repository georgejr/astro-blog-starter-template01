"This system offsets 100% of your usage" is one of the most common lines in a solar proposal, and one of the most reliably misread. Homeowners hear "100%" and picture a zero-dollar electric bill stretching out forever. What the number actually describes is narrower and more technical than that hopeful mental image: it's how much of your annual electricity *consumption* the system is designed to produce, measured in energy, not dollars. Getting that distinction right is the difference between a proposal you genuinely understand and a nasty surprise on your first annual statement — the moment a homeowner who was promised "100% offset" opens a bill that is emphatically not zero and wonders whether they were misled. Usually they weren't. They just took an energy figure and read it as a dollar figure, and no one corrected the translation.

## Offset is a production ratio, not a bill

The offset percentage in a quote is almost always a usage offset: the system's expected annual production divided by your annual consumption. A 100% offset means the panels are sized to generate, over a full year, roughly as many kilowatt-hours as your home uses in that year. An 80% offset means they'll cover about four-fifths of your kilowatt-hours, and you'll still buy the remaining fifth from the utility. The crucial thing to notice is what that ratio is measured in — kilowatt-hours, a unit of energy, not dollars, a unit of money. It's an energy target, and energy balancing out over a year is simply not the same event as money balancing out on every bill. Those two things drift apart for reasons that come down to how utilities actually structure what they charge you, and there are three of them worth understanding in turn, because together they explain nearly every gap between the offset you were quoted and the bill you receive.

The first reason is that fixed charges don't disappear. Almost every utility bill includes charges that have nothing to do with how many kilowatt-hours you consumed — a monthly service or customer charge, connection fees, and sometimes a minimum bill floor. These exist to pay for the poles, wires, transformers, meters, and billing infrastructure that keep you connected to the grid, and staying grid-tied means paying them whether your panels covered 90% of your usage or 110% of it. A home with a flawless 100% usage offset still receives a bill; it's just a much smaller one, now dominated by these fixed lines that solar was never able to touch in the first place. This is the single most common reason the promised zero never arrives, and why the bill after going solar surprises people. The full anatomy of why the bill never quite reaches zero, even with a perfectly sized system, is the whole subject of [why your electric bill isn't $0 after going solar](/blog/electric-bill-after-solar/).

The second reason is the timing of production and use. Your panels make their power at midday, in a concentrated burst around solar noon, while your home uses plenty of its electricity in the morning, the evening, and overnight, when the panels are producing little or nothing. What happens to that mismatch — the surplus you generate at noon and the deficit you run at 8 p.m. — depends entirely on your utility's compensation rules, and this is where two homes with identical 100% offsets can end up with very different bills. Under traditional net metering, the kilowatt-hours you export at midday bank at close to retail value and offset the kilowatt-hours you pull back later in the evening, so a 100% usage offset comes close to canceling the energy charges on the bill. The grid acts as a nearly costless battery, and the timing mismatch mostly washes out. [Net metering explained](/blog/net-metering-explained/) walks through exactly how that credit mechanism operates. But under net billing or avoided-cost export rules, the story changes sharply: the kilowatt-hours you export are credited at *less* than what you pay to import them, often much less. In that world, exporting 400 kilowatt-hours during the day and later importing 400 kilowatt-hours at night does not net to zero dollars, even though it nets perfectly to zero energy. You generated as much as you used, and you still owe money, because the export credit was worth a fraction of the import price.

The third reason is the annual reconciliation, or true-up. Many solar tariffs don't settle your account monthly; they run a rolling tally across the year and reconcile it once, typically on an anniversary date. Your monthly production and usage swing hard with the seasons — a comfortable surplus through the long summer days, a deep deficit through the short, gray winter ones — and the true-up reckons the whole year at once rather than month by month. A system sized to exactly 100% on paper can still owe a modest amount at true-up if the export credit values or the seasonal timing didn't fall in your favor, since a surplus banked cheaply in summer may not fully cover a deficit bought back dearly in winter. How that year-end settlement works, and why it can produce a bill even for a well-sized system, is detailed in [the annual true-up bill](/blog/solar-true-up-bill/).

Stack those three effects together and the picture is consistent: a fixed charge the offset can never touch, a timing mismatch whose cost depends entirely on your export rules, and an annual settlement that can tilt against you if the seasons and credit values didn't cooperate. None of the three is a flaw in your system or a mistake by your installer. They're features of how utilities bill, and they'd apply to any solar array producing exactly your annual usage. What they add up to is a simple caution worth carrying into every proposal you read: the offset percentage describes energy, your bill is denominated in money, and the distance between those two units is set by the rate rules of your particular utility rather than by the size of your array.

## Usage offset versus bill offset, and reading a proposal like a skeptic

The cleaner way to think about all of this is to separate two ideas that a proposal tends to blur together, deliberately or not. Usage offset is the kilowatt-hour ratio — production over consumption — and it's what the headline "100%" almost always refers to. Bill offset is the dollar ratio — how much of your annual electricity *cost* the system actually erases. These two numbers are cousins, and under the right conditions they're nearly identical, but under other perfectly common conditions they diverge by a wide margin, and the divergence always runs the same direction: bill offset lags usage offset, never the reverse.

Under full-retail net metering, the two ratios sit close together. Every exported kilowatt-hour is worth about what an imported one costs, so canceling your energy in kilowatt-hours nearly cancels it in dollars, and the only meaningful gap left is the fixed charges. A 100% usage offset in that environment might translate to a bill offset in the high nineties once those fixed lines are accounted for — not literally zero, but close enough that the two numbers feel like the same thing. Under any rule that pays less for exports than it charges for imports, though, the gap opens up. A home with a 100% usage offset might see a bill offset closer to 85 or 90%, and sometimes lower, once both the fixed charges and the export haircut are folded in. The energy balanced perfectly; the money didn't, because the exports that balanced the energy were credited at a discount.

A short labeled example makes the fixed-charge piece concrete before the export discount even enters. Assume a $10 monthly service charge that solar simply cannot touch, because it's not an energy charge. That's $120 a year of bill you will pay regardless of how much you generate — so even a flawless 100% energy offset leaves at least that $120 on the table before any export-rate discount widens the gap. Layer an unfavorable export rate on top, where your midday surplus earns meaningfully less than your evening imports cost, and the annual dollar shortfall grows well beyond that fixed floor. The two effects stack: the fixed charge sets a floor the offset can't reach, and the export discount pushes the dollar result further below the energy result.

None of this makes a 100% offset a bad target to aim for — sizing a system to match your annual energy use is a perfectly sensible design goal, and for many net-metered homes it does land close to a near-zero energy charge. The honest translation of the phrase is simply "sized to match my yearly energy use," not "my bill goes away." For homes on export-favorable net metering, that distinction is small in dollar terms. For homes on export-unfavorable tariffs, it's large, and a buyer who doesn't grasp it going in is set up to feel cheated by a system that performed exactly as designed.

When a proposal quotes you an offset percentage, two questions cut straight to what it actually means for your wallet. The first is: offset of *what* — my kilowatt-hours, or my dollars? Push the salesperson for the dollar answer, because that's the one you actually pay every month. If they can only give you the kilowatt-hour figure, you now know to mentally discount it for fixed charges and, in an unfavorable export territory, for the export haircut on top. The second question is: what export and rate assumptions produced this number? A savings projection that quietly credits every exported kilowatt-hour at full retail, in a utility territory that plainly does not do that, is overstating your result — sometimes innocently, sometimes not, but overstating it either way. Asking to see the export rate the model used is one of the fastest ways to catch a proposal that's leaning on assumptions your utility won't honor.
