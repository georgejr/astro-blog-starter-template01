"This system offsets 100% of your usage" is one of the most common lines in a solar proposal, and one of the most misread. Homeowners hear "100%" and picture a zero-dollar electric bill forever. What the number actually describes is narrower and more technical: how much of your annual electricity *consumption* the system is designed to produce. Getting the distinction right is the difference between a proposal you understand and a nasty surprise on your first true-up.

## Offset is a production ratio, not a bill

The offset percentage in a quote is almost always a **usage offset**: the system's expected annual production divided by your annual consumption. A 100% offset means the panels are sized to generate, over a full year, roughly as many kilowatt-hours as your home uses. An 80% offset means they'll cover about four-fifths of your kilowatt-hours and you'll still buy the rest.

Notice what that definition is measured in — kilowatt-hours, not dollars. It's an energy target. And energy balancing out over a year is not the same as money balancing out on every bill, for reasons that come down to how utilities actually charge you.

## Why 100% usage offset rarely means a $0 bill

Three things stand between "produced as much as I used" and "owe nothing."

### Fixed charges don't disappear

Almost every utility bill includes charges that have nothing to do with how many kilowatt-hours you consume — a monthly service or customer charge, connection fees, sometimes minimum bills. Those exist to pay for the poles, wires, meters, and billing that connect you to the grid, and staying grid-tied means paying them whether your panels covered 90% or 110% of your usage. A 100%-offset home still gets a bill; it's just a much smaller one, dominated by these fixed lines. Why the bill never quite hits zero is the whole subject of [why your electric bill isn't $0 after going solar](/blog/electric-bill-after-solar/).

### The timing of production and use

Panels make power at midday; your home uses plenty of it at night. What happens to that mismatch depends on your utility's compensation rules. Under traditional net metering, exported kilowatt-hours bank at close to retail value and offset what you pull back later, so a 100% usage offset comes close to canceling the energy charges — [net metering explained](/blog/net-metering-explained/) walks through that mechanism. But under net billing or avoided-cost export rules, the kilowatt-hours you export are credited at *less* than what you pay to import them. In that world, exporting 400 kWh and later importing 400 kWh does not net to zero dollars, even though it nets to zero energy.

### The annual reconciliation

Many solar tariffs settle up once a year in a true-up. Your monthly production and usage swing with the seasons — surplus in summer, deficit in winter — and the [annual true-up bill](/blog/solar-true-up-bill/) reconciles the whole year at once. A system sized to exactly 100% on paper can still owe a modest amount at true-up if the credit values or seasonal timing didn't fall your way.

## Usage offset versus bill offset

The cleaner way to think about it is to separate two ideas the proposal tends to blur:

- **Usage offset** is the kWh ratio — production over consumption. It's what the "100%" almost always refers to.
- **Bill offset** is the dollar ratio — how much of your annual electricity *cost* the system erases.

Under full-retail net metering, the two are close. Under any rule that pays less for exports than it charges for imports, bill offset lags usage offset — a home with a 100% usage offset might see a bill offset closer to 85–90% once fixed charges and export haircuts are accounted for. A short labeled example: assume a $10 monthly service charge that solar can't touch. That's $120 a year of bill you'll pay regardless, so even a flawless 100% energy offset leaves at least that on the table — before any export-rate discount widens the gap further.

None of this makes a 100% offset a bad target. It just means the honest translation is "sized to match my yearly energy use," not "my bill goes away." For many net-metered homes that still lands close to a near-zero energy charge; for homes on export-unfavorable tariffs, the dollar result is meaningfully short of the energy result.

## Reading an offset figure like a skeptic

When a proposal quotes an offset percentage, ask two questions. First: offset of what — my kilowatt-hours, or my dollars? Push for the dollar answer, since that's the one you actually pay. Second: what export and rate assumptions produced it? A savings projection that quietly credits every exported kWh at full retail, in a territory that doesn't do that, is overstating your result.
