Put 10 kilowatt-hours into a home battery and you will not get 10 back out. You'll get somewhere around 8.5 to 9. The missing kilowatt-hour or so didn't vanish — it turned into heat during the round trip of charging the battery and later discharging it. That ratio, energy out divided by energy in, is the battery's round-trip efficiency, and it's one of the more useful numbers on a spec sheet precisely because it's one that shoppers so rarely look at. A battery advertised at 90 percent round-trip efficiency quietly hands back only nine-tenths of everything you store in it — every single cycle, for the entire life of the system. That small, permanent haircut is easy to overlook when you're comparing headline capacities and warranties, and easy to be surprised by later when the stored energy you counted on turns out to be a bit less than you thought.

The number matters because it functions as a tax on stored energy, and it compounds over years of daily cycling. Every kilowatt-hour that takes a detour through the battery — rather than being consumed the instant your panels make it — pays this toll on the way through. Understanding exactly where the loss comes from does two things at once: it explains why the figure varies from one battery to the next, and it clarifies why the number should nudge how you actually use the battery once it's installed.

## Where the energy actually goes, and why the number varies

Round-trip efficiency is the product of losses at three stages, and it helps to see them separately because they stack on top of one another rather than substituting for each other. The first stage is conversion. Solar panels and batteries both work in direct current, but your house runs on alternating current, so energy stored from solar and later used in the home has to pass through inverters that convert between the two. Each conversion is 95-plus percent efficient on its own, which sounds negligible, but a battery that charges from AC and later discharges to AC pays that toll twice — a couple of percent each way — and the two bites add up. This is also why the wiring architecture matters more than most buyers realize: a DC-coupled battery that stores solar energy without first converting it to AC skips a conversion step compared with an AC-coupled one, which is part of why two otherwise similar setups can post noticeably different round-trip numbers. The broader picture of how a battery moves energy all the way from panel to outlet, and where those conversions sit in the chain, is laid out in [how home solar batteries work](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/). The second stage is the chemistry itself. Forcing ions into the cells during charging and pulling them back out during discharge is not a perfectly reversible process; the cell's internal resistance turns a small fraction of the energy into heat in both directions. This is the irreducible core of the loss — a physical property of the battery rather than a design flaw anyone could engineer away. The third stage is the overhead the system spends simply being ready to work. A home battery runs a controller, cooling or heating for its cells, and communications hardware, and all of that housekeeping draws a trickle of power around the clock. Over a full charge-and-discharge cycle, some of your stored energy quietly pays for the system to keep itself alive, and in cold climates the heating draw in particular can become a meaningful slice, which is one reason the same battery can post a slightly lower real-world efficiency in winter than its spec-sheet figure suggests. Here is roughly how a typical round trip spends its losses; treat the figures as illustrative shares of a modern lithium home battery's total loss, not exact specifications:

| Loss stage | What causes it | Rough share of the loss |
|---|---|---|
| Inverter conversion | DC-to-AC and back | About a third to half |
| Cell chemistry | Internal resistance, heat | About a third |
| System overhead | Controls, thermal management, standby | The remainder, larger in cold |

Not all batteries post the same figure, and the differences between them are real rather than marketing noise. Chemistry is one driver. Today's common lithium home batteries — both the LFP and NMC types — land in a broadly similar high-80s-to-low-90s percent band, but they aren't identical, and the trade-offs that separate the two chemistries, efficiency among them, are covered in [LFP vs NMC home batteries](/blog/lfp-vs-nmc-home-batteries/). Older lead-acid chemistries sit noticeably lower on efficiency, which is a large part of why lithium displaced them for daily-cycling home use in the first place. Operating conditions move the number too, sometimes more than the chemistry does. A battery worked hard — charged and discharged quickly at high power — loses more to internal resistance than one cycled gently, because resistive losses climb with current. Temperature bites at both extremes: cold cells are inherently less efficient and demand heating, hot cells demand cooling, and both of those draws come straight out of your stored energy. And the way a spec is *measured* varies from one manufacturer to the next, so a headline number tested under ideal lab conditions may run a few points better than what you'll actually see across a real year in a real garage. It also helps to keep round-trip efficiency mentally separate from two other numbers it's easily confused with, because a spec sheet lists all three and they measure different things. Round-trip efficiency is about *loss in transit* — how much of what you put in comes back out. Usable capacity, governed by the depth-of-discharge limit, is about *how much of the battery you're allowed to use* in the first place. And the warranty's throughput or cycle rating is about *how long the battery lasts*. A battery can score well on one and poorly on another, so reading them as a set rather than fixating on any one keeps the comparison honest. One last wrinkle: efficiency isn't perfectly fixed for the life of the battery. As cells age and their internal resistance creeps up, the round-trip figure tends to drift down a little, so the spec-sheet number is best understood as a fresh-from-the-factory figure that real-world conditions and years of cycling will nudge slightly lower rather than a constant you can bank on unchanged for a decade.

## What the loss actually costs you

Round-trip efficiency has a specific financial meaning: it's the discount applied to the value of every stored kilowatt-hour. Suppose your panels make surplus power in the afternoon that you store and then use after sunset. At 90 percent round-trip efficiency, 10 kWh stored returns about 9 kWh of usable energy — you've lost the value of roughly 1 kWh to the round trip. Whether that loss stings at all depends entirely on the alternative you're comparing it against, and this is the part that turns an abstract efficiency percentage into a real decision. If the alternative was exporting that afternoon surplus to the grid for a low export rate and then buying power back later in the evening at a high retail rate, the round-trip loss is trivial next to the rate arbitrage you're capturing. Storing at 90 percent efficiency still beats selling low and buying high by a wide margin, so the 10 percent haircut barely dents the value of what the battery is doing for you. If instead your utility offers full net metering, where a kilowatt-hour exported is worth a kilowatt-hour imported, the calculation inverts — routing energy through a battery only *loses* you that 10 percent for no offsetting gain, because you could have simply banked the surplus on the grid at full value and drawn it back later. That's one of the clearest reasons batteries make less financial sense under generous net metering: the efficiency loss becomes pure cost with nothing to show for it. The number, in other words, is only ever as important as the price gap it's helping you exploit; the same 10 percent is nearly free in one rate structure and a dead weight in another.

There's a sizing implication that follows directly from the loss, and it's easy to get wrong in the other direction. Because you get back less than you put in, a battery's *usable delivered* energy is smaller than its stored energy, and that shortfall stacks on top of the depth-of-discharge limit that already keeps you from draining a battery completely — a separate but related derating explained in [depth of discharge for home battery owners](/blog/battery-depth-of-discharge/). The two effects compound: if you need a certain number of kilowatt-hours delivered to your loads overnight, you have to store somewhat more than that headline figure to cover the round-trip loss, and then account again for the fact that you can't run the battery to empty. A battery sizing tool like the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) folds both the efficiency haircut and the depth-of-discharge limit into its estimate, which is exactly why its recommended capacity tends to run a bit above the raw energy you think you need. That gap between "energy I want to use" and "capacity I have to buy" isn't the calculator being cautious for its own sake — it's the round trip and the depth-of-discharge floor showing up honestly in the sizing, the way they'll show up in real life the first time you lean on the battery through a long evening.

Working a concrete case makes the arbitrage point land, because the same 10 percent loss can be nearly free or genuinely wasteful depending on the rates around it. Suppose — as illustration, not as anyone's real tariff — your utility pays $0.06 for a kilowatt-hour you export in the afternoon and charges you $0.30 for one you import in the evening. Store 10 kWh of afternoon surplus at 90 percent efficiency and you get back 9 kWh after dark, worth $2.70 at the retail rate you avoid paying. Had you simply exported that same 10 kWh instead, it would have earned $0.60. The round trip cost you the value of 1 kWh, about $0.30, to capture a swing worth far more than that, so the efficiency loss is a rounding error against the arbitrage. Now run it under full net metering, where an exported kilowatt-hour is worth the same $0.30 you'd pay to import one. Storing 10 kWh to get back 9 leaves you a full kilowatt-hour worse off than just banking the surplus on the grid, because the grid would have returned all 10 at full value. Same battery, same 90 percent, opposite conclusion — the number's importance is set entirely by the price gap it's working within. Stretch either scenario across a year of daily cycling and the pattern compounds: under a wide arbitrage spread the small daily loss stays trivial against large daily savings, while under net metering the small daily loss accumulates into a real annual cost with nothing earned against it, which is why the same efficiency figure can be a footnote in one home and a genuine mark against storage in another.

## The number in perspective

Round-trip efficiency is worth knowing, but it isn't worth obsessing over, and it's easy to let a single spec-sheet percentage loom larger in a buying decision than it deserves to. A few percentage points of difference between two otherwise comparable modern batteries will not, on their own, decide whether storage makes sense for your home. The factors that actually dominate that decision sit elsewhere: your export rates and rate structure, your priorities during an outage, the chemistry and warranty of the battery, and above all the system's cost. A battery that's a point or two more efficient but meaningfully more expensive, or backed by a weaker warranty, is not the better buy just because its round-trip figure reads higher. Efficiency is one line in a longer ledger, and treating it as the deciding line is a good way to optimize the wrong thing.

What the number does deserve is honesty, and that's the real reason to understand it. A home battery is a slightly leaky bucket. It hands back about nine-tenths of what you pour into it, reliably, cycle after cycle, and any projection that quietly treats stored energy as free and lossless is overstating the battery's value by exactly the amount of the loss. When a savings estimate assumes every kilowatt-hour you store comes back whole, it's building a small, compounding error into every year of the forecast — an error that grows with how heavily you cycle the battery. Folding the roughly 10 percent haircut into your expectations from the start corrects that, and it's not a discouraging move so much as a clarifying one. The battery still does valuable work; it just does slightly less of it than a naive reading of its capacity would imply. Aim the stored energy at the biggest price gap you can find — the wider the spread between what you'd export at and what you'd buy back at, the more the efficiency loss disappears into the arbitrage — and the number moves from a hidden surprise to a planned-for line in the math. If round-trip efficiency shouldn't dominate the decision, it's fair to ask what should, and the honest answer is a short list of bigger levers. The system's installed cost and the warranty behind it come first, because they set the floor and ceiling on whether storage can pay for itself at all. The rate structure comes next — the export and retail prices that decide how valuable stored energy is in the first place, and, as the arbitrage case showed, whether the efficiency loss even matters. Your outage priorities matter enormously if backup power is part of why you want the battery, since a battery bought mainly to keep the lights on during a blackout is being judged on resilience rather than on cents per kilowatt-hour. And the chemistry choice carries its own cluster of trade-offs around longevity, safety, and cold-weather behavior that dwarf a point or two of round-trip efficiency. Against that list, efficiency is a tiebreaker, not a headline, and a buyer who leads with it risks optimizing a small term while a large one goes unexamined.

What the number earns is not neglect but honesty in the math. A home battery is a slightly leaky bucket that reliably hands back about nine-tenths of what you pour in, and the only real mistake is to forget that when projecting savings — to model stored energy as if it came back whole and quietly overstate the battery's value by the size of the loss, compounded across every cycle of its life. Account for the roughly 10 percent haircut openly, point the stored energy at the widest price gap you can find so the loss disappears into the arbitrage, and weigh it against cost, rates, chemistry, and your reasons for wanting storage at all. Doing that also inoculates you against a common sales move, where a glossy round-trip number gets waved around as a headline reason to choose one battery over another. A high efficiency figure is a fine thing, but on its own it tells you almost nothing about whether the battery is worth buying, and a shopper who has internalized where the loss comes from and how little it moves the overall math is much harder to steer with a single flattering spec. Understood that way, round-trip efficiency stops being a gotcha buried in the fine print and becomes what it should be: a modest, known cost you account for openly, weigh against the far larger levers of rate structure and price, and then set aside, having given it exactly the weight it earns and no more.
