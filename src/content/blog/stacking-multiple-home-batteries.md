"Can I just add another battery later?" is one of the most sensible questions a homeowner asks before buying storage, and it usually gets a fuzzy answer. The honest version is: often yes, but *what* you gain by stacking depends on which limit you're actually hitting — and most people are asking about the wrong one. Adding a second battery reliably buys you more stored energy. Whether it buys you more backup *power* is a separate question with a different answer, and conflating the two is the source of most disappointment.

That conflation is the thing to untangle first, because it decides whether stacking even solves your problem.

## The two things "more batteries" can mean

A home battery is described by two numbers that do different jobs, and the mechanics are worth holding clearly — they're the same two numbers explained in [kWh versus kW for home batteries](/blog/battery-kwh-vs-kw/).

**Capacity, in kilowatt-hours (kWh)**, is how much energy the battery stores — the size of the tank, which sets how long it can run your loads.

**Power, in kilowatts (kW)**, is how fast it can deliver that energy — the size of the pipe, which sets how many things it can run *at once*.

When someone says "I want more battery," they might mean either. "My battery runs out overnight" is a capacity problem. "My battery can't start the air conditioner" is a power problem — the kind where the question is whether the system can drive a large load at all. Stacking helps both, but not equally, and knowing which you're solving for tells you whether adding a unit will actually fix it.

## Capacity stacks; power has a ceiling

Adding energy capacity by stacking is the easy, well-supported case. Most modern home battery lines are explicitly modular: you wire additional units alongside the first, and their kilowatt-hours simply add up. Two 13.5 kWh units give you 27 kWh of storage. That directly extends runtime — how long your house rides through an outage before it's spent. If your problem is that the battery empties before morning, more units are a clean answer.

Power is the constrained side. How much power your battery bank can deliver at once is limited by the inverter and battery hardware, not just by how many kilowatt-hours you've stacked. In some systems each battery unit contributes its own power output, so adding units raises the ceiling. In others, a central inverter or gateway caps the total power regardless of how many batteries hang behind it — stack all the capacity you like, and the pipe stays the same width. This is exactly why "more batteries" doesn't automatically mean "now I can run the whole house": if the bottleneck is the inverter's power rating, adding storage behind it changes runtime, not simultaneous load. Deciding how much power you truly need starts with what you want energized, the [whole-home versus partial-backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/) decision, since backing up the entire house demands far more power than protecting a handful of essential circuits.

The mechanics of where power actually comes from — the inverter, the gateway, the cells — are laid out in [how home batteries work](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/), and they're what determine whether your particular system gains power or only energy when you stack.

## Can you add units later?

The "add one later" plan works only if the original installation left room for it, in three distinct senses.

- **Electrical headroom.** The inverter, gateway, and panel provisions have to accommodate another unit. A system installed with expansion in mind reserves that capacity; one installed to exactly fit today's battery may not.
- **Physical space.** Batteries need wall or floor space, code-mandated clearances, and a suitable location. A second unit needs somewhere to live that still satisfies those rules.
- **Product availability.** The specific battery model has to still be sold years later, or be compatible with whatever replaced it — which brings up the compatibility trap.

If you suspect you'll expand, the cheapest time to make it easy is at the first install: ask the installer to size the inverter and gateway for the eventual bank and to leave the physical and electrical provisions in place. Retrofitting expansion headroom later can cost more than it would have to build in up front.

### Mixing brands and models

Here's where flexibility narrows sharply. Home batteries are generally *not* interchangeable across brands. A battery system is an ecosystem — the units, the inverter, the gateway, and the software are engineered to work together — and you typically can't bolt a different manufacturer's battery onto an existing system and expect it to integrate. Even within one brand, mixing an older model with a newer generation isn't always supported.

The practical consequence: the brand you choose today largely locks in your expansion path. If future stacking matters to you, weigh how committed each manufacturer is to a modular, forward-compatible line before you buy the first unit — that decision constrains your options more than almost anything else about the purchase.

## Common questions

**Is there a limit to how many batteries I can stack?** Yes. Each product line specifies a maximum number of units per system, and the inverter or gateway imposes its own limits. There's a ceiling, and it's set by the hardware, not left open-ended.

**Will a second battery let me run bigger appliances?** Only if it raises the system's power rating. If your bottleneck is a central inverter's power cap, more capacity extends runtime but doesn't add the ability to run more at once.

**Can I add a battery from a different company?** Generally no. Expect to stay within one manufacturer's compatible ecosystem, which is why the initial brand choice shapes your future options.

**Should I just buy all the capacity up front?** Not necessarily. Buying only what you need now and stacking later is reasonable *if* the system is installed with expansion headroom — the key is planning for it at install, not committing to the full bank on day one.

Before you decide between one large battery and a plan to stack smaller ones, get concrete about how much capacity your outages and usage actually call for.
