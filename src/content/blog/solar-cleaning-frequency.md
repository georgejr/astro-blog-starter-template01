Most people who ask how often to clean solar panels are hoping for a tidy answer like "every three months" or "twice a year." The honest reply is that there isn't one, because the thing you're really asking about — how fast your panels get dirty enough to matter — depends almost entirely on where you live and what falls out of your particular sky. A rooftop in coastal Oregon and a rooftop in the Arizona desert are running two completely different experiments, and a schedule that makes sense for one would be either paranoid or negligent for the other. The useful way to think about cleaning frequency is not as a fixed interval but as a response to a handful of local conditions: how much dust and pollen your air carries, how often it rains, how steep your roof is, and how much a lost percent of production actually costs you.

That framing matters because cleaning has a real cost of its own — your time and a little risk if you do it yourself, or a service fee if you hire it out — and soiling losses for a typical residential array are usually modest. Across a lot of ordinary suburban and rural sites, the output lost to accumulated grime lands somewhere in the range of 1% to 5% over a season before rain resets it, which means the money at stake is often smaller than people assume. The goal isn't a spotless panel. It's keeping soiling losses low enough that they never justify the effort of chasing them, and knowing the specific signs that your site is the exception where cleaning genuinely pays.

## What actually dirties a panel, and what rain quietly handles

A solar panel is a sheet of glass tilted toward the sky, and glass tilted toward the sky collects whatever the air deposits on it: fine dust, pollen in spring, soot from traffic or wildfire smoke, salt spray near the coast, bird droppings, and in some yards a slow film of organic grime that feeds moss or lichen at the edges. Not all of these matter equally. A uniform, thin layer of dust costs you surprisingly little, because light still reaches the cells through it and the loss is spread evenly across the array. The soiling that actually bites into production tends to be the concentrated, opaque kind — a cluster of bird droppings sitting on the lower cells of a panel, or a band of caked pollen and dust that never gets rinsed because the roof is shallow and the rain just isn't heavy enough to sheet it off.

Here's the part that changes the whole calculation: for most homes, rain does the cleaning for free. Panels are mounted at a tilt precisely so that water runs down the glass and carries loose dirt with it, and a good soaking rain every week or two keeps a typical array within a percent or two of clean without anyone lifting a finger. This is why the cleaning question is really a rainfall question in disguise. In a climate with regular year-round precipitation, the natural rinse cycle handles the routine soiling, and the only interventions worth making are for the things rain can't fix — a stubborn dropping, a corner colonized by lichen, or a stretch of smoke-heavy weeks when nothing falls from the sky. The broader case for when rain is and isn't enough is worked through in [whether solar panels need cleaning at all](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/), and for a lot of readers the surprising takeaway is that their panels need far less attention than a cleaning-service flyer would suggest.

Roof tilt quietly shapes all of this. A steeply pitched roof sheds water fast and dries quickly, so dirt has less time to bond and rain clears it more completely. A low-slope or nearly flat array is the opposite: water pools and evaporates in place, leaving behind the mineral residue it was carrying, and dust settles more readily because gravity isn't helping it slide off. Two identical homes in the same town can have genuinely different cleaning needs purely because one has a 30-degree roof and the other sits at 10 degrees. If your array is shallow, you should expect to intervene more often than a neighbor with a steeper pitch, and you should watch the lower edge of each panel especially, because that's where the runoff residue concentrates. Ground-mounted arrays and low-tilt commercial-style layouts are the classic cases where soiling builds faster than rooftop owners expect, and where a periodic rinse earns its keep.

The last big variable is what's in your air, and that's where climate stops being an abstraction. Dry regions with a lot of loose soil, agricultural areas during tilling and harvest, homes downwind of a gravel road or construction, sites under heavy tree pollen in spring, and anywhere touched by wildfire smoke all load the glass faster than a humid, tree-shaded suburb with frequent rain. The type of grime differs too — desert dust behaves differently from Gulf Coast salt film, which behaves differently from Pacific Northwest moss — and each has its own rhythm. Matching your schedule to the specific grime your climate produces, rather than to a generic interval, is the whole game, and it's the subject of [how panel grime varies by climate](/blog/solar-panel-dirt-by-climate/).

## Reading your own climate to set a schedule that fits

Start by placing yourself honestly in one of a few broad situations, because your rainfall and dust profile does most of the work of setting your interval. If you live somewhere with reliable rain spread through the year — much of the East, the Midwest, the Pacific Northwest, the Southeast — your panels will spend most of their life self-cleaning, and a reasonable posture is to not schedule cleanings at all. Instead, watch your production data and inspect visually once or twice a year, cleaning only when you can actually see concentrated soiling or when the numbers show a persistent dip that a rain didn't recover. Many homeowners in wet climates go years without a real cleaning and lose almost nothing to soiling, because the sky is doing the maintenance. Paying a service to visit quarterly in that setting is spending real money to recover output you were never going to lose.

Dry and dusty climates are the other end, and they're where a deliberate schedule starts to make sense. In the desert Southwest, in agricultural valleys, and in any long dry season where weeks pass without meaningful rain, dust accumulates steadily with nothing to wash it away, and losses can climb past the low single digits into territory where a rinse pays for itself. In those places, cleaning once or twice during the dry season — timed to the end of a long rainless stretch, not to an arbitrary date — is a defensible routine. The key discipline is to tie the cleaning to conditions rather than the calendar: a rinse right before your sunniest, most productive months protects the output that's worth the most, while cleaning in the middle of a rainy stretch is wasted effort. Spring pollen deserves its own mention, because a heavy pollen season can lay down a visible yellow film almost overnight in some regions, and a single rinse once the pollen drop tapers off often restores the loss in one pass.

Coastal and smoke-prone sites are the situational cases that don't fit neatly into "wet" or "dry." Salt film near the ocean builds a haze that light rain doesn't always clear, and a wildfire smoke event can coat panels with fine ash that then cements in place if a light rain wets and dries it without fully rinsing it off. In both cases the right response is event-driven rather than scheduled: clean after a notable smoke episode once the air clears, and rinse coastal panels when you can see the haze dulling the glass, but don't build a rigid quarterly habit around conditions that only occur sometimes. The point throughout is that "how often" is the wrong question if you ask it in the abstract. The right question is "what does my glass look like, and what has the weather been doing," and the answer will vary from one part of the year to another even at a single house.

Safety and access quietly cap how often cleaning is worth doing regardless of climate. A single-story home with a gentle roof and a hose from the ground is easy to rinse, so the threshold for bothering is low. A steep, tall, or hard-to-reach roof is genuinely hazardous to climb, and the honest math there is that the small production gain from an extra cleaning rarely justifies putting yourself on a slick two-story roof — which is exactly when a professional or a long-reach ground-based tool earns its place. If you do get up there, the technique matters as much as the timing: cool glass, gentle water, soft tools, and no abrasives, all covered in the [step-by-step method for cleaning panels safely](/blog/how-to-clean-solar-panels/). Cleaning too aggressively or too often can scratch the anti-reflective coating and do more harm than the dirt ever would, which is another reason the restrained, condition-driven approach usually wins.

## Letting the numbers tell you when it's actually worth it

The cleanest way to cut through all the climate variables is to let your own production data make the call, because it measures the one thing you actually care about — lost output — directly. Nearly every modern system reports daily and monthly generation through a monitoring app, and soiling shows up there as a slow, sustained sag relative to what clear days should produce, one that a good rain visibly corrects. If you watch for that pattern over a season, you don't have to guess at intervals at all; the array tells you when it's dirty enough to matter. A dip that a rainstorm erases was never worth climbing for. A dip that persists through rain, or that keeps widening through a long dry stretch, is the signal that concentrated soiling or a genuine dry-season buildup is costing you enough to act on.

Translating that lost percentage into dollars is what tells you whether to bother, and it's a small calculation worth doing once for your own situation. Take your system's annual production and your electricity rate to find what a full year of generation is worth, then ask what a few percent of that represents over the weeks the soiling actually persists. In a wet climate where soiling costs maybe 1% of annual output for a few weeks before rain resets it, the dollar value of a cleaning is often a handful of dollars — far less than a service call, and not worth a risky climb. In a dry climate where 4% or 5% accumulates and sits for months of the sunniest season, the same exercise can justify a cleaning or two a year comfortably. You can ballpark your system's annual output and its value with the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), and if you want to see how a recurring maintenance cost folds into the long-run economics of the system, the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) is the place to test whether a cleaning habit meaningfully changes your return. In most cases it doesn't move the needle, which is itself the useful finding.

What this reframing gives you is permission to stop worrying about a schedule and start responding to evidence. The homeowner who cleans on a rigid quarterly calendar in a rainy climate is usually spending time and money to fix a problem that rain already solved. The desert homeowner who never cleans through a six-month dry season is quietly leaving a few percent of their sunniest production on the table. Neither is following a rule — they're both ignoring their own conditions. Watch the sky, watch the app, glance at the glass a couple of times a year, and clean when those three things agree that it's worth it. For the large majority of American homes, that adds up to far less cleaning than the question implies, and for the dusty minority it means a couple of well-timed rinses aimed at the months that pay. Either way, the schedule that fits your roof is the one your own rainfall and dust wrote for you, not the one on a calendar.
