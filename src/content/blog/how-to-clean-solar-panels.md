A typical rooftop wash recovers a few percent of production — not the 20 or 30 percent that some cleaning services quietly imply when they knock on the door. That single number should shape how much effort, risk, and money you are willing to put into the job. For most homes in most climates, cleaning panels is a light, occasional task best done from the ground with a pole, not a reason to climb onto a wet, sloped roof with a bucket and a garden hose.

Whether your panels need cleaning at all is the prior question, and rain handles far more of it than people expect; the honest case for and against a wash is in [do solar panels need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/). What follows is the how — for the times a wash is genuinely warranted, when there is a visible film, a scattering of bird droppings, caked pollen after a heavy spring, or a layer of ash after a fire season. Knowing how to clean solar panels safely matters more than knowing how to clean them thoroughly, because the thorough version is rarely worth the fall risk it invites, and the difference in recovered energy between a careful ground wash and a white-glove rooftop scrub is almost nothing.

## Safety decides the whole job, and the math favors staying on the ground

The single most important decision happens before you ever touch a panel: do this from the ground if there is any way to do it from the ground. A fall from a roof causes vastly more harm than a slightly dirty array ever could, and wet glass on a pitched roof is genuinely, dangerously slick. When you weigh a serious injury against a wash that recovers only single-digit percentages of production, the arithmetic is not close. No amount of recovered energy justifies standing on a surface designed to shed water while you deliberately pour more water onto it. People fall off roofs cleaning gutters every year for far more tangible reasons than this; the panels do not deserve the same risk.

That logic points toward a few firm rules. Never clean panels while standing on a pitched roof in wet-soled shoes; if you cannot reach the glass from the ground with an extension tool, the right move is to hire someone who arrives with proper fall protection and does this work for a living. Keep water and tools away from wiring, conduit, and the roof edge, because while the panels themselves are sealed, the junction boxes and connectors tucked underneath them are not meant to be blasted or soaked. And work in the cool of early morning or evening rather than the middle of a sunny day — cold water hitting hot glass can thermally shock a panel and crack a cell, and a midday roof in summer gets hot enough to burn a hand, let alone to support someone moving carefully across it in wet shoes.

The practical sorting is straightforward once you accept the premise. If the array sits on a single-story home with a shallow pitch and you have stable footing on the ground around it, cleaning solar panels from the ground with a soft brush on an extension pole covers the whole job comfortably, and most residential arrays fall into exactly this category. If the array is on a steep roof, a second story, or anywhere you would need to stand on the slope itself to reach it, treat professional cleaning as the default and price it honestly against the tiny production it will actually recover. Sometimes the right answer is to leave lightly soiled panels alone and let the next rain do the work, which is both the safest option and, more often than homeowners assume, the most cost-effective one. A cleaning company that quotes you a few hundred dollars to recover a few dollars of monthly production is selling peace of mind, not a return on investment, and it is worth naming that plainly before you sign anything. The goal is to match the effort and the risk to a payoff that is, honestly, modest.

It is worth being precise about how modest that payoff usually is, because the number is what disciplines every other decision here. Field studies of soiling losses generally find that ordinary dust and pollen trim production by low single-digit percentages in most climates — enough to notice in the data, not enough to justify risk or expense. The dramatic exceptions are local and visible: a panel streaked with bird droppings, coated in construction dust, or filmed with wildfire ash can lose considerably more, but those are conditions you can see from the ground, and they are exactly the times a wash genuinely earns its keep. If your array does not look dirty, the odds are high that it is costing you very little, and a wash will recover very little in return. Treating cleaning as a response to visible, measured soiling rather than a scheduled ritual keeps the effort matched to a payoff that, for most homes most of the time, rounds to small.

When the roof genuinely rules out ground cleaning, hiring a professional is the sensible default, and it is worth pricing the job honestly against what it recovers before you book it. A crew with fall protection, extension equipment, and deionized water can clean a steep or high array safely in the time it would take you to talk yourself into a bad idea, and for a difficult roof that is money well spent once every year or two. Permanently mounted or robotic cleaning systems exist as well, mostly for large commercial arrays where the accumulated soiling loss across thousands of panels justifies the hardware; on a residential roof they rarely pencil out, because the few percent at stake cannot pay for a machine. For a house, the honest options are a pole from the ground, an occasional professional visit, or simply letting the rain do the work.

## What to use, what to avoid, and the wash itself

Solar glass is durable, but the anti-reflective coating on its surface is not indestructible, so the right toolkit is deliberately gentle and short. A garden hose does most of the real work, because plain water floats off the dust, pollen, and grit that account for the bulk of ordinary soiling. A soft brush or a microfiber cloth on an extension pole lets you reach the panels from the ground without pressing hard, and a squeegee or a soft brush-and-blade combo of the kind sold for panels or car glass handles the final pass. Soap enters the picture only if plain water fails — a few drops of mild dish soap well diluted in a bucket of water, never anything stronger. The best way to wash solar panels is almost always the least aggressive way that gets them clean, and the instinct to reach for something more powerful is exactly the instinct to resist.

The water itself matters more than people expect. Hard tap water dries into mineral spots that can dull the glass more than the dirt you just removed, which turns a cleaning into a step backward. If your area has hard water, rinse and then dry the glass with a squeegee or microfiber before it can spot, or use deionized water for a genuinely spot-free finish. In freezing weather, skip cleaning entirely and wait for a milder day, because water freezing on a panel is an efficient way to crack the glass you were trying to protect. The list of things that actively damage panels is short and worth taking seriously. Pressure washers are the worst offender, because high-pressure water can force its way past the panel's seals and delaminate it or crack the glass outright, so they should never come near an array no matter how tempting the reach and speed look. Abrasive pads, scrapers, and metal blades scratch the coating permanently, trading a temporary smudge for permanent damage. Harsh detergents, solvents, and ammonia-based cleaners can degrade the seals and coatings over time even when they leave the glass looking bright. And the thermal-shock rule cuts both ways: cold water on sun-baked glass and hot water on cold glass can each crack cells, which is one more reason the cool early morning is the right window.

With the tools and the water sorted, the wash itself follows a simple order that minimizes both risk and the chance of scratching anything. Begin by shutting the system down at the inverter, or by following your installer's specified shutdown procedure, so you are not cleaning energized equipment and can spot any issue safely while the panels are quiet. Then rinse first with plain water from the hose, letting it float off the loose dust, pollen, and grit before any brush touches the glass — this single step does most of the work on a typical residential array, and rinsing before brushing is what keeps you from dragging grit across the coating like sandpaper. Where residue remains after the rinse, brush gently with the soft brush on the pole, using light pressure and long, even strokes and letting the water and the soft bristles do the lifting rather than scrubbing hard at a spot. Stubborn deposits like dried bird droppings respond best to patience: dab on the diluted soap solution and give it a minute to soften the deposit before making a single gentle pass with the brush, rather than attacking it. Once the glass is clean, rinse thoroughly to carry away every trace of soap, because soap left to dry leaves its own film that dulls the panel much like the dirt did, and then dry the glass with a squeegee or microfiber if your water is hard. Finally, restart the system and confirm in your monitoring app that every panel or string comes back online, which doubles as a quick check that nothing was knocked loose. On a small array the whole job usually runs 20 to 40 minutes, and most of that is rinsing rather than scrubbing — a rhythm that tells you the effort is landing in the right place.

One habit prevents most cleaning mistakes: work the whole array in a single session on an overcast or early-morning day, rather than panel by panel under a climbing sun. Doing it all at once keeps the glass at a uniform, moderate temperature, avoids the thermal-shock risk entirely, and lets you rinse everything before any soap dries anywhere. It also means you handle the shutdown and restart once instead of repeatedly. The wash is not a job that rewards perfectionism — a gentle, even pass that lifts the film is the goal, and chasing every faint water spot invites exactly the hard scrubbing and midday timing the safe method is built to avoid.

## How often to bother, and how to prove the wash paid off

For most homes in the United States, once or twice a year is plenty, and in genuinely rainy regions natural rainfall may cover the entire need on its own, so a "cleaning schedule" often amounts to little more than watching and waiting. A wash earns its keep more reliably under specific conditions: long dry spells that let dust accumulate undisturbed, heavy pollen seasons, nearby farm or construction dust, coastal salt film, or a settling of wildfire ash after a bad fire season. Homes under trees with heavy bird traffic are the main exception where droppings justify more frequent spot-cleaning, because a single opaque spot can shade an entire cell string and drag down more production than its small size suggests. Outside those cases, cleaning on a fixed calendar mostly manufactures work that the weather would have done for free.

Rather than guessing whether the panels were dirty enough to bother with, you can measure it, which is the difference between maintenance and superstition. Note your daily production on a clear day before cleaning, then compare a similarly clear day afterward. If the array was genuinely soiled, you will see a modest but real bump; if the numbers barely move, the panels were not dirty enough to matter and you have learned that you can safely stretch the interval next time. That measured approach — cleaning when the data says the panels are actually costing you production, not when a calendar reminder fires or a flyer appears in the mailbox — keeps your effort proportional to the payoff and stops a cleaning service from talking you into a monthly contract for a few dollars of recovered energy. Your monitoring gives you the before-and-after baseline directly, and a quick estimate from the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) of what a clean array of your size should produce on a given day gives you a second reference point to judge the result against, so you are comparing your real output to a reasonable expectation rather than to a guess.

Where you live shapes the interval more than any general rule. In much of the rainy East and the Pacific Northwest, rainfall alone keeps panels clean enough that a deliberate wash is rarely necessary, and homeowners there can mostly watch the production data and act only if it sags. In arid regions with long dry spells — the desert Southwest, the Central Valley, anywhere dust settles for months without rain to rinse it — a wash once or twice through the dry season can be worth the trouble, because no rain is coming to do it for free. Agricultural areas, coastal zones with salt spray, and neighborhoods downwind of construction all soil faster than average and justify a closer eye. The point is not a universal schedule but a local one, set by how much your particular climate does for you and how much it leaves for you to do.

It also pays to make sure a drop in production is actually dirt before you climb up to fix it, because soiling is only one of several things that dim an array. Shading that was not there when the system went in — a tree that has grown, a new structure next door — mimics soiling in the data, but no amount of washing cures it. A failing panel or a tripped optimizer shows up as a persistent shortfall on one part of the array that cleaning will not touch. And the slow, uniform decline of ordinary degradation looks nothing like the sudden, patchy loss that dirt causes. A wash is the right response only when the loss tracks with visible soiling and recovers once you clean; if it does not, the problem sits somewhere the hose cannot reach.

It helps to keep the ceiling in view, because it governs how much this whole exercise is worth. Cleaning is maintenance, not restoration. A wash recovers soiling losses and nothing more. It does nothing about the slow, permanent decline every panel undergoes at a fraction of a percent per year, which is a separate and unavoidable story told in [solar panel degradation rates](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/). Kept reasonably clean and otherwise left alone, panels hold up remarkably well across decades, as covered in [how long solar panels last](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/) — which is really the point of a wash, and the reason not to overinvest in it. You are not chasing a dramatic recovery or extending the life of the hardware; you are simply keeping ordinary dirt from stealing the few percent that good glass would happily deliver on its own, with the least risk and effort the job allows. Treat it that way and cleaning becomes what it should be: a twenty-minute chore a couple of times a year, done safely from the ground, and skipped entirely whenever the rain has already handled it.
