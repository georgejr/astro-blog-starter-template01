If you have equity in your home and you're financing solar rather than paying cash, you're choosing between two very different ways to borrow the same money, and they're priced by completely different logic. A dedicated solar loan is a product built and sold around the installation itself, usually arranged through the installer, marketed on a low monthly payment and often an eye-catching interest rate. A home equity line of credit, or HELOC, is a general-purpose loan secured by your house that you could spend on anything — a kitchen remodel, a car, or in this case an array of panels. Both will get the system paid for. But the total amount you hand over by the time the debt is gone can differ by thousands of dollars, and the reasons why are not obvious from the headline rate each one advertises. Comparing them honestly means looking past the sticker rate to how each loan is actually constructed.

The single most important thing to understand going in is that a solar loan's advertised interest rate frequently hides a fee, and a HELOC's rate usually doesn't. That asymmetry is the crux of the whole comparison. Solar lenders offering a strikingly low rate — sometimes near zero — commonly recover the cost of that subsidized financing by marking up the system's price, a charge that can run anywhere from 10% to 30% of the system cost and gets baked silently into what you finance. A HELOC, by contrast, tends to show you its true rate more plainly, though it comes with its own costs and a serious feature that a solar loan lacks: it's secured by your home. Weighing the two is less about which advertised number is lower and more about which loan's fully-loaded cost, including the fees each one buries in a different place, comes out ahead for your situation.

## Two loans, two ways of hiding the cost

A solar loan is typically originated at the point of sale, arranged by the installer through a finance partner, and its defining feature is the low monthly payment it's designed to produce. Many are structured with attractive promotional rates, and the very lowest-rate offers are where you have to look hardest, because a lender can't actually lend money at near-zero for free. The way that circle gets squared is the dealer fee: the finance company charges the installer a fee to buy the loan at that low rate, and the installer folds that fee into the price you're quoted, so the "cost" of the cheap financing is hidden inside a higher system price rather than shown as a higher interest rate. The result is that two quotes for the same physical system — one financed at a rock-bottom rate, one paid in cash — can have meaningfully different prices, with the financed version inflated to cover the dealer fee. You're paying the interest either way; it's just been moved from the rate column into the price column where it's harder to see. How large this fee gets and how to spot it in a quote is the whole subject of [what a solar dealer fee really costs you](/blog/solar-dealer-fee-explained/), and it's the first thing to interrogate on any low-rate solar loan.

This matters enormously for the comparison, because it means you can't compare a solar loan's rate to a HELOC's rate directly — you'd be comparing a rate that has a fee hidden behind it against one that mostly doesn't. The fair comparison is total dollars paid, or equivalently the true cost of the financing once the dealer fee is accounted for as what it is: a finance charge. A solar loan advertised at a very low rate but carrying a 20% dealer-fee markup may actually cost you more, all-in, than a HELOC at a seemingly higher rate with no such markup, because that 20% is real money added to your principal. The way to cut through it is to always ask the installer for the cash price versus the financed price. The gap between them is the dealer fee, and once you know that number you can evaluate the solar loan on its honest, fully-loaded cost rather than its marketing rate. An installer who won't disclose a cash price is telling you something by the refusal.

A HELOC is a different animal built on your home equity. It's a revolving line of credit secured by your house, usually with a variable interest rate tied to a benchmark that moves with the broader rate environment, and often structured with a draw period during which you can borrow and may pay interest only, followed by a repayment period when you pay down the principal. Its costs are more transparent in the rate but come with their own baggage: there are typically closing costs or fees to open the line, appraisal and other charges can apply, and because the rate is usually variable, your payment can rise or fall over time as benchmark rates move. That variability is a genuine consideration a fixed-rate solar loan doesn't carry — with a HELOC you're accepting some interest-rate risk in exchange for the transparency and, often, a competitive rate. The mechanics of financing a system this way, including how the draw-and-repay structure interacts with when your solar savings actually start, are worked through in [solar payback when you finance with a HELOC](/blog/solar-payback-with-heloc/), which is worth reading alongside this if the HELOC path appeals to you.

There's a potential tax wrinkle with a HELOC that's worth flagging carefully rather than overstating. Interest on home equity debt may be tax-deductible in some circumstances when the borrowed money is used to substantially improve the home that secures the loan, which solar could qualify as — but the rules are specific, they depend on your overall tax situation, and they change, so this is firmly a "ask your tax professional" matter rather than something to bank on. A solar loan's interest generally doesn't carry that potential deductibility. Where it applies, deductible interest can shift the comparison in the HELOC's favor, but you should never assume it applies to your situation without confirming it, and you certainly shouldn't let a possible deduction be the deciding factor before you've verified you'd actually benefit from it. Treat it as possible upside to investigate, not a given.

## Comparing the true totals, and the risk that isn't in the rate

The disciplined way to compare the two is to compute the total amount you'll pay over the full life of each loan — every payment, plus every fee — and set the two totals side by side, because that single number captures everything the headline rates obscure. For the solar loan, start from the financed price (which includes the dealer fee) and add up all the payments over the term. For the HELOC, start from the actual system price you'd draw, add the closing costs and fees, and project the payments, being honest that a variable rate means you're estimating rather than locking in a certain figure. When you do this, the dealer-fee effect often flips the intuitive ranking: the solar loan that looked cheaper on rate can total more in dollars because its principal was inflated, while the HELOC that looked pricier on rate can total less because there's no hidden markup on top of what you borrowed. The general framework for figuring out what any solar loan actually adds to your system's cost — separating the true financing charge from the sticker rate — is laid out in [what a solar loan really adds to your system's price](/blog/solar-loan-true-cost/), and it applies directly to building the total-cost figure you'll compare against the HELOC.

Running the numbers is where a calculator earns its keep, because the interaction of rate, term, fees, and the dealer markup is hard to intuit. Model the solar loan's payments and total cost with the [solar loan calculator](/solar-loan-calculator/), being sure to enter the financed principal that includes the dealer fee rather than the pre-fee price, so the total reflects what you'll really pay. Then put the two financing paths — and paying cash, if that's on the table — against each other with the [cash vs. loan vs. lease comparator](/solar-payment-comparison/) to see how the lifetime costs stack up in one view. The exercise usually clarifies quickly which structure is cheaper for your specific rates and terms, and it protects you from being sold on a monthly payment or a promotional rate that looks great in isolation but totals poorly. The lowest monthly payment and the lowest total cost are frequently not the same loan, and the whole point of the comparison is to keep your eye on the total.

One factor sits outside the arithmetic entirely and deserves its own weight: a HELOC is secured by your home, and a typical solar loan is not. That distinction is about risk, not cost, and it can matter more than a few hundred dollars of interest either way. With a HELOC, your house is collateral — if you fell into serious financial trouble and couldn't pay, the equity in your home is on the line in a way it isn't with an unsecured solar loan. For a homeowner with stable finances who's confident in their ability to make the payments, that risk is largely theoretical and the HELOC's cost advantages may well win. For a homeowner whose situation is less certain, or who simply doesn't want to attach their house to a solar purchase, the unsecured solar loan's separation from the home has real value even if it costs a bit more. This isn't a number you can plug into a calculator; it's a judgment about how much you value keeping your home out of the equation, and it legitimately points different households toward different answers.

The clean way to end up on the right side of this decision is to refuse to compare advertised rates and insist on comparing fully-loaded totals plus the risk profile. Force the solar loan's dealer fee into the open by getting the cash price, add it to the financed total where it belongs, and stack that honest total against the HELOC's total including its fees and a realistic view of its variable rate. Factor in the HELOC's possible interest deductibility only after confirming it would actually apply to you, and weigh the fact that the HELOC puts your home up as collateral while the solar loan generally doesn't. For many homeowners with solid equity and steady income, a HELOC turns out to be the cheaper way to finance solar precisely because it doesn't hide a dealer fee in the price — but that's a conclusion to reach by running your own totals, not a rule, because the right answer genuinely depends on the specific rates, fees, and comfort with risk that only you can supply. Whichever you choose, going in with the true cost of each, rather than the marketed one, is what keeps the financing from quietly eating the savings the panels are supposed to deliver.
