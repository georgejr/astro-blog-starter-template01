Somewhere in the warranty document for a home battery, past the capacity-retention promise and the ten-year term, there's usually a second number that stops the clock early: a throughput limit. It's written as a quantity of energy — often tens of megawatt-hours — that the battery is guaranteed to deliver over its life, whichever comes first, the years or the energy. That "whichever comes first" is the part worth understanding, because it turns an abstract warranty into a question you can actually answer for your own house: given how you'll really use this battery, will you ever push enough energy through it to hit that cap before the calendar runs out? For most homeowners the answer turns out to be reassuring, but the only way to know is to do a little arithmetic on your own usage rather than taking the scary-looking megawatt-hour number at face value.

## What throughput means, and where the limit actually lives

Throughput is simply the total amount of energy that has passed through the battery over its lifetime — every kilowatt-hour it has ever discharged, added up and never reset. Think of it like the odometer on a car. A single number that only climbs, recording cumulative work done rather than anything about a given day. If your battery delivers 8 kWh today, its throughput odometer ticks up by 8 kWh, and it does that every day it's used, for years. The warranty throughput limit is the manufacturer saying, in effect, "we guarantee this battery's performance up to this many total kilowatt-hours of delivered energy, and beyond that we no longer promise the capacity-retention figure." It's a way of bounding the guarantee by actual use, not just time, because a battery worked hard every single day genuinely ages faster than one that mostly sits at the ready.

A typical home battery warranty stands on three legs, and it expires when any one of them gives out. The first is the calendar term — commonly ten years. The second is the throughput or cycle limit — a total energy quantity, or sometimes a stated number of full cycles, that amounts to the same thing. The third is a capacity-retention promise — a guarantee that at the end of the covered period the battery will still hold some percentage of its original usable capacity, often in the neighborhood of 70 percent. The warranty holds as long as you're inside all three, and it lapses on whichever boundary you cross first. For most residential owners, that first boundary is the ten-year calendar term, and the throughput cap never comes into play at all. But that's a conclusion you should reach by checking your own numbers, not an assumption, because the answer flips for people who use their batteries unusually hard. The full anatomy of these documents — the exclusions, the fine print on what counts as a covered failure — is worth reading closely, and [what's really in a solar battery warranty](/blog/solar-battery-warranty-terms/) walks through the clauses that trip people up.

It helps to translate cycles and throughput into each other, because warranties use both and they describe the same thing from different angles. A "cycle" loosely means discharging the battery's usable capacity once. If your battery holds 10 kWh of usable energy and you draw it down fully and refill it, that's roughly one cycle and 10 kWh of throughput. Do that daily for a year and you've run about 365 cycles and pushed roughly 3.65 megawatt-hours through the battery. A warranty stated as "6,000 cycles" and one stated as a throughput figure in megawatt-hours are two ways of expressing the same underlying limit — total energy delivered over the battery's life. Whichever unit your warranty uses, you can convert to the other by multiplying or dividing by the usable capacity, and once you can move between them freely the projection becomes straightforward.

## Projecting the throughput your house will actually use

The honest way to estimate your throughput is to start from how you intend to use the battery, because the three common use patterns produce wildly different numbers. A battery bought mainly for backup, sitting full and waiting for the rare outage, might cycle only a handful of times a year and accumulate almost no throughput. A battery running daily self-consumption — charging from your solar in the afternoon and discharging to cover your evening — cycles roughly once a day. And a battery working a time-of-use arbitrage strategy or a utility program might cycle once or even twice a day, deliberately, which is the heaviest ordinary residential use there is. The difference between these patterns is enormous over a decade, so lumping them together and worrying about the same warranty number is a mistake.

Work a concrete example. Assume a 10 kWh usable battery on a home that uses it for daily self-consumption — the most common reason people buy storage alongside solar. Suppose it cycles once per day on average across the year; some days it discharges fully, some days only partway, and the average works out to about one full cycle. That's roughly 10 kWh of throughput per day, 3.65 MWh per year. Over the full ten-year warranty term, that's about 36.5 MWh of cumulative throughput — and that's assuming a genuinely full cycle every single day, which is on the aggressive side, since most self-consumption batteries don't drain completely most days. Now compare that against a warranty throughput cap that might sit somewhere around 37 to 40 MWh for a battery of that size. The daily-cycling homeowner lands right around the cap only if they truly cycle fully every day for the entire ten years — which means, in practice, the ten-year calendar term and the throughput limit expire at roughly the same time, and for anyone cycling less than fully, the calendar wins comfortably. Treat those cap figures as illustrative rather than a spec for any specific product; the point is the shape of the answer, not a guarantee about your battery.

Now run the same math for a backup-focused owner, and the picture changes completely. If the battery mostly sits charged and only cycles during outages plus the occasional test — say, the equivalent of 30 full cycles a year — that's about 0.3 MWh annually, or 3 MWh over ten years. Against a 37-plus MWh cap, that owner will exit the warranty on the calendar term having used less than a tenth of their throughput allowance. The throughput limit is, for them, essentially irrelevant; it exists on paper and they will never approach it. This is the single most important thing to understand about these limits: for the large share of homeowners who buy storage primarily for resilience and modest daily shifting, throughput is not the binding constraint, and the megawatt-hour figure that looks alarming in the warranty is simply not a number they'll ever reach.

The reason this matters beyond warranty trivia is that throughput and cycling are also what physically wear a battery out, so projecting your throughput is really projecting how hard you're working the cells. A battery's usable capacity fades with use, and the more energy you push through it, the faster that fade accumulates — which is the whole logic behind [how cycles and years wear a battery down](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/). But cycling isn't the only aging mechanism. A battery also ages just by existing, through what's called calendar aging — a slow chemical degradation that happens whether or not you use it, driven by time, temperature, and the state of charge it sits at. That's why a lightly used backup battery doesn't last forever just because its throughput odometer barely moved; the calendar catches up with it regardless, a reality explored in [why batteries age even when you barely use them](/blog/battery-calendar-vs-cycle-aging/). The practical implication is that light users are limited by calendar aging and the ten-year term, while heavy users are limited by throughput and cycling — and knowing which camp you're in tells you which number in the warranty actually governs your battery's life.

## When throughput becomes the limit that bites

There is a group of owners for whom throughput genuinely matters, and it's worth being honest about who they are rather than pretending the cap is always harmless. The heavy cyclers are the ones to watch: homes running aggressive time-of-use arbitrage that charges cheap overnight and discharges during the expensive evening peak every single day, sometimes with a second partial cycle; batteries enrolled in a virtual power plant or grid-services program that dispatches them frequently on the utility's schedule; and off-grid or near-off-grid systems where the battery is the primary daily energy store and cycles hard by necessity. For these users, cycling twice a day instead of once roughly doubles annual throughput — pushing a 10 kWh battery toward 7 MWh a year rather than 3.65 — and now the throughput cap can arrive well before the ten-year calendar term. If you're planning that kind of use, the throughput limit isn't a curiosity; it's the number that defines your warranty life, and you should size the battery and read the warranty with that in mind.

For those heavy-use cases, a couple of design choices change the math in your favor. A larger battery cycled less deeply accumulates throughput more slowly for the same daily energy, because you're using a smaller fraction of a bigger tank — and shallower cycling is gentler on the cells besides. Two 10 kWh batteries sharing the daily load each cycle half as much as one would, so the pair reaches its combined throughput cap far slower than a single unit worked to its limit every day. That's part of why owners with demanding daily patterns sometimes oversize their storage relative to what a pure backup calculation would suggest: the extra headroom buys warranty life and slows degradation. Whether that trade is worth the added upfront cost depends on your electricity rates and how much the arbitrage or program actually pays, which is exactly the kind of question a [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) is built to help you weigh, by letting you test how sizing and daily cycling change both the value and the wear.

The reassuring bottom line for typical buyers is that the throughput cap is a limit designed for the worst-case heavy user, and most homeowners will comfortably retire their battery on the ten-year term with throughput to spare. The scary megawatt-hour figure is not a countdown you should feel ticking; for a backup-plus-light-shifting household it's a boundary you'll never touch. The reason to do the projection anyway is that it tells you which kind of owner you are — calendar-limited or throughput-limited — and that single distinction reframes the whole warranty. Once you know your realistic cycles per year and multiply by your usable capacity, the intimidating cap resolves into either a non-issue you can stop worrying about or a real design parameter you should plan around. Either way you've replaced a vague anxiety about a big number with an actual answer about your own house, which is the only version of the question worth asking.
