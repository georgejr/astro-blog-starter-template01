Two houses on the same street list in the same month, each with an identical array on the roof. One sale closes smoothly, the panels quietly adding to the home's appeal. The other stalls in the final week when the buyer's lender balks at a document nobody flagged earlier — a twenty-year solar lease that has to be transferred, qualified for, or bought out before anyone can sign. Same panels, same sun, opposite outcomes. The difference is a single line in the paperwork: who actually owns the equipment on the roof.

That word — *owned* versus *leased* — does almost all the work in the question of whether solar helps or hurts a sale. It's not a detail to sort out later. It's the fork the whole outcome runs through, and the two branches behave so differently that they're better understood as two separate situations that happen to share a roof.

## Owned panels behave like an asset

When you own your system outright — cash or a paid-off loan — the panels are your property, and they transfer with the house like a renovated kitchen or a new roof. The buyer inherits an asset that lowers the home's operating cost, with nothing to assume and no third party to satisfy. Research on home sales has generally found that owned solar can lift a home's value and, in strong markets, help it sell faster, because buyers can see the array as a paid-for feature rather than a liability. The mechanics of how that value shows up in a listing are covered in [do solar panels raise your home's value](/blog/solar-panels-home-value/).

The value isn't automatic, and it isn't unlimited. An appraiser has to be able to document the system and its production for it to count toward the appraised value, and a buyer pool that doesn't understand solar may not pay full freight for it. But the *direction* is favorable: an owned system is a plus that occasionally fails to be fully credited, not a hurdle. Nothing about it can block a sale.

## Leased panels behave like a contract

A leased or power-purchase-agreement system is the opposite in the one way that matters at closing: you don't own it. A third party owns the hardware, and you hold a long-term contract to pay for it or for the power it makes. That contract doesn't vanish because you're moving — it has to go somewhere, and there are only a few places it can go. Each one adds friction to a sale that an owned system simply doesn't carry.

| At resale | Owned system | Leased / PPA system |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What transfers | The equipment, as an asset | A payment obligation |
| Buyer's decision | Accept a feature | Qualify for and accept a contract |
| Lender involvement | Minimal | Lease terms can affect financing |
| Who must sign off | Just buyer and seller | Also the leasing company |
| Effect on timeline | Neutral to helpful | A potential source of delay |

The practical friction is real: the buyer usually has to credit-qualify to assume the lease, the leasing company has to approve the transfer, and the remaining payments become a number the buyer weighs against the bill savings. None of that is fatal — millions of leased systems change hands — but each step is a place a nervous buyer or a cautious lender can stall, which is why leased systems earn their reputation for [complicating a sale](/blog/selling-house-with-solar-panels/) rather than sweetening it.

## The three ways a leased sale actually resolves

When a home with a leased system sells, the contract almost always ends up in one of three places, and it's worth knowing them before you list.

- **The buyer assumes the lease.** The cleanest path when it works: the buyer qualifies, the leasing company approves, and the payments simply continue under new ownership. It depends entirely on the buyer being comfortable with the terms and able to pass the credit check.
- **The seller buys out the lease.** If the buyer won't take it on, the seller can pay the leasing company to end the contract, often folding the cost into the sale. This removes the obstacle but can be expensive, and an early buyout may not deliver much value for the money already spent.
- **The sale is renegotiated around it.** Sometimes the lease becomes a bargaining chip — a price concession, a closing credit, or a delay while terms are worked out. This is the outcome that quietly eats into whatever the panels were supposed to be worth.

The common thread is that a leased system's effect on a sale ranges from neutral to negative, while an owned system's ranges from neutral to positive. That asymmetry is the whole point.

## If you already have a lease and plan to sell

Discovering the ownership distinction after signing a lease isn't a crisis, but it does change how you prepare to sell. Pull the lease agreement and read the transfer and buyout sections specifically — those two clauses govern everything above. Know your remaining balance, your buyout figure, and whether the contract includes a production guarantee a buyer would inherit. Handing a buyer a clear, documented picture of the lease up front prevents the last-week surprise that stalls deals; ambiguity is what scares people, not the panels.
