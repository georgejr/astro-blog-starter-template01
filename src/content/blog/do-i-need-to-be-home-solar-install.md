The install date is booked, and the question that occurs to almost everyone is a practical one: do I actually have to be here for this? Between work, kids, and everything else, blocking out a day or more to sit and watch a crew work isn't always easy. The reassuring answer is that you generally don't need to be present for the entire installation — most of the work happens on the roof and outside the house, where the crew neither needs you nor particularly wants you underfoot. But "not the whole time" isn't the same as "not at all," and there are a handful of specific moments where your presence, or at least your reachable phone and some arranged access, genuinely matters. Knowing which parts need you and which don't lets you plan the day sensibly instead of either taking unnecessary time off or vanishing when the crew actually needs a decision.

## What the crew needs from you, and when

Most of a rooftop solar installation is exterior work that proceeds fine with or without you watching. The crew spends the bulk of the job on the roof — laying out and attaching the mounting rails, flashing the penetrations, setting the panels, and running the wiring between them — and none of that requires anything from you beyond having agreed to the design already. A typical residential install runs somewhere in the range of one to three days on site, and for the large middle stretch of that time, you could be at work, running errands, or asleep, and the work would continue exactly the same. So the blanket fear that you have to babysit the entire process is unfounded; the roof portion is theirs to run.

The parts that do involve you cluster around access to the inside of the house and around decisions that can't be made without you. Nearly every solar install has to connect to your home's main electrical panel, and that panel is often in a garage, basement, utility closet, or on an interior wall. The crew has to reach it to tie the solar system into your electrical service, install any new equipment like a production meter or a subpanel, and place the inverter — which frequently goes on a garage wall or the side of the house near the panel. Depending on your home's layout, they may also need to run conduit through an attic, a crawlspace, or along an interior wall, and they'll need to shut off your power for a stretch while they make the final electrical connections. All of that requires someone to let them in and grant access to those spaces. It doesn't necessarily require you specifically — a spouse, a trusted adult, or in some cases arrangements you've made in advance can cover it — but it requires that a responsible adult with authority over the house be reachable and able to open doors when the interior work begins.

There are also moments where a decision has to be made on the spot, and those are the ones you don't want to be unreachable for. An installer might reach the roof and find a rafter isn't where the plan assumed, or discover that the ideal inverter location conflicts with something they couldn't see from the design phase, or hit a question about exactly where you want conduit to run across a visible wall. These aren't emergencies, but they're choices with lasting consequences for how your system looks and works, and a crew that can't reach anyone will either guess or stop and wait. Being available by phone — even if you're not physically present — resolves most of these instantly. For the interior electrical work and the power shutoff in particular, having someone home is the smoother path, because coordinating access to your panel and confirming placement decisions in person beats doing it over a series of phone calls. The full hour-by-hour rhythm of what's happening when is laid out in our walkthrough of [solar installation day](/blog/solar-installation-day/), and reading it beforehand makes it obvious which stretches are hands-off and which are the ones where you'll be glad someone was home.

One thing worth clearing up: install day is usually not the same as the day your system turns on. After the crew finishes and leaves, your system typically has to pass one or more inspections and then get formal permission to operate from your utility before you can actually flip it on. Those steps happen after the physical install, sometimes weeks later, and they may involve a separate visit from a local inspector and a utility meter swap — for which access is again relevant, since an inspector often needs to see the interior equipment and the electrical panel. So the "do I need to be home" question really applies to two or three separate events spread across the timeline, not one. Our guide to [solar permits and inspections](/blog/solar-permits-and-inspections/) explains who shows up for each of those and what they need to see, and the [overall installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/) shows how far apart these visits can fall — useful context so you're not blindsided by a request for access days or weeks after you thought the job was done.

## Getting the house ready

Even if you don't plan to stay for every hour, a little preparation makes the install go faster, safer, and with fewer surprises — and most of it is about clearing the crew's path to work. Start with vehicle access, because the crew arrives with a lot of equipment and needs a place to put their trucks and often a ladder or lift close to the house. Clearing your driveway and, if your array involves the front of the roof, giving them room to position ladders against the eaves saves everyone time. If you're on a street with parking restrictions or a tight driveway, a quick heads-up to the crew about where they can and can't park heads off a frustrating start to the morning. Inside and around the perimeter, clearing the way to the electrical panel matters just as much: if your panel is in a garage buried behind storage, or in a basement behind a stack of boxes, the electricians will need that access, and clearing it in advance beats having a crew wait while you shuffle bins.

Pets and kids deserve real thought, because install day means open doors, people moving in and out, power tools, and stretches where your electricity is off. A crew propping the garage door open to run conduit is an easy escape route for a dog or cat, and the noise and strangers can stress animals that would otherwise be fine. The simplest plan is to keep pets secured in a room the crew won't need to enter, or out of the house entirely for the day, and to let the crew know they're there so nobody accidentally lets them out. Kids, similarly, are best kept clear of the work zones — there's overhead work, sharp materials, and live electrical work happening, none of it compatible with curious hands. If you work from home, plan around the power being off for a while: charge your laptop and phone beforehand, and expect that internet, if it runs through a powered modem, will drop during the shutoff. Knowing the outage is coming turns it from a mid-day surprise into a non-event.

A few practical arrangements round out the prep. Make sure the crew has a working number to reach a decision-maker even if that person isn't on site, so any of those on-the-spot questions get answered fast rather than guessed. Confirm ahead of time where the inverter and any new equipment will be mounted, since that's the decision most likely to come up and the one you'll live with visually for decades — settling it before the crew arrives avoids an awkward on-the-fly compromise. If there's a gate, an alarm, or a locked space the crew will need to enter, arrange the codes or keys in advance. It's also worth a quick word to close neighbors if the crew will be working near a shared driveway or property line, since a day of trucks, ladders, and noise is easier on everyone when it isn't a surprise. And if you genuinely can't be home and are relying on someone else to grant access, brief that person on the basics — where the electrical panel is, where you want the inverter and any new equipment mounted, when the crew expects to shut the power off, and how to reach you if a question comes up — so they can stand in for you competently rather than guessing on your behalf. The more of this you settle before the trucks pull up, the less the day depends on you being physically present for every hour of it.

Stepping back, the realistic picture is that you'll want to be home, or have a capable stand-in home, for the beginning of the job when access and placement get sorted, and again for the interior electrical work and the power shutoff — and you'll want to be reachable throughout. The long midday stretch of roof work is genuinely hands-off. And you'll need to arrange access again later for inspection and the utility's permission-to-operate steps, which arrive after the crew is long gone. It's less a matter of taking a full day off to supervise and more a matter of being available at the few points where the house, the panel, or a decision needs you. Plan for those points, clear the path to your electrical panel, secure the pets, and the rest takes care of itself.
