"Is solar worth it?" has no universal answer — and anyone who gives you one without asking about your electricity rate and roof is selling something. What does exist is a reliable framework: five factors that determine nearly all of the outcome. Score well on most of them and solar is one of the better home investments available; score poorly and your money is better placed elsewhere.

## The five factors that decide it

### 1. Your electricity rate

This is the single biggest driver. Solar savings are simply avoided grid purchases, so at $0.30/kWh a system saves nearly twice as much per year as at $0.16/kWh — for the same installation cost. Homeowners in California, New England, and other high-rate markets routinely see payback under 7 years; in cheap-power states it can stretch past 12.

### 2. Your usage

Bigger bills mean bigger savings potential. A $250+/month bill gives solar a lot of expensive consumption to offset; a $60 bill leaves little room for the system to earn its keep. If your bill is under about $80/month, efficiency upgrades usually beat solar on return.

### 3. Your sun and roof

Daily sun hours (roughly 3.5 in cloudier regions to 6+ in the Southwest) directly scale production. Just as important: an unshaded, structurally sound roof with good south/southwest exposure. Heavy shading can cut production 20–40% and flip the economics entirely.

### 4. Your utility's export rules

With full net metering, every excess kWh you export earns retail credit — the grid works like a free battery. Where utilities credit exports at wholesale rates instead, self-consumption timing matters and effective savings drop. Check your utility's policy before believing any calculator, including ours.

### 5. The price you actually pay

At $2.50/W a system can be a clear win; the identical system at $4.00/W may never make sense. Getting three or more quotes is the highest-value hour of the entire project. Benchmark them against [average solar panel costs](/blog/average-solar-panel-cost/).

## Run your own numbers

The [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) combines these factors into one payback figure. As a rule of thumb:

| Payback period | Verdict |
| --- | --- |
| Under 7 years | Strong yes — excellent return over a 25-year system life |
| 7–11 years | Reasonable — solid long-term investment |
| Over 11 years | Marginal — wait for better prices or rate changes |

Remember the estimate is conservative: it assumes flat electricity prices, and rates have historically risen 2–3% per year. Details in [solar payback period explained](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/).

## When solar is a clear yes

- High electricity rates (over ~$0.20/kWh) and a $150+ monthly bill
- Sunny, unshaded roof in good condition
- Full or near-full net metering
- You plan to stay in the home 7+ years, or your market values solar homes
- You can use the 30% federal tax credit

## When to wait

- Your roof needs replacement within a few years — do the roof first
- Heavy shading you cannot mitigate
- Very low usage or very cheap electricity
- Only one quote, priced well above market
- Your utility is about to change net metering rules — understand them first

## Beyond the spreadsheet

Some value never shows up in payback math: protection against future rate hikes, backup power when paired with a [battery](/blog/solar-battery-worth-it/), and lower household emissions. Reasonable people weigh these differently — just make sure the core financial case stands on its own first.

## FAQ

**Does solar increase home value?**
Studies consistently find owned (not leased) systems add value, often a few percent of the home price, though it varies by market.

**Is leasing worth it?**
Leases and PPAs reduce upfront cost but capture most of the savings for the provider and can complicate a home sale. Ownership generally wins financially if you can access the tax credit.

**How long do panels last?**
Production warranties run 25 years; panels typically keep producing beyond that at slightly reduced output.
