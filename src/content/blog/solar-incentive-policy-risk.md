Here is the test that makes a solar decision durable: run the payback twice, once with the incentives you expect and once with a chunk of them gone, and see whether you'd still sign. If solar only works with every credit and favorable rate intact, you've bought a system that depends on policy staying put — and policy is the one input you don't control. If it works even with a meaningful piece stripped out, you've made a decision that survives whatever the legislature and your utility do next.

That framing matters because incentives genuinely do change. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit currently reduces a system's cost by 30%, claimed on your taxes per IRS guidance. State rebates, utility programs, and net metering rules all shift on their own schedules, sometimes generously, sometimes not. Rather than trying to predict exactly what will change and when — a losing game — the sounder move is to structure your decision so it doesn't hinge on any single incentive surviving.

## Which incentives carry which risk

Not all incentives are equally exposed, and knowing where the fragility sits tells you where to be conservative in your assumptions.

- **The federal tax credit** is the largest single incentive for most buyers and applies as a percentage of system cost. Its value to you depends on having the tax liability to use it — the mechanics that [the solar tax credit explained](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/) walks through. Federal credits are set by law and can be revised, so treat the current 30% as today's rule rather than a permanent fixture.
- **State and utility rebates** vary enormously and are the most volatile category. Many are funded from pools that empty, tiered programs that step down as capacity fills, or budgets that get renewed or cut. These are the incentives most likely to be different next year than this one.
- **Net metering and export rates** aren't a rebate but function like one, and they've been trending toward less generous structures in many states. The rate at which your utility credits exported power directly shapes your savings, and it can be revised for new customers.

The pattern is that the more local and discretionary an incentive, the more exposed it tends to be. The federal credit is a statute; a utility's rebate pool is a budget line. Weight your assumptions accordingly.

## Grandfathering: the protection you may already have

One structural feature works in your favor, and it's worth understanding because it changes the risk calculus. Many incentive and rate changes apply only to new customers, with existing systems locked into the terms that were in place when they interconnected. This is called grandfathering, and it's common with net metering in particular.

The practical implication is a subtle argument for deciding rather than waiting. If a favorable net metering structure exists today and looks likely to be trimmed for future customers, installing while it's available can lock you into that arrangement for a set period — the exact mechanism examined in [net metering grandfathering](/blog/net-metering-grandfathering/). Waiting to see what happens can mean watching a good deal expire and enrolling under a worse one. This cuts against the instinct to hold off during uncertainty: sometimes the uncertainty is precisely the reason to act, because acting is what secures the current terms.

That said, grandfathering isn't universal and isn't unlimited — periods end, and not every incentive is protected. It's a factor to weigh, not a guarantee to lean the whole decision on.

## Building a decision that survives a haircut

The core discipline is to test your solar economics against a reduced-incentive scenario before you commit. If the numbers only work with everything stacked in your favor, you're exposed. If they work with less, you're insulated.

Concretely, that means running the payback under a conservative case: without the incentive most likely to disappear, and at a less generous export rate than today's. A system that still pays back in a range you'd accept under those conditions is a robust decision. One that only works at full incentive is a bet on policy. The scenario worth modeling explicitly is [solar payback without the federal tax credit](/blog/solar-payback-without-tax-credit/) — if the system is still worthwhile with that removed, almost everything else is upside.

There's a deeper reason this works: the most durable driver of solar value isn't any incentive at all. It's your electricity rate. Every kilowatt-hour you produce offsets power you'd otherwise buy, and that saving exists whether or not a rebate does. Incentives accelerate the payback; avoided grid costs are what carry it over the long run. A decision anchored on your rate rather than on a credit is one that policy can dent but not break.

Run both cases before you sign. Model your payback with the incentives you expect, then again with a meaningful piece removed, using the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If the conservative case still lands where you want it, you've made a decision that doesn't need to guess the future correctly — it just needs your rates to keep rising, which is the safer bet of the two.
