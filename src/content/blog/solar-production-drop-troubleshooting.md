The monitoring app said 42 kWh a day through a bright stretch of summer, and now it's reading 28. That's a third of the output gone, and the instinct is to assume a panel died or the inverter is failing. Sometimes that's exactly it. Often it isn't. A structured check, worked from the cheapest and most likely causes toward the expensive ones, usually finds the answer before a service truck is needed — and tells you whether the "drop" is even a real fault or just the calendar doing its job.

The trap is comparing today against a peak instead of against a fair expectation. Production swings enormously with the season, the weather, and the time of month you last looked. Before treating a lower number as a malfunction, you have to know what the system *should* be making right now.

## First, decide whether the drop is even real

Half of "sudden drop" reports dissolve once you compare against the right baseline. A system that made 42 kWh in late June and 28 kWh in October hasn't failed — it's responding to shorter days and a lower sun angle, which can easily cut daily output by a third between summer and fall. The same is true week to week when a run of clear days gives way to overcast.

Ground the comparison in what the array is designed to produce. A realistic annual estimate from the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), broken down by season, tells you whether October's lower number is normal or genuinely short. Two quick sanity checks separate a real fault from a seasonal one:

- Compare year-over-year, not month-over-month. If last October also ran near 28 kWh a day, the system is fine and the summer figure was the outlier.
- Check the weather for the days in question. A cloudy, hazy, or smoky stretch suppresses output across the whole array at once, and it recovers on its own when the sky clears.

If the current output roughly matches last year's same-season figure, stop here — nothing is broken. If it's well below what the same month produced a year ago under similar weather, you have a real drop worth chasing.

## Walk the cheap, physical causes first

Confirmed a real shortfall? Start with the causes that cost nothing to check and nothing or little to fix. These are far more common than dead hardware.

### Shading that wasn't there before

Shade is the leading cause of a creeping production loss, and it changes with time. A tree that was trimmed two years ago has grown back into the array's morning sun. A new neighbor's addition, a satellite dish, even a vent pipe casts a shadow that clips a string. On systems where panels are wired in series without optimizers, shade on one panel drags down its whole string, so a small shadow can cause an outsized loss. Walk outside at mid-morning and mid-afternoon and actually look at the array for shadows that weren't part of the original design.

### Soiling on the glass

A film of dust, pollen, bird droppings, or ash cuts output gradually, then a rain washes it off and production jumps back. Heavy, localized soiling — a splatter of droppings across a few cells — hurts more than an even layer of dust, for the same series-string reason shade does. Snow, of course, stops a panel cold until it slides or melts off. If a cleaning or a good rain restores the numbers, soiling was your answer.

## Then check the monitoring and the hardware

If the array is clean and unshaded and the drop is still real, move to the electronics. This is where the monitoring app earns its keep, because it can often point at the exact panel or string that's underperforming.

### Read what the app is telling you

Panel-level or string-level monitoring will frequently show the problem as an outlier — one panel flatlined while its neighbors track normally, or one string producing nothing. A whole-system drop that hits every panel at once points more toward the inverter or a communication issue than a single bad module. Learning to spot these patterns is a skill in itself, and [reading your monitoring app for warning signs](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) covers the numbers that signal trouble versus normal variation. If the app itself simply lost its internet connection, the panels may be producing fine while only the reporting is broken — a "zero production" reading that's really a dead router.

### Suspect the inverter

The inverter is the component most likely to fail first in a solar system, and its failure often looks exactly like a production drop — sometimes to zero, sometimes partial if one section of a multi-input inverter goes down. Check it for a fault light or an error code, which the [common solar panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) rundown helps decode. A tripped AC or DC disconnect, or a breaker that popped, will also kill production and is worth checking before assuming the worst; a failed inverter, covered in [the inverter as your system's likely first failure](/blog/solar-inverter-failure/), usually needs professional replacement but is a known, warrantied part rather than a catastrophe.

## When to stop troubleshooting and call for service

Escalate once you've cleared the free checks and the drop persists. Specifically, call your installer or a service tech when: the array is clean and unshaded, the weather and season don't explain the shortfall, and the monitoring shows either a persistent dead panel or string, an inverter fault code, or a whole-system output that stays well below last year's same-season number. Note what you found — which panels, what the app shows, when it started — because a clear description shortens the diagnosis and can turn a vague "it's making less" into a covered warranty claim.

## FAQ

### Is lower winter production a malfunction?

Almost never. Shorter days and a low sun angle can cut daily output by a third or more from summer to winter, entirely by design. Compare against the same month last year, not against your summer peak, before treating it as a fault.

### My app shows zero but the lights are on — what happened?

Often the monitoring lost its internet connection while the panels kept working normally. Check whether your home network or the system's communication gateway is down. If the array is genuinely producing zero on a sunny day, look next at a tripped disconnect or breaker and then the inverter.

### How do I know if it's one panel or the whole system?

Panel- or string-level monitoring usually shows it: one flatlined panel among normal neighbors points to a single-module or shading issue, while every panel dropping together points to the inverter or a wiring/disconnect problem. A system-wide zero on a clear day is an inverter, disconnect, or comms issue far more often than dozens of panels failing at once.

### Was my system just undersized to begin with?

That's a different question from a sudden drop, but worth ruling out if output has *always* felt low rather than recently fallen. Comparing your usage and expected production through [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) shows whether the array was ever sized to cover your load, separate from any fault.
