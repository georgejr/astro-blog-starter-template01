The monitoring app said 42 kWh a day through a bright stretch of summer, and now it's reading 28. That's a third of the output gone, and the instinct is to assume a panel died or the inverter is on its way out. Sometimes that's exactly what happened. Often it isn't. A structured check, worked from the cheapest and most likely causes toward the expensive ones, usually finds the answer before a service truck is needed — and, just as often, tells you whether the "drop" is even a real fault or just the calendar quietly doing its job.

The trap that catches almost everyone is comparing today against a remembered peak instead of against a fair expectation. Production swings enormously with the season, the weather, and even which week of the month you happened to glance at the app. A number that looks alarming next to your best-ever summer day may be completely normal for an October afternoon. So before you treat a lower reading as a malfunction and start bracing for a repair bill, you have to establish what the system should be making right now, in this season, under this week's weather. Get that baseline right and half of these scares evaporate on the spot.

## First, decide whether the drop is even real

Roughly half of "sudden drop" worries dissolve the moment you compare against the right baseline instead of the wrong one. A system that made 42 kWh a day in late June and 28 kWh a day in October hasn't failed anything — it's responding exactly as designed to shorter days and a lower sun angle, which can easily cut daily output by a third or more between midsummer and mid-fall. The same thing happens week to week: a run of brilliant clear days gives way to a stretch of overcast, and production sags right along with the sky. Neither of those is a fault. They're the fundamental physics of a device that runs on sunlight, and sunlight is not constant.

Grounding the comparison in what the array is actually designed to produce is the first real step. A realistic annual estimate from the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), broken out by season, tells you whether October's lower number is normal for your location or genuinely short of where it should be. From there, two quick sanity checks separate a real fault from a seasonal one, and both take minutes. First, compare year over year rather than month over month. If last October also ran near 28 kWh a day, the system is fine and the summer figure was simply the outlier your memory latched onto — peak production is memorable precisely because it's the peak. Second, check the weather for the specific days in question. A cloudy, hazy, or wildfire-smoky stretch suppresses output across the entire array at once, and it recovers on its own the moment the sky clears, no intervention required. A drop that tracks the weather and reverses when the weather does was never a hardware problem.

The most reliable baseline you'll ever have is your own system's history, and it's worth deliberately building one in the first year so you're never left guessing again. Most monitoring platforms let you look back at production month by month, so jotting down roughly what each month produced during a normal year gives you a personal yardstick far better than any generic estimate ever could. Once you have twelve months on record, a "sudden drop" has an obvious reference point: does this October match last October? That single comparison resolves the large majority of scares, because production is remarkably repeatable year to year for a fixed array under similar weather — the sun follows the same calendar it always has, and a healthy system tracks it. When you don't yet have a full year of your own data to lean on, use the seasonal shape the calculator provides and pay attention to the weather, and be especially slow to panic in fall and winter. That's precisely when a large but entirely normal seasonal decline arrives, and it's often the first time a homeowner looks closely at production at all, which sets up the classic mistake of reading the change of seasons as a hardware fault. The pull toward comparing against the summer peak is natural, because the peak is the number that stuck in memory — but it's the wrong comparison every single time. Anchor instead to the same season a year ago, or to the calculator's seasonal breakdown, and most of what looks alarming turns out to be the planet simply doing what it does.

If the current output roughly matches last year's same-season figure under comparable weather, you can stop right here, because nothing is broken and there's nothing to fix. This is the answer to the question homeowners ask most in winter — is lower cold-season production a malfunction? Almost never. Shorter days and a low winter sun angle can cut daily output by a third or more from the summer peak, entirely by design, and a snow-dusted January that produces a fraction of July is a normal January, not a failing system. The comparison that matters is against the same month a year ago, not against your best summer week. It's only when the current output sits well below what the same month produced a year earlier, under similar weather, that you have a real drop worth chasing — and at that point the rest of the troubleshooting sequence earns its keep. Everything before this step is about not calling a technician to diagnose the change of seasons.

## Walk the cheap physical causes before you blame the hardware

Confirmed a genuine shortfall against last year's same season? Then start with the causes that cost nothing to check and little or nothing to fix, because they're far more common than dead electronics and they hide in plain sight. Shade and dirt account for a huge share of real production losses, and both are things you can investigate from the ground with your own eyes on a sunny afternoon.

Shading is the leading cause of a creeping production loss, and the reason it sneaks up on people is that it changes slowly over years. A tree that got trimmed back two seasons ago has quietly grown into the array's morning sun again. A neighbor's new second-story addition, a freshly installed satellite dish, even a plumbing vent pipe the roofer added — any of them can throw a shadow that clips a corner of the array at a certain hour. The effect is worse than it looks, because on systems where panels are wired in series without optimizers, shade falling on a single panel drags down the entire string it belongs to. A small shadow on one module can cause a loss out of all proportion to its size. The fix is to walk outside at mid-morning and again at mid-afternoon and actually look at the array for shadows that weren't part of the original clean-roof design. If something is now shading panels that used to sit in full sun, you've likely found your answer, and the remedy is a pruning saw rather than a service call.

Soiling is the other cheap culprit. A film of dust, pollen, bird droppings, or settled ash cuts output gradually, and then a good rain washes it off and production jumps back on its own — which is itself the diagnostic. Heavy, localized soiling hurts more than an even layer of dust for the same series-string reason shade does: a splatter of droppings concentrated across a few cells can choke a whole string, while a thin even haze of pollen costs a more modest, uniform slice. Snow, of course, stops a panel cold until it slides or melts clear, and a snow-covered array reading near zero on a bright winter morning is doing precisely what a snow-covered array does. If a cleaning, or simply the next rain, restores your numbers, soiling was the whole story and there's nothing further to chase.

The shape of the loss over time is itself a diagnostic that costs nothing to read, and it often points straight at the cause before you've touched anything. Shading tends to show up at specific hours — a shadow that clips the array only in early morning or late afternoon — and it worsens gradually across seasons as the sun's path shifts and vegetation grows, so a shading loss is usually partial and time-of-day-dependent rather than a clean, all-day drop. Soiling builds slowly and then reverses abruptly with a hard rain or a cleaning, a sawtooth pattern in the monitoring history that's unmistakable once you've seen it. A genuine hardware fault, by contrast, often arrives suddenly and doesn't recover on its own: production steps down on a particular day and stays down regardless of weather or hour. So before you climb toward the electronics, look not only at whether output is low but at how it got low. A gradual, hourly, seasonal fade points at shade or dirt; a sudden step-change that a good rain doesn't undo points at hardware. This is where a quick physical look pays off out of all proportion to the effort — five minutes outside at mid-morning and mid-afternoon, plus a glance at whether the glass is visibly dirty, resolves most real losses without a ladder or a service call.

Two more free checks belong in this cheap-first pass. If you know anyone nearby with solar, a quick comparison tells you whether a regional cloudy stretch is suppressing everyone's output or just yours — a shared dip is weather, a lonely one points back at your own system. And glance at whether the loss lines up with anything that recently changed at the house: a new appliance, a roofer or painter who might have bumped a panel or knocked a wire loose, tree work that shifted the light, or a storm that could have moved something. Production drops that begin the week something physical happened around the array usually have a physical cause you can find, and spotting that coincidence up front can save an hour of squinting at graphs looking for a fault that has an obvious explanation. The discipline here is to exhaust the free, ground-level explanations — is it shaded, is it dirty, is it covered — before you climb toward the electronics, because those explanations are both the most likely and the cheapest to resolve.

## When it's the electronics, and when to call for service

If the array is clean, unshaded, and the drop against last year's same season is still real, it's time to move to the electronics, and this is where the monitoring app finally earns its keep. Good monitoring can often point straight at the exact panel or string that's underperforming, which turns a vague "it's making less" into a specific, actionable fault. Panel-level or string-level monitoring will frequently show the problem as an outlier: one panel flatlined while all its neighbors track normally, or one entire string producing nothing while the others carry on. That pattern points to a localized hardware or connection issue on that specific panel or string. A whole-system drop that hits every panel at once points somewhere different — toward the inverter, a tripped disconnect, or a communication glitch — because it's far more likely that one shared component failed than that dozens of independent panels all died on the same afternoon. Learning to read these patterns is a skill worth building, and [reading your monitoring app for warning signs](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) covers which numbers signal genuine trouble versus normal day-to-day variation.

That distinction also answers the two questions that come up most once the hardware is in play. If your app shows zero production but the lights are plainly on and the house has power, the most common explanation isn't a dead array at all — it's that the monitoring simply lost its internet connection while the panels kept working normally. The gateway that reports production went offline, so you're looking at a broken report of a healthy system, not a broken system. Check whether your home network or the system's communication device is down before you panic; a "zero production" reading is very often just a dead router. If the array genuinely is producing zero on a sunny day, look next at a tripped AC or DC disconnect or a popped breaker, and then at the inverter. And to tell one panel apart from a whole-system problem, let the monitoring answer it: one flatlined panel among normal neighbors points to a single-module or shading issue, while every panel dropping together points to the inverter or a wiring and disconnect problem. A system-wide zero on a clear day is an inverter, disconnect, or comms issue far more often than it is dozens of simultaneously failed panels.

The inverter deserves particular suspicion, because it's the component most likely to fail first in a solar system, and its failure often looks exactly like a production drop — sometimes all the way to zero, sometimes only partial if one section of a multi-input inverter goes down while the rest soldier on. Check it for a fault light or an error code, which the [common solar panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) rundown helps you decode into something meaningful. A failed inverter, explored in depth in [the inverter as your system's likely first failure](/blog/solar-inverter-failure/), usually needs professional replacement, but it's a known, warrantied part rather than a catastrophe, and diagnosing it yourself before the call means the technician arrives already knowing what to bring. The point at which you should stop troubleshooting and escalate is well defined: call your installer or a service tech when the array is clean and unshaded, the weather and season don't explain the shortfall against last year, and the monitoring shows either a persistent dead panel or string, an inverter fault code, or a whole-system output that stays stubbornly below last year's same-season figure. Bring notes — which panels, what the app shows, when it started — because a clear description shortens the diagnosis and can turn a vague complaint into a cleanly covered warranty claim. One separate question is worth ruling out if the output has always felt low rather than recently fallen: the array may simply have been undersized for your usage from day one, which is a design question rather than a fault. Comparing your consumption against expected production through [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) shows whether the system was ever built to cover your load in the first place, entirely apart from whether anything has since broken.
