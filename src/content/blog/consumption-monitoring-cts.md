If you've opened your electrical panel since your solar was installed and noticed two small plastic clamps hugging the thick wires at the top — usually a matched pair, one on each of the two main service conductors, with thin leads running back to your inverter or a monitoring module — those are current transformers, almost always shortened to CTs. They're not doing anything to your electricity. They're not a fuse, a breaker, or a safety device. They're sensors, and their entire job is to measure how much current is flowing through the wires they're wrapped around, so your monitoring system can tell the difference between the power your panels make and the power your house actually uses.

That distinction — production versus consumption — is more useful than it sounds, and it's the reason installers go to the trouble of adding CTs at all. Without them, your monitoring can show you how much your solar array produced, and that's it. With them, it can show you production *and* consumption side by side, which unlocks a whole layer of understanding about self-use, exports, and where your energy is actually going. Once you know what these clamps measure and why the data matters, your monitoring app stops being a vanity dashboard and becomes something you can plan around.

## How a clamp measures power without touching the wire

A current transformer works on a genuinely elegant piece of physics: any wire carrying alternating current is surrounded by a magnetic field that pulses in step with the current. A CT is a ring of magnetic material with a coil of wire wound around it, and when you clamp it around a conductor, the conductor's magnetic field induces a tiny, proportional current in that coil. Measure the induced current and you can calculate the current flowing in the main wire — without ever cutting it, splicing into it, or making electrical contact. That's why an installer can add whole-home monitoring in a few minutes: the CT just clips around an existing conductor like a clothespin. It reads the wire the way a stethoscope reads a heartbeat, from the outside.

The reason there are usually two of them comes down to how residential service is wired in the US. A typical home has a split-phase 240-volt service made of two 120-volt "legs," and your loads are distributed across both. To measure the whole house you need to know the current on each leg, so you get one CT per main conductor, and the monitoring system combines them. Direction matters too: because these are AC circuits, current can flow *into* the house from the grid or *out* of the house to the grid when your panels are overproducing, and a properly installed CT-based monitor detects which way the power is moving. That directionality is the whole trick behind consumption monitoring. The system knows, moment to moment, whether you're pulling from the grid, pushing to it, or perfectly balanced.

Placement is what determines whether the CTs measure consumption, production, or the net flow at the meter, and this is where installations differ. In a common setup, one pair of CTs sits on the main service conductors to measure the *net* flow between your home and the grid, while the inverter separately reports production directly. The monitoring software then does the arithmetic: your home's actual consumption equals what your panels produced plus what you pulled from the grid, minus what you exported. In other setups the CTs are placed to read consumption directly. The wiring detail matters less than the outcome — a correctly configured CT install gives your monitoring platform three numbers instead of one: production, consumption, and grid flow. If any of those numbers looks obviously wrong in your app, a reversed or mislocated CT is a frequent and easily fixed culprit, which is worth knowing before you assume your system is malfunctioning. This is also the boundary line between whole-home monitoring and [panel-level versus system-level monitoring](/blog/panel-level-vs-system-monitoring/): CTs on your main conductors tell you about the house as a whole, whereas per-panel optimizers tell you about individual modules on the roof. They answer different questions, and many systems have both.

It helps to place CTs in the broader monitoring picture. The inverter is already the brain of the system — converting DC from the panels to the AC your home uses and reporting production — and if you want the fuller story of everything [a solar inverter does](/blog/what-does-a-solar-inverter-do/), that's the component the CTs feed their data alongside. The CTs simply extend the inverter's view from "what did the roof make" to "what did the whole house do with it." Accuracy is generally good but not laboratory-grade; consumer CT monitoring typically lands within a couple of percent of true values when installed correctly, which is more than precise enough for the planning decisions that make the data worthwhile, even if it won't match your utility meter to the penny.

A few installation details are worth understanding so you can sanity-check your own setup, because CTs are easy to get subtly wrong. Each CT has a direction — usually marked with an arrow or a label indicating which side faces the grid — and if it's clamped on backwards, the monitoring reads power flowing the wrong way, so your app might show you exporting when you're actually importing, or vice versa. A CT clamped around the wrong conductor, or around both legs at once, throws the numbers off in other characteristic ways. The clamps also have to be sized to the conductor and the expected current; a CT rated for a 200-amp service behaves differently from one meant for a small subpanel. None of this is something a homeowner needs to fix personally, but knowing the failure modes means that when your production and consumption numbers look impossible — negative consumption, or a house that supposedly uses nothing at 8 p.m. — the likely explanation is a CT orientation or placement issue, not a broken solar system or a lying utility. A quick call to your installer to have the CT direction verified resolves most of these, and it's a far more common cause of "weird monitoring data" than any actual hardware fault.

## Why whole-home consumption data actually changes decisions

Production-only monitoring tells you a feel-good number — look how much clean energy the roof made today — but it can't answer the questions that determine what solar is worth to you. Those questions all depend on knowing what your house *did* with the power, and that's exactly what the consumption CTs supply. The single most important thing they reveal is your self-consumption ratio: of everything your panels produced, how much did you use directly in the home versus export to the grid? That ratio is the hinge on which a lot of solar economics turns, especially as more utilities move away from full retail net metering toward net-billing structures that pay less for exported power than they charge for imported power.

Here's why that matters in dollars. Under full net metering, a kilowatt-hour you export is worth the same as a kilowatt-hour you use, so self-consumption is economically irrelevant — a credit is a credit. But under net billing, where exports are credited below retail, every kilowatt-hour you use *the instant your panels make it* is worth full retail avoided cost, while every kilowatt-hour you export is worth only the lower credit rate. Suddenly self-consumption is worth real money, and you can't improve what you can't see. CT-based consumption monitoring shows you, hour by hour, when your production overshoots your usage and spills to the grid. Maybe your panels peak at midday when nobody's home and the house is drawing almost nothing, so most of that midday production exports at the low credit rate. Armed with that data, you can shift discretionary loads — running the dishwasher, charging an EV, pre-cooling the house, heating water — into the production window, converting low-value exports into full-value self-use. That's a behavioral change worth hundreds of dollars a year in a net-billing market, and it's invisible without consumption CTs.

The same data is what makes intelligent battery planning possible. A battery's job in a self-consumption strategy is to soak up midday surplus and release it in the evening, and the right battery size depends entirely on the *shape* of your daily production-versus-consumption curve — how big the midday surplus is and how large the evening deficit runs. A production-only view can't tell you that; a consumption-monitored view can, because it captures both curves at once. Reviewing a few weeks of that data before you buy storage tells you how much surplus is actually available to capture and how much evening load there is to cover, which is the difference between sizing a battery from real numbers and guessing. The broader capabilities of these platforms are covered in [what your solar monitoring app is really telling you](/blog/solar-monitoring-systems/); the consumption layer that CTs enable is the part that turns monitoring from observation into optimization.

There's a diagnostic payoff too, separate from the economic one. Because consumption monitoring watches your whole-home draw, it can surface things you'd never notice on a utility bill that only reports a monthly total. A pool pump running longer than you set it to, a water heater cycling more than expected, a phantom baseline that's higher than it should be — these show up as patterns in the consumption trace. Some homeowners discover that their "high electric bill" problem isn't the solar system underperforming at all; it's a load in the house they didn't know was running. If you're trying to reconcile what you produce, what you use, and what you're billed, pairing the CT consumption data with an [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) lets you translate the kilowatt-hours the clamps report into the dollars on your statement, and often explains a discrepancy that would otherwise look mysterious. And if you're still in the planning stage, the same consumption profile — your real usage shape, not a national average — is the best possible input for sizing the array itself; feeding your actual numbers into a [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) beats sizing off a rule of thumb, because two homes with identical annual totals can have completely different daily shapes, and the shape is what determines how much of your production you'll actually get to keep. Those two unassuming clamps in your panel are the sensors that make all of that visible.
