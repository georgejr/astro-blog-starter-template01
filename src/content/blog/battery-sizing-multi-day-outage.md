The fantasy that sells a lot of home batteries is riding out a week-long blackout with the lights on, the fridge humming, and the whole house running as if nothing happened. It's a reasonable thing to want, especially after a storm season that knocked the power out for days at a stretch. But the arithmetic behind that fantasy is unforgiving, and the mistake most people make is thinking of the problem as "how big a battery do I need?" when the real question is "how do I keep the battery from running empty?" A battery is a tank, and a multi-day outage empties the tank faster than most people imagine. What actually carries a whole house through several days without grid power isn't a bigger tank alone — it's a tank paired with something that refills it each day. That something, for most homes, is solar, and sizing for a long outage means sizing the pair together.

## Why the tank runs dry, and how fast

Start with the load, because everything follows from it. The average American home uses somewhere around 30 kilowatt-hours of electricity a day, and homes that heat, cool, cook, or heat water with electricity can run well past that. A battery, by contrast, stores its energy in a fixed amount measured in kilowatt-hours, and residential batteries commonly land in the range of 10 to 15 kWh of usable capacity per unit. Put those two numbers side by side and the problem is obvious: a single typical battery holds less than half of one average day's whole-home usage. Run everything in the house — heating or air conditioning, the electric range, the dryer, the water heater, all of it — and a single battery is empty before the first night is over. The gap between what a whole home draws and what a battery holds is the entire crux of multi-day backup, and no amount of clever sizing makes a 13 kWh battery cover 30 kWh of daily demand for even one full day, let alone several.

This is why the honest way to think about battery runtime is in terms of your actual draw, not a headline "hours of backup" figure. A battery's runtime is simply its usable capacity divided by the rate you're pulling power from it, and that rate swings enormously depending on what's running. The same battery that lasts most of a day powering a fridge, some lights, and phone chargers might last only a few hours if it's also carrying a central air conditioner and an electric oven. Working through exactly how that division plays out for different load profiles is the subject of [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/), and it's worth internalizing before you shop, because it reframes the whole purchase. You're not buying "three days of backup." You're buying a fixed number of kilowatt-hours, and how many days that lasts depends entirely on how fast you spend it.

For a single-day or overnight outage, the tank-only approach can work if the tank is big enough or the loads are trimmed enough. Stack two or three batteries, or discipline yourself to run only essentials, and a battery bank alone can genuinely carry a home from an evening outage through to the next morning. The problem is specifically the multi-day case. Once an outage stretches past a single night, a battery with no way to refill is just a countdown timer. Every kilowatt-hour you use is gone until grid power returns, and if the grid stays down for three days, you'd need three days' worth of storage sitting fully charged when the outage began — on the order of 90 kWh for a whole average home, which is six or seven batteries' worth of capacity bought and installed on the chance you'll someday need it all at once. Almost nobody sizes storage that way, because it means paying for an enormous amount of capacity that sits idle 360 days a year. The economics push hard toward a different answer.

That different answer is recharge. If the battery can be topped up during the outage, the whole equation changes: instead of needing to store the entire multi-day load in advance, you only need to store enough to bridge from one recharge to the next — essentially, enough to get through each night. During a grid outage a generator can provide that recharge, but the option most solar homeowners already have, or can build toward, is the array on the roof. A properly configured solar-plus-storage system keeps generating through a blackout and uses that generation to both run the house during the day and refill the battery for the coming night. That capability doesn't happen automatically — it depends on how the system is wired and configured, since an ordinary grid-tied array actually shuts down in an outage for safety and won't power anything unless it's paired with storage designed to island. The key point for sizing is that a system built to recharge from solar transforms a multi-day outage from an endurance test into a daily cycle.

## Sizing the pair so each day refills

Once recharge is the strategy, sizing splits into two linked questions: how much battery do you need to get through one night, and how much solar do you need to refill it plus run the house through the next day? Neither number stands alone, and getting the balance wrong in either direction wastes money or leaves you short.

The battery side is really an overnight-plus-margin calculation. You need enough usable storage to carry your overnight loads from sundown to the point the next morning when the sun climbs high enough to start recharging — plus a cushion for a cloudy day when the array underperforms. Overnight whole-home loads are much smaller than daytime loads for most families, since the big daytime draws like laundry, cooking, and much of the cooling or heating cycle are quieter at night. If your overnight draw is, say, 10 to 15 kWh, then one or two batteries covers a normal night with margin to spare. The cushion matters because the whole strategy leans on the sun showing up: a string of heavily overcast days means the array recharges the battery only partially, so you want enough stored capacity that a poor solar day doesn't leave you dark before the next morning. Sizing the battery to roughly a night and a half of overnight load, rather than exactly one night, buys that resilience without ballooning into a bank you'll rarely use fully. The mechanics of matching battery capacity to a specific load list, rather than guessing, are laid out in [sizing a battery for essential loads](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), and the same method applies whether you're covering just essentials or a fuller slice of the home.

The solar side is where multi-day outages are actually won or lost, and it's the part people most often underestimate. To sustain the house indefinitely off-grid, the array has to generate enough in a day to both run everything you use during daylight and put back into the battery whatever you'll spend overnight. That's a higher bar than simply offsetting your annual bill, which is what most grid-tied arrays are sized for. Consider a worked example with clearly labeled assumptions: suppose your outage-day whole-home usage trims down to 25 kWh (you're being a little careful, but not roughing it), and you're in a location averaging 4.5 peak sun-hours a day in the season that matters. To generate 25 kWh in 4.5 sun-hours, ignoring losses, you'd need roughly 5.5 kW of array just to break even on that day — and real-world losses from heat, wiring, and battery round-trip inefficiency push the practical figure higher, toward 7 kW or more. In deep winter, when sun-hours might fall to 2.5 or 3, the same daily energy would demand a substantially larger array or a substantially trimmed load. The season of your likely outages matters enormously: a summer hurricane region can lean on long, strong sun-hours to recharge easily, while a winter ice-storm region faces short, weak days that make solar recharge far harder and lean the strategy back toward a generator or aggressive load-cutting.

Because the array has to carry both the daytime load and the overnight recharge, sizing it purely from your electric bill will usually leave you short for true off-grid endurance. The [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool gives you a solid starting point from your annual usage, but for outage resilience you should treat the result as a floor and think about whether your worst-case season's sun-hours can still refill the battery on top of running the house. If your roof or budget can't support an array large enough to fully recharge in your bad season, that's not a failure — it just means the honest plan is partial support during long winter outages rather than seamless whole-home operation, and knowing that in advance is far better than discovering it on day three of an ice storm.

## The realistic plan most homes should actually build

Very few homeowners genuinely need, or should pay for, a system that runs the entire house at full tilt indefinitely off-grid. That's the province of remote off-grid properties, not suburban homes with occasional outages. For most people the smarter target is a system that keeps the essentials running comfortably and indefinitely while treating the power-hungry luxuries as things you throttle back during an outage. Drawing that line — deciding what stays on and what you're willing to switch off when the grid fails — is the single most important design decision, and it's the difference between a battery that lasts hours and one that lasts days. The trade-offs between backing up the whole panel versus a curated set of critical circuits are exactly what [whole-home versus partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/) is built to help you weigh, and for multi-day resilience the partial approach is usually the wiser and far cheaper answer.

The prioritized approach works because it shrinks the load the solar has to recharge each day, which in turn shrinks the array and battery you have to buy. Keep the refrigerator, freezer, furnace fan or a mini-split, well pump, internet, lights, and device charging on backup, and those loads might total 8 to 12 kWh a day — a figure a modest array can genuinely refill even in mediocre sun, and a figure one or two batteries can carry overnight. Meanwhile the electric dryer, the oven, the second air conditioner, and the hot tub simply wait for the grid to return, or run briefly during peak sun when the array has surplus to spare. This is how a real home rides out a multi-day outage: not by matching the grid's full capacity from a battery bank, but by living a slightly leaner electric life for a few days while solar quietly refills the tank each morning. During strong midday sun you can even run a heavier appliance directly off the array's excess before it goes into the battery, which is the outage-day version of doing your laundry when the sun is high.

To pressure-test whatever plan you land on, model it before you buy rather than trusting a salesperson's round number. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you plug in a battery capacity and a load and see roughly how long it carries you, which is the fastest way to sanity-check whether a proposed battery covers your overnight essentials with margin. Pair that with an honest accounting of your worst likely outage — its typical season, its typical length in your area, and the loads you truly can't do without — and the right sizing tends to reveal itself. Keep the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit in the frame while you cost it out: a battery paired with, or added to, a qualifying solar system can be eligible for the 30% federal credit under current IRS rules, which meaningfully softens the price of building recharge capacity, though the eligibility details belong in IRS guidance rather than a sales claim. What you're ultimately buying for a multi-day outage isn't a giant battery. It's a balanced pair — enough storage to cross each night, enough solar to refill it each day, and a clear-eyed decision about which loads earn a place on backup and which ones can wait for the lights to come back on.
