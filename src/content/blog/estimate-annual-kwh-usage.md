Solar sizing begins with one number — your annual kilowatt-hours — and recent movers, new-build owners, and anyone who just started renting out a property often don't have it. You have one bill, or three, or a house you've lived in for six weeks. The good news is that annual usage is a fairly stable, physical quantity: it's the sum of every appliance's draw multiplied by how long each runs over a year. You can reconstruct that total from partial information, as long as you account for the one thing a short sample hides — the seasons.

That seasonal swing is the whole reason a single bill can mislead. A home's usage rises and falls with heating and cooling, sometimes by a factor of two between the mildest and harshest months. [Why your usage swings with the seasons](/blog/seasonal-electricity-usage/) shows the shape of it; the practical consequence is that you can't just multiply any one month by twelve and trust the answer. Here are four ways to get a defensible annual figure, roughly in order of how much data they need.

## Annualize a single bill with a seasonal shape

If you have even one full monthly bill, you can annualize it — but not by multiplying by twelve. Instead, place that month on the seasonal curve. A bill from a mild month (spring or fall) is close to your monthly average, so twelve times that is a reasonable first pass. A bill from peak summer or deep winter is a high point, so twelve times it overshoots, sometimes badly.

A better approach if you have two or three bills from different seasons: average them, then multiply by twelve, and nudge upward if all your samples came from mild months (because you're missing the peaks). Suppose three shoulder-season bills average 780 kWh. Twelve times that is 9,360 kWh — then add a margin for the summer and winter peaks you haven't seen, landing somewhere around 10,000–11,000 kWh. That's an estimate with honest error bars, not a false precision. Reading the bills correctly is a prerequisite; [reading your electric bill line by line](/blog/how-to-read-electric-bill/) shows where the kWh figure hides among the charges.

## Read your own meter over a few weeks

You don't have to wait for the utility. Your meter counts kWh continuously, and you can sample it yourself. Note the reading, wait a week or two, note it again, and the difference is your usage for that span. Divide by the number of days to get a daily average, then scale to a year — again adjusting for season.

This method is powerful because it's *your* house with *your* habits, not an average. Its weakness is the same as the single-bill method: a two-week sample in October tells you little about August. If you can take a sample in a hot stretch and another in a mild one, you can bracket your range: the mild sample sets a floor, the harsh sample suggests the peak, and your annual total lives between them. A smart meter's app, if your utility offers one, does this automatically and often shows daily history you can sum directly.

## Build it up appliance by appliance

When you have almost no billing history — a brand-new home, or one you haven't moved into — you can estimate from the bottom up by inventorying the big loads. This is the most work and the least precise, but it's grounded in physics: each appliance has a wattage and a runtime, and watts times hours is watt-hours.

Focus on the heavy hitters, because a handful of loads dominate most homes:

- **Heating and cooling** — usually the largest single category, and the most seasonal.
- **Water heating** — a steady, year-round load, larger if it's electric than if it's gas.
- **Major appliances** — refrigerator, oven, dryer, dishwasher.
- **Everything else** — lighting, electronics, and standby loads, which sum to a surprising baseline.

You don't need to inventory every phone charger. Estimate the big categories, add a baseline for the long tail, and you'll land within a reasonable band. If you plan to add an EV or a heat pump, add those loads now — an EV alone can add several thousand kWh a year, and sizing for it is far cheaper than retrofitting panels later.

## Sanity-check against a benchmark

Whatever method you use, cross-check the result against a reference point so a decimal-place error doesn't slip through. The average US home uses about 10,500 kWh a year, though the real spread is enormous — an efficient apartment-sized home might use half that, while an all-electric house in a hot climate with a pool can more than double it. [How much electricity the average American home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) puts that distribution in context.

Use the benchmark as a smell test, not an answer. If your bottom-up estimate says 4,000 kWh but you have central air and electric heat, something's missing. If it says 22,000 kWh for a small, gas-heated home, you've double-counted. The average is a guardrail; your own bills and meter are the real inputs. An estimate with clear assumptions beats waiting a year for perfect data — you can always refine the design once a full billing cycle confirms the number.
