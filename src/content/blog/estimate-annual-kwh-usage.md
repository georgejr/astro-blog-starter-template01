Solar sizing begins with one number — your annual kilowatt-hours — and a surprising number of people who need it don't have it. Recent movers, new-build owners, anyone who just started renting out a property, or a homeowner who finally decided to go solar three months after buying the house: all of them face the same gap. You have one bill, or three, or a house you have lived in for six weeks, and the standard advice to "just use your last twelve months of usage" is useless to you. The good news is that annual usage is a fairly stable, physical quantity. It is the sum of every appliance's draw multiplied by how long each one runs over a year, and that total does not lurch around unpredictably from one year to the next for the same household in the same house. You can reconstruct it from partial information as long as you respect the one thing a short sample always hides, which is the seasons.

That seasonal swing is the whole reason a single bill can mislead you, and it is worth fixing firmly in mind before we get to methods. A home's electricity usage rises and falls with heating and cooling, and in many houses the gap between the mildest month and the harshest one is a factor of two or more. A bill from a temperate April tells you almost nothing about a July with the air conditioner running all day, and a July bill wildly overstates what a spring or fall month looks like. [Why your usage swings with the seasons](/blog/seasonal-electricity-usage/) shows the shape of that curve in detail; the practical consequence for estimating is blunt. You cannot take any single month, multiply it by twelve, and trust the result, because you have no idea where on the curve that month sits. Every method that follows is really a method for getting around that one problem — for recovering the shape of a full year from a sample that only shows you a slice of it.

## Working from the bills you already have

If you have even one full monthly bill, you can annualize it, but the multiply-by-twelve reflex is exactly the trap. The right move is to place that month on the seasonal curve before you scale it. A bill from a mild shoulder month — spring or fall, when neither heating nor cooling is working hard — sits close to your monthly average, so twelve times that figure is a reasonable first pass at your annual total. A bill from peak summer or the depths of winter is a high point on the curve, so twelve times it overshoots, sometimes badly enough to size you into a system half again larger than you need. Knowing which kind of month your bill came from is therefore the first thing to establish, and it takes nothing more than noticing whether your heating or cooling was running when that meter was read.

You get a much better estimate the moment you have two or three bills from different seasons. Average them, multiply by twelve, and then nudge the result upward if all your samples happen to come from mild months, because in that case you are missing the peaks entirely and your average understates the year. Suppose three shoulder-season bills average 780 kWh a month. Twelve times that is 9,360 kWh as a baseline, and then you add a margin for the summer and winter peaks you have not actually seen, which lands you somewhere around 10,000 to 11,000 kWh for the year. That is an estimate carrying honest error bars, which is exactly what you want at this stage — a defensible range rather than a false precision that pretends three bills can tell you a number to the last kilowatt-hour. The discipline is to state your assumption out loud: "these are mild-month bills, so my annual figure is probably a bit above twelve times their average." An estimate you can explain is one you can correct later; a single number with no reasoning behind it is one you will trust more than it deserves.

There is often more billing history available than you think, and it is worth chasing before you resort to estimating. Many utilities will show the account holder twelve or more months of usage history for the service address through an online portal, which means that even a few weeks into a new home you may be able to pull the previous occupants' full annual usage for the very meter you now pay for. That history is not perfectly yours — the prior household's habits, thermostat settings, and appliances were different — but it is a real, measured annual shape for your exact house in your exact climate, which is a far better starting point than a national average. The move is to take that historical annual figure and adjust it for the ways your household differs. A family that keeps the house cooler in summer than the retirees who lived there before should nudge the cooling-season months upward; someone who works from home all day adds a daytime baseline the previous commuters did not; a switch from a gas dryer or water heater to electric, or the arrival of an EV, adds specific loads you can estimate on top. Life changes matter as much as the house does. A new baby, a household that grows from two people to four, a home office that replaces a daily commute, or a hot tub added to the deck can each move annual usage by a noticeable margin, and none of them will show up in a bill from before they happened. Treat any inherited history as a well-shaped baseline to be corrected rather than a final answer, and write down the corrections you made so a future you can check them against real bills.

All of this assumes you are reading the right number off the bill in the first place, which is less obvious than it sounds. Utility bills bury the kilowatt-hour figure among a thicket of charges — generation, delivery, taxes, fixed fees, and sometimes tiered or time-of-use breakdowns that split your usage across several lines — and it is easy to grab a dollar amount or a partial figure by mistake. [Reading your electric bill line by line](/blog/how-to-read-electric-bill/) shows exactly where the usage in kilowatt-hours hides and how to separate it from the money, which is a prerequisite for every method here. If you pull the wrong number off the page, no amount of careful seasonal adjustment will save the estimate. Get the kWh figure right first, confirm it is a full billing month and not a partial or estimated read, and only then start scaling it across the year.

## When the bills run out: metering, appliance math, and a benchmark check

You do not have to wait on the utility to accumulate a year of statements. Your meter counts kilowatt-hours continuously, and you can sample it directly. Note the reading, wait a week or two, note it again, and the difference is your usage over that span. Divide by the number of days between reads to get a daily average, then scale that to a year — and, as always, adjust for where in the season your sample fell. This method has a real advantage over borrowing an average: it is your house, your appliances, your habits, and your thermostat setting, not a national figure that may not describe you at all. Its weakness is the same one that dogs the single-bill approach. A two-week sample taken in October tells you very little about August, so a single meter read gives you a point, not a curve. The way around it is to bracket the year: take one sample during a hot or cold stretch when heating or cooling is working hard, and another during a mild stretch when it mostly isn't. The mild sample sets a floor for your usage, the harsh sample suggests the peak, and your true annual total lives somewhere between the two scaled figures. If your utility offers a smart-meter app, it often does this bracketing for you automatically, showing daily history you can simply add up rather than sampling by hand.

When you have almost no history at all — a brand-new home, or one you have not yet moved into — you can build the estimate from the bottom up by inventorying the big loads. This is the most labor and the least precise of the methods, but it rests on solid physics: every appliance has a wattage and a runtime, and watts multiplied by hours are watt-hours. The key is to resist the urge to catalog everything and instead focus on the heavy hitters, because in most homes a handful of loads dominate the total and the rest is a long tail of small stuff. Heating and cooling is usually the single largest category and by far the most seasonal, so it deserves the most care and an explicit seasonal assumption. Water heating is a steady, year-round load that is much larger if it runs on electricity than if it runs on gas, so which fuel yours uses genuinely moves the number. The major appliances — refrigerator, oven, clothes dryer, dishwasher — form the next tier. Everything else, meaning lighting, electronics, and the standby draw of devices that never fully switch off, sums to a surprisingly large baseline that you can estimate as a lump rather than item by item. You do not need to hunt down every phone charger. Estimate the big categories honestly, add a baseline for the long tail, and you will land inside a reasonable band. One thing to add deliberately: if you plan to bring on an electric vehicle or a heat pump, fold those loads in now rather than later. An EV alone can add several thousand kilowatt-hours a year, and sizing a solar array for it up front is far cheaper than retrofitting panels once the load shows up.

Whatever method you land on, cross-check the result against a reference point before you build anything on it, because a single misplaced decimal or a double-counted load can quietly throw the whole estimate off. The average US home uses roughly 10,500 kWh a year, and that figure is worth keeping in your pocket as a benchmark — not because your house is average, but because the number gives you a feel for the right order of magnitude. The real spread around that average is enormous, which is exactly why it works as a check rather than an answer. An efficient, apartment-sized home in a mild climate might use half of it, while a large all-electric house in a hot region with a pool and a couple of EVs can more than double it. [How much electricity the average American home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) puts that distribution in context and shows how far a household can reasonably sit from the middle, which is what tells you whether your own estimate is plausibly high, plausibly low, or a red flag.

Use the benchmark as a smell test rather than a substitute for your own numbers. If your careful bottom-up estimate comes out at 4,000 kWh but your house has central air and electric heat, something is missing — a load you forgot or a runtime you underestimated — and it is worth finding before you size a system around it. If it comes out at 22,000 kWh for a small, gas-heated home, you have almost certainly double-counted somewhere. The average is a guardrail that catches gross errors; your own bills and meter reads are the real inputs, and no benchmark should override them when they disagree in a way you can explain.
