Something is off. Your monitoring app shows one section of the array producing less than it should, or the whole system went dark, or an inverter is throwing an error code you have never seen. Your first instinct is right: solar equipment comes with substantial warranties, and a genuine hardware failure is usually somebody's responsibility to fix on their dime. The trouble is that "somebody" could be one of three different parties depending on what failed and why, and a claim filed to the wrong one, or filed without the right proof, is a claim that stalls. Getting a solar warranty resolved quickly is less about persistence than about figuring out, before you make a single call, which warranty actually applies and who is on the hook for it.

## Figuring out which warranty you are even claiming

A residential solar system is covered by a stack of overlapping warranties, and they do not all come from the same place or cover the same failures. Sorting out which one applies is the entire first half of the job, and getting it wrong is the most common reason a claim goes in circles. There are, broadly, three layers. The panel manufacturer's warranty covers the modules themselves, and it usually splits into two parts: a product warranty against defects in materials and workmanship, and a separate, longer performance warranty guaranteeing the panels will still produce at least a certain percentage of their rated output after a given number of years. The inverter manufacturer's warranty is its own thing, covering the inverter or microinverters, and because the inverter is the component most likely to fail first, this is the warranty owners invoke most often. Then there is the installer's workmanship warranty, which covers the labor and the installation itself, the mounting, the flashing, the wiring, everything the crew did with their hands rather than the parts they bolted down.

The reason this matters so much is that the failure tells you which door to knock on. A panel with a visible defect, delamination, cracked cells, a burn mark, or one that has clearly stopped performing, is a manufacturer product-warranty matter. A whole string of panels underperforming to spec after many years might be a performance-warranty question, which is a different and often more paperwork-heavy claim. An inverter throwing fault codes or gone completely dead is an inverter-manufacturer claim. But a leak around a mounting foot, a connector that was never seated properly, a panel that came loose in wind, or wiring that was done wrong, those are workmanship issues and belong to the installer, not the manufacturer, no matter how new the equipment is. The distinction between what the equipment maker owes and what the installer owes is the single most useful thing to understand before you pick up the phone, and it is worth reading our full breakdown of [workmanship versus equipment warranties and who fixes what on your roof](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) so you knock on the right door the first time.

There is a wrinkle that catches people out, and it is the interaction between the two. A failed panel is a manufacturer part, but getting a technician onto your roof to diagnose it, remove it, and install the replacement is labor, and manufacturer warranties frequently cover the replacement part while saying little or nothing about the labor to swap it. If your installer's workmanship warranty is still in force and generous, they may cover that labor. If it has expired, or if the installer is gone, you could be on the hook for the labor even when the part itself is free. This is why the health and length of your installer relationship matters as much as the manufacturer's paperwork, and it is a big reason the choice of a stable, reputable installer pays off years down the road when something breaks. Before you decide a claim is worth pursuing at all, it is worth roughly weighing the cost of the fix against the value at stake, and our piece on [deciding whether to repair or replace a failed solar panel](/blog/repair-or-replace-solar-panels/) walks through that judgment, because occasionally the labor to chase a warranty claim on a single aging panel costs more than the production it would restore.

It helps to know the rough lengths these warranties tend to run, because the clock is often what decides which one is even available to you. Panel product warranties have commonly run in the ten-to-fifteen-year range, though many premium modules extend further, while the performance warranty on that same panel typically stretches to twenty-five years or more, which is why a module can be past its product warranty for defects yet still inside its performance guarantee for output. Inverter warranties are usually the shortest of the bunch, frequently around ten to twelve years for a string inverter and sometimes extendable for a fee, while microinverters often carry longer terms. Workmanship warranties from the installer vary the most, running anywhere from a couple of years to ten or more, which is exactly why two claims that look identical can land very differently depending on whose paper is still in force. Treat these as typical shapes rather than promises; your own documents govern, and the first thing to do when something fails is read them for the actual terms and dates rather than lean on the general pattern.

A quick note on the most likely scenario, because it shapes how you should expect this to go. The inverter is, by a wide margin, the component most solar owners will see fail first, often years before anything goes wrong with the panels themselves. If your system has stopped producing entirely, an inverter fault is the leading suspect, and our explainer on [why the inverter is your system's most likely first failure](/blog/solar-inverter-failure/) covers the symptoms and what a claim typically looks like. Knowing that ahead of time means you are not blindsided when, a decade in, the box on the wall is the thing that quits rather than the panels on the roof.

## Building a claim that does not stall

Once you know which warranty applies, the claim itself is mostly about documentation and following the right channel, and the owners who get fast resolutions are the ones who show up with proof already in hand rather than trying to assemble it after the claim is denied. Start by gathering your paperwork before you call anyone. That means your original contract and any warranty certificates, the equipment details, panel and inverter make, model, and serial numbers, and your proof of the original installation date, since warranties run from install. If you registered the equipment with the manufacturer at install, which some warranties require to be fully valid, have that registration handy; if you never registered it, do so before or while you file, because an unregistered claim can hit a wall on a technicality. Pull your monitoring data too. A system that logs production is a gift in a warranty claim, because it shows exactly when output dropped, by how much, and on which part of the array, and that record is far more persuasive than a homeowner's description of a hunch.

With paperwork in hand, route the claim correctly. In most cases the smart first call is your installer, even for an equipment failure, because a good installer will diagnose the problem, tell you which warranty it falls under, and often handle the manufacturer claim on your behalf as part of their service, which spares you the runaround of dealing with a manufacturer's warranty department directly. This is one of the real, unglamorous benefits of using an established installer that is still in business years later. If your installer is gone, out of business, or unresponsive, you go to the manufacturer directly, using the contact channel in the warranty document, and you will typically need to describe the failure, provide the serial numbers and install date, and often submit photos and monitoring data. Many manufacturers require a diagnosis or an inspection, sometimes remote and sometimes by an authorized technician, before they approve a replacement, so be ready for a step where someone confirms the failure is a covered defect rather than damage or wear that falls outside the warranty.

The documentation you provide is what determines the speed of everything after. Clear, timestamped photos of the problem, your monitoring graphs showing the production drop, and clean records of your equipment and install date turn a claim from an argument into a formality. Vague reports without evidence are what get bounced back with requests for more information, adding weeks each round. It also helps to understand the boundaries of what these warranties cover, because filing for something outside the terms wastes everyone's time: warranties cover defects and premature failure, not damage from a storm, not neglect, not problems caused by someone other than the original installer working on the system, which is one reason letting an unauthorized party touch your array can jeopardize coverage. Physical damage from hail or a falling branch is generally a homeowners insurance matter, not a warranty one, and knowing which lane your problem is in keeps you from filing the wrong kind of claim.

A worked version of the labor gap shows why confirming who pays matters before you commit to a fix. Assume a single panel fails in year twelve, the manufacturer honors it and ships a free replacement module, but your installer's workmanship warranty ran out at year ten, so the labor is now yours. A technician has to make a service call, get on the roof, isolate and unbolt the failed panel, swap in the replacement, and re-torque and re-seal the mounts. Assume that runs a half-day of skilled labor plus a truck roll; at a labeled placeholder of a few hundred dollars for the visit, the "free" warranty replacement still costs real money out of pocket for the hands that install it. Now weigh that against the panel's remaining contribution. A single aging module might produce only a few hundred kilowatt-hours a year, so at a labeled assumption of $0.17 per kWh, restoring it recovers on the order of tens of dollars of electricity annually. The part being free does not settle the question; the labor set against the recovered production does, and occasionally the honest answer is to leave one dead panel alone rather than pay to chase a covered claim.

If a claim genuinely stalls, escalation has a ladder worth climbing in order. Start by moving up a level within the same company, from the first-line rep to a supervisor or the warranty department, referencing your case number and the dates of prior contacts. If the manufacturer routes service through authorized installers, ask to be assigned a different authorized servicer when your original one is unresponsive or out of business. When a paid deposit or repair is involved and the other side simply will not perform, a credit-card chargeback within its window, a complaint to your state consumer-protection office or attorney general, and, for larger sums, small-claims court are the real backstops, and the paper trail you kept from day one is what makes any of them work. Most claims never reach this point; the ones that do are won by the owner who documented every contact, not the one who argued loudest.

Keep records of the claim itself as you go, dates, names, case numbers, and what was said, because a claim that drags is far easier to escalate when you can show a paper trail of your attempts. If a manufacturer or installer goes unresponsive, that documentation is your leverage. And set expectations on the money: a warranty claim ideally costs you nothing beyond your time, but the labor gap described earlier means you should confirm up front, in writing where possible, who is paying for the part and who is paying for the hands that install it, so there is no surprise invoice when the technician packs up. If you find yourself facing an out-of-warranty repair, or weighing whether a fix is worth it against replacing a component outright, running the numbers on the replacement cost against your system's remaining value with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) can ground the decision. A warranty claim handled well is undramatic: you identify the right warranty, you route it through your installer where you can, you back it with documentation, and you keep a record. The owners who struggle are the ones who call the wrong party, cannot prove their install date, or have let their equipment registration and their installer relationship lapse. Everything about a smooth claim is set up years earlier, in the records you keep and the installer you chose.
