"Zero down, and your monthly solar payment is less than your current electric bill." It's the most effective line in residential solar, because it sounds like getting something for nothing — panels on your roof, no check written, and a smaller bill starting immediately. The pitch isn't a lie, exactly. But it leans on a comparison designed to look better than the deal underneath it, and the mechanics that make "$0 down" possible are the same ones that quietly raise what you pay overall.

None of this makes financed solar a bad choice. A solar loan can absolutely leave you ahead, and paying nothing upfront is a legitimate reason to use one. The goal here is to see the full number, so "less than my electric bill" doesn't do your thinking for you.

## What "$0 down" actually is

There's no such thing as free solar. "$0 down" means a loan — you're borrowing the entire cost of the system and paying it back monthly, usually over 10 to 25 years. The panels are yours (which is the important distinction from a lease), the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is yours to claim if you have the tax liability to use it, and the debt is yours too.

So the honest framing isn't "solar for nothing." It's "solar financed in full, with the loan's cost baked into the deal." Everything that follows is about finding where that cost hides, because it doesn't show up as a down payment — it shows up in three quieter places.

## The monthly-savings sleight of hand

The pitch compares your new loan payment against your *old* electric bill and calls the difference savings. That comparison has a leak: solar rarely takes your electric bill to zero. You keep a utility connection, pay fixed monthly charges, and typically still draw some grid power at night or in winter. Your real monthly cost after solar is the loan payment *plus* the residual utility bill — and that total is what should beat your old bill, not the loan payment alone.

Assume a household with a $180 monthly electric bill, offered a $0-down loan with a $140 monthly payment. The pitch says $40 a month ahead. Now add the residual bill the panels don't erase.

| | Before solar | Pitch's version | Honest version |
|---|---|---|---|
| Loan payment | — | $140 | $140 |
| Remaining utility bill | $180 | $0 (implied) | $35 |
| Total monthly cost | $180 | $140 | $175 |
| "Savings" vs. before | — | $40 | $5 |

The assumptions are illustrative, not a quote, but the structure is the point: the pitch counted the loan payment against the whole old bill and ignored the connection charge and the power still drawn from the grid. The honest monthly gap is often far thinner than advertised, and it can go slightly negative in year one. This is the same trap that separates [lifetime savings from month-one savings](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/) — a deal can be a solid long-run win while barely breaking even each month at first.

## The dealer fee hiding in the price

Here's the mechanism behind the low advertised interest rates on solar loans. Lenders offering a headline rate like 2.99% recover their economics by charging the installer a dealer fee — commonly 15% to 30% of the system price — which the installer folds straight into the price you're quoted. You never see it as a line item. You see a higher total, financed at an attractively low rate.

The tell is the cash price. Ask any installer offering a low-rate loan what the same system costs in cash, and the gap between the two is the fee, roughly. A system quoted at $30,000 on a 2.99% loan might be $23,000 in cash — that $7,000 spread is the dealer fee dressed up as a low rate. You're not getting cheap money; you're paying for it in the principal. Working through [what a solar loan really adds to the price](/blog/solar-loan-true-cost/) shows how a low rate on an inflated balance often costs more than a higher rate on an honest one.

## The tax-credit paydown trap

The third surprise is structural. Many $0-down solar loans are written assuming you'll receive the 30% federal credit and immediately apply it as a lump payment against the loan — usually within the first 12 to 18 months. The low advertised monthly payment is calculated as if that paydown already happened.

If you claim the credit and hand it to the loan, fine — the payment stays low. But if you can't use the full credit (you need enough tax liability to absorb it), or you spend the refund elsewhere, the loan re-amortizes at month 18 and your payment jumps, sometimes by a noticeable amount, for the remaining term. Homeowners who didn't understand this feature are the ones blindsided by a payment increase on a "fixed" deal. Confirm whether your loan has a re-amortization feature and whether you can actually claim the credit before you count on the low number lasting.

## How to check if you're really ahead

Cut through all three with the same discipline. Get the cash price alongside the financed price and treat the difference as the cost of the financing. Compare your new *total* monthly cost — loan payment plus residual utility bill — against your old bill, not the loan payment in isolation. Confirm you can use the federal credit and understand what your payment does if you don't apply it to the loan. And run the whole thing over its full life, where a modest monthly edge compounds into real money, using a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) with honest inputs rather than the pitch's.

A $0-down deal that clears those checks is a genuinely good way to own solar without touching your savings. One that only looks good because the comparison was rigged is still a loan — just one you understood too late. The math isn't hard. It's only hidden.
