Walk down any residential street and count the solar roofs, and you'll notice they're all essentially the same panel: rigid, framed, dark blue or black rectangles bolted to racking. Those are crystalline silicon. The flexible, sometimes rollable sheets you see draped over RV roofs, printed on spec sheets, and stretched across giant desert power plants — thin-film panels — barely appear on houses at all. That near-total absence isn't marketing inertia or some conspiracy of installers pushing what they know. It's the clean result of a specific trade-off, and rooftops resolved that trade-off decisively in one direction. Understanding why tells you a lot about how to read your own quote.

## Two ways to build a solar cell, and the number that separates them

Crystalline panels are cut from silicon ingots. Monocrystalline cells come from a single continuous crystal, grown slowly and sliced into wafers, and they're the higher-efficiency, higher-cost option — the deep-black cells you see on most modern premium panels. Polycrystalline cells are cast from melted silicon fragments that solidify into many crystals, which makes them slightly cheaper to produce and slightly less efficient, with the faintly speckled blue look of older arrays. Both are rigid, framed under tempered glass, and between them they account for the overwhelming majority of what installers put on homes today. Thin-film takes an entirely different route: a thin layer of photovoltaic material — cadmium telluride, CIGS, or amorphous silicon — deposited directly onto a backing such as glass, metal, or plastic. The active layer is a fraction of the thickness of a silicon wafer, which is exactly what makes thin-film light, sometimes flexible, and cheap to manufacture per unit of area. Those are genuine advantages. They simply aren't the advantages a roof cares most about.

The number that decides the whole contest is efficiency — the share of incoming sunlight a panel converts into electricity — and it's where the two technologies split hardest. Modern monocrystalline modules commonly land around 20% efficiency, with premium lines pushing a bit higher; polycrystalline sits a little below that. Thin-film modules typically run lower, often in the low-to-mid teens depending on the chemistry. That gap sounds abstract until you remember what a roof is: a fixed, finite amount of space. Lower efficiency means you need more square footage to produce the same output, and on a roof, square footage is the one thing you can't order more of. A useful primer on why the same physical rectangle can carry very different power ratings is [solar panel wattage explained](/blog/solar-panel-wattage-explained/); the short version is that watts per panel divided by the panel's footprint is what a space-constrained roof is actually buying, and that ratio is where crystalline pulls ahead.

Here is the comparison laid out in the terms that actually decide residential systems:

| Factor | Crystalline (mono/poly) | Thin-film |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Typical efficiency | ~18–22% | ~10–14% |
| Roof area for same output | Less | More |
| Weight and profile | Rigid, framed, heavier | Light, low-profile, often flexible |
| Temperature behavior | Good | Often slightly better |
| Where it dominates | Home and commercial roofs | Utility fields, RVs, portable, curved surfaces |

The efficiency row is the entire argument for houses. Picture a roof with room for a 7 kW crystalline array. Hitting that same 7 kW with thin-film modules could demand noticeably more area than the usable roof planes offer, because each thin-film panel produces fewer watts per square foot and you'd need more of them to reach the target. When space is the binding constraint — and on the great majority of homes it is, once you subtract the shaded planes, the north-facing slopes, and the areas cluttered with vents and chimneys — the denser technology wins by default. There's no cleverness required; the math just points one way. ## Where thin film genuinely wins, and where the worry about longevity lands

None of this makes thin-film a bad technology, and it's worth saying that plainly because the residential comparison can make it sound like a loser. It isn't a loser — it's a different tool, aimed at different jobs, and its strengths are real wherever roof space stops being the limiting factor. On utility-scale fields, cheap land makes area far less precious than cost per watt, and thin-film's low manufacturing cost genuinely shines when you're covering acres rather than a single roof. On RVs, boats, and portable kits, light weight and flexibility beat maximum watts per square foot, and a curved or weight-sensitive surface can rule out heavy glass modules entirely — a rollable thin-film panel goes places a rigid framed one physically cannot. On certain façades and curved architectural structures, rigid panels simply won't conform to the shape, and thin-film is the only option that fits. In every one of those cases, the thing that disqualifies thin-film on a house — needing more area for the same output — is a non-issue, because area is abundant or the geometry demands flexibility.

Thin-film also tends to shrug off heat a little better than silicon. As panels warm on a hot afternoon, they lose some efficiency, and thin-film's temperature coefficient is often gentler, so it holds a bit more of its output when the roof is baking. That's a legitimate edge, and in very hot climates it narrows the gap slightly. But "slightly better in heat" doesn't come close to outweighing "needs meaningfully more roof area" on a house that doesn't have the extra area to give — the space penalty is the dominant term, and the heat advantage is a footnote to it rather than a rebuttal.

It helps to see where the two technologies actually compete, because framing it as a rivalry misleads. On a house, they barely compete at all — the roof's fixed area hands the decision to crystalline before the conversation starts. Where they genuinely go head to head is on a utility developer's spreadsheet, weighing acres of cheap desert land against the cost per watt of covering it, and there thin-film's low manufacturing cost and heat tolerance can win outright. The same panel that would be a poor choice bolted to your garage is a shrewd one spread across a hundred-acre field, because the constraint that governs each project is different. A homeowner who reads a glowing account of thin-film's growth in utility installations and assumes that momentum should carry onto their roof is quietly importing the wrong constraint into their own decision. Utility-scale success says nothing about what belongs on a space-limited residential roof, and the two markets have been diverging on exactly this point for years.

Buyers sometimes worry that one technology quietly dies faster than the other, as though the space efficiency comes at the cost of longevity. It doesn't, at least not for modern modules. Both crystalline and quality thin-film panels degrade gradually rather than failing suddenly, shedding a fraction of a percent of rated output per year, and both carry multi-decade performance warranties that guarantee a floor on output at year twenty-five. The underlying mechanisms differ, and it's true that some older thin-film chemistries showed a steeper drop in their first months of exposure before stabilizing, which fed a reputation for fragility that modern modules have largely outgrown. Today's panels of either type are long-lived, and longevity isn't the reason crystalline dominates roofs. There's a practical dimension to the space penalty that spec-sheet comparisons tend to skip. Reaching a given system size with lower-efficiency panels doesn't just consume more roof — it means more physical modules, more mounting points, more wiring runs, and more labor to install and later maintain the array. On a home that's already short on clean, unshaded roof planes, that larger footprint may force panels onto compromised orientations — a partly shaded slope, a less ideal angle — that a smaller crystalline array could have avoided entirely by fitting on the best roof faces. So the efficiency gap doesn't only cost you area in the abstract; it can push the array onto worse real estate and drag down the whole system's production in a way the panel's rated numbers never reveal. That downstream effect is a big part of why installers, who have to make arrays fit real roofs rather than idealized ones, reach for the denser technology almost reflexively on houses.

So what does all this mean when a quote lands on your kitchen table? If a residential proposal specifies thin-film modules, that's worth a direct, unembarrassed question about why, because it's genuinely the unusual choice for a house and the burden is on the installer to justify it. There can be perfectly legitimate reasons — an odd roof geometry that rigid panels can't work around, a structural weight limit that rules out heavy glass, a specific low-profile aesthetic the homeowner is paying for on purpose. But "they're cheaper" alone rarely survives scrutiny once you account for the extra roof area consumed and the larger panel count needed to hit your target output, which can erode or erase the per-panel savings. For nearly every home, the reason your neighbors' roofs are uniformly crystalline is that the math favored it on a space-limited surface, not that nobody ever mentioned the alternative.
