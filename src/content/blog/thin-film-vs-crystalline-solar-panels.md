Walk across a residential neighborhood and count the solar roofs, and you'll see essentially one kind of panel: rigid, framed, dark blue or black rectangles. Those are crystalline silicon. Thin-film panels — the flexible, sometimes rollable sheets you see on RVs, spec sheets, and giant desert power plants — barely appear on houses. That isn't marketing inertia. It's the result of a specific trade-off that rooftops resolved decisively in one direction.

## Two ways to build a solar cell

Crystalline panels are cut from silicon ingots. Monocrystalline cells come from a single continuous crystal and are the higher-efficiency, higher-cost option; polycrystalline cells are cast from melted fragments, slightly cheaper and slightly less efficient. Both are rigid, framed under glass, and account for the overwhelming majority of what installers put on homes.

Thin-film panels take a different route: a thin layer of photovoltaic material — cadmium telluride, CIGS, or amorphous silicon — deposited onto a backing. The layer is a fraction of the thickness of a silicon wafer, which is what makes thin film light, sometimes flexible, and cheap to manufacture per unit area. Those are real advantages. They just aren't the advantages a roof cares most about.

## The number that decides it: efficiency per square foot

Efficiency is the share of sunlight a panel converts to electricity, and it's where the two technologies split. Modern monocrystalline modules commonly land around 20% efficiency, with premium lines a bit higher; polycrystalline sits a little below that. Thin-film modules typically run lower — often in the low-to-mid teens depending on the chemistry.

That gap matters enormously on a roof, because a roof is a fixed amount of space. Lower efficiency means you need more square footage to produce the same output. A useful primer on why the same physical panel can carry very different power ratings is [solar panel wattage explained](/blog/solar-panel-wattage-explained/); the short version is that watts per panel, divided by the panel's footprint, is what a space-constrained roof actually buys.

Here's the comparison in the terms that decide residential systems:

| Factor | Crystalline (mono/poly) | Thin-film |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Typical efficiency | ~18–22% | ~10–14% |
| Roof area for same output | Less | More |
| Weight and profile | Rigid, framed, heavier | Light, low-profile, often flexible |
| Temperature behavior | Good | Often slightly better |
| Where it dominates | Home and commercial roofs | Utility fields, RVs, portable, curved surfaces |

The efficiency column is the whole argument for houses. If your roof has room for a 7 kW crystalline array, hitting that same 7 kW with thin film could demand noticeably more area than the usable roof planes offer. When space is the binding constraint — and on most homes it is — the denser technology wins by default. ## Where thin film genuinely wins

None of this makes thin film a bad technology. It's a different tool. Its strengths line up with situations where roof space isn't the limiter:

- **Utility-scale fields**, where cheap land makes area less precious than cost per watt, and thin film's low manufacturing cost shines.
- **RVs, boats, and portable kits**, where light weight and flexibility beat maximum watts per square foot, and a curved or weight-sensitive surface rules out heavy glass modules.
- **Certain façades and curved structures**, where rigid framed panels simply won't conform.

Thin film also tends to shrug off heat a little better than silicon, holding a bit more output on very hot days. That's a genuine edge — it just doesn't outweigh needing extra roof area on a house that doesn't have it.

## Degradation and longevity

Buyers sometimes worry that one technology quietly dies faster than the other. Both crystalline and quality thin-film modules degrade gradually rather than failing suddenly, shedding a fraction of a percent of output per year, and both carry multi-decade performance warranties. The mechanisms differ, and some older thin-film chemistries had a steeper early-life drop, but modern modules of either type are long-lived. If you want the full picture of how output fades over a system's life, [solar panel degradation rates](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/) covers what to expect and how it shows up in warranty terms.

## What this means for your quote

If a residential quote proposes thin-film modules, that's worth a direct question about why, because it's the unusual choice. There can be legitimate reasons — an odd roof geometry, a weight limit, a specific aesthetic — but "they're cheaper" alone rarely holds up once you account for the extra area and the panel count needed to hit your target output. For nearly every home, the reason your neighbors' roofs are all crystalline is that the math favored it, not that they never heard of the alternative.
