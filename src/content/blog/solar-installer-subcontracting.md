The name on your solar contract and the name on the truck in your driveway are often not the same. A large share of residential solar in the US is sold by one company and physically installed by another — a subcontracted crew the sales company hires to do the roof work. Homeowners usually discover this late, sometimes on installation morning, and it lands as a betrayal because nobody mentioned it.

It shouldn't. Subcontracting is normal across construction, and a good subcontracted install is indistinguishable from a good in-house one. The problem isn't that a sub does the work. The problem is what subcontracting quietly does to accountability when something goes wrong two years later — and how easy it is to sign a contract without ever learning who is actually responsible for what.

## Why the company you signed with may not touch your roof

The economics push hard toward splitting sales from installation. Selling solar and installing solar are different businesses with different skills, and many companies are built to do only one well. A sales organization can cover a huge territory with marketing and financing partnerships while owning no ladders; it books the job, then hands the physical work to a local crew it pays per install. This is especially common with the national brands, and it's one of the structural differences that makes [choosing between a national company and a local installer](/blog/national-vs-local-solar-installers/) more than a question of size.

None of this is hidden in a legal sense — it's usually somewhere in the paperwork. But it's rarely volunteered, and the sales conversation is engineered to make you feel you're hiring the brand, not a chain of contracts you never see the far end of.

## What subcontracting changes, and what it doesn't

Start with what it doesn't change. A subcontracted crew installs to the same code, pulls the same permits, and passes the same inspections as anyone else. Good subs are skilled tradespeople who install more roofs in a month than most in-house crews, and the physical quality of a subcontracted job can be excellent. Subcontracting is not a synonym for shoddy work.

What it changes is the shape of accountability. When one company sells, another installs, the equipment carries its own manufacturer warranties, and a fourth entity may service the system later, a roof leak or a production shortfall no longer has one obvious owner. Each party can plausibly point at another. That diffusion is the real risk — not bad work, but nobody clearly on the hook when work goes bad.

## The accountability gap when something goes wrong

Trace a specific failure to see why this matters. Say your array develops a leak at a mounting penetration eighteen months in. Is that a workmanship defect (the crew's responsibility), an equipment defect (the racking manufacturer's), or general wear (nobody's)? The answer determines who pays, and the answer is frequently disputed.

This is exactly where the distinction between a [workmanship warranty and an equipment warranty](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) stops being paperwork and starts costing money. A leak from a bad flashing job is workmanship. The question you need answered before you sign is who backs that workmanship warranty and who performs the repair — the brand you bought from, or a subcontractor who may have moved on, folded, or stopped working with that brand. If the sales company backs the labor warranty in its own name and dispatches service regardless of which crew originally did the work, the subcontracting is a non-issue. If the warranty effectively depends on tracking down the original sub, you're exposed.

## Questions that reveal how the work is really staffed

You don't need to interrogate anyone. A few direct questions surface everything that matters, and how comfortably they're answered tells you as much as the answers.

- Who will physically perform the installation — your employees or a subcontractor?
- Who holds the license and pulls the permit for my job?
- Who backs the workmanship warranty, in whose legal name, and who dispatches a service technician if I have a leak in year three?
- If the installing crew is no longer available, who honors the labor warranty?
- Can I have that in writing in the contract, not just verbally?

A company that subcontracts responsibly will answer these plainly, because it has a system for standing behind subcontracted work. Vagueness, deflection, or a promise that "you'll be taken care of" without a name attached is the signal to slow down. These belong alongside the broader set of [questions worth asking any installer before signing](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/), and they're the ones a subcontracting model most often can't answer cleanly.

## Subcontracting isn't the red flag — vagueness is

Plenty of reputable companies subcontract installation and stand fully behind the result, honoring their labor warranty in their own name no matter whose crew was on the roof. Plenty of in-house installers do mediocre work. The org chart is not the quality signal people treat it as.

What you're really buying is a chain of accountability that survives the day the truck leaves. Judge that directly: get the warranty-holder named in writing, confirm who services the system, and price the job on its merits — the same installation should cost roughly the same whether the crew is badged or subcontracted, so run any quote through a [cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) to make sure you're not paying a brand premium for work a sub performs. A company willing to put its name on the labor, on paper, is safe to hire regardless of who climbs the ladder. One that won't is telling you something, whether it means to or not.
