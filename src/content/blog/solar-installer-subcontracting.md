The name on your solar contract and the name on the truck in your driveway are often not the same. A large share of residential solar in the US is sold by one company and physically installed by another — a subcontracted crew the sales company hires to do the actual roof work. Homeowners usually find this out late, sometimes on installation morning when a truck with an unfamiliar logo backs up to the house, and it tends to land as a betrayal precisely because nobody mentioned it. You thought you hired the brand in the ads. You partly hired a chain of contracts you never saw the far end of.

It shouldn't land as a betrayal, though, because subcontracting is normal across construction and a good subcontracted install is genuinely indistinguishable from a good in-house one. The problem was never that a sub does the work. The problem is what subcontracting quietly does to accountability when something goes wrong two years later, long after the sales rep stopped returning calls — and how easy it is to sign a contract without ever learning who is actually responsible for what. That gap between "who sold it" and "who's on the hook" is the whole subject, and it's one you can close with a few pointed questions before you ever sign.

## Why the name on the contract isn't always the name on the truck

The economics of the industry push hard toward splitting sales from installation, and once you see why, the arrangement stops feeling sinister and starts looking like plain division of labor. Selling solar and installing solar are different businesses that reward different skills. Selling is marketing, financing, lead generation, and closing. Installing is licensed tradespeople, ladders, permits, and roofs. A company built to do one of those well is often not built to do the other, so many aren't. A sales organization can blanket an entire region through advertising and financing partnerships while owning not a single ladder — it books the job, locks in the financing, and hands the physical work to a local crew it pays per install. This is especially common with the big national brands, and it's one of the structural differences that makes [choosing between a national company and a local installer](/blog/national-vs-local-solar-installers/) more than a question of which name you recognize.

None of this is hidden in a strict legal sense. The subcontracting relationship is usually spelled out somewhere in the paperwork, in language you could find if you went looking. But it's rarely volunteered, and the sales conversation is engineered to leave you feeling you're hiring the brand itself rather than a coordinator who will dispatch someone else. That framing is the source of most of the later friction — not the practice, but the surprise.

It helps to picture the actual chain, because "subcontracting" covers a range of arrangements that aren't equally reassuring. At one end is a large regional installer that employs its own sales team and its own crews and does everything under one roof; the truck and the contract match, and accountability is simple. At the other end is a lead-generation and sales operation that owns nothing physical, sells a system under a recognizable brand, and then farms the install out to whichever independent crew is available in your area that week — a crew that may work under a dozen different brand names depending on who booked the job. In between sit plenty of legitimate companies that maintain long-standing relationships with a stable set of installation partners they've vetted and worked with for years. All three of these get described as "subcontracting," but they are not the same risk. The stable-partner model can be every bit as accountable as an all-in-house operation, because the sales company knows exactly who's on your roof and stands behind their work by name. The rotating-crew model is where the accountability genuinely thins out, because no single party has a durable stake in your particular installation. The word on the contract doesn't tell you which of these you're dealing with. The questions further down do.

It also helps to remember that this split is utterly ordinary outside of solar, which takes some of the emotional charge out of it. When you hire a general contractor to remodel a kitchen, that GC almost never personally does the plumbing, the electrical, the tile, and the cabinetry — they coordinate specialized subs and stand behind the whole. Nobody feels betrayed to learn their kitchen remodel involved subcontractors, because the general contractor's name is clearly the one on the hook. Solar feels different only because the sales-and-marketing front end is so polished that buyers reasonably assume the brand is also the builder, and because the arrangement gets discovered late rather than disclosed early. Reframing it as the normal general-contractor model, where one accountable party coordinates specialists, is closer to the truth than imagining something went wrong — provided, and this is the whole catch, that there actually is one accountable party who behaves like a real general contractor rather than a marketing shell that vanishes after the sale.

Start, then, with what subcontracting genuinely does not change, because a fair amount of the internet panic on this topic is misplaced. A subcontracted crew installs to the same electrical and building code, pulls the same permits, and passes the same municipal inspections as anyone else. The inspector doesn't care whose payroll the installer is on. Good subs are skilled tradespeople who often install more roofs in a month than an in-house crew does, precisely because installation is all they do, and the physical quality of a subcontracted job can be excellent. Subcontracting is not a synonym for shoddy work, and treating it as one will steer you away from perfectly good companies while doing nothing to protect you from bad ones. What it changes is subtler and lives entirely on the accountability side of the ledger. When one company sells the system, another installs it, the panels and inverter carry their own separate manufacturer warranties, and possibly a fourth entity services the thing years later, a roof leak or a production shortfall no longer has one obvious owner. Each party can point, plausibly, at another. That diffusion of responsibility is the real risk of the model — not bad work on the roof, but nobody clearly on the hook when the work goes bad.

## The accountability gap when something goes wrong

Trace one specific failure through the system and you can see exactly why the org chart matters. Say your array develops a leak at a mounting penetration eighteen months after install — water staining a ceiling below a spot where a lag bolt went through the roof deck. Now the question that decides who pays: is that a workmanship defect, meaning the crew flashed the penetration badly and it's their responsibility? An equipment defect, meaning the racking or flashing hardware itself failed and it's the manufacturer's? Or ordinary wear that nobody warranties at all? The answer determines whose problem it is, and in a subcontracted arrangement the answer is frequently disputed, with each party's incentive pointing at one of the others.

Notice how many separate parties can be involved in that one leak, because the count is the problem. The sales brand that took your money. The subcontracted crew that flashed the penetration. The racking manufacturer whose hardware the crew used. The panel and inverter manufacturers, each with their own warranty. And possibly a separate service company that took over maintenance after the install. A leak has to be pinned on one of them to get paid, and every additional party in the chain is one more place the responsibility can get handed off rather than owned. In an all-in-house install that same leak has essentially one candidate. The diffusion isn't a rumor about bad companies; it's a structural feature of splitting a job across many hands, and it's why the single most valuable thing you can secure upfront is a named party who agrees to own the outcome regardless of which of the others is technically at fault.

This is exactly where the distinction between a [workmanship warranty and an equipment warranty](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) stops being dry paperwork and starts costing you real money. A leak caused by a bad flashing job is workmanship — the hardware was fine, the installation of it wasn't. Which means the question you need answered clearly *before* you sign is this: who backs that workmanship warranty, in whose legal name, and who actually performs the repair? Is it the brand you thought you bought from, standing behind the labor regardless of which crew did it? Or does it, in practice, depend on tracking down the original subcontractor — a crew that may by then have moved on, folded, or simply stopped working with that brand? Those two situations feel identical at signing and could not be more different when your ceiling is dripping. If the sales company backs the labor warranty in its own name and dispatches service no matter who originally climbed the roof, the subcontracting is a non-issue and you can stop worrying about it. If the warranty effectively hinges on finding the sub who did the work, you are exposed in a way the glossy brochure never mentioned.

Watch how the dispute actually unfolds, because that's where the abstract risk turns into a real headache. You call the sales brand about the leak. They tell you the installation was performed by an independent contractor and the workmanship warranty is between you and that contractor — here's a number. You call the number and reach a crew that's no longer working with that brand, has no record of your job readily at hand, or has simply gone out of business. Meanwhile the racking manufacturer, if you get that far, tells you their hardware is fine and the problem is clearly the installation, which isn't their department. Every party has a defensible reason it isn't their problem, and each one is pointing at another. You, holding a stained ceiling and a fat folder of paperwork, are the only person in the chain with an undeniable stake in getting it fixed. That's the accountability gap made concrete: not a single villain refusing to help, but a structure where responsibility has been divided finely enough that no one piece of it clearly lands on anyone. The way you avoid landing there is to establish, before signing, a single named party who owns the labor warranty and will send someone regardless of which crew did the original work — one throat to choke, in the blunt contractor phrase.

The reason this catches so many people is that the pitch and the paper describe different things. The pitch is about the brand: its reputation, its years in business, its warranty length in big friendly numbers. The paper is about the structure: which legal entity promises what, and what has to be true for that promise to be kept. A twenty-five-year workmanship warranty is worth exactly as much as the odds that whoever issued it is still around and still obligated to send someone when you call in year nine. A national brand that self-performs warranty service through its own dispatch is a strong position. A thin sales entity whose warranty quietly routes back to whichever independent crew happened to be available that week is a weak one, no matter how long the number on the certificate. The subcontracting itself is neutral. What you're really evaluating is whether the accountability survives the day the truck leaves your driveway and the relationship goes cold.

## The questions that reveal how the work is really staffed

You don't need to interrogate anyone or accuse them of hiding something. A few direct, neutral questions surface everything that matters, and how comfortably each one gets answered tells you as much as the answer itself. A company that subcontracts responsibly has a system for standing behind subcontracted work and will walk you through it without flinching. A company that would rather you didn't ask will get vague, and the vagueness is the signal.

- Who will physically perform the installation — your own employees, or a subcontractor?
- Who holds the contractor's license and pulls the permit for my specific job?
- Who backs the workmanship warranty, in whose legal name, and who dispatches a service technician if I get a leak in year three?
- If the crew that installed my system is no longer available, who honors the labor warranty then?
- Can I have all of that written into the contract, rather than promised across the kitchen table?

The licensing question in particular is a quiet but powerful tell, because the permit is a matter of public record and it names a legal responsible party. Whoever pulls the permit is the licensed contractor of record for your job, on the hook to the local jurisdiction for the work meeting code. If the sales brand pulls the permit under its own license, it has skin in the game and can't fully disclaim the installation later. If the permit is pulled under a subcontractor's license, that subcontractor is the one the building department will look to — which tells you exactly whose competence and longevity actually protect you, regardless of whose logo you saw in the ad. You can often verify the permit and its holder through your local building department after the fact, but it's far better to ask who will pull it before you sign, and to notice whether the answer comes easily.

The pattern in the answers is what you're listening for. Plain, specific responses — a named entity, a described dispatch process, a willingness to put it in writing — mean the company has thought about accountability and built for it. That company is safe to hire whether its crews are badged employees or long-standing subcontractors, because the chain of responsibility is intact. Deflection, a change of subject, or the warm but empty reassurance that "you'll be taken care of" with no name attached is the moment to slow down. These questions belong right alongside the broader set of [questions worth asking any installer before you sign](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/), and they happen to be the ones a poorly structured subcontracting model can't answer cleanly, which is why they're so useful for telling a well-run operation from a fragile one.

Timing matters as much as the questions themselves. Ask all of this before you sign, while you still have the leverage of an unsigned contract and the company still wants your business — that's when a vague answer costs them a sale and a clear answer is cheap for them to give. After you've signed, the same questions carry no weight, and discovering the subcontracting arrangement on installation morning leaves you with almost no room to negotiate the accountability you should have nailed down weeks earlier. If you're reading this already mid-project and only now learning a sub will do the work, it's not a catastrophe, but do get the warranty-holder and service process confirmed in writing before the crew starts, and hold final payment until you have it, because paperwork completed while they still want your check is paperwork that actually gets completed. The leverage you have is entirely front-loaded; use it while it exists.

One last piece of protection is on the price side, because the fear of subcontracting sometimes gets exploited to sell you a "premium in-house" job at a premium price. The same installation should cost roughly the same whether the crew is a direct employee or a subcontractor, since the labor, hardware, and permits don't change based on who signs the installer's paycheck. So run any quote through a [cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) and check it against a reasonable range before you accept a brand premium for work a sub is going to perform anyway. Plenty of reputable companies subcontract installation and stand fully behind the result, honoring their labor warranty in their own name no matter whose crew was on the roof. Plenty of in-house installers do mediocre work and hide behind their own logo. The org chart is simply not the quality signal people treat it as. What you're buying is a chain of accountability that outlives the install, and you evaluate it directly: get the warranty-holder named in writing, confirm who services the system, and price the job on its merits. A company willing to put its own name on the labor, on paper, is a safe bet regardless of who climbs the ladder. One that won't put a name on it is telling you something real, whether it intends to or not.
