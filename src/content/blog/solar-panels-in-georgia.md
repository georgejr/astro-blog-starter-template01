Georgia hands solar owners a generous natural resource and then claws back a chunk of the reward through how it pays for exported power. The sun is genuinely excellent across the state. The compensation for anything you send back to the grid, however, tends to sit near wholesale avoided-cost levels rather than the retail rate — which flips the usual solar strategy on its head. In Georgia, the electricity you *keep* is worth far more than the electricity you sell, and everything about designing a good system follows from that one fact.

## The sunshine is real

Start with the good news, because it's substantial. Much of Georgia sees on the order of 5 or more peak sun hours a day averaged over the year, putting it among the better solar resources in the eastern half of the country. Summers are long and bright, and even the shoulder seasons produce well. A kilowatt of panels here generates more energy than the same hardware would in the cloudier Northeast or Pacific Northwest — the raw production side of the equation is firmly in your favor.

That resource matters because savings are always production multiplied by the value of each kilowatt-hour. Georgia maxes out the production term. The catch is entirely on the value side, and it's specific to how the state compensates exports.

## Where it gets stingy: avoided-cost exports

Here's the structural reality a Georgia shopper has to understand. Rather than crediting exported solar at the retail rate — the full-value, one-for-one trade that traditional net metering provides in some states — Georgia's dominant compensation approach credits surplus kilowatt-hours at something much closer to the utility's **avoided cost**: roughly what it would have paid to generate or buy that power wholesale. That's typically a small fraction of the retail rate you pay to buy electricity back.

The difference between those two worlds is enormous, and it's worth being precise about the mechanism. Under retail net metering, a kilowatt-hour you export and a kilowatt-hour you consume on-site are worth the same, so you can size to your annual usage and let the grid act as a costless battery. Under avoided-cost export — a form of the broader shift covered in [net billing vs net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/) — an exported kilowatt-hour might be worth a quarter or less of a self-consumed one. The general dynamics of how utilities set these rates are laid out in [solar export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/); Georgia is a clear example of the low end of that spectrum.

One important nuance: Georgia isn't a single utility. The large investor-owned utility, the many electric membership cooperatives (EMCs), and municipal providers each set their own solar programs, and the details differ. Some may offer more favorable terms than others, and programs change. Treat the avoided-cost framing as the general shape of the Georgia market, and verify your specific provider's current program before you model anything.

## Self-consumption becomes the whole game

When exports pay little and self-consumed power offsets full retail, the design objective is no longer "produce as much as possible" — it's "consume as much of what you produce as possible, on-site, as it's generated." That reorders the standard playbook in a few concrete ways.

- **Size toward daytime usage, not annual usage.** Every panel whose output routinely spills onto the grid at midday earns the low avoided-cost rate. A Georgia array biased toward covering the load the house actually uses during sunlight hours captures more retail value per dollar than an oversized one dumping surplus for pennies.
- **Move flexible loads into the sun.** Running the pool pump, the dishwasher, the laundry, and EV charging during the day converts what would have been cheap exports into full-retail self-consumption — with no added hardware. In a state that pays little for exports, load-shifting is close to free money.
- **Don't chase 100% offset for its own sake.** A system that overproduces relative to daytime use is exporting its extra generation into a low-priced market. The economically efficient Georgia system is often somewhat smaller and more closely matched to on-site consumption than the annual-offset rule of thumb would suggest.

A short labeled illustration. Assume a home produces 900 kWh in a summer month but only uses 400 of it as it's generated. Under avoided-cost export, the 500 exported kilowatt-hours earn a low wholesale-like credit, while the 400 self-consumed ones offset full retail. Shift even 100 kWh of flexible load into daylight and you've converted low-value exports into high-value savings — the single most reliable lever a Georgia owner has. ## Where a battery changes the calculus

Georgia is one of the states where storage does more than provide backup — it directly rescues the value of your production. A battery captures the midday surplus that would otherwise export for a pittance and releases it in the evening, when the house is drawing power it would otherwise buy at retail. That turns low-value exports into high-value self-consumption after the sun goes down, which is exactly the gap avoided-cost compensation creates.

That doesn't automatically mean a battery pays for itself here; it means the *case* for one is stronger in Georgia than in a full-retail net-metering state, because the spread between what you'd earn exporting and what you'd save self-consuming is so wide. Whether it pencils out depends on the battery's cost after the 30% federal credit and how much surplus you're currently giving away. ## Before you sign a Georgia quote

Georgia's incentive landscape is thin at the state level, so the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit — 30% of qualifying cost, per IRS guidance — is the main financial lever, and it applies to storage as well as panels. That puts the weight of a good outcome on system design rather than on stacking incentives.

Do three things before committing. Confirm your specific utility's current export compensation and how it nets your production month to month, since that number drives everything above. Pull twelve months of usage and, ideally, an hourly or daily profile so the array can be matched to when you actually consume power, not just how much. And insist any savings projection credits exports at your real avoided-cost rate rather than quietly assuming retail — a proposal that models Georgia as if it had full net metering is overstating your savings.
