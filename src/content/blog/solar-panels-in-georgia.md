Georgia hands solar owners a generous natural resource and then claws back a chunk of the reward through the way it pays for exported power. Both halves of that sentence are true at once, and holding them together is the key to designing a system here that actually works. The sun is genuinely excellent across the state — among the better resources in the eastern half of the country. The compensation for anything you send back to the grid, however, tends to sit near wholesale avoided-cost levels rather than the retail rate, which flips the usual solar strategy on its head. In most of the country, the advice is to produce as much as you can and let the grid credit your surplus. In Georgia, the electricity you *keep* is worth far more than the electricity you sell, and nearly everything about designing a good system follows from that single fact.

## Great sun, stingy exports: the two halves of the Georgia equation

Start with the good news, because it's substantial and it's the reason solar is worth considering here at all. Much of Georgia sees on the order of five or more peak sun hours a day averaged over the year, putting it among the stronger solar resources east of the Rockies. Summers are long and bright, the shoulder seasons produce well, and even the winters aren't the deep gray that hobbles production in the Northeast or the Pacific Northwest. A kilowatt of panels installed in Georgia generates more energy over a year than the identical hardware would in a cloudier state — the raw production side of the equation is firmly, unambiguously in your favor. If solar economics were only about how much electricity the panels make, Georgia would be a slam dunk.

But savings are always production multiplied by the value of each kilowatt-hour, and Georgia maxes out the production term while doing something much less generous with the value term. Here's the structural reality a Georgia shopper has to internalize before looking at any proposal. Rather than crediting exported solar at the retail rate — the full-value, one-for-one trade that traditional net metering provides in some states — Georgia's dominant compensation approach credits surplus kilowatt-hours at something much closer to the utility's avoided cost. Avoided cost is roughly what the utility would have paid to generate or buy that power on the wholesale market, and it's typically a small fraction of the retail rate you pay to buy electricity back. So the kilowatt-hour you export at midday and the kilowatt-hour you import that evening are not worth the same thing, not even close.

The gap between those two worlds is enormous, and it's worth being precise about the mechanism because it drives every design decision that follows. Under retail net metering, an exported kilowatt-hour and a self-consumed kilowatt-hour carry identical value, which means you can size your system to your total annual usage and let the grid act as a costless battery — banking your summer surplus and returning it in winter at no penalty. Under avoided-cost export, that costless battery disappears. An exported kilowatt-hour might be worth a quarter or less of a self-consumed one, so every unit of production that spills onto the grid instead of being used on-site earns a fraction of its potential value. This shift from net metering toward export compensation that pays less than retail is a broad national trend, and the general distinction between the two regimes is laid out in [net billing vs net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/); the wider dynamics of how utilities set these export rates, and why avoided cost sits so far below retail, are covered in [solar export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/). Georgia is a clear example of the low end of that spectrum, where the export credit is thin enough to reshape how you should build.

One important nuance keeps Georgia from being a single monolithic market, and it's a caveat worth taking seriously before you model anything. Georgia isn't served by one utility with one solar program. The large investor-owned utility, the many electric membership cooperatives that serve much of rural and suburban Georgia, and the municipal providers in various cities each set their own solar terms, and the details differ from one to the next. Some may offer more favorable arrangements than others, some structure their programs differently, and all of them can change their terms over time. So treat the avoided-cost framing as the general shape of the Georgia market — the right mental model to walk in with — but verify your specific provider's current program before you trust any number. The utility that serves your address, not "Georgia" as a whole, sets the rules your system will live under.

It's worth pausing on why avoided cost sits so far below retail, because the gap can feel arbitrary until you see the logic. Your retail rate bundles in far more than the raw cost of generating a kilowatt-hour: it also pays for the transmission lines, the distribution poles and wires, the metering, the billing, and the utility's fixed obligations. Avoided cost strips almost all of that away and pays you only for the wholesale energy your export let the utility skip buying — which is genuinely a fraction of what you pay to take power back. Understanding that keeps the framing honest: Georgia isn't singling out solar owners so much as declining to pay a retail price for a wholesale product. There's also a physical footnote worth knowing in a hot state. Solar panels lose a little efficiency as they heat up, so Georgia's long, hot summers shave a modest amount off the peak-hour output you'd predict from sun hours alone. It doesn't erase the state's strong resource, but it's one more reason to base any estimate on realistic, temperature-aware production figures rather than best-case numbers.

## Self-consumption, storage, and what to check before you sign

When exports pay little and self-consumed power offsets full retail, the design objective changes fundamentally. It's no longer "produce as much as possible and sell the extra." It becomes "consume as much of what you produce as possible, on-site, in the moment it's generated." That reordering of priorities plays out in a few concrete ways that would look strange in a full-retail net-metering state but make complete sense in Georgia.

The first is to size toward your daytime usage rather than your total annual usage. Every panel whose output routinely spills onto the grid at midday, when the house is empty and consuming little, earns only the low avoided-cost rate for that exported energy. A Georgia array biased toward covering the load the house actually uses during sunlight hours captures far more retail value per dollar spent than an oversized one that dumps a large midday surplus for pennies. The second is to move flexible loads into the sun. Running the pool pump, the dishwasher, the laundry, and especially EV charging during daylight hours converts what would otherwise have been cheap exports into full-retail self-consumption, with no added hardware and no new spending — just a change in timing. In a state that pays little for exports, this kind of load-shifting is close to free money, and it's the most accessible lever a homeowner has. The third is to resist chasing a 100% offset for its own sake. A system that overproduces relative to daytime use is, by definition, exporting its extra generation into a low-priced market, so the reflexive goal of matching your annual consumption panel-for-panel can actually leave money on the table here. The economically efficient Georgia system is often somewhat smaller and more closely matched to on-site daytime consumption than the annual-offset rule of thumb elsewhere would suggest.

A short labeled illustration makes the logic tangible. Assume a home produces 900 kilowatt-hours during a summer month but only uses 400 of those as they're generated, exporting the rest. Under avoided-cost export, the 500 exported kilowatt-hours earn a low, wholesale-like credit, while the 400 self-consumed ones offset full retail. Now shift even 100 kilowatt-hours of flexible load — a pool pump moved to run at noon, laundry done in the afternoon — from the evening into daylight hours. You've just converted 100 kilowatt-hours of low-value export into 100 kilowatt-hours of high-value self-consumption, with no capital cost at all. That single move is the most reliable lever a Georgia owner has, and it costs nothing but attention. A [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) helps you find the array size that matches your daytime consumption pattern rather than blindly targeting your annual total, which in Georgia is exactly the sizing discipline that protects your return.

Georgia is one of the states where storage does more than provide backup power — it directly rescues the value of your own production, which isn't true everywhere. In a full-retail net-metering state, a battery adds resilience but little pure economic value, because the grid already credits your surplus fairly. In Georgia, a battery captures the midday surplus that would otherwise export for a pittance and holds it until the evening, when the house is drawing power it would otherwise have to buy back at the full retail rate. That's a direct arbitrage against the exact gap that avoided-cost compensation creates: you're storing energy worth a quarter of retail as an export and redeploying it at full retail after dark. The wider the spread between what you'd earn exporting and what you'd pay importing, the more each stored kilowatt-hour is worth, and Georgia's spread is wide.

That doesn't automatically mean a battery pays for itself here — it means the *case* for one is stronger in Georgia than in a full-retail state, which is a different and more honest claim. Whether a battery actually pencils out depends on its cost after the 30% federal credit and on how much surplus you're currently giving away for cheap. A home that already consumes most of its production as it's generated has little surplus for a battery to rescue, so the arbitrage is small; a home that exports a large midday surplus into the avoided-cost void has much more for storage to recapture. A [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you size storage against your actual evening loads and see how much of that midday surplus a given battery could shift into high-value evening use, which is the number that tells you whether the economics are there for your specific pattern.

Georgia's incentive landscape is thin at the state level, which puts the weight of a good outcome squarely on system design rather than on stacking generous programs. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit — 30% of qualifying cost, per IRS guidance — is the main financial lever available, and it applies to storage as well as to panels, which is part of what can make a battery viable here despite the cost. With little else to stack, design discipline becomes the primary driver of your return, and there are three things worth doing before you commit to any quote. Confirm your specific utility's current export compensation and exactly how it nets your production against your consumption month to month, since that number drives everything above and varies by provider. Pull twelve months of your own usage and, if you can get it, an hourly or daily profile, so the array can be matched to when you actually consume power rather than just how much you consume in total. And insist that any savings projection credits your exports at your real avoided-cost rate rather than quietly assuming retail — a proposal that models Georgia as though it had full net metering is overstating your savings, sometimes dramatically. For the wider context of why a genuinely sun-rich state can still be only a middling solar deal, [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/) puts Georgia's sun-versus-exports tension into national perspective, and it's a useful frame for judging whether the numbers in front of you are being honest about which half of the equation they're leaning on.
