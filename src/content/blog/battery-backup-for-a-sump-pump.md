The cruel thing about sump pumps is that the exact conditions that make them work hardest are the ones most likely to cut their power. A heavy storm dumps water into the ground, the water table rises, the sump pit fills, and the pump cycles hard to keep the basement dry — and that same storm brings down a tree limb across a power line and leaves the pump dead just when the basement needs it most. Flooding and outages arrive together far more often than chance would suggest, which is why a sump pump without a backup power source isn't really a flood defense at all. It's a flood defense that fails precisely when it's tested. Battery backup exists to close that gap, and sizing it correctly starts with an honest look at how a pump actually behaves during a storm rather than how much power it draws on its nameplate.

A sump pump's saving grace, from a battery-sizing standpoint, is that it doesn't run continuously. It sits idle until the water in the pit rises to trip the float switch, then it runs for a short burst to pump the pit down, then it shuts off and waits again. Even during a serious storm, the pump spends far more time idle than running — it cycles, and the fraction of time it's actually pumping is its duty cycle. That intermittency is what makes battery backup feasible, because a battery doesn't have to power the pump around the clock; it only has to cover the running minutes scattered through the outage. A pump that draws real power while running can still be carried a surprisingly long way by a modest battery, because it's only pulling that power for a small slice of each hour.

## What a sump pump really draws, and how to size the battery around it

Start with the pump's power draw, because everything else builds on it. A common residential sump pump is a 1/3 or 1/2 horsepower unit, and a running 1/3 HP sump pump draws somewhere in the range of 800 to 1,050 watts while it's actively pumping. A 1/2 HP pump draws more, often 1,200 to 1,500 watts running. Those are the *running* figures, and there's an important wrinkle: the motor surges above its running draw for a fraction of a second at startup, as the motor overcomes inertia and gets the impeller moving. That inrush is brief but real, and it means whatever powers the pump — a dedicated backup unit or a whole-home battery — has to be able to deliver a momentary spike well above the running watts. Undersizing the inverter or backup for surge is a classic mistake: the battery has plenty of stored energy, but the pump won't start because the inverter can't supply the startup spike. Match the surge capacity, not just the running watts.

Now bring in the duty cycle, because that's what turns a scary wattage into a manageable energy number. Suppose, as a labeled illustration, that during a heavy storm your pump runs about 10 minutes out of every hour to keep up with the inflow — a moderate but not extreme duty cycle. A 1,000-watt pump running 10 minutes an hour consumes about 167 watt-hours per hour, or roughly 0.17 kWh an hour. Over a 12-hour overnight outage during a storm, that's about 2 kWh of energy — well within reach of even a modest home battery. Push the assumptions harder: a pump running 20 minutes an hour, a full 24-hour outage, and you're looking at closer to 8 kWh. The range is wide because the duty cycle is wide, and the duty cycle depends entirely on how fast water is entering your pit, which varies with the storm, your soil, and your water table. The honest way to size is to estimate your *worst realistic* duty cycle, not your average one, because the backup exists for the bad night, not the typical one.

A sump pump is one of the friendlier loads to back up precisely because its duty cycle is low; it's the surge, not the sustained energy, that trips people up.

One factor deserves a hard-nosed reality check: your pump might not keep up even with unlimited power. Battery backup solves the *power* problem, but it can't solve a *capacity* problem. If a truly catastrophic storm floods water into your pit faster than the pump can move it, no amount of stored energy helps — the pump runs continuously and the basement still floods, because the pump's gallons-per-minute rating is the bottleneck, not the electricity. For most homes in most storms this isn't the binding constraint, and the outage is the real failure point. But if you live somewhere the pump already struggles to keep up during the worst storms even with grid power, the answer is a higher-capacity pump or a second pump, not just a bigger battery. Sizing the battery correctly assumes the pump can handle the water when it's running; if it can't, that's a separate problem to solve first.

## Two ways to power it: a dedicated backup or a whole-home battery

There are two fundamentally different approaches to sump pump backup, and they suit different homeowners. The traditional route is a **dedicated sump pump battery backup system** — a purpose-built unit that pairs a deep-cycle battery (often a marine or AGM battery) with either a separate DC-powered backup pump or an inverter that runs your existing AC pump. These systems live right at the sump pit, switch on automatically when grid power fails, and are engineered specifically for the surge and duty cycle of a pump. Their appeal is focus and cost: they do one job, they're comparatively inexpensive, and they'll run a pump through a typical overnight outage. Their limits are also about focus. The battery is usually a lead-acid type that offers modest usable capacity and needs periodic maintenance and eventual replacement, and the system does nothing for the rest of your house — when the power's out, you have a working sump pump and a dark, cold home. For a homeowner whose single concern is the basement, that's often an acceptable and economical trade.

The other approach is a **whole-home battery**, the kind typically installed alongside solar, wired so that the sump pump is one of the loads it carries during an outage. Here the sump pump isn't getting a dedicated appliance; it's sharing a large home energy storage system that also keeps the fridge cold, the lights on, and the Wi-Fi up. The advantages are substantial: far more usable capacity, the surge headroom of a proper home inverter, automatic switchover, modern lithium chemistry that lasts longer and needs no maintenance, and — critically — the same battery protecting everything else you care about during the outage, not just the pump. The catch is cost and scope: a whole-home battery is a much larger investment than a dedicated sump backup, and it only makes sense if you want backup for the broader house rather than the pump alone. If you're already installing storage for outage protection generally, adding the sump pump to its protected loads is nearly free; if the pump is your *only* worry, a dedicated system is the leaner buy. Our comparison of a [home battery versus a portable power station](/blog/home-battery-vs-portable-power-station/) frames the same scope-versus-cost trade for outage protection broadly, and the sump pump slots neatly into that decision.

Whichever route you take, understand how the battery behaves the moment the grid drops, because that behavior is what actually keeps the basement dry. A properly configured backup switches over automatically and near-instantly when it detects the outage — you don't want to be woken at 3 a.m. to go flip a switch while water rises. A whole-home battery paired with solar does this seamlessly and can even *recharge* from the panels the next day if the outage runs long, extending your protection across a multi-day event in a way a standalone battery can't. What actually happens electrically when the grid fails and a home battery takes over — the switchover, the islanding, the way loads get carried — is worth understanding before you rely on it, and our piece on [what happens when the grid goes down and you have a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/) lays out that sequence. For a sump pump, the practical requirement is simple: automatic switchover fast enough that the pump never misses a cycle, and enough usable energy plus surge capacity to carry the pump's real storm-night duty cycle for as long as the power is likely to stay out.

The right answer for most flood-prone homeowners comes down to a single question: do you want to protect the basement, or do you want to protect the whole house and get the basement as part of the deal? If it's strictly the basement, a dedicated backup sized to your pump's running watts, surge, and worst-case duty cycle is the economical, purpose-built choice. If you're already thinking about broader outage resilience — or you're adding solar and storage anyway — folding the sump pump into a whole-home battery gives you a far more capable safety net for the incremental cost of adding one more circuit to the protected loads. Either way, the sizing discipline is the same: figure out the running watts, respect the startup surge, estimate the worst realistic duty cycle rather than the average, and pick storage that covers the hours the power is genuinely likely to be out. Do that, and the storm that floods the ground and kills the grid at the same moment stops being the thing that floods your basement too.
