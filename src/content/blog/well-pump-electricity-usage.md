If your water comes from a well instead of a municipal main, you own a piece of equipment most homeowners never think about: an electric pump, often sitting hundreds of feet down a borehole, that turns on every time you flush a toilet or fill a glass. It's easy to ignore because you never see it and it never asks for attention. But on a rural electric bill it can be a surprisingly large and lumpy line, and it behaves differently from almost everything else in the house. Most appliances draw a steady, predictable amount. A well pump draws nothing for long stretches, then slams on at a heavy load for a few minutes, then shuts off again. Understanding that on-off, high-draw pattern is the key to both making sense of your bill and, if you're weighing solar or battery backup, planning around one of the trickier loads a rural home carries.

The question "how much electricity does a well pump use?" has two answers that people constantly conflate: how much power it pulls while it's running, which is a lot, and how much energy it uses over a month, which is usually modest because it runs so briefly. Both numbers matter, and they matter for different reasons.

## The heavy draw, the short runtime, and the startup spike

Start with the running draw, because it's the number that surprises people. A typical residential submersible well pump, the kind that lives down in the well casing, is rated somewhere between about half a horsepower and one and a half horsepower, with one horsepower being common. Horsepower is a measure of the pump's mechanical output, but what you pay for is electrical input, which is higher because no motor is perfectly efficient. As a working rule, a one-horsepower submersible pump running against a normal residential lift draws somewhere in the neighborhood of 1.5 to 2 kilowatts while it's operating, and a deeper well or a bigger pump pushes that higher. That's a serious load, comparable to running a couple of space heaters at once, and it's why a well pump belongs in the same conversation as the genuinely heavy appliances in a home rather than the trivial ones. If you're mapping out where your electricity actually goes, the pump sits alongside heating, cooling, and the water heater as one of the big intermittent draws, a group we walk through in [the appliances quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/).

Now the part that keeps the monthly total reasonable: the pump barely runs. A well pump doesn't push water to your faucet directly on demand. It fills a pressure tank, a metal tank that holds a reserve of water under pressure, and then shuts off. When you open a tap, you draw from the tank first, and the pump only kicks on again when the pressure drops to a set point. The result is that the pump cycles on for a few minutes at a time to refill the tank, then rests. For a household using water normally, indoor use only, the pump might accumulate anywhere from roughly half an hour to a couple of hours of actual runtime across a full day, spread over many short cycles. Multiply the heavy running draw by that short runtime and the daily energy is modest. A labeled example: a 1.5 kW pump running a total of one hour a day uses about 1.5 kWh daily, or roughly 45 kWh a month. That's a real but not alarming slice of a typical bill, which is why well pumps hide in plain sight, punchy in the moment but light over time.

The exception, and it's a big one, is outdoor water use. Irrigation, filling a stock tank for livestock, topping off a pond, or running sprinklers can push the pump's runtime from minutes a day to hours, and the monthly energy climbs right along with it. A household that irrigates a garden or waters pasture in summer can see the pump's contribution jump several-fold in the warm months, then fall back in winter. That seasonal swing is often the reason a rural bill balloons in July and August in a way a city dweller's never does, and it's worth separating from the general summer air-conditioning spike when you're diagnosing a high bill. If you want to pin down your own pump's contribution rather than guess, you can estimate it from its horsepower and your rough daily runtime and feed the result into an [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) to see how much of your monthly charge is really water.

Then there's the third number, the one that doesn't show up on your energy bill at all but matters enormously if you're planning backup or solar: the startup surge. When any motor starts, it draws a burst of current far above its running level for a fraction of a second, as it overcomes inertia and gets spinning. This inrush, sometimes called locked-rotor current, can be several times the pump's normal running draw for that brief instant. It uses almost no energy because it lasts a moment, so it never meaningfully touches your kilowatt-hour total. But it's a genuine engineering challenge for anything trying to power the pump off-grid, because a battery inverter or generator has to supply that momentary spike without stalling or tripping. A pump that runs happily on 1.5 kilowatts might demand a brief burst of 5 or 6 kilowatts at the instant it starts, and a power source sized only for the running load will choke on it. This is the single most important thing to understand about well pumps if you're thinking about backup power, and it's the whole reason sizing a battery for a well pump takes more care than sizing one for, say, a refrigerator; we get into that surge-load problem specifically in [can a home battery run a well pump](/blog/battery-to-run-a-well-pump/).

## What it means for your bill and for going solar

For the ordinary business of understanding your electric bill, the well pump is usually a minor-to-moderate line unless you irrigate. If your pump serves indoor use only, expect it to add something in the tens of kilowatt-hours per month, a real but small fraction of a household total that, for the average American home, runs in the high hundreds of kilowatt-hours monthly. Where it earns closer attention is on rural properties with heavy outdoor water use, older or oversized pumps, or very deep wells, all of which push both the running draw and the runtime up. A pump that short-cycles, switching on and off rapidly because the pressure tank has lost its air charge or is failing, also wastes energy and wears the motor, and it's worth having checked if you notice the pump kicking on far more often than your water use would explain. That kind of fault is one of the sneakier reasons a rural bill creeps up, distinct from simply using more of everything, which is the broader pattern we cover in [how much electricity the average home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/).

For solar planning, the well pump has a split personality, and both halves matter. On the energy side, its modest monthly kilowatt-hours are easy to cover; the extra annual production needed to offset a well pump is small, a handful of panels' worth in most indoor-use cases, more if you irrigate heavily. In that sense a well pump is not a reason to dramatically upsize an array. Because a rural home's total consumption, including the pump, drives how many panels you actually need, it's worth folding the pump's estimate into your annual usage before you size a system, which you can do quickly with the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool. On the power side, though, the pump is one of the loads that shapes battery and inverter decisions out of proportion to its energy use, precisely because of that startup surge. A grid-tied solar system with net metering handles the pump invisibly, since the grid supplies the surge and you simply offset the annual energy. But if resilience is part of your goal, keeping water flowing during an outage, then the pump moves to the center of the plan, because a battery has to be sized not for the pump's gentle daily energy but for its violent momentary demand.

## Trimming the pump's share of your bill

If your well pump is a bigger line than you would like, a few things are worth checking before you assume you are stuck with it. The first is the pressure tank, because a healthy one is what keeps the pump resting between draws. Pressure tanks hold their reserve using an air charge behind a bladder, and that charge can slowly leak away over the years. When it does, the tank stops buffering properly and the pump short-cycles, switching on and off rapidly for small water draws instead of running a solid few minutes to refill a proper reserve. Short-cycling wastes energy through all those startup surges and wears the motor out faster. If you notice the pump kicking on far more often than your water use seems to warrant, having the tank's air charge checked or the tank replaced can cut both the energy and the wear.

Pump condition and sizing matter too. An aging pump loses efficiency, drawing more power to move the same water, and an oversized pump left by a previous owner may be pulling more than your household needs. Neither is usually worth replacing purely to save energy, since the monthly kilowatt-hours are modest, but if a pump is nearing the end of its life anyway, choosing a correctly sized, efficient replacement is a chance to trim the load for good. For very deep wells or heavy irrigation demands, a variable-speed or constant-pressure pump can run more efficiently than a simple on-off model by matching its output to demand, though the payback on that upgrade depends heavily on how much you actually run it.

Timing is the lever that matters most if you are on a time-of-use rate or you have solar. The pump does not care when it runs, so shifting big discretionary water uses, irrigation especially, into daylight hours lets you power them with your own solar production or with cheaper off-peak electricity instead of expensive peak power. You cannot reschedule a flush, but you can absolutely set the sprinklers to run at noon rather than during the early-evening rate peak. For a household that irrigates, moving that load into the solar window turns the pump from a bill you dread in July into one your own panels quietly cover, which is the same self-consumption logic that governs every other flexible load in a solar home.

The practical way to think about it: track the two numbers separately. For your monthly bill and your solar array size, use the pump's energy, its running draw times its short daily runtime, and you'll find it's a manageable load you can cover with a modest bump in panels. For any backup or off-grid ambition, use the pump's power and especially its surge, and size the battery and inverter to swallow that brief spike, which almost always means more capacity than the energy alone would suggest. Rural homeowners who confuse the two either overbuild an array to chase a load that was never large, or underbuild a battery that can't start the pump when the grid is down and the water stops. Keep them distinct, and the well pump stops being a mystery on the bill and becomes just another load you've measured, planned for, and moved on from.
