Your electricity bill hands you a single number for the month, and that number hides almost everything interesting about how your house actually uses power. Two homes can burn the same 900 kilowatt-hours in a month and behave completely differently minute to minute — one holding a steady, low hum around the clock, the other lurching between near-silence and sharp spikes whenever the air conditioner and the oven and the electric dryer all fire at once. That difference in shape, not just in total, is what decides how well solar covers your usage, what size battery makes sense, and whether a demand charge or a time-of-use rate will help you or hurt you.

The vocabulary for the shape comes down to two ideas. Base load is the power your home draws continuously, the floor it rarely dips below. Peak load is the height of the short spikes that ride on top of that floor when big appliances switch on. Learn to see your usage as a floor plus spikes rather than a monthly lump sum, and a lot of solar and storage decisions that felt arbitrary start to make sense.

## What base load is, and why it's higher than you think

Base load is everything in your house that runs whether or not you're paying attention. The refrigerator cycling on and off. The chest freezer in the garage. The Wi-Fi router, the cable modem, the security system, the smart-home hub. Standby power to every device with a clock or a remote or a little glowing LED — televisions, microwaves, cable boxes, game consoles in sleep mode, phone chargers left plugged in. In many homes a recirculating water pump, a sump pump on standby, an aquarium heater, or a well pump's controller adds to it. None of these announce themselves, and individually they're small, but they never stop, and continuous small draws add up to real kilowatt-hours.

A useful way to measure your base load is to read your electric meter or your utility's interval data in the dead of night — say 3 a.m., when no one is cooking, bathing, or running the dryer. Whatever the house is pulling then is close to your true base load. For a typical American home it lands somewhere between 300 and 600 watts, though homes with pools, well pumps, or lots of always-on electronics can run well past a kilowatt around the clock. That may not sound like much until you annualize it. A steady 500-watt base load is 12 kWh a day, roughly 360 kWh a month, and something like 4,300 kWh a year — often a third or more of a modest household's entire consumption, spent before anyone deliberately turns anything on. If your usage seems stubbornly high no matter how careful you are, an inflated base load is a common culprit, and a chunk of it is frequently the standby draw we cover in [phantom loads](/blog/phantom-loads-electricity/) — the power devices sip while doing nothing useful.

Base load matters for solar in a specific way that surprises people. Solar produces during the day; base load runs all night. Under full net metering, that timing mismatch washes out — daytime overproduction banks credits that offset the nighttime floor, and a kWh is a kWh regardless of when it flowed. But under net billing, time-of-use rates, or any scheme where exported power earns less than power you buy, your overnight base load becomes the part of your consumption that solar can never directly serve. Those are hours when the panels are dark and you're pulling from the grid at full retail price. This is precisely the load a home battery is built to cover: charge it on cheap midday solar, discharge it through the evening and overnight base load, and you shrink the slice of your usage that stays exposed to grid rates. Sizing that battery starts with knowing your base load, because the overnight floor multiplied by the hours of darkness is the energy the battery has to hold. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you turn an overnight draw and a backup-hours target into an approximate battery size in kilowatt-hours.

Reducing base load is also the highest-leverage efficiency move available, because you're cutting a cost that runs 8,760 hours a year rather than a few. Swapping an old second refrigerator, putting entertainment centers and idle electronics on switched power strips, replacing an ancient always-running pool pump with a variable-speed model, and hunting down the handful of devices that draw double-digit watts doing nothing can knock 100 to 200 watts off the floor. At a steady 150-watt reduction, that's about 1,300 kWh a year you no longer have to generate or buy — and every watt you trim off the base load is a watt your solar array or battery no longer needs to be sized to cover. If you want to know where your own floor sits relative to typical households, our look at [average home electricity usage](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) puts the numbers in context.

## Peak load, demand, and the spikes that reshape the bill

Peak load is a different animal, and confusing it with base load leads to bad decisions. Where base load is about energy — kilowatt-hours accumulated over time — peak load is about power, the instantaneous rate of draw measured in kilowatts, and it's set by the worst moment of overlap rather than by any daily total. Picture a summer evening: the central air conditioner is running at 3,500 watts, the electric oven is preheating at 3,000, the clothes dryer is tumbling at 3,000, the dishwasher's heating element kicks in at 1,200, and someone starts the microwave. For a few minutes the house is pulling 12 or 13 kilowatts, twenty-odd times its overnight base load. That brief overlap is your peak demand, and several parts of the modern electric bill are built around it rather than around total energy.

The clearest example is a demand charge. Historically a commercial-billing feature, demand charges are creeping into residential rate plans, and they bill you not for how much energy you used but for your single highest power draw during the month, often measured over a 15-minute window. Two households using identical monthly kWh can pay very different demand charges if one spreads its big loads out and the other runs everything at once. Time-of-use rates attack the same behavior from another angle: they make electricity most expensive during the late-afternoon and evening window when the grid strains hardest, which is exactly when a lot of homes hit their peak. The appliances driving those spikes are the same heavy hitters that dominate total consumption too — the electric resistance loads and the motors we break down in [what uses the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/). Knowing which of your appliances are the big spikers tells you which ones to stagger or reschedule.

This is where the base-versus-peak distinction pays off practically, because the two problems have different solutions. Trimming base load is about efficiency and eliminating waste; managing peak is about timing and coordination. If you're on a demand charge or a time-of-use plan, the move isn't necessarily to use less energy overall — it's to avoid stacking your big loads into the same expensive window. Run the dishwasher after the evening peak passes. Delay the dryer to midday when solar is covering it or overnight when rates are lowest. Precool the house before the peak window rather than during it. Stagger the EV charger so it doesn't start the moment you plug in at 6 p.m. alongside dinner. None of this reduces your kilowatt-hours, but it flattens the spikes, and on the right rate plan a flatter load shape can cut the bill even with identical total consumption. You can model how different rate structures and usage patterns translate into dollars with the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/), which is the fastest way to see whether your peaks or your total energy are driving the cost.

Solar interacts with these two quantities in opposite ways, which is the practical payoff of telling them apart. Your array is sized against energy — total annual kilowatt-hours — so it's your consumption over time, base load included, that determines how many panels you need. But solar does almost nothing for a demand charge, because your peak often lands in the evening after the panels have quit for the day; the array can slash your energy bill and leave a demand charge nearly untouched. That's precisely the gap a battery fills. Storage doesn't generate more energy, but it can discharge fast enough to shave the top off an evening peak, so a home on a demand-charge or steep time-of-use plan sometimes benefits more from the battery's ability to cover peaks than from the extra kilowatt-hours it stores. Reading your usage as base load plus peaks tells you which of the two — panels or storage — is doing the heavy lifting on your particular bill.

Peak load also governs a hardware question that trips up battery shoppers: the difference between a battery's energy capacity and its power output. A battery might store 13.5 kWh of energy — plenty to cover an overnight base load — yet only deliver 5 kW of power at once. During a peak where your house wants 12 kW, that battery physically cannot run everything simultaneously, no matter how much energy it holds in reserve. This is why sizing storage for backup means looking at both numbers: the energy figure tells you how long it can run your loads, and the power figure tells you which loads it can run at the same time. A home with high peaks — central AC plus an electric range plus a dryer — either needs a battery with higher continuous power output, needs to shed non-essential loads during backup, or needs to accept that it can't run the whole house at once off storage. Reading the shape of your usage, not just the size of it, is what keeps those specifications honest.

The larger point is that "how much electricity do I use" is really two questions wearing one coat. The energy question — total kilowatt-hours — sizes your solar array and sets the bulk of your bill under simple flat-rate, net-metered billing. The power question — the height and timing of your peaks — increasingly sets the cost under modern rate designs and governs whether a battery can actually carry your home when the grid drops. A homeowner who reads only the monthly total is flying half-blind. Pull your utility's interval data if it's available, find your overnight floor and your worst evening spike, and you'll understand your own house better than most solar salespeople who quote it. The shape of the curve, not just the area under it, is where the money and the design decisions live.
