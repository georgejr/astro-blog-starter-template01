Ask three installers what solar costs and you will get three different numbers. That is because solar is priced like a construction project, not a product — your roof, your location, and your equipment choices all move the total. Still, the market has well-established averages, and knowing them is your best defense against overpriced quotes.

## The headline numbers

Residential solar in the US typically costs **$2.50 to $3.50 per watt installed** before incentives, with a national average around **$2.75/W**. That translates to:

| System size | Gross cost @ $2.75/W | After 30% tax credit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 6 kW | ~$16,500 | ~$11,550 |
| 8 kW | ~$22,000 | ~$15,400 |
| 10 kW | ~$27,500 | ~$19,250 |
| 12 kW | ~$33,000 | ~$23,100 |

To see what size *your* home needs — and therefore what it should cost — start with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/).

## What "per watt installed" includes

A turnkey quote covers much more than panels:

- **Hardware (~45%):** panels, inverter or microinverters, racking, wiring
- **Labor (~15%):** installation crew, electrician
- **Soft costs (~40%):** permits, inspection, interconnection, sales, overhead, margin

The panels themselves are often only a quarter of the total — which is why "cheap panels" barely move a quote, and why soft costs explain most price differences between regions.

## What pushes your price up

- **Complex roofs:** steep pitch, many small planes, tile or metal roofing
- **Premium equipment:** high-efficiency panels and microinverters can add $0.25–$0.75/W
- **Electrical upgrades:** an old main panel may need a $2,000–$4,000 upgrade
- **Small systems:** fixed costs spread over fewer watts raise the per-watt price
- **High-cost-of-living areas:** labor and permitting are simply more expensive

## What pushes it down

- **Larger systems:** per-watt price drops as size grows
- **Simple roofs:** single south-facing plane, easy access
- **Competitive markets:** getting 3+ quotes routinely saves 10–20%
- **Standard equipment:** mainstream panels perform well at lower cost

## The federal tax credit

The 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit is the single biggest cost reducer for most households. It applies to the full system cost — equipment and installation — as a credit against your federal income tax. Two caveats worth knowing: you need enough tax liability to use it (it can carry forward), and it is a credit, not an upfront discount, so you finance the gross cost until tax season. We cover the details in [the solar tax credit explained](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/).

## How to judge a quote

1. Divide the total price by the system watts to get the per-watt figure.
2. Compare against the $2.50–$3.50 range for your region and roof complexity.
3. Check what is included: main panel work? monitoring? roof warranty?
4. Compare production estimates, not just prices — a cheaper system that produces less can be a worse deal.

Then sanity-check the payback with the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/): a fair price with your rates should generally land under 11 years.

## FAQ

**Are solar prices still falling?**
Hardware keeps getting cheaper, but soft costs and labor have been sticky, so turnkey prices have declined slowly in recent years rather than dramatically.

**Is financing more expensive?**
Solar loans often add dealer fees baked into the price — a "same as cash" loan quote can carry a 15–30% higher gross price. Always compare the cash price.

**Do batteries follow the same pricing?**
No — batteries are priced separately, typically $9,000–$18,000+ installed. See whether one makes sense for you in the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/).
