A home battery stores a large amount of energy in a small box on your wall, and the entire regulatory apparatus around it exists to make sure that energy only ever comes out the way you intend. The reassuring part for homeowners is that a professionally installed, properly certified battery sits inside several overlapping layers of protection — a product safety listing, a set of fire-code installation rules, and an electrical code — long before the first inspector shows up. Knowing what each layer does helps you read a proposal and ask the right questions.

## The certification that should be non-negotiable: UL 9540

The foundational mark to look for is **UL 9540**, the safety standard for a complete energy storage system. It's not a test of the bare cells alone; it evaluates the whole product — the battery modules, the electronics that manage them, the enclosure, and the controls — as an integrated system. A battery listed to UL 9540 has been assessed for electrical, mechanical, and fire safety as the unit you'd actually install. If a product can't demonstrate that listing, that alone is reason enough to walk away, and most jurisdictions won't permit an install without it.

Sitting behind that listing is a related but different document, **UL 9540A**. This one is easy to confuse with UL 9540, so it's worth keeping straight:

- **UL 9540** is a *product* safety standard — the pass/fail listing the battery carries.
- **UL 9540A** is a *test method* that measures how a battery behaves in thermal runaway: whether a failure in one cell or unit propagates to its neighbors, how much heat and gas it produces, and how it spreads.

The results of that 9540A fire testing feed directly into the code rules below. In effect, how a battery performs in the 9540A test determines how much of it you're allowed to install, and how far apart.

## The fire code that governs installation: NFPA 855

Certification tells you the box is safe as designed. The installation code — most commonly **NFPA 855**, the standard for stationary energy storage systems, alongside the local fire code that adopts it — governs how it gets mounted in your specific home. This is where setbacks, spacing, and capacity limits live. The rules are structural rather than one-size-fits-all, but the categories they address are consistent:

- **Where it can live.** Codes distinguish between installing storage in a garage or utility space, on an exterior wall, or inside habitable rooms, and they treat each differently. Living spaces and bedrooms face the tightest restrictions.
- **How much energy in one place.** There are limits on how much stored energy you can put in a given location, which is part of why larger whole-home setups sometimes get split across units or pushed outdoors.
- **Spacing between units.** When you install more than one battery, the code may require separation between them (or a tested configuration that proves they won't propagate a fire to each other), which is why multi-battery banks are laid out deliberately rather than stacked at random.
- **Setbacks from openings and exits.** Outdoor units typically must sit a required distance from doors, windows, and paths of egress, so that a failing unit can't vent into the house or block your way out.

The critical caveat: the rules that apply to you are the ones your local authority having jurisdiction has adopted and enforces, and those vary by state and municipality and change over time. Your installer should be pulling a permit against your jurisdiction's current code, and your inspector is the final word. Treat any specific spacing or capacity figure as something to confirm locally rather than a national constant.

## Where chemistry quietly does the heavy lifting

Not all of battery safety is codes and inspectors — a lot of it is decided by the cells themselves. Most residential storage sold today uses lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry, which has a substantially higher thermal-runaway threshold and a more benign failure mode than the nickel-based (NMC) chemistry common in electric vehicles. That's a large part of why LFP has become the default for home batteries: it tolerates heat better and is far less prone to the runaway scenario the codes are built to contain. The full trade-off between the two chemistries is laid out in [LFP vs NMC home batteries](/blog/lfp-vs-nmc-home-batteries/), and it's worth understanding, because the chemistry printed on the spec sheet is itself a safety feature.

The electronics matter just as much. Every reputable home battery includes a battery management system that continuously monitors cell voltage and temperature and shuts things down before a fault becomes a hazard. How that management layer fits into the overall system — cells, inverter, controls — is covered in [how home solar batteries work](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/).

## Placement is a safety decision, not just a convenience

Because temperature drives both battery longevity and safety margin, where you site the unit is part of the safety picture and not merely an aesthetic call. A moderate, ventilated location keeps the cells in their comfortable range and away from the extremes that stress any chemistry; an unconditioned spot in a scorching garage or an exposed exterior wall in a harsh climate works the cells harder. The trade-offs between garage, basement, and outdoor mounting — and how they interact with both code setbacks and temperature — are worked through in [where your battery should live](/blog/solar-battery-placement-temperature/).

For a homeowner, the practical takeaways are short. Insist on a UL 9540-listed product. Expect your installer to design to your local fire code and pull a permit, and treat a reluctance to do either as a red flag. Favor LFP chemistry unless there's a specific reason not to.
