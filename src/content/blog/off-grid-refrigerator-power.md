A refrigerator is usually the first appliance that makes off-grid living feel serious. Lights and a phone charger sip power; a fridge runs day and night, all year, and it doesn't care whether the sun is out. That combination — constant demand paired with an unforgiving cold chain — is exactly what trips up first-time off-grid setups, because a system that's fine for weekend camping can quietly let the milk spoil when three cloudy days line up. Sizing solar and storage for a fridge that never gets to take a day off is a different exercise from sizing for anything else you'll run, and getting it right is mostly a matter of measuring the load honestly and then building in enough cushion for the bad weather that's coming whether you planned for it or not.

The good news is that a fridge is a predictable load once you know its real daily energy appetite, and that appetite has fallen dramatically as appliances have gotten more efficient. The bad news is that the single biggest decision — whether to run a standard household AC refrigerator through an inverter or use a purpose-built DC compressor unit — can double or halve the amount of solar and battery you need. So before you buy a single panel, the appliance itself deserves scrutiny.

## Measuring the load and choosing the fridge

Everything downstream flows from one number: how many kilowatt-hours your fridge uses in a day. You can't size solar or storage without it, and guessing is how people end up either wasting money on an oversized system or waking up to a warm freezer. The most reliable way to get this number is to measure it. A plug-in energy meter left on an AC fridge for a full 24 hours — ideally a couple of days to average out the compressor's duty cycle — tells you exactly what it draws in your conditions, including the defrost cycles and the extra work it does when the ambient temperature climbs. For a DC fridge, the manufacturer's daily consumption spec is usually a decent starting point, though real use in a hot cabin or van runs higher than the lab figure.

The reason measurement beats the nameplate is that a fridge's running wattage tells you almost nothing about its daily energy use, because the compressor only runs part of the time. A fridge might draw 100 watts while the compressor is on but only run that compressor 40% of the day, so its actual consumption is far below what you'd get by multiplying the running wattage by 24 hours. Duty cycle is everything, and it changes with ambient temperature, how full the fridge is, how often the door opens, and how well the unit is insulated. A fridge in a 95-degree cabin works far harder — and uses far more energy — than the same fridge in a cool basement, which is precisely why off-grid fridge sizing has to account for your actual environment rather than a spec-sheet ideal.

With that framing, here's the range you're working within. A modern, efficient AC refrigerator of moderate size tends to use somewhere on the order of 1 to 1.5 kilowatt-hours per day in reasonable conditions, more if it's large, old, or running in heat. A purpose-built DC compressor fridge — the kind used in RVs, vans, and off-grid cabins — is typically far more frugal, with a well-insulated chest-style DC unit sometimes drawing well under 1 kilowatt-hour a day, and a compact DC upright landing somewhere in between. Those differences compound through the whole system, because every kilowatt-hour the fridge doesn't use is a kilowatt-hour you don't have to generate, store, or oversize for. This is the core case for the DC compressor fridge in an off-grid build.

The DC-versus-AC decision deserves real thought because it reshapes the entire system, not just the fridge line item. A standard AC refrigerator runs on the 120-volt alternating current your inverter produces, which means the inverter has to be running whenever the fridge might cycle on — effectively all day and all night. Inverters draw their own idle power just to stay awake, and they lose a slice of energy every time they convert DC battery power to AC, so an AC fridge on an off-grid system pays two hidden taxes: the inverter's standby draw around the clock and the conversion losses on every compressor cycle. A DC compressor fridge sidesteps both by running straight off the battery's DC power at 12 or 24 volts, with no inverter in the path. On a small system where the fridge is the dominant load, eliminating an always-on inverter can meaningfully cut your total daily energy use and let you shrink both the panel array and the battery bank. The trade-offs — DC fridges cost more upfront and come in fewer sizes — are real, but for a fridge that runs continuously, the efficiency usually wins over the life of the system. The broader panel math for keeping any fridge cold is worked through in [powering a refrigerator with solar](/blog/solar-panels-to-run-refrigerator/), which is a useful companion when you're deciding how much array the appliance alone justifies.

## Sizing the solar and battery around cloudy days

Once you know the fridge's daily energy use, sizing the system is a two-part problem: enough solar to replace that energy on an average day, and enough battery to carry the fridge through the nights and the stretches when the sun doesn't cooperate. The second part is where off-grid design gets serious, because a grid-tied home can lean on the utility when production falls short, and you can't. Your battery is your entire buffer against bad weather, so it has to be sized for the worst realistic run of cloudy days, not the average one.

Start with the panels. Assume, for a worked example, a DC compressor fridge that measures out at 0.8 kilowatt-hours per day in your conditions, and a location that averages roughly 4 usable peak sun-hours a day across the year — a middle-of-the-road figure that you should replace with your own site's number, since it varies a lot by region and season. To make 0.8 kilowatt-hours from 4 sun-hours, a panel array would need to produce about 200 watts under ideal conditions, but off-grid systems lose energy at every step: panels rarely hit their rated output, charge controllers and wiring take a cut, and the battery loses some energy on the round trip in and back out. Building in a generous derate — planning for the array to deliver only 60 to 70% of its nameplate in real daily conditions — pushes the practical panel size to something like 300 watts or more just for the fridge. For the AC fridge case at, say, 1.3 kilowatt-hours per day plus inverter overhead, that same logic lands you closer to 500 to 600 watts. Those figures are deliberately conservative, and conservative is the correct posture off-grid, because an undersized array doesn't just mean a smaller cushion — it means the battery never fully recharges on marginal days and slowly drifts toward empty. A [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) lets you plug in your own daily kilowatt-hour figure and local sun-hours to size the array for your specific fridge rather than relying on these example numbers.

The battery is the part that actually keeps the food cold when the sun is gone, and it's sized around a concept called days of autonomy — how many days the system can run the fridge on stored energy alone, with no meaningful solar input. For a fridge, one day of autonomy is not enough, because a single cloudy day is common and a fridge that warms up is a real problem. Two to three days is a more honest target for most off-grid fridge setups, and cabins in cloudy climates or anyone who can't afford a spoilage risk should lean toward the higher end. Take the 0.8-kilowatt-hour DC fridge and aim for three days of autonomy, and you need roughly 2.4 kilowatt-hours of usable storage. Crucially, usable is not the same as the battery's nameplate rating. Lithium iron phosphate batteries can safely use most of their rated capacity, so a battery rated around 3 kilowatt-hours might give you close to that 2.4 usable. Older lead-acid batteries should only be drawn down partway to avoid shortening their life, so you'd need to roughly double the nameplate capacity to get the same usable energy — which is a large part of why lithium has taken over off-grid builds despite the higher sticker price. The full logic of turning days of autonomy and depth-of-discharge limits into a real battery size is laid out in [battery bank sizing for off-grid living](/blog/off-grid-battery-bank-sizing/), and a [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) will do the arithmetic for your target autonomy and chemistry.

Before you buy your way to a bigger array, it's worth spending a little effort shrinking the load itself, because on an off-grid system every watt-hour you don't consume is a watt-hour you never have to generate, store, or size for — and that ripples through the whole build. A fridge in a hot space works far harder than one in a cool spot, so simply keeping it out of direct sun and away from other heat sources cuts its duty cycle and its daily energy. Adding insulation around or above a chest-style unit, keeping the fridge reasonably full so it holds cold better, and making sure the door seals are tight all trim the compressor's run time. Good airflow around the condenser coils matters too, since a fridge crammed into a tight cabinet can't dump its heat and runs longer to compensate. None of these moves is dramatic on its own, but together they can knock a meaningful fraction off the daily kilowatt-hours — and because that saved energy compounds through the panel and battery sizing, a small reduction in fridge consumption can mean a smaller array and a smaller battery bank, which is real money on an off-grid build. The discipline of reducing the load before sizing the system is one of the biggest differences between an efficient off-grid setup and an oversized, overpriced one.

Two practical realities keep this from being a pure spreadsheet exercise. The first is seasonality: your 4-sun-hour average hides the fact that a northern winter might deliver half that, while summer delivers far more. If you rely on the fridge year-round, size the panels for the weakest season you'll actually be there for, or accept that you'll run a generator to top off the battery during the darkest weeks — many off-grid setups do exactly that, treating a small generator as insurance rather than a primary source. The second is that the fridge rarely lives alone. It shares the battery with lights, water pumps, phones, and everything else, and those loads compete for the same stored energy, especially at night. Sizing the fridge in isolation is a fine way to understand the appliance, but the real system has to carry the whole load list, and the disciplined approach is to add up every load's daily energy the same way, then size solar and storage for the total. That whole-system method — building an off-grid design up from an honest load inventory rather than a guess — is exactly what [sizing an off-grid solar system from your load list](/blog/off-grid-solar-system-sizing/) walks through, and it's the right next step once you've nailed down the fridge, which is usually the largest single piece of that list. Get the fridge measured, choose the efficient appliance where you can, and give the battery enough autonomy to shrug off a gray week, and the cold chain holds without forcing you to buy a system twice the size you needed.
