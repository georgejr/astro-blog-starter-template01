A solar salesperson tells you the system will cost you nothing out of pocket, that a low monthly payment simply replaces your electric bill, and that the loan is effectively free because the interest rate is so low. It's a compelling pitch, and the low advertised rate is the hook. What almost never gets said in that conversation is that the lender charges the installer a fee to offer that low rate, the installer builds that fee into the price you're quoted, and you pay for it — just not on a line labeled "interest." The rate looks cheap because the cost moved somewhere you're less likely to notice. Understanding exactly where it went, and how much it is, is the single most useful thing a solar shopper can learn, because a "no-cost" loan is not free money; it's a repriced system, and once you can see the repricing you can decide whether it's still a good deal for you.

## The dealer fee and the rate buydown, explained

The mechanism behind almost every low-rate solar loan is something called a dealer fee, and it works the way a mortgage point does, just less visibly. When a lender offers a solar loan at an attractive rate — well below what an unsecured personal loan of the same size and term would normally carry — they're giving up interest income they'd otherwise collect. They make that back by charging the installer a fee up front, deducted from what they pay the installer when the loan funds. If a lender advertises a very low rate, that fee might be 20%, 25%, even 30% of the financed amount; a higher rate carries a smaller fee, and a true zero-fee loan carries a market rate. The installer, understandably, doesn't absorb that fee — they add it to the price they quote you. So the same physical system that would cost, say, $20,000 in cash gets quoted at $26,000 or more when financed at a headline-grabbing low rate, because roughly $6,000 of dealer fee got folded into the number.

That's the whole trick, and it's worth restating plainly because it's counterintuitive: the low interest rate isn't a discount, it's a cost you prepaid inside a higher principal. You can pay a low rate on a big number or a market rate on a smaller number, and lenders have simply learned that most buyers fixate on the monthly payment and the advertised rate rather than the total. The fee doesn't appear on your loan documents as a fee — it's already baked into the amount financed before you ever see the paperwork, which is exactly why it's so easy to miss. The broader anatomy of how these fees inflate a financed system's price, and how large they can get, is worked through in [what a solar dealer fee really costs you](/blog/solar-dealer-fee-explained/), and it's the concept that unlocks every "no-cost" pitch.

The way to see through it is to always, always ask for the cash price — the number the installer would accept if you paid in full with no financing — alongside the financed quote. The gap between those two numbers is the dealer fee, roughly, and it tells you what the "low rate" actually costs. A reputable installer will give you the cash price without a fuss; one who dodges the question or claims there's no difference is a warning sign, because there's virtually always a difference when a below-market rate is on the table. Once you have both numbers, you can evaluate the financing honestly: a low-rate loan on an inflated price versus a higher-rate loan (or cash) on the real price. Sometimes the low-rate loan still wins, especially if you value keeping your cash. But you can't make that call until you've forced the hidden fee into the open, and the "no-cost" framing is designed to keep it hidden.

## Why the cash-equivalent price is the number that matters

Once you understand that a low rate is bought with a higher price, the right way to compare financing options becomes clear: reduce everything to the cash-equivalent cost, the total you'll actually pay over the life of the loan, and compare that against the honest cash price. A monthly payment is a terrible basis for comparison because it can be made to look attractive by stretching the term, lowering the rate through a bigger buydown, or both — three different loans with the same comfortable monthly payment can have wildly different total costs. The payment tells you what fits your budget; it tells you almost nothing about whether the deal underneath it is actually good.

Consider a simplified, clearly labeled comparison. Assume the real cash price of a system is $22,000. Offer A is a "no-cost" loan at 2.99% over 25 years, but to hit that rate the installer priced the system at $28,600 (a 30% dealer fee). Offer B is a straightforward loan at 8.49% over 15 years on the true $22,000 price, with no buydown. Offer A's low rate feels like the obvious winner, but run the totals and the picture flips: on the $28,600 principal, even at 2.99%, you repay a large sum over 25 years, and the inflated starting number means a big chunk of every payment is really paying down that hidden fee plus interest on the fee. Offer B, at a scarier-looking rate but on a much smaller and honest principal over a shorter term, can easily cost less in total dollars — and you own the system outright a decade sooner. The precise winner depends on the exact rates, terms, and fee, which is the whole point: you have to run the totals, not trust the rate. Modeling both scenarios in the [solar loan calculator](/solar-loan-calculator/) with the real principal each one implies shows you the total-cost gap that the monthly payment hides, and it's the exercise that turns a confusing pitch into a clear decision.

There's a further wrinkle that "no-cost" pitches tend to gloss over: the federal tax credit and how it interacts with the loan. The Residential Clean Energy Credit — currently 30% of the system cost under IRS rules — is calculated on what you paid for the system, and if a dealer fee inflated that price, there's nuance in what the IRS considers the qualifying cost, so this is a point to raise with a tax professional rather than assume. More practically, many solar loans are structured with the expectation that you'll apply your tax credit as a lump-sum prepayment in the first year or so, and if you don't — because you can't use the full credit, or you spend it elsewhere — your monthly payment can jump when the loan re-amortizes. That re-amortization surprise catches a lot of borrowers who were told the payment would stay flat. How financing and the credit actually interact, including this prepayment structure, is spelled out in [solar loans and the tax credit](/blog/solar-financing-tax-credit/), and it's essential reading before you sign a loan that assumes a credit you haven't confirmed you can fully use.

## Deciding whether "no money down" is right for you

None of this means financing solar is a mistake, and it's worth being clear about that, because the honest conclusion isn't "always pay cash." Not everyone has $22,000 sitting idle, and for many households a loan is the only way to go solar at all — spreading the cost over years while the system offsets a chunk of the electric bill is a perfectly reasonable trade. The problem isn't borrowing; it's borrowing blindly, accepting an inflated price because a low rate and a comfortable payment obscured what the system really cost. A financed system can still be a good decision even with a dealer fee baked in, provided you saw the fee, understood you were paying it, and decided the convenience or the cash-preservation was worth it. That's an informed choice. Being told the loan is "free" and never learning about the fee is not.

The practical way through is a short discipline that costs you nothing but a little assertiveness. Get the cash price in writing for every quote, so you can see the buydown. Compare total cost of ownership across your real options — cash, a low-rate loan on an inflated price, a market-rate loan on the true price, and if it's on the table, a lease or PPA — rather than comparing monthly payments, because the payment is the one number engineered to mislead. Line those options up side by side and the differences that a payment hides become obvious; the [cash vs. loan vs. lease comparator](/solar-payment-comparison/) is built for exactly that comparison, letting you see lifetime cost and ownership rather than just the monthly hit. And read the loan terms for the re-amortization clause and any prepayment assumptions before you sign, so a surprise payment jump doesn't ambush you a year in.

Above all, treat the phrase "no cost" as a prompt to dig, not as a reason to relax. There is always a cost; the only question is where it was put and whether you can see it. The deeper look at how a solar loan's structure quietly adds to the true price of a system — beyond just the dealer fee — is developed in [what a solar loan really adds to your system's price](/blog/solar-loan-true-cost/), and the reality behind the specific "$0-down" framing, including how the savings math holds up, is examined in [the real savings math behind $0-down solar deals](/blog/zero-down-solar-savings-math/). A homeowner who reads those, insists on the cash price, and compares total costs rather than payments will end up with a financing decision they can defend — whether that turns out to be a loan, a lease, or writing a check. The ones who get burned are the ones who heard "free" and stopped asking questions.
