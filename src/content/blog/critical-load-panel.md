When the power goes out and a battery-backed home stays lit, something behind the wall already made a decision about what stays lit. In a lot of systems that something is a critical load panel — a second, smaller electrical panel wired to hold only the circuits you genuinely can't do without. It's an unglamorous gray box screwed to the wall near the main panel, easy to overlook entirely, and yet it's frequently the single component that decides whether a modestly sized battery feels like a lifeline during an outage or a disappointment. Get the panel and its circuit list right and a small battery carries the essentials for days. Get it wrong, or skip it in favor of trying to back up the whole house, and you're either spending far more than you needed to or watching the battery drain in an afternoon. The box is cheap and boring; the choice it represents is neither.

## The problem it solves, and what goes on it

A home's main electrical panel feeds everything at once — the air conditioner, the electric range, the clothes dryer, every outlet and light in the house. Ask a typical home battery to back up that entire panel and you slam straight into a wall of physics and cost. The battery would have to be large enough to handle the instantaneous surge of the heaviest appliances the moment they switch on, and it would have to store enough energy to run all of them together, which adds up to a far bigger, far pricier system than most people need or want just to ride out a power outage. A critical load panel sidesteps that problem elegantly. Instead of backing up the whole house, the installer moves a chosen subset of circuits onto a separate subpanel, and that subpanel is what the battery supports when the grid fails. Everything else in the house goes dark during an outage, exactly as it would with no battery at all. You've drawn a deliberate line between what must stay on and what can wait — the fundamental trade-off at the heart of [whole-home versus partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/). The payoff of drawing that line is leverage: a small, affordable battery can keep a carefully chosen set of essentials running for a long time, precisely because it isn't wasting its finite capacity on loads that simply don't matter at two in the morning during a storm.

The circuits that belong on a critical load panel are the ones whose loss ranges from genuinely dangerous to seriously disruptive. A typical panel carries some combination of the following:

- **Refrigerator and freezer** — protecting food, and drawing power intermittently rather than constantly, which makes them cheap to sustain.
- **Key lighting** — enough fixtures to move safely through the house at night, not every light in every room.
- **Internet, phone charging, and a few outlets** — communication and information, which matter more in an emergency than people expect.
- **Furnace fan or heating controls** — in cold climates, keeping the heat running is a safety issue rather than a comfort one.
- **Medical equipment** — anything health-critical is a non-negotiable inclusion, and it often drives the whole design.
- **A well pump**, where the home's running water depends on one.

The common thread is that these are low-to-moderate electrical draws that matter disproportionately when they stop. None of them is a heavy load, which is exactly why a modest battery can carry them. The circuits deliberately left off the panel are the mirror image: the big, brief, or optional loads. Central air conditioning, electric ovens and ranges, clothes dryers, electric water heaters — these pull enormous power, and putting any of them on the critical panel would force a much larger and more expensive battery just to handle the surge and the sustained energy draw. The most demanding of the lot is central cooling, and whether a battery can run an air conditioner at all is its own real sizing question, examined in [can a home battery run your air conditioner](/blog/can-battery-run-air-conditioner/). For most partial-backup designs, the honest answer is that the AC stays off during an outage, and that trade — accepting a warm house for a few hours in exchange for a battery you can actually afford — is what keeps the whole approach practical rather than aspirational.

## Why the load list drives the battery size

The circuits you choose don't merely determine what stays on during an outage — they set how big a battery you need and how long that battery will last, which is the part people tend to get backward. A critical panel loaded with only a refrigerator, some lights, and a router sips power, and a modest battery can carry it through a long outage without breaking a sweat, because the total draw is small and steady. Start adding heavier loads and the runtime shrinks fast, because every additional watt is pulling from the same fixed pool of stored energy. Put a well pump and an electric heating element on the panel alongside the fridge and the lights, and a battery that would have lasted two days now lasts a fraction of that, because you're drawing the same reservoir down far more quickly. The relationship is direct and unforgiving: the load list and the battery capacity together determine runtime, and you can't change one without changing the other.

This is why the smart sequence is to build the load list first and then size the storage to it, rather than the tempting but backward approach of buying a battery you like the look of and hoping it covers what you care about. Working the problem in the right order means adding up the realistic draw of your essential circuits — not their nameplate maximums, but what they actually pull in normal operation — and then matching a battery to that number with some honest margin. That's the exact method laid out in [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), and it's the difference between a system designed to a target and one bought on a hunch. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) turns a list of circuits into a concrete capacity and runtime estimate, which lets you see, before any equipment is ordered, how each circuit you add or drop moves the number. That feedback loop is the whole value of doing the list first: you can watch the runtime fall as you drag the electric water heater onto the panel, decide it isn't worth halving your backup duration, and take it back off — all on paper, at no cost. Do it in the other order and you discover the same trade-off the hard way, during an actual outage, when the battery you already bought and installed turns out to empty faster than you assumed because you loaded it with more than it was sized for.

There's a subtler point buried in here too. Because the load list drives the size, two homes that both want "a battery for backup" can end up needing wildly different systems depending on what they insist on keeping alive. A household content with a fridge, lights, and phones can be covered by a small, inexpensive battery. A household that wants the well pump, the furnace fan, and enough outlets to work from home needs meaningfully more storage, and a household that insists on air conditioning needs more still — potentially a different class of system entirely. The battery isn't really the thing being sized. The list is. The battery is just the consequence of the list, which is why every serious backup design starts with a conversation about circuits, not a conversation about batteries.

## Where climate and household change the list, and getting the design right

The "right" critical load panel isn't a fixed template you can copy from a catalog — it shifts with where you live and how your particular house runs, and ignoring that is how people end up with a backup that protects the wrong things. A home in a cold northern climate treats the furnace fan and heating controls as true, non-negotiable essentials, because a multi-day winter outage without heat isn't a comfort problem, it's a burst-pipes-and-hypothermia safety emergency, and those circuits jump straight to the top of the list. A home in a hot climate faces the harder version of the problem: cooling is simultaneously the load people most want backed up and the one hardest to support, which pushes those households either toward larger and more expensive batteries or toward accepting that a fan and some shade will have to do during the worst of it. Water is another regional swing that reorders the whole list. A home on a private well can't get so much as a glass of water without powering the pump, so that circuit is essential; a home on municipal water pressure can leave it off entirely and never notice. The same logic ripples through medical needs, work-from-home dependencies, and simply how outage-prone the local grid is in the first place — a household that loses power twice a year approaches the list very differently from one that loses it twice a month. Two identical houses on the same street can end up with genuinely different critical panels because their climates, water sources, medical situations, and outage histories ask different things of them.

A few principles keep the design honest regardless of the specifics. Choose circuits by consequence, not convenience — the real test is "what is genuinely unsafe or unworkable without this," not "what would be nice to keep running," and that distinction is what separates a lean, affordable backup from a bloated one. Keep the heavy, brief loads off the panel unless you're deliberately and knowingly paying for a battery large enough to carry them, because a single high-surge appliance can force the whole system up a size class. And be realistic that partial backup means real, accepted limits: some rooms stay dark, the big appliances rest, the house gets warm or cool, and all of that is the design working exactly as intended rather than failing. People who expect a critical-load system to feel like the grid never went out will be disappointed; people who understand they're keeping the essentials alive will be well served. Done well, the critical load panel is precisely what makes battery backup practical on an ordinary budget. It's the difference between "we'd need a huge, expensive system to back up this house" and "a right-sized battery keeps our essentials running for days," and the line between those two very different outcomes gets drawn on that small, unremarkable subpanel, one carefully chosen circuit at a time.
