When the power goes out and a battery-backed home stays lit, something behind the wall made a decision about *what* stays lit. In many systems that something is a critical load panel — a second, smaller electrical panel wired to hold only the circuits you can't do without. It's an unglamorous gray box, but it's often the component that decides whether a modestly sized battery feels like a lifeline or a disappointment during an outage.

## The problem it solves

A home's main electrical panel feeds everything: the air conditioner, the electric range, the dryer, every outlet and light. Ask a typical home battery to back up that entire panel and you run into a wall of physics and cost. The battery would have to be large enough to handle the instantaneous surge of the heaviest appliances and store enough energy to run them all — a far bigger, pricier system than most people need or want just to survive a power outage.

A critical load panel sidesteps that. Instead of backing up the whole house, the installer moves a chosen subset of circuits onto a separate subpanel, and *that* panel is what the battery supports when the grid fails. Everything else goes dark during an outage, exactly as it would with no battery at all. You've drawn a line between what must stay on and what can wait — the fundamental trade-off explored in [whole-home versus partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/).

The payoff is leverage. A small, affordable battery can keep a carefully chosen set of essentials running for a long time, because it isn't wasting capacity on loads that don't matter at 2 a.m. during a storm.

## What belongs on it

The right circuits are the ones whose loss ranges from genuinely dangerous to seriously disruptive. A typical critical load panel carries some combination of:

- **Refrigerator and freezer** — protecting food, and running intermittently rather than constantly.
- **Key lighting** — enough to move safely through the house, not every fixture.
- **Internet, phone charging, and a few outlets** — communication and information during an emergency.
- **Furnace fan or heating controls** — in cold climates, keeping heat running can be a safety issue, not a comfort one.
- **Medical equipment** — anything health-critical is a non-negotiable inclusion.
- **A well pump**, where the home's water depends on one.

The common thread is that these are low-to-moderate draws that matter disproportionately when they stop.

### What usually gets left off

The circuits deliberately excluded are the big, brief, or optional loads: central air conditioning, electric ovens and ranges, clothes dryers, electric water heaters. These pull enormous power, and putting them on the critical panel would force a much larger battery to handle the surge and the energy. The most demanding of them is central cooling — whether a battery can run it at all is its own sizing question, examined in [can a home battery run your air conditioner](/blog/can-battery-run-air-conditioner/). For most partial-backup designs, the honest answer is that the AC stays off during an outage, and that's the trade that keeps the battery affordable.

## Why the load list drives the battery size

The circuits you choose don't just determine what stays on — they set how big a battery you need and how long it lasts. A critical panel loaded only with a fridge, some lights, and a router sips power, and a modest battery can carry it through a long outage. Start adding heavier loads and the runtime shrinks fast, because you're drawing down the same stored energy more quickly.

This is why the smart sequence is to build the load list *first*, then size storage to it, rather than buying a battery and hoping it covers what you care about. Adding up the realistic draw of your essential circuits, then matching a battery to that number, is the exact method in [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/). The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) turns that list into a capacity and runtime estimate, so you can see how each circuit you add or drop moves the number before any equipment is ordered.

## Where climate and household quietly change the list

The "right" critical load panel isn't a fixed template — it shifts with where you live and how your house runs. A home in a cold northern climate treats the furnace fan and heating controls as true essentials, because a multi-day winter outage without heat is a safety emergency. A home in a hot climate faces the harder problem: cooling is the load people most want backed up and the one hardest to support, which pushes those households toward larger batteries or toward accepting that a fan and shade will have to do.

Water is another regional swing. A home on a private well can't get water without powering the pump, so that circuit jumps to the top of the list; a home on municipal water can leave it off entirely. The same logic touches medical needs, work-from-home dependencies, and how outage-prone the local grid is in the first place. Two identical houses can end up with very different critical panels because their climates, water sources, and outage histories ask different things of them.

## Getting the design right

A few principles keep a critical load panel honest. Choose circuits by consequence, not convenience — the test is "what's genuinely unsafe or unworkable without this," not "what would be nice to keep." Keep the heavy, brief loads off unless you're deliberately paying for a battery large enough to carry them. And be realistic that partial backup means real limits: some rooms stay dark, the big appliances rest, and that's the design working as intended, not failing.

Done well, the critical load panel is what makes battery backup practical for an ordinary budget. It's the difference between "we need a huge system to back up the house" and "a right-sized battery keeps the essentials running for days" — and the line between those two outcomes is drawn on that small subpanel, one chosen circuit at a time.
