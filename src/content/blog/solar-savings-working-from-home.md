An empty house in the middle of the day is a quietly wasteful thing when it has solar on the roof. The panels are working their hardest at noon, the sun is directly overhead, and the meter is spinning backward because nobody is home to use any of it. All that production flows out to the grid, where — depending on where you live — it may be worth full retail credit, a fraction of retail, or somewhere in between. A remote worker changes that picture completely. When you're home at 1 p.m. running a laptop, a couple of monitors, the coffee maker, and the air conditioning that keeps your office bearable, you're consuming the very electricity your panels are making at the exact moment they make it. That single fact — being present during peak production — is the reason solar tends to pay off faster for people who work from home, and it's worth understanding why the timing matters as much as the total.

## Why the noon hours are worth more when you're there to use them

Every kilowatt-hour your system produces has two possible fates. It's either consumed on the spot by something running in your house, or it's exported to the grid. Which one happens changes what that kilowatt-hour is worth to you, and the gap can be large. A kilowatt-hour you consume directly offsets one you would otherwise have bought at the full retail rate. A kilowatt-hour you export is credited under whatever arrangement your utility offers — and across much of the country, that credit has drifted below retail. Some utilities still run full one-to-one net metering, where an exported kilowatt-hour is worth the same as a consumed one and the timing barely matters. But a growing number use net billing or tiered export rates, where midday exports earn noticeably less than what you pay to buy power back in the evening. That arrangement — net metering in its full form, net billing in its reduced ones — determines how much an exported kilowatt-hour is worth, and the direction of travel almost everywhere is toward compensating exports at less than the retail price.

Here's where the remote worker pulls ahead. Solar production follows a predictable arc: it ramps up through the morning, peaks around solar noon, and tapers off through the afternoon. A household where everyone leaves for work and school at 8 a.m. and returns at 6 p.m. consumes almost nothing during that peak. Their refrigerator cycles, maybe a few standby loads hum along, and that's it — call it a few hundred watts against an array that might be pushing five or six thousand. The rest exports. When that same household comes home, the sun is setting, production is collapsing, and now they're drawing heavily from the grid to cook dinner, run laundry, and cool the house — buying back power at retail that they sold at a discount hours earlier. The share of their own production they actually use for themselves, their self-consumption rate, might sit somewhere around a quarter to a third.

A person working from home flips the load curve so it overlaps the production curve. The office equipment draws power all day. If it's summer, the air conditioning is running during the hottest, sunniest hours precisely because you're there and want the room comfortable — and that cooling load lines up almost perfectly with peak solar output. Lunch gets cooked at home instead of bought near the office. The dishwasher can run after lunch instead of after an evening meal. Suddenly a much larger fraction of what the panels make is being soaked up in real time. It's reasonable to model a work-from-home household landing in the range of 50 to 65% self-consumption where a comparable empty-daytime home might sit near 30%. In a full net-metering market that difference is mostly academic, because exports and consumption are valued the same. But in every market where exports are worth less than retail — which is an expanding list — that higher self-consumption converts directly into more money kept. The broader question of what actually drives the size of a solar bill reduction is worked through in [how much do solar panels save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/), and self-consumption timing is one of the levers that quietly moves it.

## Lining up the appliances that draw the most with the hours the sun is up

Once you accept that a self-consumed kilowatt-hour beats an exported one under most modern rate structures, the practical move is to run your heaviest loads while the panels are producing — and working from home makes that natural rather than a chore. The appliances worth thinking about are the ones that actually move the needle, which are almost always the ones tied to heating, cooling, or heating water. A detailed look at which loads dominate a typical home lives in [the appliances quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), but the short version is that a central air conditioner, an electric water heater, an electric dryer, and an EV charger dwarf everything else. Line even a couple of those up with midday sun and you've captured most of the available benefit.

Air conditioning is the easy win because it doesn't require any behavior change at all. If you're home and it's hot, you're cooling the house during the exact hours the array is at full tilt. That's not load-shifting; it's just what a summer workday looks like when you're in it. The others take a little intention. Running the dishwasher after lunch instead of before bed, starting a load of laundry mid-afternoon, and — if you have an electric water heater — nudging its heaviest recovery into the daytime all pull consumption under the production curve. A heat pump water heater or a tank with a simple timer makes this almost automatic. If you drive an EV and can plug in during the day rather than overnight, that's often the single largest load you can move, and daytime charging on your own solar is dramatically cheaper than pulling from the grid after dark. None of this requires living like an energy accountant. It's mostly a matter of defaulting your discretionary heavy loads to the hours you're already awake, at home, and generating.

There's a limit to how far this goes, and it's honest to name it. Your baseline daytime load — the office equipment, lights, fridge, and the like — is real but modest, often just a few hundred watts. Even a busy work-from-home day won't consume a large array's full midday output on electronics alone; the big self-consumption gains come from the thermal loads. If you have gas heat, gas hot water, and gas cooking, your ability to soak up solar during the day is more limited, because your controllable electric loads are smaller. That's not a reason to skip solar — it just means the work-from-home advantage is largest for homes that are heavily electric, and more muted for homes that run most of their big loads on gas. It also means that as you electrify — swapping a gas furnace for a heat pump, adding an EV — the value of being home during production hours grows, because you have more daytime load to point at the sun. ## Sizing and expectations when your load curve already fits the sun

The instinct after reading all this is to buy a bigger system, on the theory that more panels means more of that valuable self-consumed power. That's only half right, and the half that's wrong can cost you. Self-consumption is a percentage of what you produce, and it's bounded by how much your house actually draws during daylight. Doubling your array doesn't double your daytime load — the office still only needs its few hundred watts, the AC still only pulls what the thermostat calls for. Past a certain point, extra panels just mean extra midday production with nothing at home to absorb it, so it exports at whatever your utility pays, which is exactly the low-value fate you were trying to avoid. The sweet spot for a work-from-home household is a system sized close to annual usage but not wildly beyond it, so that a healthy share of production gets consumed on-site rather than dumped to the grid. A rough sizing pass using your real annual kilowatt-hours is what the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) is built for, and it's a better starting point than a round number pulled from a sales sheet.

Consider a stylized comparison to see the shape of it. Assume a home that uses 12,000 kWh a year and installs a system producing about 12,000 kWh annually — a roughly 100% offset on paper. Assume the utility pays $0.05 per exported kWh but charges $0.20 to buy power back, a spread that's increasingly common under net billing. If this home is empty all day and self-consumes 30% of production, it uses 3,600 kWh directly (worth $0.20 each, or $720 avoided) and exports 8,400 kWh (worth $0.05 each, or $420 in credits), then buys back its evening and night usage. If instead the household works from home and self-consumes 55%, it uses 6,600 kWh directly ($1,320 avoided) and exports 5,400 kWh ($270 in credits). The consumption side jumped by $600 while the export credit fell by $150 — a net gain of roughly $450 a year from the same panels, same production, same everything except who's home at noon. Those figures are illustrative and depend entirely on your rate spread; in a true one-to-one net-metering market the difference nearly vanishes, and in a market with a wide import-export spread it grows. The point stands regardless of the exact numbers: the wider the gap between what you pay and what you're paid, the more being home during production hours is worth.

That's also why the work-from-home advantage isn't a fixed bonus — it tracks your local rate structure and your own electric load. Someone on old-style net metering in a low-rate state gets very little extra from daytime presence, and for them solar's value comes almost entirely from the total kilowatt-hours offset rather than the timing. Someone on net billing in a high-rate state with electric heating, electric hot water, and an EV can capture a meaningful premium simply by being there and running their big loads under the sun. If you're trying to decide whether solar makes sense for your situation at all, the general framework in [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/) is the place to start, and the work-from-home factor is best treated as something that improves an already-reasonable case rather than rescuing a marginal one. The honest summary is that remote work doesn't change how many kilowatt-hours your roof can make — it changes how many of them you get to keep at full value, and in most modern rate environments, keeping them is where the money is.
