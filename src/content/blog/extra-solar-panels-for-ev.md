Buying an electric car is, from your electric meter's point of view, a bit like adding a second household to your home. The car does not sip power the way a phone charger does; it moves a two-ton object down the highway, and doing that for a year of ordinary driving adds a load comparable to a major appliance running around the clock. If you already have solar or are sizing a new system, the natural question is how much more array it takes to feed the car with sunshine rather than grid power. The good news is that the answer is a clean, checkable calculation — it flows directly from how far you drive, not from anything mysterious about the car — and for a typical driver it usually lands in the range of six to eight additional panels. Getting to that number, and understanding why your own figure might sit higher or lower, is a matter of following two simple conversions.

The first conversion turns miles into kilowatt-hours. An electric car's efficiency is measured in miles per kilowatt-hour, and most EVs land somewhere between about 3 and 4 miles per kWh in real-world mixed driving — call it 3.5 as a reasonable middle-of-the-road planning figure, with efficient sedans doing better and heavy trucks and SUVs doing worse. Divide your annual mileage by that efficiency and you have the energy the car needs at the battery. But there is a wrinkle worth building in from the start: charging is not perfectly efficient. Some energy is lost as heat in the charger and the battery, so the power you actually pull from the wall is roughly 10 to 15 percent more than what ends up stored in the car. That charging overhead is real and easy to forget, and skipping it is the most common reason a homeowner's back-of-envelope estimate comes in low. Account for it, and a driver covering 12,000 miles a year at 3.5 miles per kWh needs about 3,430 kWh at the battery and closer to 3,900 kWh drawn from the wall to put it there.

## From annual miles to a panel count

The second conversion turns those kilowatt-hours into panels, and it works exactly the way sizing any solar array does: figure out how much energy one panel makes in a year at your location, then divide. A modern 400-watt panel in a moderately sunny place — think roughly 4.5 peak sun hours a day with a normal derate for real-world losses — produces on the order of 500 to 550 kWh over a year. In the sunny Southwest that same panel might make 600 or more; in the cloudy Northeast, closer to 400. Take the middle figure of about 525 kWh per panel and divide it into the roughly 3,900 kWh our example driver needs, and you get about seven and a half panels — call it seven or eight to be safe. That is the origin of the six-to-eight-panel rule of thumb for a typical driver: it is not a guess, it is 12,000 miles run through two honest conversions. The table below works the same math across a few common driving profiles so you can find yourself in it.

| Annual miles | kWh needed (incl. charging loss) | Panels (@ ~525 kWh each) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 8,000 | ~2,600 | 5 |
| 12,000 | ~3,900 | 7–8 |
| 15,000 | ~4,900 | 9–10 |
| 20,000 | ~6,500 | 12–13 |

Two variables move your real number well away from the table, and both are worth pinning down rather than assuming. The first is your car's actual efficiency, which depends heavily on the vehicle and how you drive it. A compact EV cruising gentle suburban miles might genuinely hit 4 miles per kWh, shrinking the panel count; a large electric pickup towing in cold weather can drop below 2.5, which can nearly double the energy — and the panels — for the same distance. Cold climates take a further bite, because batteries are less efficient in winter and cabin heating draws directly from the pack, so a northern driver often needs more panels than a mild-climate driver covering identical miles. The second variable is your location's sun, which sets how much each panel produces. The same seven-panel result in Arizona might require nine or ten panels in Seattle to deliver the same annual energy, because each northern panel makes less. This is precisely why a table can only get you close, and why plugging your specific car, mileage, and location into the [EV charging calculator](/ev-charging-calculator/) is worth the two minutes — it turns "roughly six to eight" into a number built from your own numbers. The deeper mechanics of charging an EV from panels, including how home charging rates and timing interact, are worth understanding alongside this, and [how many extra panels an EV really requires](/blog/solar-panels-for-ev-charging/) digs further into the charging side of the equation.

It helps to translate the panel count back into something physical, because seven or eight panels is not a trivial addition to a roof. At roughly 18 square feet per module plus spacing, eight panels claim on the order of 150 square feet of usable, unshaded roof — a real chunk of a typical home's south-facing area, and enough that on a small or cut-up roof the EV's share can be the difference between a system that fits and one that does not. That is worth knowing before you commit to an electric car on the assumption that solar will cover it, because the roof, not the budget, is sometimes the binding limit. A driver with a long commute and a thirsty electric truck might need a dozen panels just for the car, and if the roof was already full covering the house, those panels have nowhere to go. The mileage-to-panels math tells you what the car asks for; the roof survey tells you whether you can grant it. When the two disagree, the honest options are a partial offset, a ground mount if you have the yard, or simply accepting that some of the car's miles will run on grid power — none of which are failures, just the ordinary trade-offs of a real house.

One planning subtlety separates the naive version of this math from the honest one: adding the EV's panels to an existing array is not always the same as covering the car with your own sunshine in real time. The panels feed a shared meter, and whether the energy those extra modules make actually goes into the car depends on when you charge. Charge at midday while the sun is up and the array is producing, and the car draws largely from the roof directly — the cleanest and cheapest case. Charge overnight, as most people do out of habit and convenience, and the car pulls from the grid in the dark while your extra daytime production was exported hours earlier. Whether that time-shift costs you anything comes down to your net metering or export rules, but the physical panels still do their job of generating the annual energy the car consumes; they just may not power it in the same instant. Sizing the array to your EV's annual appetite is the right first step regardless, and the charging-timing question is a second, separate optimization worth revisiting once the panels are on the roof.

## Sizing the whole system, not just the car's slice

Thinking about the EV's panels as a bolt-on is useful for the arithmetic but a little misleading for the actual purchase, because in practice you rarely buy "the car's seven panels" as a distinct thing. You size one system to your household's total electricity appetite, and the EV is simply a large and growing part of that total. The right way to plan is to add the car's annual kWh — the roughly 3,900 in our example — to your existing household usage and size the array against the combined figure. A home that used 9,000 kWh a year before the EV and now needs 12,900 with it should be sized to that larger number, and whether that pencils out as adding seven panels to an existing system or building one system to cover everything from the start depends on your situation. If you are installing solar fresh and know the EV is coming, building the capacity in from day one is almost always cheaper than adding panels later, because the fixed costs of permitting, design, and mobilizing a crew get paid once rather than twice. To size against your real combined load rather than a rule of thumb, feed your household usage plus the car's annual kWh into the [how many solar panels calculator](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/), which works backward from total consumption to a panel count for your location.

There is a cost dimension to sizing for the car that is easy to overlook when you are focused on panel counts. Because so much of a solar project's price is fixed — permitting, design, the crew's trip to your house, and a share of company overhead cost roughly the same whether the array is small or large — the incremental panels you add for an EV are among the cheapest watts on the whole system. The first panel carries a heavy load of fixed cost; the eighth panel is almost pure hardware and labor. That is the arithmetic reason building the EV's capacity into a system from the start beats bolting it on two years later as a separate job: the second install would make you pay those fixed costs a second time, on just a handful of panels, which is the worst possible ratio. If an electric car is anywhere on your horizon when you are already sizing solar, the cheapest moment to add its panels is now, folded into the larger project, rather than later as a small standalone one.

The honest exception to "just add the panels" is when your roof or budget cannot stretch to cover the full EV load, which is common and not a failure. Even a partial offset — enough panels to cover half the car's miles, say — still displaces real grid purchases and still saves money; you simply cover the rest from the grid as you would have anyway. There is no rule that says an EV's panels must be all-or-nothing, and a household that can fit five extra panels rather than eight is still meaningfully ahead. What changes the value of those panels most is not the panel count itself but what the alternative costs: charging an EV on grid electricity at retail rates versus filling it from a roof you have already paid for is a large swing over the years you own the car, and that gap is where the real return on the EV's panels lives. Our look at [what charging an EV with solar does to your savings](/blog/ev-charging-solar-savings/) walks through that side of the ledger, and it is the piece that turns the sizing math here from an engineering exercise into a financial one. Start with the miles, run them through the two conversions, and you will know within a panel or two how much roof your car actually asks for — then size the whole system to your combined life rather than treating the car as a separate project.
