An outage that knocks out a whole neighborhood for four hours is a minor inconvenience for most households and a lost afternoon of income for anyone who works from home. The refrigerator coasts, the lights can wait, but the moment the power drops, your monitors go black, your desktop reboots, and — the part people forget — the internet dies even if your laptop is still running on its own battery, because the modem and router in the closet have no power of their own. For a remote worker, that is the difference between riding out a blip and calling clients to reschedule. The good news buried in the frustration is that a home office is one of the easiest things in the entire house to back up, because the loads involved are genuinely small.

The instinct is to assume that keeping the office running through an outage requires the same heavy-duty storage that runs a refrigerator or an air conditioner, and that instinct is wrong by a wide margin. A desk full of working electronics draws a fraction of what a single kitchen appliance pulls. Once you actually measure what your setup consumes, the sizing problem shrinks from "how big a home battery do I need" to "how small can I get away with," and for many remote workers the honest answer is smaller and cheaper than they feared. The trick is knowing which loads truly matter, and not overbuilding around loads that don't.

## Your desk draws less than a single hair dryer

Start with the actual numbers, because they reframe the whole exercise. A modern laptop under a normal working load draws somewhere in the range of 30 to 65 watts including its charger, and it carries its own internal battery besides, so a laptop is nearly a non-problem — it keeps running on its own for a couple of hours whether or not the wall has power. An external monitor adds roughly 20 to 40 watts depending on size and brightness, and a second monitor adds about the same again. A desktop computer is heavier: a typical office desktop might pull 60 to 150 watts under ordinary work, and a powerful workstation with a discrete graphics card can climb higher when it's actually being pushed, though most office tasks never push it there. Add the small stuff — a desk lamp, phone charging, a USB hub, maybe a headset base — and you're adding tens of watts, not hundreds.

The loads that genuinely decide whether you can keep working during a power outage are the ones people overlook precisely because they're small and hidden: the internet gear. A cable or fiber modem, sometimes called an ONT on fiber, draws only a handful of watts, perhaps 5 to 15. A Wi-Fi router draws a similar small amount, maybe 5 to 20 watts. Together they might total 20 to 30 watts — a rounding error in energy terms — and yet they are the single most important thing to back up, because without them the laptop that's still happily running on its own battery has nothing to connect to. This is the detail that separates a home office backup that works from one that doesn't. Plenty of people carefully back up the computer and forget the modem, then discover during the first outage that they have a working machine and no way to reach a video call. A battery for the computer and internet has to explicitly include that closet full of blinking boxes, and the reassuring part is that they cost almost nothing in energy to keep alive.

Add a realistic desk up and you land somewhere modest. A laptop-based setup with two monitors, the networking gear, and phone charging might draw 120 to 180 watts total while you're actively working. A desktop-based setup runs higher, maybe 200 to 350 watts depending on the machine. Call it, for planning, somewhere around 150 watts for a lean laptop office and up to roughly 300 for a heavier desktop one. Those are continuous draws you can actually hold against a battery's capacity, and they're small enough that the sizing math turns out friendly. This is a very different exercise from [sizing a battery for a home's essential loads](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), where the refrigerator, the well pump, and the furnace fan dominate the list — the office is the gentle end of the load spectrum, and treating it as if it belonged with the heavy appliances leads you to buy far more storage than the job requires.

There's one category worth a warning, because it's the exception that breaks the friendly math: anything that makes heat. A space heater under the desk, a mug warmer, an electric kettle for coffee, a laser printer mid-job — these are hundreds to over a thousand watts each, and any one of them dwarfs the entire rest of the office combined. If your "office" includes a 1,500-watt space heater running through the winter, that single appliance is the load you're really sizing for, and it will empty a small battery in short order. The clean move during an outage is simply to keep the resistive-heat devices off the backed-up circuit and run them, if at all, only when grid power is present. Back up the electronics, not the heat, and the numbers stay small.

## Turning watts into hours of runtime

Once you know your office draws something like 150 watts, sizing the battery is a matter of deciding how long you want to keep working and multiplying. Energy is power times time, so 150 watts sustained for one hour is 150 watt-hours, or 0.15 kilowatt-hours. Four hours of that lean laptop office is roughly 0.6 kilowatt-hours. A full eight-hour workday is about 1.2 kilowatt-hours. Even a heavier 300-watt desktop office over a full workday lands around 2.4 kilowatt-hours. Set those figures against what home batteries and portable power stations actually hold and the conclusion almost writes itself: a modest amount of storage covers a remarkable amount of remote work.

A large portable power station in the 1 to 2 kilowatt-hour class — the kind you can carry and plug in without an electrician — will run a lean laptop office through most or all of a workday, and a stack of them or a slightly larger unit handles a desktop setup comfortably. A permanently installed home battery, which typically starts around 10 kilowatt-hours and goes up from there, would run an office for days on its own, which is precisely why sizing a whole home battery around the office alone makes no sense — you'd be buying a reservoir to fill a teacup. The method for turning any battery's capacity into a realistic number of runtime hours, accounting for the fact that you never get every rated watt-hour back out, is the same one laid out in [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/); the office is simply the case where that arithmetic comes out generously because the load is so light.

A practical decision falls out of this, and it's really a question of scale and how the rest of your house is set up. If keeping the office online through outages is the whole goal, a portable power station is often the right-sized, lowest-friction tool — no installation, no permits, you plug the modem, router, and computer into it and it seamlessly carries them when the grid drops if it has a fast enough transfer, or you plug in within seconds if it doesn't. If you already want backup for the fridge, some lights, and the furnace anyway, then a properly installed home battery covers the office as a trivial side effect of covering everything else, and the office loads barely register against its capacity. The trade-offs between those two paths — cost, capacity, installation, and how seamlessly each one catches an outage — are worth weighing deliberately, and the [comparison of a home battery against a portable power station](/blog/home-battery-vs-portable-power-station/) walks through exactly where each one fits. For a renter or someone who just wants their workday protected without a construction project, the portable route usually wins; for a homeowner already planning whole-house resilience, the office rides along for free.

Two details make the difference between a backup that quietly works and one that fails at the worst moment. The first is switchover speed. A desktop computer will reboot — losing whatever you hadn't saved — if power is interrupted even for a fraction of a second, so if you run a desktop you want either a battery with a genuine uninterruptible transfer or a small dedicated UPS on the machine itself to bridge the instant of the cutover. A laptop sidesteps this entirely because its own battery covers the gap, which is one more quiet argument for laptop-based remote work if outages are a regular feature of where you live. The second detail is the part of the outage you don't control: your home network can be perfectly powered and you can still be offline if the internet service provider's own equipment down the street has lost power. Cable and fiber networks have their own backup at the provider's facilities, but it isn't unlimited, so in a long, widespread outage the connection can drop even though your modem is happily lit. Backing up your own gear is necessary but not always sufficient, and for work that absolutely cannot go down, a phone on cellular data as a hotspot is the honest fallback — which is itself just another small load your battery can easily carry.

One habit separates a backup that works from one that fails at the worst moment, and it costs nothing: test it before you need it. Plug your actual gear into the battery or power station, pull the wall power, and confirm that the modem, the router, and your computer all keep running and that your internet connection actually stays up. People discover surprising things this way — a power station whose transfer is too slow to keep a desktop alive, a modem plugged into the wrong outlet, a router that reboots and takes three minutes to reconnect. It's far better to learn those quirks on a calm afternoon than during a real outage with a client call in ten minutes. A five-minute dry run turns a theoretical backup into one you can actually trust, and it tells you your real runtime under your real load rather than the number on a spec sheet.

To size the specific battery against your specific desk rather than a generic figure, add up the wattages of the equipment you actually run, decide how many hours of autonomy you want, and check the result with the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/), which lets you enter the load directly and see the capacity it implies. The number that comes back will almost always be smaller than you expected walking in, and that's the real point: a home office is not a heavy load pretending to be light, it's a genuinely light load that a modest, well-chosen battery can carry through the outages that would otherwise cost you a working day. Pair it with solar and the equation gets easier still, because a few hundred watts of panels can refill an office-sized battery faster than a laptop and a router can drain it — but even without panels, storage sized to the desk in front of you is one of the most cost-effective pieces of resilience a remote worker can buy.
