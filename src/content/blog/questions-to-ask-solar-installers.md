A solar sales meeting has a built-in information asymmetry: the person across the table has done this hundreds of times, and you have done it exactly never. Good questions are how you close that gap — not because the answers are secret, but because how an installer answers reveals far more than any brochure ever will. A company that welcomes pointed questions and answers them specifically is showing you, in real time, what its service will feel like when something goes wrong two years from now. A company that deflects, redirects to its national reputation, or pressures you toward a signature is showing you exactly the same thing. The questions below are grouped by what each one exposes, and for each it is worth knowing in advance what a strong, specific answer sounds like and what an evasive one sounds like, because the contrast is usually obvious the moment you are listening for it.

## Who is actually doing the work, and how good is the design

Start with the company itself, because a warranty is only as real as the entity standing behind it. Ask who holds the contractor license for this job and who will actually be on your roof. A great many solar brands are sales organizations that subcontract the physical installation to whoever is available, and that is not automatically bad — but you deserve to know whose license, whose insurance, and whose crew, because those are the people who determine build quality and the ones you will be chasing if the roof leaks. A good answer names the licensed installing entity plainly and describes the crew relationship without squirming. An evasive answer pivots to the strength of the national brand, which is not the thing that flashes your roof penetrations. Follow it with how long that specific entity has been installing in your area and how many systems it has completed, and pay attention to the phrase "under the same legal name," because the industry has a well-documented pattern of companies dissolving and re-forming, shedding their warranty obligations in the process. Longevity under an unchanged name is worth more than a big number attached to a brand that has been reincarnated twice. Then ask, without apology, to see proof of license, bonding, and insurance. A professional outfit produces these without friction, and you can verify the license against your state's contractor board yourself in about five minutes. The full vetting sequence — references, review patterns, the specific red flags — is laid out in [choosing a solar installer](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/), and it is worth running in parallel with these conversations rather than after them.

From the company, move to the design, and here one question outperforms all the others: what assumptions are behind your production estimate? This is the single most revealing technical thing you can ask, because a credible answer has visible machinery behind it. It names the modeling inputs — your roof's azimuth and pitch, a shade analysis drawn from actual measurements or imagery rather than a glance, local weather data, and a stated system-loss assumption — and a weak answer is a confident round number with no reasoning you can inspect. Cross-check whatever annual kilowatt-hour figure they give you against an independent baseline from the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/); an estimate that diverges from your own by more than roughly 10 percent is not necessarily wrong, but it is something the installer should be able to explain, and the explanation itself tells you a lot. Push further by asking for the year-one production estimate alongside the year-25 estimate. Panels degrade slowly and predictably — commonly modeled at something like 0.4 to 0.7 percent a year — so an installer who quotes only year-one output and then attaches it to a 25-year financial projection is quietly inflating the math, while one who shows you the whole degradation curve is doing the arithmetic honestly. Ask, too, why this particular equipment for this particular roof, because you are not testing whether the salesperson can recite panel brands; you are testing whether the design was fitted to your house. "This panel-and-inverter combination handles your east-west split and works around the chimney shade" is a design. "This is simply the best panel on the market" is a slogan, and the difference between the two is the difference between an engineer and a closer. One last design question saves a great deal of mid-project pain: will my main electrical panel need an upgrade, and if so, is that already priced into this quote? A service panel upgrade is a frequent source of surprise charges, and you want the answer in writing either way.

Two more questions in this cluster save real aggravation. Ask what the full timeline looks like from signed contract to the day the meter is approved to switch on, broken into its stages — design, permitting, installation, inspection, and utility interconnection — because the install itself is often the shortest part, and a company that gives you a crisp stage-by-stage answer has clearly done this many times in your jurisdiction. A vague "a few weeks" usually means they have not, or that they are quietly hiding a slow local permitting office. Then ask who handles the permit and the interconnection paperwork, and whether those costs are included in the quote. Reputable installers manage both as a matter of course; an outfit that expects you to chase permits, or that leaves interconnection fees off the quote, is either inexperienced or setting up a later charge. These logistics questions rarely feel dramatic in the moment, but the difference between a company that has the local process down cold and one improvising it on your roof is measured in weeks of delay and a stack of surprises you will feel keenly once you are the one living through them.

## The money questions that separate honest quotes from padded ones

The price conversation is where evasiveness costs you the most, so it deserves the bluntest questions. Ask for the total gross price and, immediately, what that works out to per watt — then do the division yourself, because it is the number that makes otherwise incomparable quotes comparable. Divide the gross price by the system's watts and hold the result against the broad US planning band of roughly $2.50 to $4.00 per watt before incentives. A quote inside that band is unremarkable; one far outside it, high or low, needs a specific story rather than a shrug. [Cost per watt](/blog/solar-cost-per-watt/) explains what legitimately pushes a system around inside that band — premium equipment, a genuinely difficult roof, a high-cost local market — so you can tell a justified premium from padding. The point of computing it yourself is that you stop negotiating against a lump sum you cannot evaluate and start negotiating against a unit price you can.

If you are financing, the most important question you will ask all day is what the dealer fee is, and what the cash price would be instead. Many solar loans carry a dealer fee — frequently 15 to 30 percent of the entire project — folded invisibly into the financed amount as the mechanism that buys down the advertised interest rate to something that sounds attractive. The gap between the cash price and the financed price is that fee, sitting in plain sight once you know to ask for both numbers. An installer who states the fee plainly is being straight with you, and one who insists there is no difference between cash and financed pricing while the loan paperwork quietly says otherwise has answered a different, easier question than the one you asked. On a system in the low twenties of thousands, a dealer fee can add four or five thousand dollars that buys you not a single additional watt, which is why this one question can be worth more than every other negotiation combined. Close the money section by asking which incentives you are actually eligible for, and who files what. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is worth 30 percent of qualifying cost and is claimed on your own tax return, which means no installer can guarantee your personal tax situation will support it — a pitch that treats the credit as an automatic, guaranteed discount is overpromising. Be especially wary of any pitch that folds in programs sounding too generous to be true, or that names incentives without naming the program; ask for the program's actual name and go look it up yourself before you let it change the price you are willing to pay.

Two more money questions belong here because they catch the traps that live in the contract rather than the pitch. Ask what happens to the price if the scope changes mid-project — the change-order policy — because a discovery like a weak roof rafter or an undersized service panel can turn into an open-ended charge if the contract does not define how such surprises get priced. A straight answer describes how change orders are quoted and approved before any extra work proceeds; a shrug is a warning that the final number could drift well above the one you signed. Then ask, plainly, whether this is a purchase, a loan, a lease, or a power purchase agreement, and make the salesperson name which one, because the four are radically different financial products that a pitch can blur together. Ownership, whether by cash or an honest loan, is what earns you the federal credit and the full savings; a lease or a power purchase agreement hands those to a third party in exchange for a lower or zero upfront cost and a long-term contract whose escalator and transferability terms you need to read closely, especially if you might sell the house before it ends.

If a production guarantee is part of the offer, interrogate it rather than admire it. A guarantee that the system will produce a stated number of kilowatt-hours sounds reassuring, but its value lives entirely in the remedy: what, specifically, does the company pay you if production falls short, how is that shortfall measured, and for how many years does the promise run? A guarantee with a vague or toothless remedy is decoration. A guarantee that pays you the difference at your retail rate, measured against honest weather-adjusted modeling, is worth something real. The same skepticism applies to any savings guarantee, which depends on future rates and your own usage and is therefore far harder to stand behind than a production number the company actually controls. Ask what backs the promise and who administers it, and you will quickly learn whether it is a genuine commitment or just a closing line.

## Warranties, service, and reading the answers side by side

Warranties are where the fine print earns its keep, and the right question is to have the installer walk you through the three separate ones, because there almost always are three and they have different owners. The panel product warranty comes from the manufacturer and commonly runs 12 to 25 years, covering defects in the panel itself. The panel performance warranty also comes from the manufacturer, typically around 25 years, and guarantees the panel will not degrade faster than a stated curve. The third is the one that matters most for the thing you actually worry about, and it is the one people forget to ask about: the workmanship warranty, which comes from the installer, not the manufacturer, and covers their labor, the racking, and the roof penetrations. It is the workmanship warranty that pays when a penetration leaks, and it is only ever as good as the company standing behind it — which loops straight back to the longevity questions from the start. Make the scenario concrete: ask exactly who fixes it and who pays if your roof leaks at a mounting point in year six. A clear, confident answer with a named process is reassuring; hesitation there is worth more than any glossy warranty certificate. While you are on the subject of the years after installation, ask what monitoring you get and who actually watches it. Some companies actively monitor their fleets and will call you about underperformance before you notice; most leave the watching entirely to you. Either arrangement can work, but you should know which one you are buying, and you should insist that the monitoring account be in your own name with full access, so that a falling-out with the installer never locks you out of your own system's data. Round it out by asking what a service call costs after the workmanship warranty expires and what their current response time is for warranty work, because present-tense service behavior is the best available predictor of future warranty behavior.

One warranty scenario is worth raising precisely because salespeople rarely do: what happens if a manufacturer or the installer goes out of business. Ask who honors the panel product and performance warranties if the panel maker folds, and who covers the workmanship warranty if the installing company dissolves — the honest answer acknowledges that a defunct manufacturer's warranty is often worth little, which is exactly why the installer's track record and financial stability matter as much as the paper. Some installers carry third-party warranty insurance that survives the company's own failure; ask whether yours does. Then insist that every promise which made it into the sales conversation makes it into the written contract, because a warranty term, a production estimate, an included panel upgrade, or a price that lives only in a salesperson's spoken assurance is worth nothing on the day you need it. Read the contract for what it obligates the company to do, not for what the meeting made you feel, and treat any reluctance to put a verbal promise in writing as the clearest possible answer about whether that promise was ever real. The paperwork is where the relationship actually lives once the crew has driven away, and every question above is only as good as your willingness to see its answer committed to the document you sign rather than the pitch you heard.

None of these questions does its full job in isolation; their power comes from asking the same ones of at least three companies and laying the answers side by side. Do not compare only the prices — compare the production estimates against each dollar quoted, the warranty terms, and, above all, the specificity of the answers themselves, because specificity is the trait that most reliably predicts how the installation actually goes. Normalize every quote to a price per watt so the totals are genuinely comparable, sanity-check them with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), and read the fine print against the structured checklist in [comparing solar quotes line by line](/blog/compare-solar-quotes/), which catches the contract terms these interview questions can miss. Two patterns are worth trusting once the answers are in front of you. The first is encouraging: the company that answered your hardest questions with the most specificity is very often the one whose install goes the same way, precisely and without drama. The second is a warning that overrides everything else on the page: any answer, on any topic, that requires you to sign today to keep the price is a reason to walk, not a reason to hurry. Real numbers from a real company survive a week of you thinking about them, and a discount that evaporates overnight was never a discount — it was a countdown clock aimed at your judgment.
