If you live in a state with an SREC market, your solar array is doing two jobs at once. It is cutting your electric bill by making power you would otherwise buy, and it is minting a tradeable certificate for every megawatt-hour it generates, a certificate that utilities in your state are required to buy to meet their renewable obligations. That second job is real money, sometimes a substantial income stream over the years, but unlike the bill savings, it does not happen automatically. You have to register your system, get the certificates issued, and then actually sell them, and there are several ways to do that with meaningfully different tradeoffs. Homeowners routinely leave this money on the table simply because nobody walked them through the mechanics. Here is how the process actually works, from getting registered to choosing how you sell.

## Getting registered and certified so certificates can be issued

Before you can sell a single certificate, your system has to be recognized by the body that issues them, and this registration step is where most of the friction and most of the missed money lives. An SREC, a Solar Renewable Energy Certificate, is generated for each megawatt-hour, that is, each 1,000 kilowatt-hours, that your system produces, but the certificate only exists once an official tracking system credits it to your account. That means you, or someone acting for you, has to register the system with the appropriate state or regional tracking authority and get it certified as eligible before the meter has any official standing. If you never register, your panels still make power and still cut your bill, but the certificates simply never get created, and years of potential SREC income evaporate quietly. This is the single most important thing to get right, and the most common thing owners get wrong, usually by assuming it happens on its own or that their installer handled it when nobody confirmed that they did.

Registration involves a few pieces. You establish that the system is in an eligible state and meets the program's rules, you provide documentation about the system, its size, location, install date, and equipment, and you set up a way for production to be reported into the tracking system so certificates can be issued as power is generated. Some programs certify based on your existing production meter or monitoring; others may require specific metering. The details vary by state and program, and they are exactly the sort of thing worth confirming rather than guessing, but the through-line is that certification is a prerequisite and it has a paperwork burden. Many installers in SREC states will handle or help with registration as part of the sale, and it is a fair and important question to ask before you sign: will you register my system for SRECs, and if not, who does and when? If the answer is that you are on your own, budget the time to do it promptly after the system goes live, because certificates are generally issued based on production going forward, and delay can mean forfeiting the credits for power you have already made.

Once registered and certified, your account starts accumulating certificates as your system produces, and only then do you have something to sell. It helps at this stage to understand what these certificates fundamentally are and where their value comes from, because that shapes every selling decision that follows; our primer on [what SRECs are and how you earn certificates for the power your panels make](/blog/srecs-explained/) covers the underlying market mechanism, the fact that utilities buy them to meet a legal renewable requirement, and why their price rises and falls with supply and demand in your state. That price volatility is the crux of the selling decision, because unlike a fixed rebate, an SREC's value is a market price that moves, sometimes a lot, and how you sell determines how much of that movement you are exposed to.

It is also worth situating SREC income within your broader picture, because it is one layer among several that combine to make solar pencil out in the states that offer it. SRECs stack on top of the federal tax credit, net metering, and any other applicable incentives, and understanding how these layers combine without cannibalizing each other is its own useful exercise, laid out in our guide to [how credits, rebates, and exemptions stack together](/blog/stacking-solar-incentives/). For a homeowner in an SREC state, the certificate income can be the difference-maker that turns a marginal payback into a good one, which is why it is worth folding the expected SREC revenue into your return calculation from the start; running your system through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) and adding a conservative estimate of annual SREC income on top of your bill savings gives a fuller picture of the return than bill savings alone.

## Spot sales, brokers, aggregators, and long-term contracts

With certified certificates accumulating, you face the actual selling decision, and there are essentially two philosophies: sell into the market as certificates are issued and take whatever the going price is, or lock in a price ahead of time through a longer contract. Each has a clear tradeoff between potential upside and certainty, and the right choice depends mostly on your appetite for price risk. Selling on the spot market means you sell certificates at the current market price whenever you have them, capturing today's price whatever it happens to be. When prices are high, this is the most lucrative path; when prices sag, you sell into the sag. It offers the most upside and the most volatility, and it requires either doing the transactions yourself through a trading platform or, far more commonly for a homeowner, working through an intermediary who handles the sales for you.

What moves an SREC's price is worth understanding structurally, because it explains why the same certificate can be worth wildly different amounts in different states and years without ever quoting a figure. Demand comes from a legal requirement: the state's renewable portfolio standard sets a solar carve-out, a share of electricity that suppliers must cover with solar certificates, and that required share generally scales up year over year. Supply is the certificates that all the solar systems in the state actually generate. When installations lag the rising requirement, certificates are scarce and prices firm up toward their ceiling; when a building boom floods the state with panels faster than the requirement climbs, supply outruns demand and prices soften. The ceiling itself is set by a penalty the state charges suppliers who fall short, often called an alternative compliance payment, which functions as the most a rational buyer would ever pay for a certificate, since no one pays more for an SREC than the fine it lets them avoid. That penalty-defined ceiling and the supply-against-requirement balance are the two structural forces behind every price you will be quoted, and they are why a market can be rich one year and thin a few years later. The dollar levels move; the mechanism does not.

That intermediary is usually a broker or an aggregator, and the distinction matters. A broker acts as a middleman who sells your certificates on the market on your behalf, typically taking a fee or commission, and you generally still get exposure to the market price, minus their cut. An aggregator pools the certificates from many small residential systems together and manages the selling for the whole pool, which gives small producers access to a market that can be awkward to enter individually and often smooths out some of the transaction friction, though it too comes with fees and sometimes with terms about how and when you get paid. The practical reality for most homeowners is that you will not be trading certificates yourself; you will sign up with a broker or aggregator, and the comparison worth making is on their fees, how transparent they are about the prices they get you, whether they offer a spot arrangement or a fixed one, and how and when they pay out. Reading those terms with the same care you would give any financial contract is the difference between capturing most of your certificates' value and handing a large slice to a middleman.

The alternative to riding the market is a long-term contract, where you agree to sell your certificates at a fixed price for a set number of years, often arranged through an aggregator or a company that wants a predictable supply. The appeal is certainty: you know exactly what your SREC income will be, which makes your solar return predictable and protects you if market prices fall. The cost of that certainty is upside; if prices rise, you are locked in below the market and someone else captures the gain. A fixed contract typically prices at a discount to the current spot price precisely because you are buying insurance against a drop, and the counterparty is taking on the price risk you are shedding. Whether that trade is worth it depends on how volatile your state's market is and how much you value predictable income. A homeowner who wants their solar return nailed down, and who would lose sleep watching certificate prices swing, is often well served by a fair long-term contract even at a discount. A homeowner comfortable with risk who believes prices will hold or rise may do better on the spot market. There is no universally correct answer; there is only the tradeoff between certainty and upside, and your own tolerance for it.

A rough illustration ties the income to your system size, with the price left deliberately as a placeholder, because real SREC prices vary enormously by state and year and are not something to quote as a fact. Since one certificate is minted per megawatt-hour, an assumed system producing 8 megawatt-hours a year, that is 8,000 kWh, generates about 8 certificates annually. Plug in an arbitrary illustrative price of, say, $50 per certificate and that is roughly $400 a year on top of your bill savings; plug in $200 and it is $1,600; plug in $15 and it is a modest $120. The spread across those placeholder prices is the whole point: the same array's certificate income can be a rounding error or a meaningful second revenue stream depending entirely on where your state's market sits, which is why you check the current market for your state rather than bank on any number, and why a conservative estimate belongs in your payback math rather than an optimistic one. Scale the certificate count with your own expected annual production, apply whatever your state's market is actually paying at the time you sell, and you have a grounded figure rather than a guess.

State context colors all of this, because SREC markets differ enormously in size, price level, and stability from one state to the next, and a strategy that makes sense in a robust, high-priced market may not in a thin, soft one. New Jersey, for example, has been one of the more developed solar certificate markets in the country, and the way its program is structured directly shapes what a homeowner there can expect to earn and how they should sell; our look at [how New Jersey's performance certificates change the math](/blog/solar-panels-in-new-jersey/) shows a specific market in enough detail to illustrate how much the state you are in drives the decision. Wherever you are, the sequence is the same. Confirm your state has a market and register and certify your system promptly so certificates actually get issued, understand that their value is a moving market price rather than a fixed payout, choose an honest broker or aggregator and read their fee terms closely, and then decide between the upside-and-volatility of spot sales and the certainty of a long-term contract based on how much price risk you want to carry. Do that, and the second job your panels are quietly doing turns into real income rather than certificates that were never claimed.
