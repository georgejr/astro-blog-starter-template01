The moment you drop the gas line, every kilowatt-hour your household burns lands on a single bill. No more splitting energy between a gas meter that quietly heats the house and warms the water, and an electric meter that runs everything else. It all rides on one number now, and that number can look alarming the first winter — not because an all-electric home wastes energy, but because you're finally seeing the whole picture in one place instead of two.

The honest answer to "what does it cost" is that it depends on four things: how cold your winters get, how big and leaky the house is, whether you drive on electricity too, and what your utility charges per kilowatt-hour. Those variables swing the monthly figure more than any appliance choice does. A tight 1,500-square-foot house in a mild coastal climate might run on 700 to 900 kWh a month year-round. The same square footage in a cold, poorly insulated house heated by electric resistance can pull three or four times that in January. So rather than quote you a single number that would be wrong for most readers, it's more useful to build the bill up load by load, show you the math, and let you swap in your own climate and rate.

## Building the monthly bill load by load

Start with the loads that exist in every home whether it burns gas or not: refrigerator, lights, laundry, dishwasher, electronics, and the dozens of small always-on devices that make up your baseline. For a typical household this cluster runs somewhere around 250 to 400 kWh a month and barely changes with the seasons. It's the floor your bill never drops below. If you want to see where that baseline hides, the appliances [quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) are rarely the ones people suspect — it's the steady, unglamorous loads, not the occasional big ones, that dominate.

Now layer on the loads that gas used to handle, because this is where an all-electric home earns or loses its reputation. Space heating is the giant. A heat pump is the reason all-electric homes are viable in cold climates at all: instead of turning one unit of electricity into one unit of heat the way a baseboard or space heater does, a modern cold-climate heat pump moves two to three units of heat for every unit of electricity it draws. That efficiency — expressed as a coefficient of performance, or COP — is the single biggest lever on your winter bill. Assume a house that needs roughly 40 kWh of delivered heat on a cold January day. With electric resistance heat at a COP of 1.0, that's 40 kWh straight off the meter. With a heat pump averaging a COP of 2.5 across the month, the same comfort costs about 16 kWh. Over a 30-day cold stretch that's the difference between 1,200 kWh and 480 kWh just for heating — hundreds of dollars, from the same thermostat setting. If you're weighing the swap, running your own numbers through a [heat pump cost calculator](/heat-pump-calculator/) is worth more than any rule of thumb, because the delivered-heat requirement depends entirely on your climate and envelope.

Water heating is the next tier down. A heat-pump water heater sips electricity the same way a space heat pump does, typically drawing 250 to 400 kWh a month for a family of four depending on hot-water habits and how cold the incoming water is. Swap in a standard electric-resistance tank and that figure roughly doubles, because resistance heating has no efficiency multiplier to lean on. Cooking is almost a rounding error by comparison — an induction or electric range for a household that cooks most nights adds maybe 30 to 60 kWh a month, real but small next to heating. Electric drying adds another 40 to 80 kWh depending on how many loads you run and whether it's a heat-pump dryer or a conventional one.

Put those pieces together for a concrete case. Assume a moderately insulated 2,000-square-foot house in a cold-but-not-brutal climate, a family of four, a cold-climate heat pump, a heat-pump water heater, induction cooking, and a standard electric dryer. In a mild shoulder month — April or October — heating and cooling are near zero, so the bill is baseline plus water heating plus cooking and laundry: call it 350 + 300 + 50 + 60, roughly 760 kWh. At an assumed all-in rate of $0.18/kWh, that's about $137. In the depth of winter, space heating dominates and the same house might pull 350 baseline + 700 heating + 350 water + 50 cooking + 60 laundry, close to 1,510 kWh, or about $272. A brutal cold snap, or a leakier house, easily pushes a winter month past 1,800 kWh. Summer sits in between: cooling is far cheaper than heating because you're only fighting a 15–20 degree gap instead of a 50-degree one, so a hot-climate summer month often lands around 900 to 1,200 kWh. The [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) lets you plug your own kWh estimate and utility rate into that framework to get a figure that actually reflects where you live.

The variable doing the most damage to any single estimate is the building envelope — how well the house holds the heat it makes. The same heat pump, in the same climate, at the same thermostat setting, can produce wildly different winter bills depending on whether it's heating a tight, well-insulated, air-sealed house or a drafty one with single-pane windows and a leaky attic. That's because the heat pump doesn't set the demand; the house does. A leaky envelope loses heat faster, so the pump runs longer and draws more, and no efficiency rating on the equipment can fully compensate for a house that hemorrhages warmth through its walls. This is why two neighbors with identical mechanical systems can see winter bills that differ by 50% or more, and why the highest-value pre-electrification move is often not choosing a fancier heat pump but sealing and insulating the shell first. Every kilowatt-hour of demand you eliminate at the envelope is one the heat pump never has to serve. If your house is old and leaky, budget toward the high end of the ranges above until you've addressed the envelope; if it's newer and tight, the low end is realistic.

## Why the total often beats the two bills it replaced

Homeowners fixate on the winter peak, and it's understandable — watching a single bill cross $270 feels worse than paying $150 for electricity and $120 for gas separately, even when the totals are close. But the comparison that matters isn't your new electric bill against your old electric bill. It's your new all-electric total against the *sum* of your old gas and electric bills, plus the fixed monthly connection charge the gas utility levied whether you used much or not. That standing charge — often $10 to $20 a month just for the privilege of having a gas meter — disappears entirely when you cut the line, and it's pure savings twelve months a year regardless of what you're heating with.

Whether the all-electric total comes out ahead depends heavily on the ratio between your local gas price and your local electricity price, and that ratio varies wildly across the country. In regions with cheap natural gas and expensive electricity, a well-insulated home with a high-efficiency gas furnace can be cheaper to run on gas for heating alone — the heat pump's efficiency edge isn't always enough to overcome a 3-to-1 price gap between the two fuels per unit of delivered energy. In regions where electricity is moderately priced and gas is not especially cheap, the heat pump's COP advantage wins clearly, and going all-electric lowers the combined annual cost. The break-even isn't about the technology being good or bad; it's arithmetic between two fuel prices, and you can only settle it with your own utility's numbers.

Then there's the electric vehicle, which changes the conversation entirely because it replaces a cost that never touched either home-energy bill: gasoline. An EV driven 12,000 miles a year at roughly 3.5 miles per kWh needs about 3,400 kWh annually, or somewhere near 285 kWh a month, though it clusters around whenever you charge rather than spreading evenly. At $0.18/kWh that's about $51 a month of "fuel." The number looks like a big addition to your electric bill — and it is, often the single largest new load after winter heating — but it's displacing a gasoline bill that might have been $130 to $180 a month for the same driving. Counting the EV's electricity as a pure increase to your utility bill, without subtracting the gas-station spending it eliminated, is the most common way people convince themselves electrification costs more than it does. If you drive on those electrons, the fair comparison folds fuel savings into the ledger, and the math usually tilts further toward all-electric. The same logic runs through the economics of [solar savings for an all-electric home](/blog/solar-savings-all-electric-home/), where every load you've moved onto the meter becomes a load solar can offset directly.

The practical takeaway for anyone budgeting an all-electric home is to plan around the annual total and the winter peak separately. The annual total is what determines whether electrification saved you money; budget for it as a smoothed monthly average, which for the moderately-insulated example above lands somewhere around $180 to $200 a month before any EV or solar. The winter peak is a cash-flow event, not a cost problem — those two or three high-bill months are baked into any heating fuel, gas included, and many utilities offer level-billing plans that spread them across the year so the January shock never arrives. What you don't want to do is judge the whole decision by a single February statement, because that's the one month the arithmetic looks worst and the one month it's least representative of the year you'll actually live in. Pairing electrification with rooftop generation is where the numbers get genuinely interesting, and [heat pumps and solar together](/blog/heat-pump-solar-savings/) tend to compound: the heat pump shrinks the load, and solar covers a large share of what's left.
