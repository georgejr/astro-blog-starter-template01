People frame this as a contest — solar versus generator, pick the winner — but the two are built for different failure modes. A fuel generator is a runtime machine: give it gas or propane and it will keep your house lit for as long as the tank lasts. A solar-plus-battery system is a resilience-and-value machine: it rides through outages silently and automatically, and it does useful work every single day the grid is up. Deciding between them starts with an honest answer to how your grid actually fails.

## First, a clarification about "solar"

A rooftop of panels, by itself, does nothing for you in a blackout. Grid-tied solar is required by safety rules to shut down the instant the grid goes dark, so that it can't energize lines that utility crews believe are dead. Without a battery (or specific islanding hardware), your panels go dark right along with the neighborhood — a surprise that catches many new owners off guard.

So the real comparison isn't solar versus generator. It's **solar paired with a battery** versus a **fuel generator**. Once framed that way, the trade-offs line up cleanly.

## The generator's superpower is unlimited runtime

The single thing a generator does better than any battery is run a long time. A battery holds a fixed amount of energy; when it's empty, it's empty until the sun or the grid refills it. A generator's ceiling is fuel, not stored energy. Keep feeding it and it will carry a house through a multi-day ice storm or a week-long regional outage without flinching.

That makes generators the natural choice where outages are rare but *long* — the kind of extended, grid-down events where a battery would deplete and a solar recharge might be throttled by short, cloudy winter days. If your worst-case scenario is "no power for four days," raw fuel-fed runtime is hard to beat.

The costs of that runtime are just as real:

- **Fuel logistics.** A portable unit needs gasoline stored and refreshed; a standby unit needs a propane tank or a natural-gas line. During a widespread outage, gas stations without power can't pump, which is exactly when you need fuel most.
- **Noise and fumes.** Generators are loud and must run outdoors, well clear of windows, because of carbon-monoxide risk. A battery is silent and safe indoors.
- **Maintenance.** Engines need oil changes, exercise cycles, and occasional repair. A standby generator that sits unused for a year may not start when you finally need it.
- **It earns nothing between outages.** A generator is pure insurance. For the 360-odd days a year the grid is fine, it's a depreciating asset sitting in the side yard.

The standby-versus-battery decision on its own terms is worked through in [standby generator or home battery](/blog/generator-vs-home-battery/), which is worth reading if backup is your only goal.

## The battery's superpower is that it's always working

A solar battery flips the generator's weakness into a strength. It kicks in the instant the grid drops — no startup delay, no walking outside in a storm, no fuel — and the transition is seamless enough that sensitive electronics never notice. Then, crucially, it keeps working when the lights are on:

- It stores cheap or self-generated daytime power to use in expensive evening hours, trimming your bill.
- On time-of-use rate plans it shifts consumption out of peak windows.
- Paired with panels, it raises how much of your own solar you actually keep instead of exporting cheaply.

That dual role is the whole argument. The same hardware that protects you during an outage also chips away at your electric bill the rest of the year, which is why a battery can be evaluated as an investment and not just an insurance premium. What actually happens the moment the grid fails — the switchover, what stays powered, how solar keeps recharging you — is detailed in [what happens when the grid goes down and you have a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/).

### The battery's limit, honestly stated

A battery's constraint is capacity. It holds a set number of kilowatt-hours, and heavy loads drain it faster. Whether it lasts a few hours or a couple of days depends entirely on what you ask it to run and whether the sun is refilling it each day — the arithmetic of that is spelled out in [how long a home battery can power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/). Pair a battery with solar and a sunny outage can stretch indefinitely as the panels recharge it by day; a stretch of dark winter storm days is where that recharge falters and the generator's fuel advantage reasserts itself.

## Matching the tool to your outages

The decision usually resolves along two axes: how long your outages run, and whether you want the equipment to do anything the other 99% of the time.

- **Frequent short outages, plus a desire for daily bill value:** solar and a battery. Silent, automatic, and it pays partial rent through everyday savings.
- **Rare but long multi-day outages, backup as the only goal:** a generator's fuel-fed runtime is the more literal fit, especially in cloudy winter climates where solar recharge is weak.
- **Both risks, and the budget to cover them:** some households run a battery for the seamless, silent, everyday coverage and keep a generator as the deep-reserve backstop for the once-a-decade week-long event. The battery handles the common case; the generator handles the tail.

If the everyday-value side appeals to you, the honest next step is to check whether the battery pencils out on more than resilience alone.
