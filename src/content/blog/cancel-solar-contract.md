The signature felt right in the moment — a salesperson at the kitchen table, a screen full of savings, a sense that the deal might vanish if you waited. A day later the certainty is gone and the questions arrive. Can you still get out? The answer is usually yes, but *how easily* depends almost entirely on timing and on which set of rules applies to your situation. Cancellation is far simpler in the first few days than it is a month in, and knowing where you stand on that timeline is the difference between a clean exit and a costly one.

## The cooling-off window: your strongest position

Federal consumer protection includes a cooling-off rule for sales made somewhere other than the seller's normal place of business — the classic example being a contract signed in your own home. For qualifying sales, it grants a right to cancel within **3 days**, with no penalty and no reason required, giving buyers a window to reconsider a deal made under the pressure of an in-person pitch. Because a great many residential solar contracts are signed exactly this way — at the homeowner's kitchen table — this protection reaches a large share of solar sales.

Several states layer additional or longer cancellation rights on top of the federal floor, and some have rules written specifically for home solicitation or solar sales. The structure varies by state, but the pattern is consistent: the law tends to protect buyers most in the days immediately after an in-home signing, precisely because that's the setting where high-pressure tactics do their work. The relevant point isn't a specific statute to memorize — it's that your window is widest right after you sign, and it narrows from there.

To use it, act in writing and act fast. Cancellation rules typically require written notice delivered within the window, so a dated email or letter beats a phone call you can't later prove. Keep a copy. The clock is short by design.

## After the window: the contract's own terms take over

Once the statutory cooling-off period closes, your ability to cancel — and what it costs — is governed by the contract you signed. This is where the fine print you skimmed becomes the whole story, and where outcomes diverge sharply depending on how far the project has progressed.

Early on, before any equipment is ordered or work begins, many agreements allow cancellation for little or nothing. As the installer sinks real costs into your project — engineering, permits, ordered hardware, scheduled crews — contracts increasingly provide for cancellation fees or forfeited deposits meant to cover those costs. The further along the project, the more you're likely to owe to walk away. The specific triggers and penalties live in the cancellation and default clauses, which is exactly why those sections deserve a careful read *before* signing, not after — the ones that matter most are flagged in [solar contract terms to read twice](/blog/solar-contract-terms/).

### Financing adds a second contract

If the system is financed, there may be two agreements in play: the installation contract and a separate loan or lease. Cancelling one doesn't automatically unwind the other, and the financing agreement carries its own cancellation terms and timelines. Anyone backing out of a financed deal needs to address both, or risk cancelling the install while remaining on the hook for the financing.

## Why people want out — and what to check first

Signer's remorse usually traces to one of a few sources, and it's worth diagnosing which, because it changes whether cancelling is the right move or just the first instinct.

- **Pressure, not fit.** If the sale hinged on urgency — a "today only" price, a deadline that felt artificial — that pressure itself is a signal worth heeding. Manufactured urgency is one of the tactics catalogued in [solar sales tactics that should make you pause](/blog/solar-sales-tactics-red-flags/), and it's a common reason a deal that felt right at 8 p.m. feels wrong by morning.
- **Numbers that don't hold up.** Sometimes the doubt is quantitative: the savings looked great in the pitch but thin out under scrutiny. Before cancelling outright, it's worth re-running the economics independently — the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) lets you rebuild the payback picture without the salesperson's assumptions, which either confirms the worry or dissolves it.
- **Better information, later.** Occasionally the issue is simply that you learned what to ask only after signing. Comparing the deal against the [questions you should have asked before signing anything](/blog/questions-to-ask-solar-installers/) can reveal whether the contract is genuinely flawed or merely unfamiliar.

If the deal is sound and only felt rushed, a conversation with the installer sometimes resolves it. If it's genuinely wrong for you, move on the cancellation while your window is open.

## Common questions

**Can I cancel after the cooling-off period ends?**
Usually yes, but the terms are set by your contract rather than by law, and there may be a cancellation fee or lost deposit that grows as the project progresses. Read the cancellation clause to know your exposure.

**Does cancelling the install also cancel my solar loan?**
Not automatically. A financed system involves a separate loan or lease agreement with its own cancellation terms. Handle both, and confirm each in writing.

**What if installation has already started?**
Your leverage shrinks once the installer has incurred real costs — permits, hardware, labor. Cancellation is still possible, but expect the contract to allocate those sunk costs to you. The specifics depend on the default and cancellation provisions you signed.

**How should I deliver a cancellation notice?**
In writing, dated, and within any applicable window, keeping a copy for yourself. A provable written notice protects you far better than a phone call.

## The takeaway on timing

The one principle that runs through all of this: your position is strongest the moment after you sign and weakens with every passing day and every step the project takes forward. If you're having doubts, don't sit on them. Read the cancellation terms, note whether an in-home signing gives you a cooling-off right, and act in writing while the window is open. A deal that turns out to be wrong is far cheaper to leave in the first few days than after the crew is on the calendar.
