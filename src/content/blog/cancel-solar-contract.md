The signature felt right in the moment — a salesperson at the kitchen table, a screen full of savings projections, and the quiet sense that the deal might evaporate if you slept on it. A day later that certainty is gone and the questions start arriving. Can you still get out? The answer is usually yes, but how easily depends almost entirely on two things: how much time has passed, and which set of rules applies to your particular situation. Cancellation is dramatically simpler in the first few days than it is a month in, and the reason is straightforward — early on the law is on your side, and later on your options narrow to whatever the contract you signed happens to allow. Knowing where you stand on that timeline is the whole difference between a clean, costless exit and an expensive fight over sunk costs.

## The cooling-off window: your strongest position

Federal consumer protection includes a cooling-off rule for sales made somewhere other than the seller's normal place of business — the classic example being a contract signed in your own home. For qualifying sales, it grants a right to cancel within **3 days**, with no penalty and no reason required. You don't have to justify the change of heart, negotiate, or prove the deal was bad; you simply exercise the right, and the sale unwinds. The rule exists precisely because a deal made under the pressure of an in-person pitch, in your living room, at the end of a long presentation, isn't the same as one you sought out and considered at arm's length. The law builds in a short window to reconsider a decision made in exactly that setting. And because a great many residential solar contracts are signed exactly that way — at the homeowner's kitchen table, after an evening sales visit — this protection reaches a large share of solar sales. If your contract was signed in your home rather than at the company's office, there's a strong chance you're covered.

Many states layer additional or longer cancellation rights on top of that federal floor, and some have rules written specifically for home solicitation sales or for solar contracts in particular. The exact structure varies from state to state, but the pattern underneath is remarkably consistent: the law tends to protect buyers most heavily in the days immediately after an in-home signing, because that's the setting where high-pressure tactics do their work and where a rushed decision is most likely. The relevant point for you isn't a specific statute number to memorize — those change and vary — it's the shape of the protection. Your window is widest right after you sign, and it narrows steadily from there. That single fact should govern how quickly you act if you're having doubts. Every day you wait is a day the strongest, simplest, no-questions-asked version of your exit gets closer to expiring, after which you fall back on the far messier world of contractual terms.

Using the cooling-off right correctly matters as much as having it, and the mechanics are simple but unforgiving. Act in writing, and act fast. Cancellation rules typically require written notice delivered within the window, which means a dated email or a letter beats a phone call you can't later prove happened. A verbal cancellation that the company later claims never occurred leaves you with nothing; a dated, saved, provable written notice leaves you with a record. Send it, keep a copy, and note the date and time you sent it. If the contract or the accompanying paperwork specifies a method or an address for cancellation notices, follow it exactly rather than improvising, because a technically defective notice can be treated as no notice at all. The clock here is genuinely short by design — the whole point of a cooling-off period is that it's brief — so the moment you decide you want out, the writing should go out the same day rather than after another night of thinking it over. There is no downside to sending a timely written cancellation and later changing your mind back; there is significant downside to missing the window while you deliberate.

## After the window, the contract takes over

Once the statutory cooling-off period closes, your ability to cancel — and what it costs you — is governed no longer by consumer-protection law but by the contract you actually signed. This is the point where the fine print you skimmed at the kitchen table becomes the entire story, and where outcomes diverge sharply depending on how far the project has progressed. Early on, before any equipment has been ordered or any work has begun, many agreements still allow cancellation for little or nothing, because the installer hasn't yet sunk real money into your specific project. But that grace doesn't last. As the installer starts incurring genuine costs on your behalf — engineering and design work, pulling permits, ordering hardware sized to your roof, scheduling crews — contracts increasingly provide for cancellation fees or forfeited deposits meant to recover those costs. The general rule is intuitive once you see the logic: the further along the project has moved, the more you're likely to owe to walk away from it, because the more the company has already spent that it can't recover. A cancellation the day after the cooling-off period ends is a very different financial event from a cancellation the week before the crew was scheduled to arrive.

The specific triggers and penalties live in the cancellation and default clauses of the contract, which is exactly why those sections deserve a careful read before you sign, not after you want out. They spell out what happens at each stage — what's refundable, what's forfeited, what fees attach once permits are filed or equipment is ordered — and they're among the most consequential provisions in the whole document precisely because they govern the one scenario where the relationship goes wrong. The clauses that matter most, and the language to watch for in them, are flagged in [solar contract terms to read twice](/blog/solar-contract-terms/). Reading them before signing costs you ten minutes; discovering them after signing, when you're trying to cancel, can cost you a deposit.

There's a second complication that catches financed buyers off guard: if the system is financed, there may be two separate agreements in play, not one. The installation contract is its own document, and the loan or lease is another, often with a different company entirely. Cancelling one does not automatically unwind the other. You can cancel the installation and still be legally on the hook for the financing, or find that the loan has its own cancellation terms and timelines that don't line up with the install contract's. Anyone backing out of a financed deal has to address both agreements deliberately — confirm in writing that each is cancelled, and don't assume that killing the installation makes the loan disappear. It's entirely possible to unwind the install while remaining bound to a loan for a system that will never be built, which is exactly the trap the two-contract structure sets for people who assume they're dealing with a single agreement.

## Diagnosing the doubt before you pull the trigger

Signer's remorse usually traces back to one of a few distinct sources, and it's worth figuring out which one you're feeling, because the diagnosis changes whether cancelling is the right move or merely the first instinct. The most common source is pressure rather than genuine misfit. If the sale hinged on urgency — a "today only" price, a rebate deadline that felt suspiciously artificial, a sense that hesitating would cost you — that pressure itself is a signal worth heeding, because manufactured urgency is a sales technique, not a real market condition. Legitimate solar pricing doesn't evaporate overnight. That particular tactic is one of several catalogued in [solar sales tactics that should make you pause](/blog/solar-sales-tactics-red-flags/), and it's a common reason a deal that felt right at eight in the evening feels wrong by morning: the pressure that produced the signature fades, and without it the decision looks different. A second source is quantitative — the numbers that dazzled in the pitch thin out under sober scrutiny. Before cancelling outright over the economics, it's worth re-running them independently, without the salesperson's assumptions baked in. The [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) lets you rebuild the payback picture from your own inputs, which either confirms the worry, in which case cancelling is justified, or dissolves it, in which case the deal may be fine and only felt rushed. A third source is simply better information arriving too late — you learned what you should have asked only after signing. Comparing the deal you signed against [the questions you should have asked before signing anything](/blog/questions-to-ask-solar-installers/) can reveal whether the contract is genuinely flawed or merely unfamiliar, which are very different conclusions that call for very different responses.

That distinction matters because cancelling isn't free once the cooling-off window closes, so it's worth being sure the problem is real. If the deal turns out to be fundamentally sound and only felt rushed, a direct conversation with the installer sometimes resolves the discomfort without cancelling at all — reputable companies would generally rather keep a reassured customer than fight a cancellation. If the deal is genuinely wrong for you, though, move on it decisively while your window is open, and deliver the cancellation the right way: in writing, dated, within any applicable window, following any method the contract specifies, and keeping a copy for yourself. A provable written notice protects you far better than a phone call the company can later deny receiving. The one principle that runs through every part of this is about timing. Your position is strongest the moment after you sign and weakens with every passing day and every step the project takes toward completion — every permit filed, every panel ordered, every crew scheduled shifts leverage away from you and toward the contract's penalty clauses. If you're having doubts, the worst thing you can do is sit on them. Read the cancellation terms, note whether an in-home signing gives you a cooling-off right, and act in writing while the window is still open. A deal that turns out to be wrong is far cheaper to leave in the first few days than after the crew is already on the calendar.
