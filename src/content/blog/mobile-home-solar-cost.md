Ask what solar costs on a manufactured home and the honest first answer is a question back: what's your roof made of, and do you own the land it sits on? For a conventional stick-built house, the roof is a given — a sloped assembly of rafters and decking that will hold panels without much thought. On a mobile or manufactured home, the roof is the whole conversation, and the land underneath is a close second. Get those two facts straight and the cost of going solar snaps into focus quickly. Skip them and you'll get quotes that seem to swing wildly for no reason, when in fact they're pricing two completely different installations — one bolted to your roof, one planted in your yard.

## Why the roof is where the money question really lives

The defining feature of a manufactured home is that it was built light, in a factory, to be transported down a highway. That engineering priority — keep it light enough to move — shapes the roof in ways that matter for solar. Many manufactured homes use lightweight roof trusses spaced and sized for the loads they were designed to carry, which did not originally include an array of glass-and-aluminum panels plus racking plus the wind and snow loads that come with them. Some manufactured roofs can carry solar without any trouble; others genuinely cannot support a rooftop array without reinforcement, and the only way to know is to have the structure evaluated. This is the single biggest way a manufactured-home solar project diverges from a stick-built one, and it's why a reputable installer will want engineering or structural documentation for your specific home before committing to a roof mount.

Then there's what the roof is made of and how panels attach to it. A lot of manufactured homes have metal roofs — often a low-slope or nearly flat metal panel roof — and metal is generally a friendly surface for solar, since panels can sometimes clamp to standing seams without penetrations, or attach with well-flashed mounts. But manufactured-home roofs vary enormously: some are the classic rounded, low-pitch metal roof, some are shallow-pitch shingle over a light deck, and some are membrane or rolled roofing. Each attachment method carries different labor and different risk. A low or nearly flat roof also changes the array's tilt — panels laid nearly flat produce less than optimally angled ones and are harder to keep clear of debris and snow — so an installer may propose tilt-up racking to angle the panels, which adds hardware, cost, and wind load back onto that light structure. The interplay between roof type, attachment, and how much the structure can bear is exactly why two manufactured homes on the same street can get very different roof-mount quotes.

Age and condition compound all of this. If the home is older and the roof is near the end of its life, mounting a twenty-five-year array on a roof with ten years left in it is a poor sequence — you'd be paying to remove and reinstall the panels when the roof needs replacing. The roofing question that every solar buyer faces is sharper on a manufactured home, where roofs are sometimes lighter-duty and the temptation to defer maintenance is real. And because manufactured homes are built to a federal HUD construction standard rather than to local residential codes, the permitting and inspection path for attaching solar can differ from a site-built house, which occasionally adds engineering steps and paperwork. None of this makes rooftop solar on a manufactured home impossible — plenty of these installs happen and work fine — but it explains why the roof, not the panels, is the variable that drives the quote, and why "can my roof even take it" is the first thing to resolve.

## When the answer is a ground mount

When the roof can't carry an array, or can't carry a well-angled one, the answer is often to skip the roof entirely and put the panels on the ground beside the home. A ground mount sidesteps every structural worry about the roof: the panels sit on a rack built into the earth, engineered from scratch for the exact wind and snow loads of your site, tilted to the ideal angle for production, and easy to reach for cleaning and maintenance. For a manufactured home with a marginal roof, a ground mount is frequently the cleaner engineering answer, and because you can orient and tilt it freely, it often produces more per panel than a compromised roof install would.

The trade-off is cost. A ground mount generally costs more than a comparable rooftop system because it involves work a roof doesn't: excavation and footings or ground screws, a steel or aluminum racking structure, a concrete or driven-pier foundation, and a trench to run wiring from the array back to the house. Those are real materials and real labor that a roof mount avoids by using the structure that's already there. The full breakdown of why putting panels on the ground costs more than putting them on a roof — footings, framing, trenching, and the rest — is worth understanding before you assume a ground mount is a bargain, and it's laid out in [why ground-mounted solar costs more than rooftop](/blog/ground-mount-solar-cost/). The upside that partly offsets the extra cost is production and longevity: an ideally angled, ground-level array in full sun can outproduce a flat or shaded roof mount and is far easier to service, so the higher price buys real performance and access.

Here's where the second big question — do you own the land? — becomes decisive, and it's the one that catches people off guard. If you own both the home and the lot it sits on, a ground mount is straightforward: it's your land, and you can build on it subject to permits. But a large share of manufactured homes sit in land-lease communities, where the homeowner owns the structure but rents the pad from a park. In that situation you generally cannot install a permanent ground mount — or even, in many parks, a rooftop system — without the park owner's permission, and permanent improvements to land you don't own raise obvious complications. Some park owners allow it; many don't; and the lease terms govern. This is not a minor footnote. For a renter-of-land, solar may be limited to portable or removable options, or may not be practical at all, and it's the first thing to confirm before spending time on quotes. Ownership of the land beneath the home quietly determines whether the ground-mount option — the one that solves most manufactured-home roof problems — is even on the table.

There's a middle situation worth naming, because it catches people who own their land but on a smaller lot: the ground mount has to physically fit somewhere with good sun. A ground array needs an unshaded patch of yard, ideally to the south, set far enough from the home and trees to avoid shadows across the day, with room for the racking footprint and a maintenance path around it. On a generous rural lot that's no obstacle; on a tight lot hemmed in by neighbors, outbuildings, or mature trees, the good solar real estate at ground level can be scarcer than it looks. It's worth walking the property with the sun in mind before assuming a ground mount is available — sometimes the roof, marginal as it is, turns out to be the only spot with clean exposure, which pushes the decision back toward reinforcing the structure rather than planting panels in the yard.

## What it costs, and how to size a system that fits

Manufactured homes are usually smaller and use less electricity than large stick-built houses, which is genuinely good for the cost side: you often need a smaller system, and smaller systems cost less in absolute dollars even when the per-watt price runs a touch higher. Installed residential solar has broadly landed somewhere in the range of $2.50 to $4.00 per watt before incentives in recent years, with smaller systems and more complex installs tending toward the upper end of that band and larger, simpler ones toward the lower end. A manufactured home that needs only a modest array — enough to cover a smaller bill — might land in the lower kilowatt range, so the total sticker can be considerably less than the figures you see quoted for big suburban houses. The dynamics of pricing a small system, where fixed costs like permitting and the truck roll get spread across fewer watts, mirror what happens on any [small home going solar](/blog/solar-cost-small-house/), and it's why cost-per-watt often creeps up as system size drops even though the total price falls.

Because the roof and land questions swing the price so much, the way to get a real number is to separate the two decisions. First, size the system to your actual usage — not your roof, not a guess — because that's what determines how many panels you need and therefore the bulk of the cost. A [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) that starts from your electricity usage tells you the array size to aim for, and for a smaller manufactured home that number is often reassuringly modest. Then price that system under both scenarios — roof mount if your structure supports it, ground mount if it doesn't or if the roof is marginal — using a [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) to see how the mounting choice moves the total. Comparing those two paths side by side, with your real system size plugged in, turns a confusing spread of quotes into a clear either/or, and it keeps an installer from steering you toward the more expensive mount without justification. It's also worth checking your figures against [typical installed solar costs](/blog/average-solar-panel-cost/) so you know whether a manufactured-home quote is in a reasonable range or padded for the perceived complexity.

One piece of good news on the incentive side: a manufactured home you live in generally qualifies for the same federal Residential Clean Energy Credit as any other home. That credit is 30 percent of the eligible cost of the solar system, claimed on your federal taxes, and it applies to a home you use as a residence — it isn't limited to conventionally built houses. The IRS guidance on the credit is the authority on eligibility and what counts as a qualifying residence, and it's worth confirming your situation there rather than relying on an installer's summary, but the headline is that going solar on a manufactured home doesn't forfeit the main federal incentive. The credit reduces the effective cost by nearly a third, which matters most on the smaller systems typical of these homes, where every dollar of upfront cost weighs heavily on the payback. Between a modest system size, a potentially friendly metal roof or a freely angled ground mount, and the same federal credit everyone else gets, solar on a manufactured home can pencil out well — as long as you answer the roof and land questions honestly up front, because those two facts, far more than the panels themselves, decide what it costs.
