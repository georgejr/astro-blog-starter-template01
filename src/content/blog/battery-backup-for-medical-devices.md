When a household depends on medical equipment, a power outage stops being an inconvenience and becomes a genuine risk, and the question of backup power changes character entirely. For most homes, sizing a battery is an exercise in comfort and economics — how long can we keep the fridge cold and the lights on, and is it worth the cost. For a home running a CPAP every night, an oxygen concentrator around the clock, or other life-supporting gear, it's an exercise in guaranteeing that a specific device keeps running for a specific number of hours no matter what the grid does. That shift in stakes should change how you approach the whole problem. You don't size for "probably fine." You size for the worst realistic outage, with margin, and you make sure the power actually reaches the device when the grid drops.

The good news is that the arithmetic is very approachable, because a medical device draws a knowable, steady amount of power, and a battery stores a knowable amount of energy. The two numbers meet in a simple division that tells you how many hours of runtime you'll get. A CPAP is a famously light load, so a modest home battery can carry it through many nights; a low-draw unit can run for more than ten nights on a single 10-kilowatt-hour battery. An oxygen concentrator is a much heavier, continuous load, so the same battery might carry it for a day or so rather than weeks. Everything starts with measuring the real draw of your specific equipment, because guessing here is exactly the kind of error you don't want to make when the stakes are health rather than convenience.

## Measure the real draw of every device you can't do without

Before you think about batteries at all, build an honest list of the medical equipment that must keep running during an outage, and find the actual power each one draws — not the label's peak rating, but its real running wattage in the mode you use it. This matters because medical devices vary enormously in their appetite, and the difference determines whether your plan needs to cover hours or days. A CPAP without a heated humidifier or heated tubing typically draws only 30 to 60 watts while running, which is why it's such a manageable load; turn on the heated humidifier and heated hose and that can climb past 100 watts, sometimes well past, so the humidifier setting alone can double or triple the energy the machine uses over a night. An oxygen concentrator is in a different class entirely: a home unit often draws 300 to 600 watts continuously, all day and all night, because it's compressing and separating air without pause. Powered wheelchairs, nebulizers, feeding pumps, dialysis equipment, and monitors each have their own profile, and some run intermittently while others run constantly.

The reliable way to get these numbers is to check each device's documentation for its running watts and, better still, measure it with an inexpensive plug-in power meter over a real period of use, since manufacturer ratings often state a maximum that the device rarely sustains. For a CPAP, measure a full night in your normal configuration, humidifier and all, so you capture the energy it truly consumes rather than the idealized minimum. For a concentrator or any always-on device, the running wattage times 24 gives you a daily energy figure that you'll lean on heavily, because a continuous load is what drains a battery fast. Write down, for each device, both its running watts (which determines whether the battery's inverter can even supply it) and its energy per relevant period — per night for a CPAP, per day for a concentrator. Those two numbers, watts and watt-hours, are the entire basis for sizing, and getting them from real measurement rather than guesswork is the most important step in the whole process.

It's worth separating the truly non-negotiable devices from the merely nice-to-have ones at this stage, because the size and cost of the battery scale directly with how much you ask it to carry. A CPAP is a comfort-and-health device that, for many users, could tolerate a night or two of interruption in a true emergency, though for others it's essential; an oxygen concentrator often cannot be interrupted at all. Being clear-eyed about which devices are life-critical, which are important, and which can wait lets you size a battery around a defensible core load rather than an inflated wish list, and it mirrors the discipline of building a backup plan around a genuine essentials list, which is the whole approach in [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/). For a medical household, the essentials list simply has different, higher-priority members — the medical gear goes to the top, above even the fridge — but the method of separating must-haves from would-likes is identical, and it keeps the system affordable and focused.

One caution belongs here plainly: a home battery is a valuable layer of resilience, but for genuinely life-critical equipment it should be one layer among several, not the only plan. Devices like oxygen concentrators and other life-sustaining gear warrant conversations with your medical provider and equipment supplier about their own backup recommendations, which often include device-specific batteries, backup tanks, or registration with your utility's medical priority program for faster restoration. This article is about sizing a home battery sensibly as part of that broader plan, not a substitute for professional guidance on life-support equipment. With that framing clear, the sizing itself is straightforward and worth doing well.

## Sizing for guaranteed hours, with margin for the worst outage

Once you have each device's energy draw, sizing the battery is a matter of deciding how long you need to run the load and multiplying. The core relationship is simple: usable battery energy divided by the load's average power gives you runtime. A 10-kilowatt-hour battery holding, say, 9 or so usable kilowatt-hours after accounting for the reserve it holds back and conversion losses, running a 50-watt CPAP for eight hours a night, uses about 0.4 kilowatt-hours per night — which is why that single battery can carry a light CPAP for well over ten nights, even far longer if the machine runs without a heated humidifier. Put a 400-watt oxygen concentrator on that same battery and the picture inverts: 400 watts continuous is about 9.6 kilowatt-hours a day, so the battery covers roughly a single day of continuous oxygen before it's spent. Same battery, wildly different runtimes, entirely because of the load. This is the single most important thing to internalize — the device's continuous power draw, not the battery's headline capacity, is what usually sets your runtime.

That contrast should drive how much storage you buy. If the CPAP is your critical load, a single modestly sized battery gives you generous multi-night coverage with room to spare, and pairing it with even a small solar array to recharge during daylight can extend that indefinitely through a prolonged outage. If a continuous, heavy load like a concentrator is critical, you need to think in terms of how many days of autonomy you require and size accordingly — possibly multiple batteries, and almost certainly solar to recharge them, because a battery alone that lasts a day is a stopgap, not a solution, for a multi-day outage. The general logic of translating a load into hours or days of runtime, and how quickly heavy loads exhaust a bank, is worked through in [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/), and the medical case is just that same math applied to the loads you cannot afford to lose. You can test specific scenarios — a given battery size against a given daily medical load, with or without solar recharging — using the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/), which lets you see how many hours or days of coverage a candidate system actually delivers.

Build in real margin, because the whole point of medical backup is to eliminate the gamble. Size for an outage longer than you think you'll ever face, not the average one, since the outages that matter most are the severe storms and grid failures that last for days rather than hours. A comfortable rule for a medical household is to target enough storage to carry the critical load through the longest outage that's plausible in your area — and if that's a multi-day event, to include solar so the battery refills each day rather than draining to nothing. Account for the losses that shrink real-world runtime below the nameplate figure: batteries hold back a reserve to protect their cells, the inverter loses a few percent converting stored DC into the AC most devices use, and cold temperatures reduce available capacity, all of which mean you should plan on somewhat less usable energy than the label suggests. Sizing to the optimistic nameplate number and discovering the real runtime is shorter is precisely the failure you're trying to prevent, so err toward more capacity than the bare math requires. Storage is cheaper than the consequence of running out.

## Making sure the power actually reaches the device

Sizing the battery is only half the problem; the other half is making sure that when the grid fails, the critical devices are actually the ones that stay energized, instantly and automatically. A battery that's technically large enough does you no good if it's wired to back up the whole house indiscriminately, drains itself powering non-essential loads, and leaves nothing for the concentrator — or if the transfer from grid to battery is slow or manual and the device blinks off in the gap. The standard way to solve this is to put your medical equipment, along with a short list of other essentials, on a dedicated backup circuit that the battery serves as a priority. That's the function of a critical load subpanel: your installer moves the circuits that must stay alive onto a separate panel that the battery backs up, so the stored energy is spent only on what matters and isn't squandered on the air conditioner or the electric range. How that subpanel is planned and wired is covered in [critical load panels and wiring your home for backup priorities](/blog/critical-load-panel/), and for a medical household it's arguably the most important design decision in the whole system, because it's what guarantees the battery's energy goes where it's needed.

Just as important is that the switchover happens automatically and without interruption. A medical device cannot tolerate a manual process where someone has to notice the outage, go flip a transfer switch, and hope the timing works — especially at 3 a.m. or when the person who knows how is away. A properly installed home battery with backup capability includes automatic transfer that detects the grid failure and takes over in a fraction of a second, so devices keep running through the transition without the user doing anything. For the most sensitive equipment, an uninterruptible design — where the device is effectively always drawing from a source that can't blink — is the gold standard, and it's worth confirming with your installer that the specific transfer behavior of the system meets the tolerance of your equipment, since some devices restart cleanly after a brief interruption and others must never lose power. Ask the direct question during design: when the grid drops, does my critical device see any interruption at all, and if so, how long. The answer determines whether the system is adequate for life-support use or merely for convenience.

Redundancy and testing round out a responsible setup. Even a well-sized, well-wired battery can have a fault, run down during an unusually long outage, or be offline for maintenance, so a medical household should keep a secondary layer — a device-specific backup battery, backup oxygen supply, a portable power station kept charged, or a generator — as a fallback the home battery doesn't replace. And a backup system is only trustworthy if you've actually tested it: periodically simulate an outage, confirm the critical devices stay powered through the transfer, and verify the runtime matches what you sized for, because the middle of a real emergency is the worst time to discover a wiring assumption was wrong. A home battery, correctly sized to measured loads, wired to a critical load panel with fast automatic transfer, backed by a second layer of protection, and tested regularly, turns a power outage from a crisis into a non-event for a medical household — which is exactly what this kind of planning is for.
