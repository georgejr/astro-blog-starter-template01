A central air conditioner is, electrically, one big motor. The compressor pumps refrigerant around a loop, and pumping refrigerant takes a lot of power — power that is concentrated in exactly the months you are already producing the most solar. That overlap is convenient, and it is a real reason solar and air conditioning pencil out well together in hot climates. But the question "how many panels to run the AC" hides two different questions inside it, and answering the wrong one is where sizing goes off the rails. One question is about power: can your array supply the compressor's electrical draw at the moment it switches on? The other is about energy: how many panels does it take to offset a whole summer of cooling? Those have different answers, they are set by different physics, and conflating them leads people either to buy far more capacity than they need or to worry about a startup surge that a grid-tied system handles without breaking a sweat.

## From tons to kilowatts to kilowatt-hours

Cooling capacity is measured in tons or BTUs, where one ton equals 12,000 BTU per hour of heat removal. That number describes the cooling the unit delivers, not the electricity it consumes, and the gap between the two is efficiency, rated as SEER or EER — a higher number means more cooling wrung out of each watt. To get from a nameplate to a real electrical figure you have to translate through that efficiency, and it is worth doing carefully because the two figures answer the two different questions.

Take the power question first, because it is the one people fixate on and it usually matters least. As a planning assumption, a modern central unit pulls somewhere around 1 kilowatt of electricity per ton while the compressor is actively running. So a 3-ton system draws roughly 3 kW when it is cooling, plus a few hundred watts for the air handler's blower moving air through the ducts. That instantaneous draw is the number that matters for the power question, and it is easily covered: a 6 kW array at midday, producing near its rating in full sun, supplies a 3 kW compressor with room to spare and sends the leftover to the house or the grid. Even the compressor's startup surge — the brief spike of current when the motor first kicks on — rarely dictates anything on a grid-tied system, because that momentary demand is drawn seamlessly from the grid the same way it always was, with the panels simply reducing the net draw. In other words, on a normal grid-connected home the power question almost answers itself. You do not need to match your array to the compressor's peak; the grid is still there to fill any instantaneous gap, and the panels just offset whatever they happen to be producing at that moment.

Efficiency is the lever that connects the two questions, and it is worth understanding because it moves both the draw and the seasonal total at once. A unit's SEER or EER rating tells you how much cooling it delivers per unit of electricity, so a high-efficiency condenser doing the same cooling work simply pulls fewer watts while running and burns fewer kilowatt-hours over the season than an older, lower-rated unit removing the same heat. The gap between a decades-old air conditioner and a modern high-efficiency one can be large enough to change your panel count on its own, which is why replacing an aging unit at the same time you go solar sometimes does more for your summer bill than adding panels would. A rough way to feel the scale: assume a 3-ton unit that runs the equivalent of six full-power hours on a typical summer day, drawing around 3 kW while it runs. That is roughly 18 kWh a day, and across a hundred-odd meaningful cooling days a season it lands in the low thousands of kilowatt-hours — an assumption-based figure, not a measurement, but enough to show why the seasonal energy is a much bigger number than the compressor's instantaneous draw suggests. The same unit in a milder climate running half as many hours uses half as much, and a less efficient unit running the same hours uses more. The nameplate on the condenser is only the starting point; the runtime your climate and habits impose on it is what fills in the rest.

The energy question is the one that actually sets panel count, and it works differently because an air conditioner does not run continuously. It cycles on and off to hold a temperature, so over a full day it might run at full power for only a handful of hours even during a heat wave, and far less on a mild day. Panel count follows the total kilowatt-hours the unit consumes across the cooling season, not its peak draw. That seasonal total depends far more on your climate, your house, and your thermostat than on the nameplate on the condenser. The same 3-ton unit that sips power through a mild coastal summer will devour it through a Phoenix August, because the compressor spends far more of each day running when the outdoor air is 110°F and the house is fighting to stay at 75. Insulation, ductwork, shading, window quality, and the temperature you actually set all move the number, sometimes by a factor of two or more between two houses with identical equipment. [Air conditioning and your kWh](/blog/air-conditioner-electricity-usage/) breaks down what really drives that seasonal total, and the short version is that the equipment's rating is only the starting point — how hard you make it work is the rest of the story.

## A panel count you can use, and how it fits the whole system

To turn a cooling load into a panel count, treat the cooling season's kilowatt-hours the way you would treat any other load you want to offset: divide the annual cooling kWh by what a single panel produces in a year. As a planning assumption, a 420 W panel in a sunny region produces very roughly 500 to 600 kWh per year after the real-world losses — inverter conversion, wiring, heat, soiling, and imperfect orientation — that always shave a system below its nameplate. The table below pairs assumption-based cooling-season usage with the panels needed to cover it, and every figure in it should be read as a round starting point rather than a specification.

| AC size | Running draw (approx) | Cooling-season use (assumption) | Panels to offset |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 ton | ~2 kW | ~1,500–2,500 kWh | 3–5 |
| 3 ton | ~3 kW | ~2,500–4,000 kWh | 5–8 |
| 4 ton | ~4 kW | ~3,500–5,500 kWh | 7–10 |

For a typical central system working through a genuinely hot summer, that lands at roughly 6 to 10 panels of added capacity dedicated to the cooling load. Treat that band as an estimate, not a spec, because the extremes fall well outside it. A well-insulated house running a high-SEER unit and a reasonable thermostat setting sits at the low end or below it, while a leaky house cooled aggressively to 68°F in a desert climate can run right off the top of the table. Because your own usage can swing so far from the average, the honest move is to size against your actual bills rather than a generic chart — the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) calculator applies your region's sun hours to your real numbers, which is the only way to turn a rough band into a figure you can build to.

What makes the air conditioner an unusually good match for solar, beyond the panel count, is timing. Most household loads are scattered across the day and evening, but cooling demand peaks precisely when the sun is highest — the hot, bright afternoons when your array is producing at its best. That alignment means a large share of your AC's electricity can be consumed directly from your own panels as it is generated, rather than bought from the grid or exported at a discount. Cooling is one of the few big residential loads that naturally self-consumes solar, and self-consumed solar is the most valuable kind because it offsets power at the full retail rate you would otherwise pay. The alignment is not perfect, though, and it is worth being honest about the gap. Cooling frequently lingers into the evening as a house sheds the day's stored heat, well after production has fallen off, and those evening kilowatt-hours come from the grid on a standard system — or from a battery, if you have one. Whether storage is worth adding just to keep the air conditioner running through a summer evening or a grid outage is a separate and stricter calculation than the offset math here; [can a home battery run your air conditioner](/blog/can-battery-run-air-conditioner/) works through the power and duration side of it, which asks not just "can the panels cover the energy" but "can the battery deliver the compressor's draw for long enough to matter."

The cleanest way to handle cooling is counterintuitive: in most cases, do not size a separate "AC array" at all. Your air conditioner's consumption already lives inside your electricity bills, buried in the summer months, and the standard sizing method starts from your full annual kilowatt-hours. If the usage figure you size the whole system from already includes a complete year of summer bills, then the cooling load is baked in automatically, and layering an extra panel count on top of it would double-count the very load you are trying to cover. This is the most common sizing mistake around air conditioning: treating a load that is already in the annual total as if it were a new addition, and buying capacity twice for it. The panel counts in the table above are genuinely useful, but for a narrower purpose than most people assume — they matter mainly when the cooling load is not yet reflected in your billing history.

That specific case is worth spelling out, because it is where the numbers earn their keep. Suppose you are adding central air to a house that never had it, or replacing a couple of window units with a whole-house system, or moving into a home whose previous owners barely ran the AC and whose bills therefore understate what your household will use. In any of those situations your billing history does not contain the cooling load you are about to impose, and sizing straight from those bills would leave the array short every August. The fix is to estimate the added cooling kilowatt-hours using the table and the drivers above, add that estimate to your current annual usage, and then size the system once against the new combined total. Buying that capacity while the crew is already on your roof, the scaffolding is up, and the permit is pulled costs a fraction of what it costs to come back and add panels later for a load you could have seen coming.
