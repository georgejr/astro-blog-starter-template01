Buying a new build hands you a decision that retrofit shoppers never face: the builder can roll a solar array into the house before you ever get the keys, or you can close on the home and add panels yourself once you're moved in. The salesperson in the design center makes the first option sound effortless — a line item folded into the mortgage, no second contractor to chase, panels installed while the roof is still open and crews are already crawling over the structure. That convenience is real, and in some cases it genuinely saves money. But a bundled package also buries the one number that lets you judge whether the deal is fair, and homeowners who sign for it without asking a few pointed questions often pay a premium they never see itemized. The choice isn't obvious in either direction, and it turns almost entirely on how the builder prices the array and what you'd be giving up in exchange for the convenience.

## What the builder's package actually bundles

The strongest argument for letting the builder handle solar is that some of the cost drivers that make a retrofit expensive simply don't exist during construction. When an installer retrofits an existing home, a crew mobilizes to your site for a job that might take a day or two, sets up fall protection, works around a finished and occupied house, and packs up. All of that overhead — the trip, the setup, the site-specific planning — gets spread across a single system. On a new build, the roof is already being framed and shingled, electricians are already pulling wire through open walls, and adding conduit and a few roof penetrations is a marginal task on a site that's already fully staffed. Running the electrical rough-in before the drywall goes up is genuinely cheaper than fishing a new circuit through a finished home. In principle, that efficiency should make new-construction solar cost less per watt than the same array bolted onto a standing house.

In practice, whether you actually capture that saving depends on how the builder marks up the package, and that's where the picture gets murky. Builders are not solar companies. Most subcontract the array to a regional installer and resell it to you as an option, the same way they resell upgraded countertops or a bigger garage. The markup on options is how a lot of production builders make their margin, and solar is easy to mark up precisely because buyers can't easily comparison-shop a line item that appears only in that builder's price sheet. You might be quoted a flat figure — "solar package: add to base price" — with no system size, no panel count, and no price per watt attached. That flat number is the problem. Without knowing how many kilowatts you're getting and what you're paying per watt, you have no way to tell a fair price from a padded one, and the design-center setting is engineered to make you decide quickly rather than go home and run the math.

The convenience of financing is the other half of the pitch, and it cuts both ways. Folding solar into the purchase means the array gets financed at your mortgage rate over the loan's full term, which is usually a lower interest rate than a standalone solar loan and spreads the cost across decades. That can make the monthly impact feel painless. The flip side is that a 30-year mortgage stretches the interest cost of the panels across the entire loan, so a system you might otherwise pay off in ten years quietly rides along for thirty, and the total interest you pay on that portion can be substantial even at a low rate. It also means the solar is baked into your down payment and your loan-to-value from day one, which is fine if the appraisal reflects the added value and awkward if it doesn't. None of this makes bundled financing a bad deal — the low rate is a genuine advantage — but "it's just a small bump in the mortgage" is a framing that hides how much the array actually costs.

There's a coordination benefit worth weighing honestly, too, because it isn't purely marketing. A bundled system gets designed alongside the roof, so the array layout, the conduit runs, and the electrical panel are planned together rather than retrofitted around finished work. The roof is new, so you're not putting a 25-year array on shingles with ten years left. Permitting and inspection happen inside the builder's existing process rather than as a separate project you manage. For a buyer who wants solar and doesn't want to run a second procurement after moving in, that integration has real value. The question is only whether the value is worth whatever premium sits inside that flat package price — and you can't answer it until you force the price into a form you can compare.

## Adding panels yourself after you close

The retrofit path trades convenience for control, and the control is mostly about price transparency and choice. When you solicit your own quotes after closing, installers compete for the job, and competition is the single most reliable force pushing a solar price toward fair. You can get three bids, compare them line by line, choose your own equipment and inverter architecture, and check the installer's licensing and reviews yourself rather than accepting whoever the builder happened to subcontract. You also control the timing — you can wait until you've lived in the house for a few months and have real electricity bills, which tells you far more accurately what size system you actually need than a builder's guess based on square footage. That last point matters more than it sounds: a system sized to your genuine usage almost always beats one sized to a builder's standard package, which tends to be either a token array meant to hit a marketing checkbox or an oversized one meant to maximize the option's price.

The cost you're accepting in exchange is the retrofit overhead the bundled package avoids. A standalone install pays for its own mobilization, its own permit, and its own crew day, and those fixed costs don't shrink just because the roof is only two years old. You're also drilling into a finished roof rather than one that's open during framing, though on a modern asphalt-shingle roof that's routine work that a competent installer flashes and seals without drama — it does not meaningfully shorten the roof's life when done correctly. Financing is separate, too. You'd pay cash, take a dedicated solar loan, or use a home equity product, each at its own rate rather than the mortgage rate, and a solar loan's rate is typically higher than a mortgage's even if its term is shorter. When you compare the true cost of the two paths, the retrofit's transparency has to be weighed against this stack of standalone costs that the bundled version folds into a construction project already in motion.

Where the retrofit reliably wins is on the number that makes any solar quote legible: cost per watt. Because you're collecting competitive bids that each state a system size and a total price, you can divide one by the other and compare apples to apples — and you can hold every bid against the broad market band. Installed residential prices have generally landed somewhere between about $2.50 and $4.00 per watt before incentives, with plenty of variation by region, installer, and equipment tier; a quote well above that band is a prompt to ask what you're paying for, and one suspiciously below it is a prompt to check what's being left out. A builder's flat package price rarely lets you run that check at all. ## Running the two paths against each other

The honest way to compare is to force both options onto the same footing: system size, total pre-incentive price, price per watt, financing terms, and the federal credit. Here's a worked comparison using labeled assumptions rather than any specific quote. Assume you want roughly a 7 kW system. A competitive retrofit at $3.20 per watt would run about $22,400 before incentives. Suppose the builder's bundled package for a similar 7 kW array is presented as "add $26,000" with no per-watt figure — that works out to about $3.71 per watt, near the top of the market band, and the $3,600 gap is effectively the markup you're paying for the convenience and integration. Whether that gap is worth it is a real judgment call: you're buying coordination, a new roof underneath the array, and mortgage-rate financing, and for some buyers that's worth a few thousand dollars. The point is that you can only make the call once you've translated the flat package price into a per-watt number and set it beside a market benchmark. If the builder won't give you a system size and a price you can divide, treat that refusal as the answer.

The federal tax credit adds a wrinkle that specifically favors keeping the numbers clear. The Residential Clean Energy Credit is a federal credit worth 30% of a qualifying solar installation's cost, and it applies to solar on a home you own and use as a residence, including new construction — but you have to be able to identify the cost of the solar itself. If a bundled package folds the array into a single new-home price with no separate solar figure, documenting the credit's basis at tax time gets harder, whereas a standalone install produces a clean invoice showing exactly what the solar cost. The timing also differs from a retrofit in ways worth understanding before you assume the credit is automatic; our guide to [claiming the credit on new construction](/blog/solar-tax-credit-new-construction/) covers when the system is considered placed in service and why that matters for which tax year you file. For any credit question, the IRS's own guidance on the Residential Clean Energy Credit is the authority to rely on rather than a builder's sales sheet, and it's reasonable to ask the builder in writing for a documented solar cost you can substantiate.

Once you have both paths reduced to comparable numbers, the decision tends to sort itself by temperament and situation. If you value a single coordinated project, want the array on a brand-new roof, and the bundled per-watt price lands inside the fair band, taking the builder's package is a defensible choice — you're paying a modest premium for genuine convenience. If the package price is opaque or sits well above the market band, or if you'd rather choose your own equipment and installer and size the system to real bills, closing first and retrofitting almost always gives you a better price and a system fit to your actual usage. The convenience of a bundled array is real, but it's only a good deal when the price behind the convenience survives being written out in dollars per watt.
