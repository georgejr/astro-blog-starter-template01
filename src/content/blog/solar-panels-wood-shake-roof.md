Wood shake and cedar shingle roofs are beautiful, and they are also the roofing material that makes solar installers wince. The short answer to whether you can put panels on one is yes — it's done, and there are crews who do it well. The longer answer is that a wood roof turns solar into a more specialized, more expensive, and more consequential decision than the same array on asphalt shingles, because three separate issues pile on top of each other: the shakes are genuinely hard to mount to, wood is combustible in a way that intersects with fire codes, and a shake roof's lifespan often doesn't line up with the twenty-five-plus years the panels want to sit up there. None of these is a flat disqualifier, but each one is a real cost or risk, and a homeowner with a cedar roof should walk into the conversation understanding all three rather than discovering them one at a time.

## Why mounting is the hard part

Standard rooftop solar mounting was designed around asphalt shingles, and the mismatch with wood shakes starts at the most basic level. On an asphalt roof, an installer locates a rafter, drives a lag bolt through the shingle into solid wood, and seals the penetration with a flashing that tucks under the course of shingles above it — a clean, waterproof, repeatable detail refined over millions of installs. Wood shakes break that routine in several ways at once. Shakes are thick, irregular, and split-prone; they're often hand-split with uneven surfaces and natural gaps, and they don't lie flat against the roof the way a machine-made asphalt shingle does. Trying to seat a mounting foot on top of a lumpy, uneven shake and drive a bolt through it invites cracking the shake, a poor seal, and a mount that isn't sitting on a true flat plane. You can't simply lag through a shake the way you lag through a shingle and trust it to be watertight.

Because of that, mounting solar on a wood roof usually means a different technique entirely, and it's more labor. The common approach is to remove the shakes at each mounting point, attach the standoff or mounting base directly to the roof deck or a rafter beneath, install proper flashing to waterproof that penetration, and then work the surrounding shakes back around the mount — sometimes trimming or replacing individual shakes to fit cleanly around the hardware. This is careful, slow, custom work compared to the fast, standardized process on asphalt, and it requires an installer who has actually done it before, not one improvising on your roof. The number of penetrations, the fussiness of each flashing detail, and the higher chance of needing to replace cracked shakes all push the labor cost up. This is one of the clearest cases where roofing material drives the install price, a dynamic our overview of [how your roof type changes what solar costs to install](/blog/solar-installation-cost-by-roof-type/) walks through in detail — a wood shake roof sits firmly in the "adds cost and complexity" column, alongside slate and clay tile, well above simple asphalt.

The condition of the shakes makes or breaks the whole endeavor, which is why a real inspection has to come before any commitment. Wood roofs age in ways asphalt doesn't: shakes cup, split, curl, and grow brittle as they weather and dry out, and a roof that looks charming from the driveway can be far more fragile up close than it appears. Brittle, aging shakes crack when a boot steps on them or a mount is fastened near them, so an installer working an old wood roof may end up replacing a meaningful number of shakes just to complete the array — and finding matching replacement shakes for an older roof isn't always easy. If the roof is already near the end of its life, the labor and shake-replacement cost of installing panels on it, only to face a full reroof in a few years that requires removing and reinstalling those same panels, can make the whole approach uneconomical. This is exactly the situation our guide to judging whether [your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) is built to catch: the material and its age together, not just one or the other, decide whether the roof is ready.

## Fire, codes, and the lifespan mismatch

The second issue is fire, and it deserves plain treatment because it's where the wood-roof conversation gets genuinely different rather than merely more expensive. Wood shake roofs are combustible. Many are treated for fire resistance and can achieve better fire ratings than untreated wood, but as a class, a wood roof is a more fire-relevant surface than a non-combustible asphalt, metal, or tile roof — which is precisely why some jurisdictions in high-wildfire-risk areas restrict or ban new wood roofing outright. Solar itself is not a significant fire hazard when properly installed, and the real-world risk is low; our look at [whether solar panels cause fires](/blog/do-solar-panels-cause-fires/) puts that risk in honest context. But installing an electrical system with its wiring and connections onto a combustible roofing material raises the stakes of doing the electrical work correctly, and it interacts with local fire codes that may impose stricter setback requirements, access pathways for firefighters, or rapid-shutdown provisions. In some fire-prone regions, the practical result is that a wood roof complicates the permit or even prompts the authority to require roof upgrades as a condition of the install.

That regulatory friction is worth investigating early, because it varies enormously by location. In a low-risk area, a treated wood roof in good condition may present no permitting obstacle beyond the extra care the mounting demands. In a designated wildfire-hazard zone, the local building department may have rules that make solar on wood shakes difficult or effectively require re-roofing with a non-combustible material first. There's no national answer here; the only reliable move is to have your installer confirm what your specific jurisdiction requires for solar on a combustible roof before you're deep into a contract. An installer experienced with wood roofs in your area will know the local rules cold, which is another reason to seek out one who's done this exact work rather than a high-volume crew that mostly touches asphalt.

The third issue is timing, and it's the one homeowners most often overlook. Solar panels are a twenty-five-to-forty-year commitment on the roof; a wood shake roof typically lasts somewhere in the range of 20 to 30 years, and often less if it hasn't been well maintained, treated, and kept clear of moss and debris in a damp climate. That mismatch is the crux of the decision. If your cedar roof is ten or fifteen years into a thirty-year life, putting panels on it means you'll very likely have to remove the entire array, reroof, and reinstall the array partway through the panels' life — an expensive round trip that can run into the thousands and that no one enjoys paying. Mounting long-lived panels on a shorter-lived roof is asking for that bill. The clean way to avoid it is to line the two lifespans up: if the roof is aging, seriously consider replacing it before or at the time of the solar install, so the new roof and the panels start their clock together. The general advice to replace an aging roof before going solar applies with extra force to wood, precisely because wood's shorter service life makes the mismatch more likely and the reroof more disruptive.

## Making the decision and pricing it

Put together, a wood shake roof doesn't rule out solar — it narrows your options and raises the bar on doing it right. The homeowner who succeeds here does a few specific things. They get the roof honestly assessed for condition and remaining life before anything else, because a brittle or near-end-of-life shake roof changes the entire calculus. They confirm the local fire and building requirements for solar on combustible roofing, so there are no permit surprises. They deliberately seek out installers with genuine wood-shake experience and ask to see examples, rather than accepting a bid from a crew that will be learning on their roof. And they weigh the reroof question directly: if the roof has many good years left, panels on wood can be entirely reasonable; if it's aging, bundling a reroof with the solar install is usually the sounder financial move even though it raises the upfront number.

Pricing all of this means expecting a higher install cost than the asphalt quotes you might see advertised, and building the possible reroof into the total rather than treating it as a separate surprise later. Because the mounting labor, the shake replacement, and any fire-code upgrades all add to the job, a wood-roof solar project sits at the pricier end of residential installs, and a [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) is useful for establishing the baseline system cost that these roof-specific extras get added on top of — just don't expect the roof premium to show up in a generic estimate, since it's exactly the kind of site-specific factor a national average leaves out. On the production side, a [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) that estimates output for your location and roof orientation tells you what the system will actually generate, which is the number that has to justify the elevated cost of putting it on wood in the first place. If the roof faces well and gets good sun, a higher install cost can still pencil out over the panels' long life; if the roof is marginal on orientation or shading and expensive to mount on, the combination may push the whole project past the point of making sense.

The honest bottom line is that solar on a wood shake roof is a "yes, with conditions" rather than a simple green light. It's more expensive to install, more entangled with fire codes, and more likely to collide with a roof that won't outlast the panels. A homeowner who addresses those three issues head-on — roof condition, local fire rules, and lifespan alignment — can put a solid, long-lived array on a cedar roof and be glad they did. A homeowner who treats it as an ordinary asphalt install and hopes the differences don't matter is setting up either a compromised installation or an expensive reroof-and-reinstall down the road. The beauty of the roof is real; so is the extra homework it demands before the first panel goes up.
