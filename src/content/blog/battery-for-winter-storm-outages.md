Winter outages are the ones that frighten people, and for good reason: a summer blackout is an inconvenience, but a multi-day power failure in a cold snap is a genuine safety problem, because a house without heat can become dangerous long before the food in the fridge spoils. That's exactly the scenario homeowners have in mind when they size a battery for backup — the ice storm that takes the grid down for days while the temperature outside sits well below freezing. It's also the scenario a battery is hardest to size for, because winter stacks two problems on top of each other. The cold that's causing the outage also shrinks the battery's usable capacity, and the single thing you most need power for — staying warm — is one of the heaviest electrical loads a home has. Get the sizing right and a battery carries you through the storm in comfort; get it wrong by assuming the battery performs like it does in July and by underestimating what heat costs, and you'll watch the reserve drain overnight with the worst of the cold still ahead.

## Cold cuts the battery just as you lean on it hardest

The first thing to understand is that a battery does not deliver its full rated capacity in the cold, and winter is precisely when you're asking it to deliver the most. Lithium batteries lose usable capacity as their temperature drops, because the chemistry that moves energy in and out of the cells slows down when it's cold. A pack that gives you its full rated kilowatt-hours at room temperature can hand back noticeably less on a frigid night — and a frigid night is exactly when a winter-storm outage puts the battery to work. This is a cruel coincidence of timing: the derate and the demand peak on the same night. A battery you sized on its spec-sheet capacity, assuming it'll deliver every rated kilowatt-hour, may deliver meaningfully fewer when the thermometer drops, and the shortfall shows up at the worst possible moment. Winter-outage sizing has to be built on the battery's cold-weather capacity, not its mild-weather rating, which means building in headroom for the derate rather than sizing to the nameplate number.

Where the battery lives changes this equation more than almost any other factor, so it deserves a decision rather than a default. A battery mounted on an exterior wall or in an unconditioned detached garage in a cold climate takes the full brunt of the temperature, and its winter derate is at its worst exactly when you need it. A battery installed in a heated basement, an interior utility room, or a garage that stays above freezing barely notices the cold, because the ambient temperature around it stays in the range where it performs well. For a home that expects to rely on the battery through winter outages, keeping it somewhere temperature-controlled isn't a nicety — it's the difference between counting on most of the capacity you paid for and losing a real chunk of it on the coldest nights. Many battery systems also have their own operating-temperature limits and may include heaters or shut down protection in extreme cold, which is another reason the installation location is worth discussing with your installer specifically in the context of winter performance.

The practical takeaway is that winter sizing starts from a discounted capacity, not the full one. If you plan on the nameplate figure and the battery is somewhere cold, you've overestimated what you actually have to work with before you've even added up your loads. The right mental model is to take the usable capacity, apply a realistic cold-weather derate for where the battery is installed, and treat that reduced number as your true winter reserve — then size everything else against it. This is the same discipline that governs backup sizing generally, and our walkthrough of [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/) lays out the underlying runtime math that the cold simply makes more demanding. Winter doesn't change the arithmetic; it changes the inputs, shrinking the supply side of the equation right as the demand side grows.

## What "staying warm" actually costs in a blackout

The reason winter backup is hard is that heating is expensive in electrical terms, and how expensive depends enormously on how your home makes heat. The best case, counterintuitively, is a home heated by a gas or propane furnace, because the fuel provides the heat and the only electricity the furnace needs is to run its blower and controls. A furnace blower is a manageable load — as a rough figure, often somewhere around 500 watts while running, though it varies by furnace — which a battery can sustain for a long time. If your heat comes from gas but your furnace can't run without grid power for the blower, then a battery that keeps the blower and controls energized effectively keeps your whole heating system alive through the outage, and that's one of the most efficient uses of backup capacity there is. You're not powering the heat itself, just the fan that distributes it, and that's a load a modestly sized battery handles well.

Electric heat is a different and much harder story, and it's essential to be honest about it. Electric resistance heating — baseboard heaters, electric furnaces, resistance space heaters — draws enormous power, often thousands of watts per zone, because it makes heat directly from electricity with no leverage. Trying to run whole-home electric resistance heat off a battery through a multi-day outage is generally not realistic; the loads are so large they'd drain even a big battery in hours. A heat pump is far more efficient than resistance heat, delivering several units of heat for each unit of electricity, which makes it much more battery-friendly — but it's still a substantial load, and in the cold its efficiency falls just as heating demand rises, so a heat pump run continuously through a cold outage is a serious draw on the reserve. If your home is all-electric, the realistic winter-backup strategy is usually not to power full heating for the whole house but to heat a smaller area for limited hours, or to keep a single efficient heat source running intermittently rather than the whole system continuously. The [heat pump calculator](/heat-pump-calculator/) can help you estimate what your heat pump draws under cold conditions, which is the number you need before you can judge how long a battery could sustain it.

The rest of the winter load list is comparatively light, which is the small mercy of a cold-weather outage. Refrigeration matters less in winter than summer — the fridge and freezer still cycle, but a cold house helps them, and in a pinch a garage or porch can serve as cold storage. Lighting, phone and device charging, internet equipment, and a few outlets are all modest loads a battery covers easily. The loads that deserve specific attention beyond heat are any well pump (a home on a private well loses running water when the pump loses power, and pumps draw a meaningful surge to start) and any medical equipment, which is non-negotiable and should anchor the sizing. When you build your essential-loads list for winter, heat dominates it, and everything else is a rounding error by comparison — which is why the heating question decides almost the entire sizing. Our guide to [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/) walks through assembling that list, and in winter the exercise is really about deciding how much heat you're trying to back up, because that choice sets the whole budget.

## Sizing for the storm, and where solar fits (or doesn't)

Putting it together means matching your cold-derated reserve against your winter load list for the duration you want to cover, and being realistic about all three. Here's a worked illustration with labeled assumptions. Suppose you have a battery with 13 kWh of usable capacity, installed somewhere cold enough to knock roughly 15% off in a hard freeze, leaving about 11 kWh to actually work with. Suppose your home has a gas furnace, so heating costs you only the blower — assume it averages around 200 watts over the day once you account for it cycling on and off rather than running constantly — plus a fridge, lights, and devices totaling perhaps another 300 watts on average. That's roughly 500 watts of continuous average draw, which against 11 kWh of usable reserve gives you on the order of 22 hours before recharge — most of a day, enough to bridge a typical outage and stretch further if you conserve. Now change one assumption: make the home all-electric with a heat pump you want to run, adding perhaps 1,500 watts of average cold-weather draw, and total consumption jumps toward 2,000 watts, cutting runtime to something like 5 to 6 hours. Same battery, same storm — the heating choice moved runtime from a day to an evening. That contrast is the entire point of sizing winter backup deliberately.

The decision that follows from this math is usually whether to back up the whole home or just the essentials, and in winter the honest answer leans hard toward partial backup for most households. Backing up an entire all-electric home's heating through a multi-day cold outage would require a battery bank so large that few homeowners would pay for it, whereas backing up a furnace blower or a single zone of efficient heat, plus the light loads, is achievable with a sensibly sized system. Deciding what stays on and what goes dark is its own worthwhile exercise, and our comparison of [whole-home versus partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/) lays out how to draw that line. For winter specifically, the line almost always runs through the heating system: you back up the cheapest reliable way to stay warm and let the luxuries go, because warmth is the load that matters and everything else is negotiable.

Solar deserves a clear-eyed caveat in the winter-storm context, because it's tempting to assume panels will simply recharge the battery each day and extend the runtime indefinitely. In a winter storm, that assumption often fails. The very weather causing the outage — heavy snow, thick overcast, short days — is the weather that suppresses solar production, and snow sitting on the panels can cut output to nearly nothing until it clears. You may get some recharge on the clear day after the storm passes, but during the storm itself the prudent plan is to treat the battery's stored capacity as a fixed reserve that has to carry you, not a tank the panels will keep refilling. That's the opposite of a summer outage, where long sunny days can recharge the battery daily and make runtime almost open-ended. To size for the realistic winter case, model your loads against the cold-derated capacity with little or no solar recharge, using the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) to test how the runtime changes as you vary the heating load and the amount of daily recharge you assume. And it's worth naming the honest alternative: for homeowners whose priority is riding out long, cold, sunless outages, a fuel-burning generator can sustain heavy heating loads for days in a way a battery realistically can't, so the right winter-backup answer for an all-electric home in a harsh climate is sometimes a battery for the quiet essentials paired with a generator for the heat — or a candid decision about which loads you're truly willing to keep running when the storm settles in.
