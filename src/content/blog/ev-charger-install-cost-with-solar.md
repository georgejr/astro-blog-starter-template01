The cheapest afternoon to put in a Level 2 EV charger is the one most homeowners miss entirely: the day the solar crew already has your service panel open, a permit on file, and a licensed electrician standing in your garage. On its own, a home charger install is a modest electrical job with a surprisingly wide price range. Folded into a solar project that is already touching the same panel and pulling the same kind of permit, a big share of that price quietly disappears.

That timing is worth understanding before you sign a solar contract, because the decision usually gets framed backward. People treat the charger as a separate purchase to sort out "later," once the car actually shows up in the driveway. By then the electrician who was in the garage has gone home, the panel is closed up, and the shared costs that could have been split across two jobs have to be paid twice. The [EV charging cost calculator](/ev-charging-calculator/) will show you what home charging does to your monthly running costs, but the install itself — the one-time wiring bill — follows its own logic, and that logic quietly rewards doing it at the right moment rather than the convenient one.

## What actually goes into the wiring bill

A Level 2 charger runs on a 240-volt circuit, the same kind that feeds an electric range or a clothes dryer, and the install is essentially the job of getting that circuit from your electrical panel to wherever the car parks. Break the bill into its parts and the wide range of quotes stops looking mysterious.

The circuit breaker itself is cheap. A double-pole 240-volt breaker sized for the charger — commonly 40 or 60 amps for a residential unit — is a small line item, a matter of tens of dollars in hardware. The real money is in the wire run and the labor to make it. You need copper conductors rated for the amperage, usually run through conduit, and both are priced by the foot. A charger mounted on the garage wall directly behind the panel might need ten feet of wire; one across the garage, up into an attic, or out to a detached structure could need sixty feet or more, routed through conduit that has to be strapped, bent around obstacles, and sometimes trenched underground. That distance is the single largest swing factor in an otherwise simple job. Assume basic materials and a short, clean run inside an attached garage and the wire-and-conduit portion stays modest; stretch that run across the house or bury it out to a detached garage and it can multiply several times over.

Labor tracks the same variable. An electrician who can drop straight down from the panel into the garage below is looking at an hour or two of work. One who has to fish wire through finished walls, cut and patch drywall, drill through a foundation, or trench across a driveway is looking at most of a day, and the labor line reflects every hour of it. The physical difficulty of the path from panel to parking spot, not the charger hardware, is where most of the cost variance actually lives — which is why two houses on the same street can get charger quotes hundreds of dollars apart for identical equipment.

Then comes the question the electrician answers first: does your panel have room? A 240-volt, 40-to-60-amp charger circuit is a substantial load, and if your service panel is already full or near its capacity, adding it can force the same conversations solar does — a subpanel, a load-management device that prevents the charger from running flat-out at the same moment as another big appliance, or in some cases a service upgrade. If the panel has an open double-pole slot and spare capacity, that entire branch of cost never appears. If it doesn't, the charger install inherits a version of the same electrical constraints that shape a solar interconnection. That overlap is precisely why the two jobs belong together: the panel's headroom gets evaluated once, for both new loads, instead of twice with a nasty surprise the second time around.

Permitting and inspection round out the bill. A hardwired Level 2 charger is an electrical alteration that most jurisdictions want permitted and inspected, which means a permit fee plus the staff time to pull it, schedule the inspector, and pass. On a standalone charger job, that overhead is spread across one small project and can feel disproportionate to the actual work involved. The charger hardware — the wall unit itself — is the one cost that stays roughly constant no matter when you buy it, varying more by brand and features (plug-in versus hardwired, amperage, whether it has smart-charging and scheduling built in) than by anything the installer does on the day.

## Why doing it alongside the solar job costs less

The savings from bundling aren't a goodwill discount an installer chooses to hand you. They come from real costs that only get paid once instead of twice, and once you see where they are, the case for combining the jobs makes itself.

Start with the truck roll. Every time a crew or an electrician drives to your house, sets up, and works, that mobilization is baked into the price whether the job takes one hour or a full day. A solar installation already pays for that trip. Adding the charger circuit while the electrician is standing in the garage means the charger doesn't have to fund its own separate visit — and that separate visit is the single most avoidable expense in a standalone install. It is a fixed cost that gets amortized across a much bigger project instead of landing entirely on one small one.

The panel work overlaps almost perfectly, too. A grid-tied solar system ties into your service panel through a dedicated backfeed breaker, which means the installer already has to open the panel, read the busbar rating, check the main breaker size, and confirm there's capacity for a new load. That is the exact same analysis a charger circuit requires. Doing it once, for both, means you pay for one panel evaluation — and if an upgrade turns out to be genuinely necessary, one upgrade sized for everything at once, rather than a solar-sized upgrade followed a year later by a "wait, now the charger won't fit" second upgrade. The reason this matters so much is the same reason [a solar installation's price is mostly not hardware](/blog/solar-installation-cost-breakdown/): the fixed costs of mobilizing, permitting, and touching the panel dominate the bill, and anything that lets you pay them a single time is real money kept in your pocket.

Permitting is the third overlap, and a meaningful one. When an installer is already assembling a plan set and permit package for your solar system, adding the charger circuit to that submission is a marginal addition rather than a fresh application from scratch. The jurisdiction reviews one project. The inspector makes one trip and signs off on the whole electrical scope at once. Filing separately for the charger weeks or months later means starting that clock over — another fee, another queue, another inspection appointment. None of that paperwork makes your car charge any faster, but all of it costs, and consolidating it is free savings.

There's a quieter efficiency in the physical work as well. If the solar job involves any drywall being opened, conduit being routed, or an electrician already tracing paths through your garage and attic, the charger's wire run can often piggyback on that same disturbance. Fishing one more circuit while the wall is open is cheaper than opening the same wall twice. None of these overlaps individually transforms the price, but stacked together — one truck roll, one panel evaluation, one permit, one round of wall work — they routinely shave a real fraction off what the charger would cost as its own project. The homeowners who pay the most for home charging are almost always the ones who did it as a standalone job after the fact, paying every fixed cost a second time for a circuit that could have ridden along for far less.

## Sizing the circuit and charging on your own sunshine

Bundling the install is half the story; sizing it sensibly is the other half, and here the goal is to match the circuit to the car and the habit rather than to reflexively buy the biggest charger on the shelf. Most home Level 2 chargers are installed on a 40- or 50-amp circuit, delivering somewhere in the range of 7 to 11 kilowatts, which adds tens of miles of range per hour of charging — far more than an overnight session realistically needs for typical daily driving. A larger 60-amp circuit and a charger to match cost more in wire, breaker, and sometimes panel capacity, and for a household that charges overnight and drives an average number of miles, that extra amperage often sits unused. It's worth being honest about your driving before paying for headroom you'll rarely tap. The one strong argument for oversizing the circuit is the same argument for oversizing anything electrical: pulling heavier wire while the wall is already open is cheap insurance against a future car that charges faster, and it costs far less now than re-running the circuit later.

The reason to think about all of this alongside solar, rather than as an unrelated errand, is that the panels change the economics of the fuel. An EV charged from the grid runs on whatever your utility charges per kilowatt-hour. An EV charged from your own roof, during the hours the array is producing more than the house is using, runs on power you generated at a cost far below the retail rate — often below the value you'd otherwise get for exporting that surplus. That gap is the whole reason to schedule charging for daylight when you can. A smart charger that lets you set a charging window makes this easy: point it at the midday and early-afternoon hours when the panels are flush, and a share of your driving effectively runs on sunshine you would otherwise have sold back to the utility at a lower rate. How much that actually saves depends on your rates and your schedule, and [charging an EV with solar](/blog/ev-charging-solar-savings/) walks through what it does to the numbers in more detail.

There is a sizing consequence on the solar side worth naming, because it changes the array conversation, not just the charger one. An EV is a large new annual load — a meaningful bump in the kilowatt-hours your house consumes in a year — and if you want solar to cover it, the array has to be sized with that consumption included rather than tacked on afterward. This is the same trap as the standalone charger, one layer up: sizing a solar system for your pre-EV usage and then buying the car six months later means either living with a system too small to cover the new load or paying the fixed costs of a solar expansion to bolt a few more panels on. If an EV is anywhere on your horizon, the honest move is to fold that expected consumption into the system size from the start, and [how many extra panels an EV really requires](/blog/solar-panels-for-ev-charging/) lays out roughly what that addition looks like. Run your own numbers — system price, expected charging load, and local rates — through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) before you finalize a size, and you'll end up with an array, a charger, and a wiring job that were all planned as one project instead of three expensive afterthoughts.
