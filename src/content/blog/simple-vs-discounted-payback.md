A quote says your solar system pays for itself in 8.3 years. Is that the year you actually break even? It depends on which payback the number refers to — and most quotes use the version that looks best. There are two ways to count the years until a system pays for itself, they give different answers, and the difference isn't a rounding error. It's usually one to three years, and it always runs in the direction that flatters the sales pitch.

The two methods are simple payback and discounted payback. They start from the same inputs and ask the same question — how long until cumulative savings equal net cost — but they disagree about what a dollar of future savings is worth today.

## The dollar-tomorrow problem

Simple payback treats every year's savings as equal. A $1,800 bill reduction ten years from now counts exactly the same as $1,800 saved this year. Divide net cost by annual savings and you have your answer: it's the version behind almost every quoted figure, and the one explained as the baseline in [the solar payback period](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/).

Discounted payback rejects the idea that all those dollars are equal. Money has time value — a dollar in hand today can be invested, or it's a dollar of debt not accruing interest — so a dollar of savings years from now is worth less than a dollar saved now. Discounted payback shrinks each future year's savings by a discount rate before adding it to the running total. Because every year's contribution is smaller, the cumulative savings climb more slowly, and the break-even point arrives later.

## How far apart they land

The gap is easiest to see on a worked example. Assume a net system cost of $15,000 after the federal credit and level savings of $1,800 a year, and apply a 4% discount rate to the discounted column. These are illustrative assumptions, not a quote — the shape of the result is what matters.

| Year | Simple cumulative | Discounted cumulative (4%) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | $9,000 | $8,014 |
| 8 | $14,400 | $12,119 |
| 10 | $18,000 | $14,600 |
| 11 | $19,800 | $15,769 |

Against the $15,000 net cost, the simple total crosses break-even partway through year 9 — about 8.3 years. The discounted total doesn't get there until partway through year 11 — roughly 10.3 years. Same system, same savings, a two-year difference created entirely by acknowledging the time value of money. Raise the discount rate and the gap widens; lower it and the two converge. For the discount rates most homeowners would reasonably use, the discounted figure lands one to three years past the simple one.

## The discount rate is a judgment call

The honest complication is that discounted payback requires a number nobody can hand you as a fact: the discount rate. It represents what your money could otherwise earn or what your capital costs, and reasonable people choose differently. A homeowner paying off a solar loan might use the loan's interest rate. Someone who'd otherwise invest the cash might use their expected investment return. A cautious buyer might use a low rate close to inflation.

That subjectivity is why simple payback dominates marketing — it needs no such assumption, so it can't be argued with, and it always produces the smaller number. It's also why you shouldn't accept any discounted figure without knowing the rate behind it. A "9-year discounted payback" computed at a 2% rate and one computed at 6% describe very different levels of caution. When a calculator or a spreadsheet reports a discounted result, the rate is the assumption to check first — it's one of the quiet levers behind the [payback math mistakes](/blog/solar-payback-math-mistakes/) that trip up shoppers.

## When the distinction actually matters

For a homeowner who'll keep the house for decades, the two paybacks mostly agree on the big conclusion: a system that pays back in 8 simple years and 10 discounted years is a good buy under either lens, because both fall comfortably inside the ownership horizon. Here the simple number is a fine screen, and reaching for discounting adds precision you don't need.

The distinction earns its keep at the margins. If your simple payback already runs long — pushed out by a weak export rate, a high price, or modest usage — discounting can move it past the point where you'd realistically still own the home, which changes the decision. It also matters when you're comparing solar head-to-head against another use of the same money, since that comparison is fundamentally about time value; that's the territory of a full [ROI analysis](/blog/how-to-calculate-solar-roi/), where discounting isn't optional.

The practical habit is to treat simple payback as the quick screen and the discounted view as the stress test. Run your own inputs through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/), note the simple result, then mentally add a couple of years for time value before you judge whether the system clears your personal bar. If it still clears with the years added, the discount rate you'd have quibbled over never mattered. If it only clears on the simple number, you were relying on the more flattering of the two answers.
