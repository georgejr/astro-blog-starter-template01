A 2,000-square-foot roof does not hold 2,000 square feet of panels. It rarely holds even half that. The gap between a roof's total area and the part that can actually carry solar is where most do-it-yourself estimates go wrong — homeowners measure the whole roof, divide by a panel's size, and arrive at a panel count roughly double what will ever fit. Getting a useful number means measuring the right area, not the biggest one, and you can do the whole thing from the ground.

The goal here isn't the centimeter-accurate figure an installer produces with aerial software and a site visit. It's a defensible ballpark — enough to know whether your roof can plausibly cover your usage before you invite anyone to quote it, and enough to sanity-check the numbers when they do. That estimate is worth having, and it doesn't require a ladder.

## Gross area is the wrong starting number

The distinction that fixes everything is gross versus usable roof area. Gross area is the entire roof surface. Usable area is only the portion where panels can legally and productively sit — which excludes a surprising amount:

- The faces pointing the wrong way. In the northern hemisphere, north-facing planes give up so much production they're usually skipped, and east/west faces are second-best. Orientation decides which planes even enter the calculation, a point developed in [how roof size affects solar](/blog/how-roof-size-affects-solar/).
- The setbacks. Fire codes typically require clear pathways along ridges and edges so firefighters can move on the roof, carving margins off every usable plane.
- The obstructions. Vents, chimneys, skylights, and dormers each take their own footprint plus a shaded margin around them — the layout problem covered in [planning panels around roof obstacles](/blog/roof-obstructions-solar-layout/).
- The shaded zones. Any area a tree or neighboring structure darkens for much of the day is effectively unusable even if it's structurally fine.

Measure the gross area and you've measured a ceiling nobody will ever reach. The usable area — the good-facing planes, minus setbacks, minus obstructions, minus shade — is the number that predicts panel count.

## Getting the footprint without going up

You can capture the roof's dimensions two ways from the ground, and the best estimates use both to cross-check.

### The satellite-imagery method

Open a free overhead map of your address and use its measuring tool to trace each roof plane you'd consider using. Overhead imagery gives you the *footprint* — the roof as seen from directly above — which is the horizontal projection, not the true sloped surface. That's fine, because you'll correct for slope in the next step. Trace only the planes with acceptable orientation, and note each one's length and width.

### The ground-measurement cross-check

For a simple gable roof, you can estimate the footprint by measuring the building at ground level. The horizontal run of one roof plane is roughly half the building's width (from outside wall to ridge), and its length matches the building's length. Pacing or tape-measuring the outside walls gives you numbers to check against what you traced on the map. When the two methods roughly agree, trust the figure.

## Correcting for pitch

Both methods so far give you the flat footprint. The actual roof surface is larger, because it's tilted — a sloped plane covers more material than its shadow on the ground. Multiply the footprint by a pitch factor to recover the true area. Steeper roofs have larger factors.

| Roof pitch (rise/run) | Approximate angle | Multiply footprint by |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3/12 | ~14° | 1.03 |
| 4/12 | ~18° | 1.05 |
| 6/12 | ~27° | 1.12 |
| 9/12 | ~37° | 1.25 |
| 12/12 | 45° | 1.41 |

You can estimate pitch from the ground by eye against these categories, or read it off a photo of the roofline. Apply the factor to each plane's footprint and you have its true surface area. This is also the point where roof angle starts affecting production, not just area — a relationship worth understanding on its own, but for measurement it's purely about converting flat square feet into sloped square feet.

## From square feet to panels

Now subtract the unusable portions — setbacks along edges and ridges, the footprint of each obstruction plus a working margin — from each plane's true area. What remains is your usable area. Convert it to a panel count using the size of a standard residential module.

A typical home panel measures roughly 3.3 by 5.5 feet, which works out to about 18 square feet each. That single figure is the bridge from area to count: divide your usable square footage by roughly 18 and you have the rough number of panels the roof can hold. Build in a little extra per panel for the small gaps between modules and around the array, so in practice each panel lays claim to closer to 18 to 20 square feet once spacing is included. Panel sizes do vary by model — the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) lets you run the division with specific dimensions, and [solar panel dimensions](/blog/solar-panel-dimensions/) covers how much modules differ.

Worked through with round assumptions: a single south-facing plane with a 22-foot footprint width and 30-foot length has a 660-square-foot footprint; at a 6/12 pitch that's about 740 true square feet; strip perhaps 20% for setbacks and a vent and you're left with roughly 590 usable square feet, or about 30 to 33 panels. That's the kind of number worth having before a single installer visits.

## What your estimate is good for — and what it isn't

A ground-based measurement is a screening tool, not a final design. It tells you whether the roof is in the right ballpark to cover your usage — pair the panel count with your kilowatt-hour needs using [how many solar panels you need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) and you'll know quickly whether the roof is roomy, tight, or a genuine constraint. It also arms you to question a quote that assumes far more panels than your usable area could hold.

What it can't do is replace the installer's site survey. A professional uses precise aerial data and an on-site inspection to account for exact shading through the seasons, structural condition, obstruction clearances, and the electrical layout — the full suitability picture no ground-based estimate can capture. Your estimate and their survey should land in the same neighborhood; if a quoted design is wildly larger than your usable area suggests, that's the discrepancy to ask about before you sign.
