Picture a 5 kW array installed around 2013: twenty 250-watt panels covering most of a south-facing roof. At an assumed 0.5% annual degradation, it still puts out roughly 94% of its original rating after a dozen years — respectable, and nowhere near dead. The reason owners of systems like this start shopping isn't failure. It's that the household grew, an EV showed up in the driveway, or the panels that would fit that same roof today are rated 400 watts and up. The old array works fine; it just no longer matches the house.

That gap opens up three genuinely different paths, and homeowners tend to blur them together into one vague idea of "upgrading." You can add capacity to what you already own, replace whatever specific piece has failed, or tear the existing system down and rebuild it from scratch. Each of those answers a different question, costs a different amount, and makes sense in a different situation. Pick the one that matches your actual problem and you spend the minimum to fix it. Pick the wrong one and you either overspend on a system that was mostly fine, or you patch something that should have been retired. So before comparing prices or calling installers, the useful work is diagnostic: figuring out what is actually limiting you.

## Three problems hiding under one word

"Upgrading" collapses three distinct situations into a single word, and untangling them is most of the decision. Adding panels answers a production shortfall — you're generating less than you now consume, usually because a new load arrived or your bill crept up, and you have unused roof to work with. Replacing hardware answers a discrete failure — one component died on a system that was otherwise doing its job, most often the inverter. Repowering answers obsolescence — the panels are old and low-wattage, the electronics are past warranty, and the whole thing is simply behind what the same roof could do today.

The reason it matters to name the problem first is that the paths interfere with each other when you get them out of order. Bolt four new panels onto a system whose inverter is in its final years and you've stranded that new hardware behind a box that's about to quit. Try to replace three cracked panels on a 2013 array one-for-one and you're hunting for discontinued 250-watt modules that electrically match the surviving strings. Decide to repower a system that only needed a $2,000 inverter swap and you've thrown away a decade of perfectly good panels. Each path assumes something about the state of the rest of the system, and those assumptions have to hold.

Start with a blunt inventory. How old is the array, and what were the panels rated when they went up? Is the inverter original, and is it inside or outside its expected service window? Is anything actually broken right now, or is the system healthy but too small? And critically, how much life is left in the roof underneath it? A twelve-year-old array producing 94% of nameplate on a roof with fifteen years left is a very different animal from a fifteen-year-old array with a failing inverter on shingles that are due for replacement. The first wants, at most, a modest addition. The second is a repower candidate. Nothing about the panel count tells you which — the surrounding condition does.

There's a temptation, once you start looking at modern equipment, to treat any of this as an excuse to modernize the whole system. Resist it until the diagnosis earns it. A solar array that quietly loses a fraction of a percent of output each year is aging exactly as designed, not failing, and that slow fade rarely justifies more than an eventual inverter replacement. Knowing how gentle that decline actually is helps here: the year-by-year losses covered in [what your system loses each year](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/) are small enough that "the panels are old" is, by itself, a weak reason to replace anything. Old and working is a perfectly good state for a solar panel to be in.

## Adding capacity to a system that still works

Expansion is the cleanest move when the diagnosis comes back "healthy but undersized." You keep everything that works and add production on top of it. The appeal is obvious and mostly real — you're not discarding functional hardware, just supplementing it. The complications are electrical rather than mechanical, and they're the reason a "just add a few panels" quote often reads higher than a homeowner expects.

The first constraint is your inverter. Every inverter has a maximum input it's rated to handle, and if your current array already sits near that ceiling, new panels can't simply share the connection — there's no headroom for them. In that case the addition needs its own path to the grid: its own microinverters, or a second string inverter dedicated to the new panels. That's ordinary and safe, but it means the expansion carries more gear than the panel count alone suggests, which is a large part of why the arithmetic isn't linear.

The second constraint is mixing old and new panels. Modern 400-plus-watt modules are physically larger and electrically different from a 2013-era 250-watt panel, and you generally can't string mismatched panels together without the weaker ones dragging on the string's performance. The standard fix is to run the new panels as an electrically separate subsystem — their own string or their own microinverters — rather than trying to blend them with the original array. Again, workable, but it shapes the design and the cost. The full picture of what a real-world addition runs, including these not-obvious line items, is laid out in [the cost of adding panels to an existing solar system](/blog/cost-to-add-solar-panels-existing-system/), and it's worth reading before you assume expansion is the cheap option by default.

Then there's the utility question, which is easy to overlook and occasionally decisive. Increasing your system's size can trigger a fresh interconnection review, and in some territories crossing a size threshold can move you off the arrangement you originally signed up for and onto a newer, less generous one. If you interconnected years ago under a favorable net metering deal, that legacy arrangement can be worth more than the extra kilowatt-hours a few new panels would produce. It's genuinely possible for an expansion to add production while subtracting value, if it costs you a grandfathered rate. Confirm what your utility does with additions before you commit, because that answer can flip the whole calculation.

To scope an addition sensibly, work backward from what you're short. Look at how much your consumption has grown, translate that into the extra annual kilowatt-hours you need, and check whether your remaining roof area can physically hold enough panels to cover it. Sizing that gap — rather than guessing at a round number of panels — is what the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) is for: it starts from target output and available space, which is exactly the framing an addition needs. The goal is to add enough to close the gap and not much more, because every extra panel that pushes you into a new inverter or a new utility tariff earns its keep only if the math survives those thresholds.

## Replacing a failure versus repowering the whole array

The other two paths both involve taking something off the roof, but they sit at opposite ends of the scale, and the line between them is where homeowners most often overspend or underspend. Replacement is surgical: one component has failed on a system that's otherwise sound, and you swap the weak link. Repowering is wholesale: the entire array comes down and a new one goes up. The judgment call is knowing when you've crossed from the first into the second.

Replacement is almost always about the inverter, because that's the component most likely to fail first. Panels themselves are remarkably durable and rarely quit; the electronics running hot in an enclosure are what wear out, typically somewhere in a ten-to-fifteen-year window depending on the type and how much heat they've endured. When an inverter dies on an eight-year-old system with healthy panels, the decision writes itself — you replace the inverter, not the array, and the system goes back to work. The broader pattern of what tends to fail and when is covered in [how long solar panels really last](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/), and the short version is that "the system stopped producing" usually points at one box, not the whole roof.

Panel replacement is the trickier flavor of the same idea. If hail cracked a few modules or a couple are showing hot spots, you replace those specific panels — but matching a discontinued 2013 panel is the catch that makes it less simple than it sounds. You can't always drop a modern module into an old string without upsetting its voltage, so installers work around it with a compatible substitute and some rewiring, or by pulling the odd panel onto its own microinverter. That's fine for one or two panels. It stops being sensible when the count climbs, because at some point you're rebuilding the array piece by piece at retail prices.

That's the fork into repowering. Repowering means removing the old array entirely and installing a new one, often reusing the roof and sometimes the racking. It's the most expensive path and the one that makes sense least often, but for the right system it's the only option that fully resets the clock. The case for it tends to stack up: the panels are old and low-wattage, the inverter is out of warranty, the same roof could hold 40% or more capacity with modern modules, and — the load-bearing condition — the roof itself has years of life left. When all of that is true, pouring money into targeted repairs is spending on a system that's structurally behind, and a repower buys you current efficiency, a fresh warranty, and a size matched to today's household rather than the one you had a decade ago.

The shrewd version of a repower is timing it with a re-roof. If the shingles are due within a few years anyway, doing the roof and the new solar install together avoids paying twice to get panels off and back on. The economics of a repower live or die on reusing the roof and structure, so the right way to evaluate it is to run the payback on the new system as if it were a fresh install — because functionally it is. Feed the new system's cost and your actual electricity rate into the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) and see whether the production gain justifies the outlay, then compare that against the far cheaper option of simply replacing the failed inverter and living with the older panels a while longer. Often the targeted repair captures most of the value for a fraction of the cost, and the calculator is what shows you whether the full repower's extra spend actually pays back or just buys you newer-looking hardware.

Work from the failure, not the temptation, at every step. The cheapest fix that resolves your real constraint is almost always the right one, and a working twelve-year-old array is worth considerably more than upgrade marketing implies. Match the intervention to the diagnosis, confirm what any change does to your utility arrangement before you sign, and let the payback math settle the close calls rather than the appeal of a roof full of new panels.
