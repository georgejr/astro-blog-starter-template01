Picture a 5 kW array installed around 2013: twenty 250-watt panels covering most of a south-facing roof. At an assumed 0.5% annual degradation, it still puts out roughly 94% of its original rating after a dozen years — respectable, and nowhere near dead. The reason owners of systems like this start shopping isn't failure. It's that the household grew, an EV showed up in the driveway, or the panels that would fit that same roof today are rated 400 watts and up. The old array works fine; it just no longer matches the house.

That gap creates three distinct paths, and they are not interchangeable. You can add capacity, replace what's broken, or tear the whole thing down and rebuild. Each solves a different problem, and choosing the wrong one wastes money on a working system.

## Three paths, three different problems

The right move depends on what's actually wrong, and the three options map to three different problems. Adding panels answers too little production — the trigger is usually a new load, a higher bill, or roof space going unused. Replacing parts answers a specific failure, most often a dead inverter or a handful of bad panels. Repowering answers aging and undersizing at once: old low-wattage panels, out-of-warranty hardware, and a roof that still has years left.

The mistake is treating these as a single "upgrade" decision. Adding panels to a system whose inverter is near the end of its life can strand that new hardware behind a failing box. Replacing panels one-for-one on a 2013 array means hunting for discontinued 250-watt modules to match the strings. Knowing which problem you have comes first.

## Adding panels to what you already own

Expansion is the cleanest option when the existing system is healthy and you simply need more kilowatt-hours. The appeal is obvious: you keep everything that works and bolt on capacity. The complications are electrical, not mechanical.

Your existing inverter has a maximum input, and if the current array already sits near that ceiling, new panels need somewhere to go — often their own microinverters or a second string inverter rather than a shared connection. Mismatched panel ages and wattages don't play well on the same string, so most expansions run the new panels as an electrically separate subsystem. That's normal, but it means the "just add four panels" quote often includes more than four panels' worth of gear.

There's also the interconnection question. Growing your system's size can trigger a fresh utility review, and in some territories it can move you onto a different, less generous export arrangement than the one you originally signed up for. That's worth checking before you commit, because a favorable legacy net metering deal is sometimes worth more than the extra production. The full picture of what expansion runs is worked through in [the cost of adding panels to an existing solar system](/blog/cost-to-add-solar-panels-existing-system/). To sanity-check how much you'd actually need to add, start from roof area and target output with the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/).

## Replacing what fails, not what works

Panels rarely fail; the electronics around them do. An inverter is the component most likely to give out first, typically somewhere in a 10-to-15-year window depending on type and heat exposure. When one dies on an otherwise sound system, replacement is the obvious call — you're swapping the weak link, not the array.

Panel replacement is a narrower case. If a few modules are cracked by hail or showing hot spots, you replace those, but matching a discontinued panel is the catch. A modern 400-watt module is physically larger and electrically different from a 2013-era 250-watt panel, so you can't always drop one into an existing string without upsetting its voltage. Installers often work around this with a compatible substitute panel and some rewiring, or by relocating the odd panel to its own microinverter.

The judgment call is whether piecemeal replacement is throwing good money after old equipment. A single dead inverter on a 6-year-old system? Replace it. Three failed panels plus a tired inverter on a 13-year-old array with obsolete modules? That's the signal to stop patching. The decision framework for that fork is laid out in [repairing versus replacing a failing panel](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/), and the degradation curve behind "how much am I really losing" is covered in [what your system loses each year](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/).

## Repowering: starting over on a roof that's still good

Repowering means removing the old array and installing an entirely new one, reusing the roof and sometimes the racking. It's the most expensive path and the one that makes sense least often, but for the right system it's the only one that fully resets the clock.

The case for it usually stacks up like this: the panels are old and low-wattage, the inverter is out of warranty, the same roof could hold 40% or more capacity with modern modules, and — critically — the roof itself has years of life left. When all of that is true, spending on repairs is pouring money into a system that's structurally behind. A repower gives you current efficiency, a fresh warranty, and a system sized to today's household rather than 2013's.

Timing it with a re-roof is the shrewd version of this move. If the shingles are due within a few years anyway, doing the roof and the repower together avoids paying twice to get panels off and back on. The economics of a repower live or die on that reuse of roof and structure, so run the payback on the new system as if it were a fresh install — because it essentially is. The [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) will tell you whether a repower's production gain justifies its cost given your rates, or whether targeted replacement gets you most of the benefit for a fraction of the outlay.

## How to decide without overspending

Work from the failure, not the temptation. Start by identifying what's actually limiting you: production, a broken component, or genuine obsolescence. A system that merely produces a little less each year is aging normally and rarely justifies more than an eventual inverter swap. A system that can't keep up with a bigger house wants panels added. A system that's old, undersized, and out of warranty on a sound roof is the repower candidate.

The trap at every step is over-solving. The cheapest fix that addresses your actual constraint is almost always the right one, and a working twelve-year-old array is worth more than the upgrade marketing suggests. Match the intervention to the problem, confirm what any change does to your utility arrangement, and let the payback math — not the appeal of new hardware — settle it.
