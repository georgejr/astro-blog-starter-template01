If you lease your solar panels or sign a power purchase agreement, you do not get the federal solar tax credit. The company that owns the equipment does. This single fact surprises more homeowners than almost anything else in solar, because the panels are on your roof, feeding your house, and it feels like they should be yours in every way that counts. But the tax credit does not follow the roof; it follows ownership, and in a lease or PPA arrangement, a third party owns the hardware from the day it is installed. That ownership is what earns them the credit and denies it to you, and understanding the rule behind it clears up a lot of the confusion that leasing salespeople are not always eager to resolve.

The stakes are not small. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is currently worth 30% of a system's cost, which on a typical residential array runs into five figures. Whether that money lands in your pocket, in the leasing company's, or somewhere in between depending on how the deal is structured, is one of the biggest financial differences between buying and leasing, and it deserves to be understood clearly before you sign anything.

## Why ownership is the dividing line

The credit at the center of this is the Residential Clean Energy Credit, the federal incentive that lets a homeowner reduce their federal income taxes by 30% of what they spend on a qualifying solar system, subject to the eligibility and documentation rules the IRS lays out. The word doing the heavy lifting in that description is "spend." The credit is designed to reward the party that pays for the system, and it is claimed by that party on their own tax return. When you buy panels, whether with cash or a loan, you are the one who paid for them, you own them, and you claim the credit. The mechanics of doing so, and the tax-liability requirements that come with it, are worth understanding in full through the general guide to [how the solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/), because the credit has real conditions attached even for owners.

A lease or a power purchase agreement inverts the ownership from the start. In both structures, a third-party company, the leasing provider or the PPA financier, buys and installs the equipment and retains ownership of it. You are not purchasing the panels; you are purchasing something else. Under a lease, you pay a fixed monthly amount to rent the system and use the power it makes. Under a PPA, you pay a per-kilowatt-hour rate for the electricity the system produces, buying the output rather than renting the hardware. In neither case do you own the equipment, and in neither case did you make the capital purchase the credit rewards. So the credit belongs to the owner, the third party, and they claim it, typically under the business version of the incentive, the commercial Investment Tax Credit, since they own the system as a business asset. The homeowner claims nothing, because from the tax code's point of view the homeowner did not buy a solar system. They bought a service.

A prepaid lease sits in an awkward middle ground that trips up even careful shoppers, so it is worth naming directly. Some companies offer a lease you pay off in a single lump sum at the start rather than in monthly installments, and because you are handing over a large amount of money up front, it can feel like buying. It is not. Ownership of the equipment still rests with the leasing company throughout the term, which means the tax credit still belongs to them, not to you, no matter how large your upfront payment was. The same holds for a PPA where you prepay for years of electricity. The test is never how much you paid or when you paid it; the test is who owns the hardware, and in every lease and PPA structure, prepaid or not, that owner is the third party. If a deal is being described to you as ownership because of a big upfront payment, the question that cuts through it is simple: whose name is on the equipment, and who is claiming the federal credit on their tax return? If the answer to either is "the company," you are leasing, whatever the paperwork is called.

This is why the answer to "do leased solar panels qualify for the tax credit" is genuinely yes and no at the same time, and the ambiguity is where marketing gets slippery. The panels themselves qualify, and someone absolutely claims the 30% credit on them. It is just not you. A leasing company can truthfully tell a prospect that the system "qualifies for the federal tax credit," and a homeowner can walk away believing they will get a check from the IRS, when in fact the company is the one collecting it. The precise question to ask is not whether the system qualifies but who claims the credit on it, and the honest answer for any lease or PPA is the third-party owner. If a salesperson is vague about this, that vagueness is itself information.

There is a genuine upside worth stating fairly, because leasing is not a scam and the credit does not simply vanish into the company's coffers with no benefit to you. A well-run leasing company uses the tax credit it collects to lower the price of the deal it offers you, folding the value of that 30% into a monthly payment or a per-kilowatt-hour rate that is lower than it could otherwise be. In that sense you can receive part of the credit's value indirectly, passed through as a better price, without ever claiming it yourself. The problem is that you cannot easily see how much, if any, of the credit was passed through, because the lease payment is a single number that bundles the equipment cost, the financing, the company's margin, and whatever share of the credit they chose to hand back. You are trusting that the competition among leasing companies has driven that pass-through to a fair level, which it may or may not have. Contrast that with buying, where the 30% comes to you directly, transparently, and in an amount you can calculate yourself with a [federal tax credit calculator](/federal-tax-credit-calculator/) rather than having to take on faith.

## What this means when you are choosing how to pay

The tax credit's ownership rule is one of the strongest arguments for buying over leasing, and it is worth weighing with clear eyes rather than letting it settle the question by itself. When you buy, you capture the full 30% credit directly, assuming you have enough federal tax liability to use it, and you own an appreciating asset that adds value to your home and keeps saving you money long after any loan is paid off. When you lease or sign a PPA, you forgo the direct credit, you own nothing at the end of the term, and you have signed up for years of payments that, depending on the escalator built into the contract, may rise over time. The difference in lifetime cost between the two paths is often substantial, and the lost tax credit is a meaningful part of that gap. The full comparison of what leasing versus buying actually costs over the life of the system is worked through in the guide to [the cost difference between leasing and buying](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/), which puts real numbers on a decision that the monthly-payment framing tends to obscure.

That said, leasing exists because it solves a real problem for some households, and the tax credit rule, while a genuine drawback, does not automatically make leasing the wrong choice for everyone. The clearest case is a homeowner who cannot use the tax credit at all. Because the credit is nonrefundable, you need federal tax liability to absorb it, and a retiree on a modest fixed income, or anyone whose tax bill is smaller than the credit, may not be able to claim the full 30% even if they buy. For that person, the credit is not really "lost" in a lease, because they could not have fully used it anyway, and letting a leasing company monetize it and pass part of the value back through a lower payment can be a rational way to capture something rather than nothing. Whether your tax situation lets you use the credit is a question worth resolving early, and the guidance on tax-liability limits in the broader treatment of the credit is the place to work that out before you assume buying gives you the full 30%.

One caution belongs alongside the case for leasing, because the same feature that makes the tax credit irrelevant to a low-tax-liability homeowner can bite later. A lease or PPA is a long contract, often twenty years or more, and it can complicate selling your home, since a buyer has to be willing to assume the agreement or the seller has to buy it out. An owned system, by contrast, is an asset that generally adds value and transfers cleanly with the house. So the decision to lease trades away not only the direct tax credit but some flexibility down the road, and that trade should be weighed with the same clarity as the credit itself. For a homeowner who plans to stay put and cannot use the credit anyway, it may be a fair bargain; for one who might move, or who could use the full 30% by buying, the leasing path gives up more than the credit alone.

The other honest point in leasing's favor is that a PPA or lease shifts the responsibilities of ownership onto the company. They own the equipment, so they handle the maintenance, the monitoring, and the repairs, and you are buying a predictable outcome rather than taking on a piece of hardware you are responsible for over twenty-five years. Some homeowners value that offloading of risk enough to accept the worse economics, and there is nothing irrational about it, as long as you go in understanding that you are paying for that convenience partly with a tax credit you will never see. The structure and true cost of a PPA specifically, where you pay for power rather than panels, is laid out in the guide to [paying for power instead of panels](/blog/solar-ppa-costs-explained/), and it is worth reading before you assume a PPA's low headline rate tells the whole story.

The clean way to decide is to put all three paths side by side rather than comparing a lease payment against your current electric bill, which flatters the lease. A [payment comparison tool](/solar-payment-comparison/) that lines up cash, loan, and lease over the full life of the system lets you see where the tax credit lands in each case and what the total cost of each path really is. When you do that, the ownership rule stops being an abstract tax detail and becomes what it actually is: one of the largest single line items separating the path where a five-figure credit comes to you from the path where it goes to someone else. Neither path is wrong for every household, but no one should sign a lease believing they will collect a credit that, by the plain logic of ownership, belongs to the company that owns their panels.
