Home battery shopping goes sideways for a predictable reason: the marketing leads with the brand and the sleek enclosure, while the numbers that actually determine whether the thing will run your house get buried on page three of the datasheet. Two batteries can look nearly identical in a glossy comparison and behave very differently in your garage during an outage. The good news is that you don't need to know one manufacturer's reputation from another's to compare them well. You need four numbers, read the same way for every unit on your list, and a clear sense of which ones matter for what you're trying to do. Line the candidates up on usable capacity, continuous power, round-trip efficiency, and warranty terms, and the brand names stop mattering — the specs sort themselves out.

## Usable capacity, not the headline number

The first number every battery leads with is its capacity in kilowatt-hours, and it's also the first place comparisons go wrong, because the figure on the front of the brochure is often the *rated* or *nominal* capacity — the total energy the cells hold — not the *usable* capacity you're actually allowed to draw. Those are different numbers, sometimes meaningfully different, and comparing one battery's rated figure to another's usable figure is how people end up buying the wrong thing.

The gap exists because lithium batteries don't like being run all the way to empty. Fully discharging a cell repeatedly shortens its life, so manufacturers reserve a slice of the total capacity that the system simply won't touch, protecting the chemistry. The share you're permitted to use is the depth of discharge, and the usable capacity is the rated capacity multiplied by that percentage. A battery rated at 13.5 kWh with a 100% usable spec gives you all 13.5; another rated at 14 kWh but limited to 90% usable actually delivers about 12.6, less than the smaller-sounding unit. If you compared only the headline numbers, you'd pick the worse battery. This is exactly the distinction that trips people up between the total energy stored and the energy you can spend, and it's worth reading [usable versus rated capacity through the kWh-versus-kW lens](/blog/battery-kwh-vs-kw/) before you shop, because the same confusion resurfaces in the power spec too.

So the first rule of comparison is to find the *usable* kilowatt-hour figure for every battery — and if a spec sheet only advertises the rated number, treat the omission as a question to ask, not a detail to assume away. Once you have usable capacity for each candidate, you can translate it into something concrete: how long it runs your loads. A battery holding 12 kWh usable will power a load drawing 1 kW for roughly 12 hours, or a 2 kW load for about six, before efficiency losses and reserve settings are accounted for. That back-of-envelope math is how you turn an abstract spec into "will this get my fridge, some lights, and the internet through a typical outage," and it's the foundation any honest sizing exercise builds on once you feed it your actual essential loads. The point isn't to memorize a runtime; it's to compare batteries on the same usable basis so a bigger-sounding unit doesn't win on a number you'll never get to use.

## Power, efficiency, and the specs that decide what it can actually run

Capacity tells you how much energy a battery stores. It says nothing about how fast that energy can come out — and speed is what determines whether the battery can start your air conditioner or just trickle power to a few lights. That's the power spec, measured in kilowatts, and it's the number people most often overlook because it doesn't appear in the headline. A battery is really two ratings working together: how much it holds (kWh) and how hard it can push (kW). You need both to be adequate, and a shortfall in either one caps what the battery can do regardless of how strong the other looks.

Power comes in two flavors, and comparing batteries means checking both. Continuous power is what the unit can deliver steadily, hour after hour — the figure that decides how many appliances you can run at once. Peak or surge power is a brief, higher output the battery can supply for a few seconds to handle the startup jolt of motors: the compressor in a refrigerator or air conditioner, a well pump, a sump pump. Motor loads can momentarily draw several times their running wattage at the instant they switch on, so a battery with healthy continuous power but a weak surge rating can stall exactly when a big appliance kicks in, even though it could easily run that appliance once it's going. When you compare batteries for backup, put their continuous and surge numbers side by side against the real loads you care about. A unit rated for 5 kW continuous and, say, 7 kW surge is a very different backup machine from one rated at 3.8 kW continuous with little headroom, even if they store identical energy. Which one you need depends entirely on what you're trying to keep running, and the honest way to decide is to add up the running and starting demands of your intended loads first — the logic laid out in [how home solar batteries work](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/) — rather than assuming any battery can "back up the house."

Round-trip efficiency is the third number, and it's the one almost nobody asks about even though it quietly taxes every cycle. No battery returns all the energy you put into it; some is lost as heat in the charging and discharging process and in the electronics that convert between the battery's DC and your home's AC. Round-trip efficiency is the share that survives the round trip, and typical home batteries land somewhere in the high eighties to low nineties percent. The difference between 86% and 92% sounds small, but it compounds. Every kilowatt-hour you send in comes back as only 0.86 or 0.92, day after day, for the life of the system. Over thousands of cycles that gap adds up to real energy — and real money, if you're storing solar you could otherwise have exported for credit, or grid power you bought to shift into peak hours. When two batteries are close on capacity and power, efficiency is a legitimate tiebreaker, and a suspiciously low efficiency figure is worth a second look. The full weight this number carries in the economics is worked through in the broader question of whether storage earns its keep at all, covered in [is a solar battery worth it](/blog/solar-battery-worth-it/).

One caution on power ratings ties the section together: some batteries can raise their continuous output when you stack multiple units together, so a single unit's spec may understate what a two- or three-battery configuration delivers. If you're comparing a plan for one battery against a plan for two, make sure you're reading the power figure for the configuration you'd actually install, not the per-unit number, because stacking changes the math on both capacity and power at once.

## Warranty, chemistry, and turning specs into a decision

The fourth number is the warranty, and it's the closest thing you get to the manufacturer telling you how long they expect the battery to last. Battery warranties are more involved than panel warranties because they usually promise several things at once, and comparing them means reading all of the terms rather than the headline year count. Look for the guaranteed term in years, the number of cycles or the total energy throughput covered — sometimes expressed in megawatt-hours delivered over the life — and, critically, the end-of-warranty capacity guarantee, which states how much of the original usable capacity the manufacturer promises will remain at the end. A battery warrantied to retain 70% of capacity after ten years is making a stronger promise than one that only guarantees a term with no capacity floor. Read whichever limit comes first, because most warranties expire at either the year mark or the throughput mark, whichever you hit sooner — a heavily cycled battery in daily use may reach its energy limit well before its calendar years run out.

Chemistry is worth a glance because it shapes the specs above and the safety profile, though it's rarely a decision you make in isolation. Most current home batteries use one of two lithium chemistries, and they trade off differently: one prioritizes energy density and the other prioritizes cycle life and thermal stability. You don't need to become a chemist to compare batteries, but knowing that a given unit's long cycle warranty or its placement flexibility often traces back to its chemistry helps you understand *why* two batteries differ, rather than just *that* they do. The practical point is that chemistry mostly shows up already baked into the four numbers you're comparing — so compare the numbers, and let the chemistry explain the differences rather than drive them.

Two practical attributes sit alongside the four core numbers and can quietly break a tie. The first is where the battery is rated to live: some units carry an outdoor and wide-temperature rating that lets them hang on an exterior wall in a hot or cold climate, while others need a conditioned garage or basement, and a unit that can't tolerate your local temperature swings is a poor fit no matter how good its specs. Cold, in particular, saps usable capacity and charging speed in most chemistries, so a battery mounted in an unheated space in a northern winter won't deliver its rated numbers when you need them most. The second is expandability: if there's any chance you'll want more storage later, a battery designed to stack additional units on the same system is worth more than one that would force a parallel, separately managed install down the road. Neither attribute shows up in the headline capacity figure, and both are cheaper to get right at purchase than to fix afterward.

Putting it together, the way to compare home batteries without drowning in brand claims is to build a simple side-by-side on four rows: usable kWh, continuous and surge kW, round-trip efficiency, and warranty terms (years, throughput, and end-of-life capacity). Fill those in for every candidate from the actual datasheet — not the marketing page — and normalize them to the same basis, usable to usable, configuration to configuration. Then weight them against your goal. If you want whole-home backup, power ratings and surge headroom dominate. If you want to shift solar or ride out shorter outages on essentials, usable capacity and efficiency matter more. If you plan to cycle the battery hard every single day, the throughput warranty is the number that tells you whether it'll survive the duty you're planning.
