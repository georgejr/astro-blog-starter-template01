An all-electric home is a wonderful thing right up until the grid goes down in January. In a house with a gas furnace, a gas range, and a gas water heater, a power outage is an inconvenience: you lose lights and the refrigerator, but you can still cook and stay warm. Strip the gas out and hand every one of those jobs to electricity, and suddenly the same outage threatens your heat, your hot water, and your dinner all at once. That's the double edge of electrification. The house is cleaner, often cheaper to run, and simpler to power from your own solar, but it concentrates every essential function onto a single utility, which raises the stakes on backup power considerably. The question "how much battery do I need?" therefore has a very different answer for a gas-free home than for a conventional one, and the honest version of that answer starts with a hard truth about heating.

Electric heat is the load that breaks naive battery sizing. Cooking, lights, the refrigerator, even the water heater in short bursts, these are manageable. Space heating in cold weather is a different order of magnitude, and it's the reason all-electric backup planning has to be deliberate rather than optimistic.

## Why electric heat dominates the sizing problem

To size any battery, you first have to know what you're trying to run and for how long, and in an all-electric home the appliances sort themselves into wildly different weight classes. Lighting, phone charging, internet, and a modern refrigerator together might draw only a handful of kilowatt-hours across a full day. Induction cooking is punchy but brief: a burner can pull a couple of thousand watts while it's on, but you cook for minutes, not hours, so a day of meals adds only a few kilowatt-hours. An electric or heat-pump water heater is heavier, but it heats in cycles rather than running constantly, so its daily total is bounded. If those were the only loads, backing up an all-electric home for a day or two would be straightforward and affordable.

Heating blows that up. A cold-climate house being kept comfortable by electric resistance heat can pull the equivalent of a space heater in every room, tens of kilowatt-hours across a frigid day, easily dwarfing everything else combined. A heat pump is far more efficient, delivering several units of heat per unit of electricity, which is exactly why all-electric homes lean on them. But even an efficient heat pump working against a genuinely cold outdoor temperature, when its efficiency sags and its backup resistance elements may kick in, can consume a large share of a home's daily energy. Put a realistic number on it: a well-insulated all-electric home might draw somewhere around 25 to 40 kilowatt-hours on a cold winter day with heating running, and a leaky or larger one considerably more. Treat that as a planning band, not a guarantee, because it swings hard with your climate, your insulation, and how warm you keep the house. The point is the scale. That single number tells you immediately that trying to run whole-home electric heat off batteries through a multi-day winter outage is a very expensive proposition, because you'd need a wall of storage to do it.

This is where the seductive phrase "whole-home backup" collides with arithmetic. A single popular home battery holds on the order of 10 to 13 kilowatt-hours. If your all-electric home burns 30 kilowatt-hours on a cold day, one battery covers roughly a partial day of everything, or a full day if you're careful and the weather cooperates. Backing up unrestricted whole-home heating for two or three days of a real cold snap could take three, four, or more batteries, which pushes the cost into territory that rarely makes sense for the handful of long outages most homes actually experience. Understanding your home's real daily consumption is the foundation for all of this, and it's worth pinning down your own figure first; our look at [what it costs to run an all-electric home each month](/blog/all-electric-home-running-cost/) helps you translate your bills into the daily kilowatt-hours a battery has to cover.

The way out isn't a bigger checkbook. It's being honest about what you actually need to keep running when the grid fails, which almost never turns out to be the whole house at full comfort.

## Sizing backup you can actually afford

The productive move is to split your loads into "keep alive" and "let it go," then size storage for the first group only. This is the essential-loads philosophy, and in an all-electric home it's less about saving money and more about making backup physically possible. Your keep-alive list is short and non-negotiable: refrigerator and freezer so food doesn't spoil, enough lighting and device charging to function, internet and communications, and any medical equipment. That bundle typically runs only a few kilowatt-hours a day, which a single battery can carry comfortably for a day or more. Everything else, the water heater, the oven, the dryer, and above all the space heating, goes on the "let it go or ration it" list during an outage. We walk through how to draw that line cleanly in [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), and it's the single most important decision in all-electric backup planning.

Heat is the hard case, because in a cold climate "let it go" isn't really an option, but "run it flat out" isn't affordable. The compromise most people land on is partial, rationed heating. Rather than keeping the whole house at 70 degrees, you back up the heat pump enough to keep one zone or the main living area at a survivable temperature, accept that bedrooms get cold, and lean on the heat pump's efficiency to stretch the battery. A modern heat pump running in mild-cold conditions, where its efficiency is still strong, sips far less than resistance heat would, so backing up limited heating becomes plausible with one or two batteries where whole-home resistance heat would take a small warehouse of them. If the cold is severe enough that the heat pump falls back on its resistance elements, though, the draw spikes, and no reasonable battery bank keeps that going for long. Knowing where your particular heat pump lands on that efficiency curve at your design temperature is genuinely useful for sizing, and you can get a feel for its consumption with the [heat pump calculator](/heat-pump-calculator/) before you decide how much of it to back up.

A worked sketch makes the trade-off visible. Assume your keep-alive loads total 5 kWh a day, and you want to add partial heat via a heat pump that, in the moderate cold you're planning for, draws about 1.5 kilowatts while running and cycles maybe eight hours across the day, so roughly 12 kWh for heat. That's about 17 kWh a day of backed-up load. A pair of 13 kWh batteries, discharged sensibly, could carry that for a bit over a day between charges. If you have solar and the outage falls in sunny weather, the panels recharge the batteries each day and you can potentially ride out an extended outage indefinitely, because you're only using storage to bridge the nights. That solar-plus-storage combination is the real endgame for a resilient all-electric home, and it's also where the everyday savings live, because the same panels that recharge your battery in an outage are trimming your heating and cooking bills the other 360 days of the year. If the outage falls during a dark, snowy stretch when the panels make little, you're back to rationing hard, which is simply the reality of winter storage math and worth accepting up front.

Two practical cautions round this out. First, cold hurts batteries too. A lithium battery mounted in an unconditioned garage or on an exterior wall in a South Dakota-style winter loses usable capacity and charges more slowly when it's cold, so the winter performance you plan around should be discounted from the summer nameplate, and placement in a conditioned or insulated space matters more in an all-electric cold-climate home than almost anywhere else. Second, plan for the home you'll have, not just the one you have now. All-electric households tend to keep electrifying, adding an EV or a second heat pump zone, and every new electric load raises the daily total the battery might need to cover during an outage. Sizing with a little headroom, or choosing a system you can expand later, saves you from re-doing the whole install in three years.

## A step-by-step sizing pass for a gas-free home

Pulling the pieces together, here is the order of operations that keeps an all-electric backup plan honest. Start by writing down every load you might want during an outage and sorting it into keep-alive and optional, then attach a rough daily kilowatt-hour figure to each keep-alive item: a few kilowatt-hours for the fridge, freezer, lights, internet, and devices, plus anything medical. That first subtotal is your non-negotiable floor, and it is usually small, on the order of a single battery's worth for a day or more.

Next, decide how much heating you actually need to back up, because this is where the number balloons or stays sane. Rather than asking whether you can heat the whole house, ask what the minimum is that you can live with, and price out backing up a single heat-pump zone at a moderate temperature. Estimate its draw at the outdoor conditions you are realistically planning for, not the coldest night on record, and multiply by the hours it would cycle. Add that to your keep-alive floor to get a target daily energy the battery must supply. If that target lands around 15 to 20 kWh a day, you are in one-to-two-battery territory; if you insisted on whole-home resistance heat and it lands near 40, you have just discovered why that is impractical.

Then apply two reality discounts before you commit. Knock usable capacity down for cold if the battery lives anywhere unconditioned, since a chilly lithium pack delivers less than its summer nameplate. And decide how many days of autonomy you want without recharging, because a pack that carries your loads for one day covers most outages but not a multi-day winter event. If you have solar, factor in that panels recharge the battery during daylight, which effectively multiplies your autonomy in sunny weather and shrinks it toward zero during a dark, snowed-over stretch.

Finally, sanity-check the whole thing against your budget and your realistic outage history. Most regions see frequent short outages and rare long ones, so a pack sized to comfortably carry keep-alive loads plus limited heat through a day or two, backed by solar, hits the sweet spot for the money. Chasing multi-day whole-home heat off batteries almost never pencils out, and accepting that before you shop is the single most useful thing you can do.

So how much battery does an all-electric home really need? Enough to carry your genuine keep-alive loads for a day or two, plus as much rationed heating as your budget and your heat pump's efficiency make affordable, ideally paired with solar so the pack recharges during daylight. That's usually one to three batteries, not the wall of storage that "whole-home backup for a week" implies. The homes that get this wrong are the ones that either underbuy and lose their heat on night two, or overbuy chasing a multi-day resistance-heat fantasy that no reasonable battery bank can sustain. Start from your real daily kilowatt-hours, decide honestly what has to stay on, and size the pack to that.
