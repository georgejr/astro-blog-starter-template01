The app on your phone showing a friendly green energy graph is doing something less magical than it looks: it's reading numbers your inverter already measures and drawing them for you. A solar monitoring system doesn't sense sunlight or "watch" your panels directly. It reports data the inverter collects as it does its job of converting DC to AC, and — if you paid for the extra hardware — data from sensors clamped around your home's electrical feed. Knowing where each number comes from is what lets you tell a healthy reading from a warning sign.

## Two different measurements, often confused

The single biggest source of confusion is that "monitoring" bundles two separate things that answer different questions.

### Production monitoring

Production monitoring tracks what your system *makes*. It comes essentially for free, because the inverter has to measure its own output to do its job — so it already knows, moment to moment, how many watts the array is delivering and how many kilowatt-hours it has produced today, this month, and this year. Every grid-tied system has this. When people say "my solar app," they usually mean the production view. [What a solar inverter does](/blog/what-does-a-solar-inverter-do/) explains why the inverter is the natural place this data originates.

### Consumption monitoring

Consumption monitoring tracks what your *home uses*, and it requires extra hardware — current-transformer clamps installed around the main feed in your electrical panel. Without those sensors, the app can show production but not usage, so it can't tell you how much of your solar you're self-consuming versus exporting. With them, the app can draw the full picture: production, consumption, and the flow between your home, the panels, and the grid at any moment. Consumption monitoring is optional and worth it mainly if you want to shift usage into sunny hours or size a future battery from real data.

## The numbers that actually matter

Once you know what's being measured, a handful of readings tell you whether the system is well.

- **Current power (kW).** The instantaneous rate right now. It swings all day with the sun and clouds, so a low reading at 7 a.m. or under a passing cloud means nothing.
- **Energy today (kWh).** The day's cumulative production. This is the number to sanity-check against expectations.
- **Lifetime or monthly energy (kWh).** The long-run totals, useful for comparing this month to the same month last year.
- **Per-panel or per-string data**, if your equipment supports it — the granularity that turns "the system is down 10%" into "panel 14 is the problem."

That last point is worth dwelling on. Systems built on microinverters or power optimizers report data for each panel individually, so the app can flag a single underperforming module. Systems on a plain string inverter report one number for the whole array, which is cheaper but blinder — a single shaded or failing panel shows up only as a slightly lower total, with no clue which one. Neither is wrong; they're different levels of diagnostic resolution, and it's useful to know which you have before you go hunting for a fault.

## Reading production against a sensible expectation

A production number only means something next to what you *should* be producing. The honest benchmark isn't the system's nameplate — it's the nameplate adjusted for sun and losses. As a rough planning rule, a healthy residential array produces on the order of 1,300–1,600 kWh per year for each kilowatt of panels in a decently sunny location, less in cloudier regions. On a daily basis, expectations swing enormously with season and weather, which is exactly why one low day proves nothing. [What one solar panel really produces in a day](/blog/solar-panel-output-per-day/) shows how wide that daily range legitimately is.

The useful comparison is a trend, not a snapshot:

- **A clear, sunny summer day** should approach the high end of what your system can do. If a perfect June day produces far less than a similar day last year, that's a signal.
- **Month-over-year comparisons** wash out weather noise. This August versus last August is a cleaner health check than today versus yesterday.
- **A steady, unexplained decline** across weeks of similar weather is the pattern that warrants a look — as opposed to the normal, gentle year-over-year fade from panel degradation.

Distinguishing a real problem from normal variation is the whole skill, and it's easy to overreact to a cloudy week. [Reading your solar monitoring app](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) goes deeper on the specific patterns that signal trouble versus the ones that are just weather.

## What monitoring can't do

It's worth being clear about the limits, because the friendly app can lull you into thinking you're fully covered. Production monitoring tells you the array's *output*, not its *cause* — it can show that production dropped, but not whether the culprit is a failed panel, a tripped breaker, soiling, new shade from a grown tree, or an inverter fault. Diagnosis still takes a human. Monitoring also generally won't catch a slow, uniform loss across every panel as anything but a slightly lower total, which is why an annual comparison against expectation matters more than staring at the daily graph.

Treat the app as an early-warning instrument, not a diagnosis. Its job is to tell you *when* something deserves attention; figuring out *what* is the installer's job under your workmanship warranty.
