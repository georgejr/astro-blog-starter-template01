A clay or concrete tile is a rigid, brittle plate. Step on it wrong and it cracks; drill through it carelessly and it shatters. That one physical fact — tile does not flex — shapes everything about putting solar on it, from the hardware a crew chooses to the number of days they spend on the roof to the line item that eventually lands on your quote. The panels themselves are indifferent to what's underneath them. The mounting system is not; it cares enormously.

None of this makes tile a bad candidate for solar. Tile roofs across the Southwest and California carry arrays by the hundreds of thousands, and a well-built tile install can outlast the panels bolted to it. But mounting on tile is a genuinely different job than screwing racking into asphalt shingles, and knowing why helps you read a tile quote with clear eyes, spot a crew that's out of its depth, and tell the legitimate costs from the padding.

## Why tile changes the mounting problem

On an asphalt-shingle roof the process is almost boring. An installer locates a rafter, drives a lag bolt through the shingle into the wood, seals the penetration under a flashing, and moves to the next one. The shingle is thin, forgiving, and self-sealing beneath that flashing. A tile roof offers none of those conveniences. The load-bearing structure — the rafters — sits several inches below a layer of rigid tiles that overlap like scales, and beneath those tiles is the part that actually keeps the house dry: an underlayment membrane. The tiles are a rain shield and a wear surface. They are not the seal, and that distinction is the whole puzzle.

A mount on tile has to reach down through the tile plane to bolt into a rafter, penetrate the waterproof underlayment at that point, and reseal it — all without cracking the surrounding tiles or leaving the tile layer resting on the new hardware. Get the load path wrong and a tile ends up bearing weight it was never designed to carry, and it snaps. Get the sealing wrong and you've opened a hole in the roof's real waterproofing. Neither failure mode exists on shingles, which is exactly why tile demands purpose-built parts and a slower, more deliberate hand.

Crews solve that problem with one of a few approaches, and it's entirely fair to ask a bidder which one they use and why. The most common is the tile hook — an L- or S-shaped bracket that bolts to the rafter under the tile and reaches out through the gap between overlapping tiles, curving up to carry the rail above the surface. The tile above is usually ground or notched slightly so it sits flat over the hook instead of resting on it. Done well, the original tile stays put and the roof keeps its finished look; done poorly, the hook bears on the tile and the tile cracks under load or thermal movement. Hooks work across many profiles, including clay barrel tile, but they reward care at every single attachment. A second approach removes the tile at each mounting point entirely and swaps in a metal flashing "tile" — a formed piece shaped like the tile it replaces, with a flashed, sealed bolt penetration built in and a stud rising to carry the rail. This takes the brittle tile out of the load path completely, which many installers consider the cleanest solution, at the cost of removing and storing your originals and buying the replacement flashings. It's worth setting aside a few spare matching tiles for this method, since the removed ones get handled repeatedly. A third family of solutions combines a flashed base under the tile with a hook or standoff through it, or on fragile and historic tile, raises the array on a structure that minimizes penetrations altogether. The right choice depends on the tile itself — flat concrete, S-shaped clay barrel, and low-profile interlocking tiles each behave differently — which is precisely why tile experience matters more than brand loyalty when you pick a crew.

Age plays into the method choice as much as profile does. A twenty- or thirty-year-old tile roof has a difference between its two layers that a new one doesn't: the tiles themselves may still be perfectly serviceable, since quality clay and concrete last for decades, but the underlayment beneath them has a shorter life and may be near the end of it. That matters because every mount penetrates and reseals the underlayment, and flashing a new penetration into an underlayment that's already brittle and failing is building on a weak foundation. A conscientious installer will look at the age and condition of both layers before quoting, and on an older roof may recommend replacing the underlayment — lifting the tiles, laying fresh membrane, and setting the tiles back down — before or during the solar install, precisely because doing it later means pulling the array off again. That recommendation isn't upselling; it's the same logic that applies to any roof nearing the end of its waterproofing, and it's worth taking seriously rather than treating as an obstacle to the lowest bid. The brittleness of the tile and the age of the underlayment together are why a tile install rewards a crew that slows down, inspects carefully, and prices the job it actually finds rather than the job it hoped to find.

## Where the breakage risk actually lives

Cracked tiles are the defining hazard of a tile install, and they come from two very different sources that call for different judgments. The first is simple foot traffic. A roof full of installers walking a brittle surface for two or three days will crack some tiles just by stepping on unsupported spans, and even careful crews expect a small breakage rate and carry replacements for it. Walking on the lower third of each tile, where it's backed by the tile beneath, keeps the number down, but zero breakage across a large tile array is not a promise anyone can honestly make. If a salesperson swears not a single tile will crack, they're either inexperienced with tile or telling you what you want to hear.

The second source is the mounting work itself: over-torqued hooks, tiles left bearing on hardware, penetrations cut without relieving the tile around them. This kind of breakage isn't a cost of doing business — it's a symptom of rushed or unskilled work, and it's avoidable. The distinction matters because the tiles you can see and swap are a minor issue, easily handled if the crew stocks your profile. The ones that actually threaten your house are the hairline cracks that let water reach the underlayment and go unnoticed until a brown stain appears on a ceiling months later. That's why the sealing quality of every penetration matters as much as the tile count: the underlayment, not the tile, is what keeps you dry, and a good install treats each penetration as a waterproofing detail rather than just a bolt hole. Done right, a tile roof is no more vulnerable after the array goes on than before it, which connects to the broader worry about whether mounting hardware threatens a roof at all — a question examined in [is your roof a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/).

Because the dangerous cracks are the ones you can't see from the ground, the homeowner's protection is partly procedural. A workmanship warranty on the roof penetrations — separate from the panel and inverter warranties, and covering leaks caused by the installation for a meaningful number of years — is the single most valuable thing to confirm is in writing, because it puts the risk of a hidden crack back on the crew that made the holes. It's also reasonable to ask what happens after the array is up: a careful installer walks the finished roof looking for tiles that shifted or cracked during the work, and some will note the array's layout so a future leak can be traced to a specific penetration rather than a guessing game across the whole roof. As the owner, you can do a little watching of your own in the first season after the install. Attic inspections after a few hard rains, and an eye on the ceilings directly below the array, catch the rare failed penetration while it's a stain rather than structural damage. None of this is meant to make tile sound fragile — a properly flashed tile roof stays watertight for decades under an array — but the combination of a written workmanship warranty and a season of attention is cheap insurance against the one failure mode that tile makes easier to hide than a shingle roof does. A crew confident in its flashing will offer the warranty without being pushed; hesitation there is its own answer.

This is also where a few practical questions separate a capable tile crew from an optimistic one. Ask a bidder how they handle breakage, and a good answer treats broken tiles as expected and replaced while treating hidden cracks as unacceptable. Ask whether they stock or can source your specific tile profile, because a discontinued profile can turn a routine replacement into a scavenger hunt — having a small stock of matching or salvaged tiles set aside before the job starts protects you either way. And ask what their penetration flashing actually looks like, because a crew that can describe its waterproofing detail in specifics is a crew that thinks about the underlayment. Vague answers to any of these are a warning worth heeding before you sign.

## What tile adds to the price, and why

Tile mounting costs more than shingle mounting, and the reasons are concrete rather than arbitrary markup. The hardware is more expensive to begin with: specialized flashings and hooks cost more than the simple lag-and-flashing that suffices on shingles. The work is genuinely slower, because every attachment involves lifting, notching, or replacing tile, so a crew places far fewer mounts per hour than it would on asphalt. Breakage adds real cost, since replacement tiles have to be sourced and installed, sometimes after hunting down a discontinued profile. And there's a skill premium that's entirely legitimate: fewer crews do tile well, and the ones that do price their expertise accordingly. When a tile quote comes in above a shingle quote for the same array, those four factors — not padding — are usually where the difference lives.

Roof type is one of the larger swing factors in installation labor overall, and tile sits toward the high end of it, above shingle and often above metal. The full picture of how roofing material moves a quote, with tile placed alongside the alternatives, is laid out in [how your roof type changes what solar costs to install](/blog/solar-installation-cost-by-roof-type/). Rather than pin a fixed dollar figure to the tile premium — it depends too heavily on tile profile, roof complexity, and local labor to generalize honestly — treat a tile roof as adding a meaningful step-up over the same array on asphalt shingles, and confirm the actual amount by comparing real quotes. A reasonable way to sketch it is to model the base array cost with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) and then mentally layer the roof premium on top, keeping in mind the tool can't know your tile profile or how complex your roof planes are.

It's worth ending on what tile gives back for that premium, because the trade isn't one-sided. The most common roof in the country is the straightforward asphalt shingle, and mounting on it is fast, cheap, and well understood, as described in [solar on asphalt shingles](/blog/solar-panels-asphalt-shingle-roof/). What that roof can't match is longevity. Quality clay and concrete tile routinely outlasts the panels installed over it, so a well-flashed tile array rarely runs into the mid-life re-roof problem that quietly erodes the value of solar on aging shingles — the scenario where you have to pull a working array off a roof that's ready to be replaced, then pay to reinstall it. Tile asks for more hardware, more hours, and more specialized hands up front. In exchange, it offers a long-lived roof beneath a long-lived array, and for a homeowner planning to stay put, that pairing can be worth every dollar of the premium.

The practical way to keep the tile premium honest is to gather bids specifically from crews that do tile regularly, and to compare them against each other rather than against a shingle number you found online. A quote from a shingle-focused company that treats your tile roof as an afterthought is the one most likely to either under-price the labor — and then either lose money or cut corners to make it back — or to pad the number out of unfamiliarity. Tile-experienced installers, by contrast, tend to price the work realistically because they know exactly how many mounts a day they can set and how many replacement tiles a job typically consumes, and their quotes cluster more tightly for that reason. When you compare two or three tile-literate bids, the spread tells you something real; when you compare a tile bid against a shingle assumption, the spread tells you nothing. It's also worth remembering that the premium buys a genuine advantage the shingle owner doesn't get, which reframes it from a penalty into a trade. Solar and tile are both long-horizon investments, and putting them together on a roof you intend to keep aligns their lifespans in a way that avoids the single most wasteful scenario in residential solar — paying to remove and reinstall a working array so an aging roof underneath it can be replaced. On tile, that scenario is rare by design. That's the quiet payoff hiding inside the higher line item, and it's the reason a tile roof, correctly installed, tends to look like a bargain in hindsight even though it never looks like one on the quote.
