When the roof under a solar array wears out, the panels have to come off before the shingles can be replaced and go back on afterward. Installers call this a detach-and-reset, or an R&R — remove and reinstall. It's ordinary work, but it catches homeowners off guard because it's a cost that has nothing to do with the roof itself and everything to do with the panels sitting on it.

As an orientation figure, budget for a residential detach-and-reset in the low thousands — treat a rough $1,500 to $5,000 as the assumption band for a typical array, wider for large or complicated systems. That range is deliberately broad because the price is driven almost entirely by the specifics of your system and roof, not by a standard menu. Understanding what moves it is what lets you read a quote and tell a fair one from a padded one.

## What you're actually paying for

An R&R is two labor events wrapped around your roofing project. First a crew disconnects the system, unbolts the panels, removes the racking, and stores everything safely. Then, after the roofers finish, another crew re-mounts the racking, re-installs the panels, reconnects the wiring, and re-commissions the system so it produces again. Both halves are skilled electrical and rooftop work, which is why this isn't something the roofing contractor typically handles.

The cost breaks down along familiar lines, most of it labor. The following is a labeled assumption to show where the money goes, not a fixed price sheet.

| Line item | What it covers | Share of the job |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Removal labor | Disconnect, unbolt, palletize panels | Large |
| Reinstall labor | Re-mount, rewire, re-commission | Large |
| New flashing and mounts | Fresh roof penetrations and seals | Moderate |
| Electrical reconnection | Inverter, conduit, inspection | Moderate |
| Permits and re-inspection | Local requirements | Small |

Labor dominates because a panel array is essentially installed twice. The [breakdown of what you pay a solar crew for](/blog/solar-installation-labor-cost/) applies here directly — the same hourly skilled work that set the system originally is what takes it down and puts it back.

## What pushes the price up or down

A handful of factors explain most of the spread between a $1,500 job and a $5,000 one.

- **System size.** More panels mean more to remove, store, and re-mount. Cost rises roughly with panel count, since each module is handled twice.
- **Roof and mount complexity.** A simple single-plane roof is quick; steep pitches, multiple planes, tile, or metal roofing slow everything down and raise labor.
- **Racking condition and reuse.** If the existing rails and mounts are sound and compatible with the new roof, they may be reused. If they're corroded or the roof type changes, new racking adds cost.
- **Storage and staging.** Panels have to go somewhere while the roof is redone, and the coordination between roofer and solar crew affects how many trips and how much downtime the job carries.
- **Age of the system.** Older mounts and connectors are sometimes brittle or discontinued, occasionally forcing replacement parts that a newer system wouldn't need.

The single biggest cost-saver is doing the roof and the R&R as one coordinated project rather than two disconnected ones. When the solar crew and roofer schedule around each other, you pay for one mobilization each way instead of paying for delays, re-trips, and a system left offline longer than necessary.

## Timing is the real money decision

The most expensive version of this scenario is the one nobody plans for: paying for a full detach-and-reset a few years after installing solar because the roof underneath was already near the end of its life. A worn roof under new panels is a scheduling mistake that costs a second, avoidable labor bill.

That's why the timing question is worth settling before you ever install: if your roof is within several years of needing replacement, doing it first is almost always cheaper than doing it later around live panels. The full case for sequencing is laid out in [whether to replace your roof before going solar](/blog/replace-roof-before-solar/). If the panels are already up and the roof is failing anyway, the goal shifts to coordinating both jobs tightly so you pay the R&R once and cleanly.

One reassurance worth stating plainly: a competent detach-and-reset does not harm a sound roof, and it's an expected part of a long solar lifespan rather than a sign of trouble — the same evidence behind [whether solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/) covers the removal case too. The panels will outlast at least one roof under them, so at some point this bill arrives for nearly everyone who keeps a system long enough.

To fold an eventual R&R into your longer-term solar budgeting, price your system's baseline with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) and treat a future detach-and-reset as a known line item rather than a surprise — because on a 25-year system, that's exactly what it is.
