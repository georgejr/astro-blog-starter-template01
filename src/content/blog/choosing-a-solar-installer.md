The panels on your roof will come from one of a handful of large manufacturers, built to the same certifications, warranted for the same 25 years, and performing within a few percent of each other. The company that designs, sells, and bolts them to your house is where the real variance lives. Quote spreads of $4,000–$8,000 for identical systems, production estimates ranging from rigorous to fictional, workmanship that stays watertight for decades or leaks in year three, warranty support from a thriving local firm or from a bankruptcy trustee — all of that is decided when you pick the installer, not the panel.

Which reframes the whole shopping exercise. You aren't really buying solar equipment; you're hiring a contractor for a 25-year relationship that begins with a construction project, and the hiring process deserves the same rigor you'd apply to any five-figure contractor decision. What follows is how to run that hire properly, from first quote to signature.

## Get three or more quotes, then force them onto one yardstick

Everything downstream depends on having real alternatives, so the first rule is mechanical: get at least three quotes, from different kinds of companies. Three is the practical minimum to see the market's spread, and four or five is better if your area has many installers, because the spread between quotes for the same roof is routinely several thousand dollars, which makes each additional quote one of the highest-hourly-wage activities in the whole project. A useful mix is one large national or regional player, one established local installer, and one referral from a solar-owning neighbor or friend. Each type has a signature. Nationals bring scale, standardized processes, and financing breadth but carry heavy sales overhead and slower service queues; established local firms often price lower and respond faster but vary widely in polish, so you vet them individually; referrals come pre-vetted by someone with no commission at stake. Neither the national nor the local category wins automatically, and the credential checks below matter far more than the size of the logo on the truck.

Then force the quotes onto one yardstick, because they will not arrive on one. For each proposal, extract five numbers. Start with the system size in kW of DC nameplate and the panel count. Then compute price per watt — total contract price divided by watts — which is the great normalizer, so a $24,000 quote for 8.5 kW ($2.82/W) beats a $21,000 quote for 6.8 kW ($3.09/W) even though the sticker looks bigger. Pull out the cash price, stated separately from any financed price, because zero-down loan pricing routinely embeds dealer fees of 15–30% and the cash-versus-financed gap is exactly what exposes them. Note the estimated first-year production in kWh plus the assumptions behind it — shading, orientation, degradation — since two installers modeling the same roof should land within a few percent of each other, and an outlier is either sloppier or telling you what you want to hear. Finally, get the adders itemized: panel upgrades, trenching, steep-roof charges, reroofing. Vague totals hide padding; itemized ones invite questions. Benchmark the per-watt figures against [average solar panel costs](/blog/average-solar-panel-cost/) and run the totals through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), because a quote well outside the normal band in either direction needs an explanation you can verify. It's worth understanding before the first sales visit that customer-acquisition costs and margin are the flexible parts of a quote while permits and labor are not — that knowledge alone changes how you negotiate. One discipline makes the comparison fair: ask each company to quote a system of roughly the same size so you're weighing price against price rather than being nudged toward whoever proposed the biggest array. When sizes differ, the per-watt figure reconciles them, but it's easier to spot padding and dealer fees when the systems on the table are genuinely comparable to begin with.

None of the credential checks are glamorous, and all of them are checkable in an evening, which is what makes them the cheapest way to filter out most future problems. Confirm the contractor license: every state licenses the electrical or contracting work, and your state's licensing board website will show whether the license is active, matches the company name on your contract, and carries any disciplinary history — and confirm the license holder is the company itself, not a borrowed qualifier. Ask for certificates of general liability and workers' compensation insurance, because a crew member injured on an uninsured jobsite can become your legal problem. Look for NABCEP certification, the North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners credential that is the industry's serious professional standard; it isn't legally required and plenty of good installers operate without it, but a NABCEP-certified professional on staff signals investment in doing the work correctly. Weigh time in business locally, since warranty promises are only as durable as the company making them and solar has been through repeated boom-bust cycles that erased many players — five-plus years operating under the same name in your market is meaningful, while a two-year-old LLC offering a 25-year workmanship warranty is making a promise arithmetic doesn't support. Read reviews with a filter, ignoring the star average to read the recent one-star reviews and, more revealing, how the company responded to problems, because every installer at scale has installation hiccups and what varies is whether they show up afterward; ask for two or three references from installs at least a year old and actually call one. And ask directly who physically installs the system, employees or subcontractors, because subbing isn't disqualifying but you want to know whose workmanship warranty covers the roof penetrations, and quality control is structurally harder when the seller and the installer are different companies.

## Warranties, and reading the sales process as an audition

Solar involves four separate warranties, routinely blurred together in sales conversations, and pulling them apart is what tells you which promises will actually hold. The panel product warranty, typically 12–25 years, covers manufacturing defects, while the separate panel production warranty, typically 25 years, guarantees output degrades no faster than a stated curve; both come from the manufacturer and survive your installer's disappearance. The inverter warranty runs commonly 10–12 years for string inverters and up to 25 for microinverters, and since inverters are the component most likely to fail, this one has the highest odds of actually being used. The workmanship warranty comes from the installer and covers the labor — roof penetrations, wiring, racking, leaks — commonly for 5–25 years, and this is the one to scrutinize, because it's only as good as the company's continued existence: a 10-year workmanship warranty from a 15-year-old local firm beats a 25-year one from a startup. Get clarity on process, too. Who do you call when production drops, the installer or a manufacturer hotline? Does the workmanship warranty explicitly cover roof leaks at penetrations, and for how long? Is there a labor charge for warranty swaps of failed components? The answers separate companies that plan to service what they sell from companies that plan only to sell. It's also worth asking how a service call actually begins: does the company monitor your system's production and reach out when it drops, or does the burden fall entirely on you to notice a fault and start the claim? The best installers watch a fleet dashboard and catch dead inverters before the homeowner does; the weakest never look, so a failed string can bleed production for a year before anyone opens a ticket. That difference in posture is invisible on the day you sign and very visible in year seven.

You can learn most of what matters from how a company sells to you, before checking a single reference, because the sales process is itself the audition. The good signals are concrete: they ask for your actual usage history, twelve months of kWh rather than just a monthly dollar figure, before quoting; they assess or model your specific roof, shading included, rather than quoting off a satellite glance; their production estimate comes with stated assumptions; they explain your utility's export rules accurately; they present the cash price without being cornered into it; and they're unbothered by you getting other quotes. The red flags have survived in the industry precisely because they work. The today-only discount — "this price expires when I leave" — is manufactured urgency that exists to prevent comparison shopping, because real pricing doesn't expire in an afternoon. "Free solar" and "government program" framing is a tell, since there is no free solar, only a loan with the cost embedded, and vague invocations of government programs usually mean the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit dressed up as a limited-time secret rather than the durable tax provision explained in [how the solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/). Savings projections built on aggressive assumptions — utility rate escalations of 5–6% annually, zero shading losses, full retail export credit unverified against your actual tariff — inflate the pitch. Pressure to sign before a site visit, and a monthly-payment-only conversation that never quite states the system price, round out the list. Any one of these is a reason for caution; two or more is a reason to thank them for their time. A useful stress test cuts through all of it: ask each rep, "What would make my house a bad fit for solar?" A professional has an answer — shade, roof age, low usage, your utility's rules — because honest answers exist, while a script has none.

Door-to-door salespeople deserve a specific note, because they generate so much of the industry's anxiety. Not all of them should be avoided — some legitimate companies canvass — but treat the doorstep pitch as a lead, never a decision. Take the information, decline to sign anything same-day, and put the company through the identical vetting and multi-quote comparison as everyone else. Companies whose economics depend on same-day signatures usually don't survive that process, which tells you what you need to know. The handful of questions worth pressing every finalist on are the load-bearing ones: who physically installs the system, what the cash price is, what DC-to-AC ratio and shading assumptions the production estimate uses, what exactly the workmanship warranty covers and for how long and who services equipment failures, and how many systems the company has installed in your utility's territory and how that utility's export policy works. Clear, specific answers to all of those is itself the strongest credential a company can offer.

## The contract, the timeline, and making the final call

Before a signature, know what the document commits both sides to, and what the next few months should look like, because project management is part of what you're hiring. The contract should state, in writing, the exact equipment by model number — not "Tier 1 panels," a marketing phrase with no enforceable meaning — along with the system size in DC watts, the total price and payment schedule, who owns the incentives, the production estimate, all warranty terms, and who pays for what if the design changes after the site survey or structural review. A common and legitimate sequence is a small deposit at signing, a payment at installation, and the balance at final approval, so be wary of any structure demanding most of the money before work occurs. Check the cancellation terms too, because many states mandate a multi-day right of rescission on home-improvement contracts, and a company that resists putting cancellation terms in writing is answering a question you didn't have to ask.

The payment structure inside that contract is its own quiet test of a company's health and intentions. A firm confident in its cash flow and its work asks for a modest deposit, bills the bulk at installation, and holds the final tranche until the system passes inspection and earns permission to operate; a firm demanding most of the money up front is either thinly capitalized or planning to be scarce if problems surface. Financing deserves the same scrutiny as the price, because the loan is where the industry hides its margins — a "zero-down, low-monthly" offer routinely carries a dealer fee of 15% to 30% folded into the financed amount, so the honest move is to get the cash price in writing and compare it against a loan from your own bank or credit union. If the in-house financing still wins after that comparison, take it; if it doesn't, you've just found several thousand dollars that the monthly-payment framing was designed to keep you from seeing.

Then comes the part sales conversations skip: the wait. A typical residential project runs several weeks to a few months from signature to switch-on, and almost none of that is construction. The sequence is a site survey and final design, a permit application to your jurisdiction, the installation itself over usually one to three days, a municipal inspection, and finally the utility's permission to operate — the PTO — before which the system must legally stay off. Permitting and utility queues, not crews, set the pace, and they vary enormously by jurisdiction. This belongs in a vetting guide because how a company handles the pipeline is a real differentiator: ask each finalist for their current average signature-to-PTO time in your specific city and utility territory, and who your point of contact is during the wait. Installers who process permits daily know the answer cold, while a hedge or a suspiciously instant "two weeks" is data in itself. Slow milestones are usually the jurisdiction's fault; silence about them is the installer's.

With comparable quotes and vetting done, the last step is judgment. The cheapest bid is only the best bid if the company behind it clears the same bar as the others, because a rock-bottom price from a thinly capitalized installer is a discount on a warranty you may never collect, while the most expensive bid usually buys sales overhead rather than craftsmanship. In practice the strongest pick is typically an established local firm, priced inside the normal per-watt band, with verifiable licensing and insurance, employee crews, a specific and plausible production estimate, and a workmanship warranty its history makes credible. Before you sign, run the winning quote's numbers — its real price and its production estimate under your utility's actual export rules — through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If the payback still clears your bar using your assumptions rather than the sales deck's, you've done the hire properly, and the full set of variables worth pressure-testing in that calculation is laid out in [the solar payback period explained](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/). If two finalists come out close on price and both clear the vetting, let the tiebreakers be the things that surface during a 25-year relationship rather than a one-day install: which company answered your questions without hedging, which put its production estimate and warranty terms in writing without being pushed, which crew is made of employees rather than a rotating cast of subcontractors, and which has the longer local track record standing behind the workmanship warranty. Those signals cost nothing to weigh and predict the ownership experience far better than a few hundred dollars of price difference. The homeowner who picks on rapport and gut feeling alone often does fine, but the one who picks on rapport plus verified credentials plus comparable quotes almost never regrets it.

The whole exercise comes down to a single reframing: you are not shopping for the lowest number on a proposal, you are hiring the company most likely to still be answering the phone, and still standing behind its work, a decade after the crew drives away.
