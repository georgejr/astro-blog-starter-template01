The Desert Southwest gets more usable sun than almost anywhere else in the country, and on paper that makes it the perfect place to put panels on a roof. The reality is more interesting, because the same conditions that deliver all that sunlight, the relentless heat, the dry dusty air, the violent late-summer storms, each take a bite out of production in ways a homeowner in a mild coastal climate never has to think about. Solar in the desert Southwest is not about whether there is enough sun; there is a spectacular surplus of it. It is about engineering a system so that the region's specific hardships shave as little off that surplus as possible.

The three forces that matter are distinct, and lumping them together as "desert conditions" obscures how differently they behave. Heat is constant and predictable and trims output during the hottest hours. Dust is gradual and cumulative and quietly erodes production between rains. Monsoon storms are episodic and violent, threatening the hardware itself while also, paradoxically, offering the one reliable free panel wash the desert provides. Designing well means respecting all three, and a system built for the Southwest looks meaningfully different from the same system built for Oregon.

## Heat and dust: the everyday drains on desert output

Start with heat, because it is the most counterintuitive fact about solar in hot climates and the one that trips up buyers who assume more heat means more power. It does not. Solar panels are rated at a cell temperature of 25 degrees Celsius, about 77 Fahrenheit, and their output falls as the cells run hotter than that reference. The relationship is set by a temperature coefficient, typically around -0.3 to -0.4 percent of rated power for every degree Celsius above 25, printed on the panel's spec sheet. On a summer afternoon in Phoenix, Las Vegas, or Tucson, dark glass baking in direct sun can push cell temperatures to 65 Celsius or higher, which sits 40 degrees above the rating and implies an instantaneous drop of roughly 12 to 16 percent from the nameplate figure during the hottest part of the day. The panel genuinely makes less than its label promises while it is that hot, and no amount of marketing changes the physics.

The saving grace, and it is a large one, is that this is a peak-hour penalty rather than an all-year one. Mornings, evenings, and the cooler two-thirds of the calendar run far closer to the rated numbers, and the desert simply has so many strong sun hours that the annual total stays elite despite the summer haircut. A well-designed Southwest system still produces more energy per installed kilowatt than the same system would almost anywhere else. Good design softens the heat penalty further without pretending to erase it: mounting panels with an air gap of a few inches above the roof lets them shed heat far better than a flush mount, and choosing modules with a temperature coefficient closer to zero holds up better in the heat, a spec that deserves real weight here when it would be a footnote in a mild climate. Because Arizona in particular pairs this heat behavior with time-of-use rate structures that change when your power is worth the most, the state-specific economics are worth the deeper look in the guide to [Arizona solar and extreme heat](/blog/solar-panels-in-arizona/), where the rate design often matters more than the temperature coefficient.

Heat does not stop at the panels, either, and a desert design that only accounts for module derating misses half the problem. Inverters also lose efficiency and can throttle their output when they run too hot, a behavior called thermal derating, and an inverter mounted on a west-facing wall in full afternoon sun will cook itself right when the array is trying to deliver its peak. Siting the inverter in shade, on a north wall, in a garage, or under an awning, and giving it room to breathe rather than boxing it into a hot enclosure, protects production at the far end of the system where a struggling inverter can quietly waste power the panels worked hard to make. The same logic extends to any batteries, which dislike heat even more than inverters do and last longer in a conditioned or shaded space than baking on a sun-struck exterior wall. In the desert, where every component runs hotter than its designers' comfort zone, thinking about where the equipment lives is not fussiness; it is the difference between hardware that ages gracefully and hardware that spends its summers derating.

Dust is the second drain, and it is the one most unique to the desert and most often ignored in a sales pitch. In a wet climate, rain washes panels often enough that soiling, the accumulation of dust and grime on the glass, rarely costs much. In the arid Southwest, where months can pass with no meaningful rain, dust builds on the panels and stays there, and a film of desert dust scatters and blocks light before it ever reaches the cells. The losses are real and cumulative: a lightly soiled array might give up a few percent, while heavily soiled panels in a long dry stretch, especially after a dust storm, can lose 10 to 15 percent of their output until something cleans them. That is a larger loss than the heat penalty over a full year in many desert locations, and unlike heat, it is partly within your control.

The complication is that cleaning panels is not free of trade-offs, and the desert rewards a thoughtful approach over either neglect or obsession. Rooftop panels are awkward and sometimes dangerous to reach, water is scarce and expensive in much of the region, and aggressive cleaning with the wrong tools or hard mineral water can scratch glass or leave deposits that make things worse. For most desert homeowners the sensible rhythm is a periodic gentle rinse during the dry season rather than a scrub every week, and a realistic sense of when the effort actually pays for itself in recovered production. The full case for when rain is not enough and cleaning becomes worthwhile is laid out in the guide to [whether solar panels need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/), and it applies with particular force in the Southwest, where the dry-season dust load is heavier than almost anywhere else in the country. The practical upshot is that a desert homeowner should budget for occasional cleaning as a normal part of ownership, mentally if not financially, rather than assuming the panels take care of themselves the way they largely do in rainier regions.

## Monsoon season and sizing for the desert's real output

The third force is the summer monsoon, the seasonal pattern that brings towering thunderstorms, dust storms, flash flooding, and hail to much of the Southwest from roughly July into September. Its effect on solar is genuinely two-sided, and an honest account has to hold both halves at once. On the harmful side, monsoon storms are the main hardware threat a desert array faces. High winds test the mounting and racking, hail can crack glass or damage cells if it is large enough, and the storms bring heavy cloud that cuts production sharply during the storm hours themselves, right in the middle of what would otherwise be the strongest season. A system in monsoon country needs racking rated and installed for the local wind loads, and panels certified to standard hail-impact ratings, which most quality modules meet but which is worth confirming rather than assuming, particularly in the parts of the region where hail is common.

On the beneficial side, the monsoon rains are the desert's one dependable panel wash. The dust that accumulates through the long dry spring gets rinsed off by the summer storms, and production often jumps noticeably in the days after a good soaking rain, not because the sun got stronger but because the glass got clean. This is why the soiling problem is seasonal rather than permanent: the dust builds through the rainless months and then largely clears when the monsoon arrives, and a homeowner who understands that rhythm can time any manual cleaning to bridge the worst of the dry season rather than fighting a losing battle year-round. The storms are a threat to the hardware and a gift to the glass, and the region's solar systems live with both.

Insurance and structural planning round out the monsoon picture, and they are worth raising with your installer rather than discovering after a storm. In hail-prone parts of the region, confirm that the panels carry a standard hail-impact certification and that your homeowner's policy actually covers roof-mounted solar against storm damage, because coverage varies and a surprise exclusion is a bad thing to learn during a claim. Racking should be specified and installed to the local wind load, which in monsoon and dust-storm country can be significant, and the mounting hardware and roof penetrations should be flashed and sealed to shed the heavy, brief downpours the season delivers. These are ordinary parts of a competent desert install, but they are exactly the corners a rushed or low-bid job cuts, and the consequences show up years later as a leak, a loosened panel, or an uninsured loss. Asking about wind rating, hail certification, and insurance coverage up front costs nothing and tells you quickly whether the company has actually built for the desert or is selling a mild-climate system into a harsh one.

All of this feeds into how you should size a desert system, and the central lesson is to size against realistic production rather than the elite sun figure the region's reputation suggests. The raw solar resource in the Southwest is world-class, but the number that actually reaches your meter is that resource minus the heat derating, minus the average soiling between rains, minus the monsoon-season storm hours. A good estimate folds those losses in rather than assuming the panels hit their nameplate rating on a clean, cool, cloudless day that the desert summer rarely provides. Nevada homeowners in particular should note that the export credit structure there differs from Arizona's in ways that change the value of every kilowatt-hour, which the guide to [Nevada's tiered export credits](/blog/solar-panels-in-nevada/) works through in detail.

The realistic bottom line for the region is that the Desert Southwest deserves its reputation as premier solar country, but the elite sun comes bundled with three specific taxes on output that a homeowner has to plan around rather than wish away. Heat trims the hottest hours, dust erodes production between rains, and the monsoon both threatens the hardware and cleans the glass. None of these come close to negating the region's advantage; a well-designed desert array still ranks among the most productive in the nation. The difference between a system that quietly meets expectations and one that disappoints is whether it was engineered for the heat, maintained against the dust, and built to survive the storms, or whether it was sold on the strength of the sun alone as if the desert asked nothing in return.
