Plenty of homeowners sign a solar loan in the excitement of the sale and only later, reading the amortization schedule over a cup of coffee, realize what they agreed to. A rate that felt fine in the moment turns out to be well above what their credit would command on an ordinary loan, and the reason is usually buried in a line item most buyers never see spelled out: the dealer fee. So the question comes up naturally once the panels are humming and the novelty wears off — can you refinance out of a solar loan into something cheaper, and if you do, does the math actually reward you or just move the money around?

The short answer is that you almost always *can* refinance, because the loan on your panels is, in the end, just a debt with your name on it, and debts can be paid off and replaced. The longer and more useful answer is that refinancing only pays when the new loan is genuinely cheaper after every fee is counted, and that requires understanding how the original loan was priced in the first place. A solar loan is not a mysterious instrument. It's a personal loan or a secured loan dressed up in solar marketing, and once you strip away the packaging you can compare it to any other borrowing option on equal terms.

## Why your solar loan rate is probably worse than it looks

The advertised rate on a dealer-arranged solar loan tends to look reasonable, sometimes even low. The catch is that a chunk of the system's price is a **dealer fee** — a payment the lender charges the installer for offering that attractive rate, which the installer quietly rolls into the amount you finance. Industry-wide, these fees commonly land somewhere between 15% and 30% of the amount borrowed, and the lower the advertised rate, the fatter the fee tends to be. A 0.99% loan isn't charity; it's a high-fee loan wearing a low-rate costume. You paid for that rate up front, whether or not anyone described it that way.

The practical result is that your true cost of borrowing is higher than the rate on the paperwork suggests. If a $30,000 system carries a 25% dealer fee, roughly $7,500 of what you financed bought nothing but the low headline rate. Had you paid cash or used a fee-free loan, that same system might have been priced closer to $22,500. This is the single most important thing to understand before you refinance, because it reframes the whole exercise. You're not just chasing a lower interest rate. You may be trying to escape a principal balance that was inflated to subsidize the rate you're now unhappy with. The deeper mechanics of how these fees get embedded, and why the sticker price and the cash price diverge, are worth reading in full in our breakdown of [what a solar loan really adds to your system's price](/blog/solar-loan-true-cost/) before you decide the original deal was bad — sometimes it was competitive, and sometimes it wasn't.

There's a second wrinkle specific to solar loans that shapes any refinance decision: the **tax-credit buydown**. Many dealer loans are structured with two parts, a smaller "bridge" portion sized to match the expected 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit and a larger long-term portion. The loan assumes you'll receive that credit and apply it to the bridge within a set window — often 12 to 18 months — after which your monthly payment re-amortizes based on whatever balance remains. If you never applied your credit because you didn't have the tax liability to use it, or you spent it elsewhere, your payment can jump sharply when that window closes. Anyone weighing a refinance needs to know exactly where they stand on this. The federal credit is worth 30% of qualifying costs and is claimed on your tax return, not paid to you directly by the government, and the specifics of who can use it live with current IRS guidance; our overview of how [solar loans and the tax credit interact](/blog/solar-financing-tax-credit/) walks through the buydown structure in more detail. Refinancing right before a scheduled payment jump can be a smart move, or it can be a way to lock in an inflated balance permanently — it depends entirely on whether you've already captured the credit.

Prepayment terms are the last piece to check before you go shopping. Most reputable solar loans allow you to pay the balance off early without penalty, which is exactly what a refinance requires — you take out a new loan, use it to pay off the solar loan in full, and continue with the new, cheaper debt. But a minority of loans carry prepayment penalties or are structured so that paying early forfeits some benefit. Read the payoff terms in your loan agreement, or call the servicer and ask for a payoff quote, before you assume the door is open. If there's a penalty, it becomes one more cost to weigh against the savings.

## What you can refinance into, and what each option costs

Once you've decided the original loan is worth escaping, the question becomes what you replace it with. There are four realistic paths, and they differ mostly in whether they're secured by your home and what fees they carry to set up. Each one is just a different way of borrowing the same payoff amount, so the comparison comes down to interest rate, term, and upfront cost.

A **home equity loan or HELOC** is the option that most often produces real savings, because it's secured by your house and therefore priced closer to mortgage rates than to personal-loan rates. If you have equity and decent credit, a home equity product can undercut a dealer solar loan's true cost meaningfully. The trade-offs are that it puts your home on the line as collateral, it usually involves an appraisal and closing costs that can run several hundred to a couple thousand dollars, and a HELOC in particular often carries a variable rate that can drift upward. There's also a genuine benefit specific to home equity borrowing: the interest may be tax-deductible when the funds are used to improve the home, though solar refinancing muddies that question and you'd want a tax professional to confirm your situation rather than assuming it applies.

A **cash-out mortgage refinance** folds the solar balance into a new, larger first mortgage. This can work if mortgage rates happen to sit below your current mortgage rate anyway, so you're improving the whole loan and absorbing the solar balance along the way. But if it means giving up a low mortgage rate you already hold just to capture a solar balance, the math almost never works — you'd be repricing hundreds of thousands of dollars of debt upward to save on a few tens of thousands. Cash-out refinancing also carries the heaviest closing costs of any option here, frequently 2% to 5% of the entire new loan, so it only makes sense in the narrow case where you were going to refinance the mortgage regardless.

An **unsecured personal loan** from a bank, credit union, or online lender is the path that doesn't touch your home. Rates are higher than home equity borrowing, but a well-qualified borrower can still land a personal loan whose true cost beats a dealer solar loan that was padded with a 25% fee — because the personal loan has no dealer fee baked in, only the honest rate. Credit unions in particular sometimes offer competitive fixed-rate personal loans with minimal fees, which makes them worth a call. The appeal here is simplicity and speed: no appraisal, no lien on the house, and often funding within days.

The fourth path is not refinancing at all but **accelerated payoff**, and it deserves mention because it's frequently the best answer for people who have some cash available. If the barrier is a high effective rate rather than an unaffordable payment, throwing savings at the principal — or simply paying the loan off entirely if you have the funds — sidesteps every closing cost a refinance would incur. Before assuming you need a new loan, it's worth asking whether you're really trying to lower the rate or just resenting the debt, because those call for different moves. Our comparison of [paying cash versus financing solar](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/) lays out how the payment method reshapes the break-even, and the same logic applies in reverse when you're deciding whether to refinance or pay down.

To see whether a given refinance genuinely helps, run both loans side by side. Take your current payoff balance and remaining term, then model the replacement loan's rate and term in the [solar loan calculator](/solar-loan-calculator/) and compare total interest paid over the life of each. Do not compare monthly payments alone — a longer term can lower the monthly payment while raising total interest, which feels like relief but costs more. If you want to see the cash, loan, and lease framing side by side rather than just two loan structures, the [payment comparison tool](/solar-payment-comparison/) is built for exactly that, and it keeps you from fixating on the payment while ignoring the total.

## Running the break-even so the fees don't eat the savings

Every refinance has a break-even point, and it's the number that decides whether the whole exercise was worth your afternoon. The logic is simple: you pay some upfront cost to set up the new loan, you save some amount each month from the lower rate, and the break-even is how many months of savings it takes to recover the upfront cost. Beyond that point you're genuinely ahead; before it, you'd have been better off leaving the old loan alone.

Assume, purely to illustrate the shape of the math and not as a quote, that you're carrying a $25,000 solar loan balance at an effective rate that's costing you meaningfully more than a home equity loan would. Say the refinance saves you $60 a month in interest and the closing costs come to $1,200. Divide $1,200 by $60 and you get a 20-month break-even. If you plan to stay in the house and keep the loan for well past 20 months — which most owners will — the refinance clears the bar and every month after month 20 is money kept. If you're planning to sell in a year, it doesn't, and you'd pay the closing costs only to move out before they paid for themselves. The break-even reframes the decision from "is the new rate lower" to "will I hold this long enough for the lower rate to overcome the setup cost," which is the question that actually matters.

Two adjustments make that calculation honest. First, count *all* the upfront costs, not just the ones labeled "closing costs." An appraisal fee, an origination fee, a title search, and any prepayment penalty on the old loan all belong in the numerator, because they're all cash you spend to make the switch. Lowball the setup cost and you'll compute a break-even that arrives sooner than reality. Second, compare total interest over matched terms rather than trusting the monthly payment. The most common way a refinance quietly loses money is by stretching a loan that had eight years left into a fresh fifteen-year term. The payment drops, the borrower feels the win, and the total interest paid over those extra seven years swamps whatever the lower rate saved. If you refinance a solar loan, try to keep the new term at or below the remaining term of the old one — or at least model what the longer term costs you in total, not just per month, before you accept it.

The buydown timing deserves one more look here, because it can flip the whole decision. If your dealer loan is about to re-amortize upward because you captured and applied your tax credit, then your current payment is roughly as low as it will ever be, and comparing a refinance against today's artificially low payment understates the benefit — you should be comparing against the higher payment that's coming. Conversely, if you have a tax-credit bridge portion still outstanding and you intend to pay it down with your refund, refinancing the whole balance now can accidentally lock that bridge amount into long-term debt at interest, when it was designed to be retired quickly and cheaply. Sort out where you are in the buydown cycle first; refinancing at the wrong point in that cycle can turn a good idea into an expensive one. When the numbers do line up — a lower true rate, a term no longer than what you had, closing costs you'll recover well within the time you plan to keep the house, and the tax credit already settled — refinancing a solar loan is one of the cleaner money-saving moves available to a homeowner, precisely because the original loan so often carried a fee you never needed to pay twice.
