New solar owners often get rattled by their second winter. The panels that zeroed out the electric bill in July are suddenly not covering the December load, a real charge appears where there had been none, and the worry sets in that something is broken or that the whole system was oversold. Almost always, nothing is wrong. Solar production is deeply seasonal, household electricity use is seasonal in its own separate rhythm, and the two rarely line up month to month. The savings a well-designed system delivers are entirely real, but they arrive as an *annual* result stitched together from wildly uneven months, and understanding that one distinction is what keeps a perfectly normal winter from feeling like a failure. The monthly bill was never the scoreboard. The year is.

## Two seasonal curves that refuse to line up

The swing in your bill is really the collision of two independent seasonal patterns, and seeing them as separate is the key to not panicking. The first is production, and it varies more dramatically than most people expect before they own a system. A solar array generates far more energy in summer than in winter, and the gap is wide. Long June days with a high sun angle can produce roughly twice the daily energy of short, low-angle December days, before you even factor in that winter also brings a greater share of overcast weather in most of the country. Two forces are working together against the winter side of the ledger here: the days are shorter, so the panels have fewer hours to work, and the sun sits lower in the sky, so even the hours it does have are less productive. A panel's daily output rises and falls with day length and sun height across the year, and that alone drives a large seasonal spread in what your roof sends to the meter, entirely independent of anything happening inside your house.

The second curve is consumption, and its shape depends almost entirely on how your particular home is heated and cooled. A house with electric air conditioning peaks in summer, right when production also peaks, which is a happy alignment: the panels are working hardest exactly when the air conditioner is pulling hardest, so the roof feeds the load directly. A house with electric heat peaks in winter, precisely when production bottoms out, which is the worst possible timing: demand is highest in the very months the array can least support it. Most homes sit somewhere between these two extremes, with a mix of gas heat and electric cooling, but the general truth holds regardless of the details. Your usage curve and your production curve are two different shapes laid over the same twelve months, and the monthly bill is simply whatever falls out of the mismatch between them on any given billing cycle.

Put the two curves together and the seasonal pattern becomes easy to predict, which itself is reassuring once you expect it. Spring and fall often overproduce, because the days are getting long-ish while neither heating nor cooling is running hard, so the panels comfortably outpace a light load. Summer may overproduce or roughly break even depending on how heavy your cooling load runs. Winter almost always underproduces, sometimes dramatically, because the production curve has bottomed out while the heating load, if you heat with electricity, has climbed. The result is a bill that bounces around the calendar even though the system is behaving exactly as it was designed to. A homeowner who understands that the bounce is baked into the physics stops reading the December statement as a verdict on the whole investment and starts reading it as one expected data point in a longer story.

The size of the swing surprises people who have never watched their own meter across a year. It is not unusual for a roof to send several times more energy to the grid in a peak summer month than in the depth of winter, and the household using that energy may have flipped its own pattern in between, leaning on the air conditioner in August and the heat in January. Two curves moving in opposite directions, each with its own peak months, guarantee that some statements look triumphant and others look grim even though the hardware never changed its behavior. The trap is treating any single month as the truth. A brilliant June statement is no more the real story than an alarming December one; both are single frames from a film that only makes sense played all the way through.

## How annual banking and the year-end true-up settle everything

If each month had to stand entirely on its own, winter solar really would look like a bad deal, and this is where a lot of the anxiety comes from. But the months do not stand alone, because of a mechanism that ties them together across the calendar: net metering. When your panels produce more than you use, the surplus flows out to the grid and earns you a credit; when you use more than you produce, you draw those banked credits back down instead of paying full price for the shortfall. The full mechanics are worth reading in our [explainer on net metering](/blog/net-metering-explained/), but the seasonal consequence is the part that matters right here, and it is the part that turns a scary winter charge into a non-event.

Net metering effectively lets your sunny months bank energy credits that your dark months then spend. The surplus your array pushes onto the grid in May and June does not evaporate at the end of the billing cycle. Under a retail net metering arrangement it carries forward and offsets the deficit you run in December and January, so the grid becomes, in effect, a seasonal savings account. You deposit energy in summer and withdraw it in winter, and the withdrawal is why a winter charge is not evidence of failure. A system sized to cover your *annual* usage can therefore leave you with small or even zero net energy charges across a full year, even though roughly half the individual months, taken in isolation, ran a deficit. This is exactly why the right question is never "did solar cover my bill this month?" but rather "did solar cover my usage across the year?" The monthly statement is a snapshot of one uneven month, sometimes a flattering one and sometimes an alarming one; the annual result is the picture that actually measures whether the system is doing its job.

Most net-metered accounts formalize this once a year in a single statement called the true-up, and knowing it is coming reframes the whole seasonal swing. Instead of squaring the books completely every month, the utility tracks your running balance of credits and charges across twelve months and settles the net at the end of that period. If your summer surplus outweighed your winter shortfall, the annual balance can land near zero or even in your favor; if your winter usage ran ahead of what summer managed to bank, the true-up is where you pay the difference, all at once, for the year. The details of that annual reckoning, and the reason your monthly statements can look strange in the months leading up to it, are laid out in our [guide to the true-up bill](/blog/solar-true-up-bill/). Once you understand the true-up, a winter charge stops reading as a malfunction and starts reading as exactly what it is: you spending credits you banked months earlier, or making a modest year-end settlement for the gap between what summer stored and what winter consumed. The seasonal swings are just the interim scenery on the way to that annual number.

It helps to hold one more expectation steady alongside all of this: even a perfectly sized system rarely produces a literal zero across every month, and seasonality is only part of the reason. Fixed monthly charges from the utility continue regardless of how much your panels produce, so there is usually a small floor to the bill no matter what. Any month where you consume more than your banked credits can cover will still show usage charges on top of that floor. All of this is normal, and it is the subject of our [look at why the electric bill isn't zero after solar](/blog/electric-bill-after-solar/). Seasonality layers on top of those fixed realities, making some months look like a clear win and others like a loss, when in truth both are just fragments of the same annual outcome. Expecting a few dollars every month, and a real charge in the deep of winter, keeps the whole thing from feeling like a broken promise.

## Sizing against the year, not the best or worst month

Because the annual total is what actually determines your savings, the sizing decision should be made against yearly usage rather than any single good or bad month, and this is where seasonal thinking turns practical. A system sized to your best summer month will fall short all winter, leaving you buying power in the months it can least support. A system sized to your worst winter month will pour unused surplus onto the grid all summer, often compensated at less than the retail rate you paid, which quietly wastes capacity you paid to install. The balanced target sits between those two mistakes: size to your annual usage and let the fat months carry the lean ones, which is precisely the arrangement net metering was built to reward.

Two details sharpen that target. The first is what your utility actually pays for surplus once your banked credits run out or reset. Under full retail net metering, an overbuilt system is only mildly wasteful, because the extra summer production still offsets winter usage at full value. But where exported energy is credited at less than retail, oversizing gets more expensive fast, since the surplus you cannot use up is compensated at the lower export rate rather than the retail rate you would have paid. In that setting, a system aimed slightly below your total annual usage, sized to the energy you can genuinely consume or bank, often returns more per dollar than one built to zero out every last kilowatt-hour. The second detail is the shape of your own usage. A household that heats with electricity carries a winter-heavy load that the weakest production months struggle to cover even with banking, which pushes the balanced size a little higher so the summer surplus is large enough to carry that heavier winter. A household that mostly cools in summer has the easier problem, because its demand peak lines up with the production peak. Neither of these is visible in a single month's statement, which is exactly why the annual view is the only one that sizes a system correctly.

Whichever shape your usage takes, the discipline is the same: gather a full year of consumption, match the system to that yearly figure with an eye on how your utility treats exports, and then judge the result over the same full year rather than reacting to any one billing cycle.
