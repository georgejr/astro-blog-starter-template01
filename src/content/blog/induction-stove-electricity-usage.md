The wattage on an induction cooktop looks alarming at first glance. A single large burner might be rated at 2,400 or 3,700 watts, and a full range can list a total connected load of 7,000 watts or more, which sounds like something that would spin your meter into a blur. That number is real but misleading, because it describes the instantaneous power the burner can draw at full blast, not how much energy you actually consume over a meal. Cooking is a series of short, intense bursts separated by long stretches of low or no power — you crank a burner to boil water, then drop it to a simmer, then turn it off — and it's the energy summed over that whole pattern, measured in kilowatt-hours, that shows up on your bill. Once you look at it that way, induction turns out to be one of the more modest line items in a typical home's electricity use, not one of the scary ones.

The reason induction lands lighter than its wattage suggests is that it's genuinely efficient at getting energy into the pot. An induction burner heats the cookware directly through a magnetic field rather than heating a coil or a flame that then heats the pan, so very little energy is wasted warming the air, the burner surface, or the kitchen. That efficiency, combined with the fact that most cooking is intermittent, keeps the monthly total low. For a household that cooks the way most Americans do — a mix of quick weeknight meals and more involved weekend cooking — induction typically adds something on the order of 25 kilowatt-hours a month, give or take with how much and how you cook. That's a small slice of a home's usage, and putting it in context against the appliances that actually dominate your bill is the quickest way to stop worrying about it.

## From nameplate watts to the kWh that hit your bill

The gap between an induction burner's rating and its real energy use comes down to duty cycle — the fraction of time it's actually pulling power, and at what level. A burner rated at 2,000 watts running flat out for a full hour would consume 2 kilowatt-hours, since a kilowatt-hour is simply 1,000 watts sustained for an hour. But almost nothing you cook keeps a burner at full power for a solid hour. Boiling a pot of water for pasta might mean 2,000 watts for the six or eight minutes it takes to reach a boil, then a drop to maybe 800 watts to hold the boil, then off. Searing and sautéing are bursts of high power followed by lower settings. A long simmer or a slow braise might sit at a few hundred watts for an hour or more, which is a lot of clock time but not much power. The energy is the area under that jagged curve, and for most meals it adds up to a fraction of a kilowatt-hour per dish.

Working a realistic example makes the scale concrete. Assume a typical dinner uses roughly the equivalent of one burner running at an average of 1,000 watts for about 30 minutes of active cooking — some of that at high power, most at lower settings. That's about 0.5 kilowatt-hours for the meal. Cook one such dinner a day and you're near 15 kilowatt-hours a month from that alone; add breakfast cooking, more involved weekend meals, boiling water for coffee or tea, and the occasional big-batch cook, and a household lands in the neighborhood of 25 kilowatt-hours a month for all its stovetop cooking combined. A home that cooks lightly — a lot of takeout, quick reheats, minimal from-scratch cooking — might use half that. A household that cooks large meals daily, cans and preserves, or has several people all making their own food could run noticeably higher. The point is that the monthly figure is driven by how much you cook and for how long, not by the frightening peak wattage on the spec sheet.

A week of real cooking makes the monthly figure feel less abstract. Picture a household that cooks most nights: pasta on Monday, a stir-fry on Tuesday, a slow-simmered sauce on Wednesday, quick eggs and toast several mornings, and a bigger multi-pot meal on the weekend. The weeknight dinners land around a third to a half of a kilowatt-hour each, breakfasts a fraction of that, and the weekend cook maybe a full kilowatt-hour or a bit more because several burners run at once for longer. Sum a typical week and you're in the neighborhood of five or six kilowatt-hours; multiply out to a month and you arrive back at that 25-ish figure without any single meal ever feeling like a heavy draw. The reason the total stays modest is that even an ambitious home cook rarely has burners at full power for more than a few minutes at a stretch — the intense moments are brief, and the long, low simmers that feel like they must be costly are actually sipping only a couple hundred watts.

To turn that into a dollar figure, you multiply the monthly kilowatt-hours by your electricity rate, and the honest range of outcomes is small. At a rate of, say, $0.17 per kilowatt-hour — a mid-range assumption; your own rate could be higher or lower — 25 kilowatt-hours a month costs about $4.25. Even a heavy cooking household at 40 or 50 kilowatt-hours is looking at single-digit dollars a month for cooktop energy. That's why induction rarely moves a bill in a way you'd notice; the cooking load is real but small next to the household's big consumers. If you want to see exactly what your own cooking habit costs, you can plug an estimated monthly kilowatt-hour figure and your actual rate into the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) and watch how little the cooktop contributes relative to the whole. And if you're not sure what your rate even is, pulling it off your statement is a five-minute exercise covered in [reading your electric bill line by line](/blog/how-to-read-electric-bill/), since the per-kilowatt-hour number is what turns any energy estimate into money.

One thing the wattage rating does genuinely affect is the wiring an induction range needs, and it's worth separating that from the energy question because people conflate the two. A full induction range with a built-in oven usually calls for its own dedicated 240-volt circuit, often rated at 40 or 50 amps, the same kind of heavy circuit an electric range or dryer uses — that's a consequence of the high peak power the appliance can momentarily demand, not of how much energy it uses over a month. A standalone induction cooktop may need a similar circuit depending on its size, while smaller portable single-burner units run on an ordinary 120-volt outlet. The practical takeaway is that the intimidating wattage on the spec sheet is a wiring specification, telling your electrician how much current to provision, rather than a bill forecast. The energy that lands on your statement is still governed by the modest duty cycle of real cooking, not by the circuit's capacity to deliver a big burst when you crank a burner to boil.

## Where induction sits against gas and against the rest of your home

The comparison people most want is induction versus the gas stove they might be replacing, and it splits into two different questions: energy used and cost. On energy, induction wins clearly at the appliance, because it delivers far more of its input energy into the food than a gas burner does. A gas flame loses a large share of its heat to the surrounding air and the pan's sides, while induction couples almost directly into the cookware, so the same pot of water reaches a boil using less delivered energy on induction. That efficiency is why induction cooktops feel so responsive and why the kitchen stays cooler. On raw energy terms, moving from gas to induction reduces the total energy your cooking consumes.

Cost is a more nuanced comparison, because gas and electricity are priced differently and the answer depends on your local rates. Natural gas is often cheap per unit of energy, so a household that had very inexpensive gas might find the monthly cooking cost roughly a wash after switching, while a household with pricey electricity might pay a little more per meal on induction despite using less total energy — and a household on the opposite pricing could save. The energy-efficiency advantage is consistent; the dollar outcome swings with the ratio of your electric rate to your gas rate. What's reliably true is that the cooktop is a small enough load either way that the switch is rarely made or broken on the energy bill alone — people move to induction for the cooking performance, the cooler kitchen, and getting combustion out of the house, and the modest running cost is a footnote rather than the headline.

The most useful reframing is to see where cooking actually ranks among your home's electricity uses, because that context dissolves most of the anxiety about induction. Cooking, even a lot of it, is a minor player next to the loads that genuinely dominate a home's consumption. Heating and cooling, water heating, and in many homes the clothes dryer each dwarf the cooktop, and those are the places where kilowatt-hours pile up month after month. A single load of laundry through an electric dryer can use as much energy as several days of cooking. The appliances quietly eating most of your electricity are laid out in [which loads dominate a typical home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), and the cooktop almost never makes the top of that list. Seeing induction in that lineup — a few dozen kilowatt-hours a month against a household total that runs into the hundreds or beyond — is what turns the scary wattage rating back into the small line item it really is.

That perspective also helps if you're thinking about induction as part of a broader move toward an all-electric, and possibly solar-powered, home. Adding an induction cooktop nudges your total electricity usage up by a small, predictable amount, and it's a much gentler addition than electrifying your heat or your hot water. If you're sizing your overall consumption — to compare against a solar array, to understand a rising bill, or just to know where you stand — the cooktop belongs in the tally, but it won't be the swing factor. Knowing roughly where a typical household lands overall gives you the baseline to slot cooking into, and [how much electricity the average American home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) is the reference point for that. Against a whole-home number, the 25-ish kilowatt-hours a month that induction cooking adds is exactly the kind of small, manageable load that makes electrification easy to absorb rather than something to fear on your bill.
