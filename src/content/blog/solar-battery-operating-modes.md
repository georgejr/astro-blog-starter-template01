Open your new battery's app for the first time and you're handed a decision the salesperson never mentioned: an operating mode, usually with a friendly name and a slider, sitting on whatever setting the installer happened to leave it on. That one setting quietly decides whether your battery spends its life saving you money, standing guard for outages, or playing your utility's rate schedule against itself — and the factory default is very often not the setting that matches the reason you bought the thing. Someone who spent thousands specifically to keep the lights on during storms can discover their battery has been cheerfully draining itself for tiny bill savings, leaving nothing in the tank the night the grid actually fails. The mode is the difference, and almost nobody points it out.

The modes aren't complicated once you see what each one is actually optimizing for, and the underlying picture is simpler than the marketing names suggest. A battery can only ever be in one of three physical states at any moment: charging, holding steady, or discharging. Every operating mode is really just a rule for choosing among those three states based on a different goal — save money, stay ready for an outage, or beat the peak rate. Pick the goal that actually matters to you, and the right mode mostly picks itself. It helps to be clear first on [how a home battery moves energy in the first place](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/), because once you can picture power flowing in from solar and back out to the house, the modes stop being mysterious app settings and start being obvious strategies.

## The three jobs a battery can prioritize

Underneath the branded names, home batteries do one of three jobs, and the modes are just those jobs made selectable in software. The first is backup-only, the battery as pure insurance. In this mode the battery charges up, reaches full, and then simply sits there doing nothing day to day — holding its entire capacity in reserve for a grid outage, at which point it seamlessly takes over and keeps your home running without you lifting a finger. You get no bill savings from this arrangement, because the battery never cycles to offset expensive grid power; it's a fully charged safety reserve, not a money-making asset. That's the honest trade for anyone whose real reason for buying was resilience: frequent outages, medical equipment that can't lose power, a well pump that means no electricity equals no water, a home with someone in it who genuinely can't sit in the dark and cold. You're treating the battery the way you treat any insurance policy — you pay for it, it sits idle, and you're grateful it's there the one week a year you need it. What actually happens the moment the grid drops, and how long a full battery will really carry your home, is its own subject worth understanding before you lean on this mode, and it's covered in detail in [what happens when the grid goes down with a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/).

The second job is self-consumption, using your own solar after the sun sets. Here the battery's task is to keep your solar energy for yourself instead of shipping it back to the utility. Through the day, surplus production that your house isn't using charges the battery; in the evening, when the panels go quiet and your usage climbs, the battery discharges to cover the home rather than letting you buy that power back from the grid. The whole aim is to purchase as little utility electricity as possible by storing your cheap daytime solar for your expensive evening hours. How much this actually saves depends entirely on your export rules, and this is where a lot of batteries get set up wrong. If your utility credits the power you export at the full retail rate — classic net metering — then storing your own solar saves you almost nothing, because a kilowatt-hour you export at noon and get full credit for offsets a kilowatt-hour you buy back at night anyway; the battery is cycling itself, and burning through its finite lifetime cycles, for no financial gain whatsoever. Self-consumption only earns its keep where exports pay poorly: under net billing or a low export rate, every kilowatt-hour you keep and use yourself is worth the full retail price you'd otherwise pay, while that same kilowatt-hour exported would have earned you pennies. In that environment, self-consumption is the mode that actually makes the battery a financial asset instead of an expensive habit.

Before the third mode, it's worth holding onto the thread that ties the first two together, because it prevents a common confusion. A battery is only ever doing one thing at any given instant — it can't simultaneously hold a full charge in reserve and cycle that same charge for savings, because the energy is either sitting there or it isn't. The modes feel like distinct products in the app, but underneath they're competing claims on one finite pool of stored energy. Backup-only claims all of it for outages. Self-consumption claims it for offsetting evening grid power. That's why you can't simply have everything at once, and why the reserve slider covered later exists at all: it's the mechanism for dividing that single pool between the jobs, rather than pretending the battery can serve two masters with the same kilowatt-hours.

The third job is time-of-use, and it's the most active of the three — the battery as a small arbitrage machine. In this mode the battery watches the clock against your utility's pricing calendar, charging itself when power is cheap (from your own solar, or from the grid during the off-peak overnight window) and discharging during the expensive peak window to spare you the highest rates of the day. It's buying energy low and using it high, timed deliberately around the rate schedule rather than around when the sun happens to shine. This only makes sense, and only makes money, if you're actually on a time-of-use rate plan with a meaningful gap between the peak and off-peak prices — no spread, no point. But on a plan with a wide spread, it can beat plain self-consumption, because instead of just avoiding grid power in general it targets the specific expensive hours where each avoided kilowatt-hour is worth the most. The interaction between storage and these rate structures is where batteries get genuinely clever, and it's worth seeing exactly how [time-of-use rates reward shifting power](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/) to judge whether your plan even has enough of a peak-to-off-peak gap to make the effort worthwhile.

## The backup reserve slider is where the real decision happens

Most batteries don't force you to pick one of those three jobs to the exclusion of the others. They let you blend them through a single control that ends up being the most consequential setting in the whole app: the backup reserve. It sets the floor the battery won't discharge below during normal daily operation, holding that percentage of its capacity untouched in case of an outage while the rest is free to work at saving you money. The two extremes make the logic obvious. Set the reserve to 100% and you've effectively chosen backup-only — the battery holds everything back and never cycles for savings. Set it to 0% and the battery will happily drain itself all the way down chasing every last cent of savings, which is wonderful right up until the grid fails at 9 p.m. on a fully depleted battery and you get nothing. Almost everybody actually wants some point in between those two, and finding that point is the real decision the mode names distract from.

A worked example makes the trade concrete. Say you own a 13 kWh battery and your essential loads — refrigerator, some lights, phones, the internet, a furnace fan — draw enough that a fully charged battery would carry them for the better part of a day. Set the reserve to 30% and you're keeping roughly 4 kWh permanently in the tank for outages while letting the other 9 kWh cycle for savings each day. That reserved 4 kWh might buy you several hours of essentials if the grid drops in the evening, which is plenty for the brief, common outages most areas actually experience. Push the reserve to 70% and you're holding back around 9 kWh — most of a day of essentials — but now only about 4 kWh is free to work on your bill, so your daily savings shrink to less than half of what the full battery could deliver. Neither setting is wrong; they're two different answers to how much daily savings you're willing to trade for how much outage cushion, and the right one depends entirely on how your grid actually behaves.

One factor tips the balance more favorably than people expect: if you have solar, an outage doesn't necessarily drain a reserve straight to zero. As long as the sun comes up, your panels can recharge the battery during the day even while the grid is down, so a properly configured solar-plus-battery system can cycle through a multi-day outage — powering essentials at night, refilling by day — rather than simply running the reserve down once and quitting. That changes the reserve calculation, because you're not sizing the reserve to cover the entire outage on its own, only to bridge from one sunny day to the next. A homeowner with solar can often run a lower reserve than they'd guess and still ride out long outages, while a battery without solar behind it has only what's in the tank and argues for a more conservative reserve. The slider is where your two competing goals — savings and resilience — trade off against each other explicitly and visibly, and there is no universally correct number, because the right setting is a judgment about your own circumstances rather than a technical fact. Someone living in an area with a stable grid, who bought the battery mostly to shave their bill, might hold just 20% in reserve — enough to ride out the occasional brief blip while letting the great majority of the capacity work on savings every day. Someone in wildfire country or a storm-prone region, facing long and genuinely disruptive outages, might hold 50% or more, deliberately accepting smaller monthly savings in exchange for a deeper safety margin when an outage could last days rather than minutes. The honest way to set it is to ask two questions and answer them for your actual situation: how often does my grid really fail, and how much does a failure actually cost me in spoiled food, lost work, discomfort, or worse? A rarely-failing suburban grid points toward a low reserve and more savings; a fragile rural line in a region that loses power for days points toward a high reserve and less. What you shouldn't do is leave the slider on whatever the installer set and assume it reflects your priorities, because it almost certainly reflects theirs or the factory's instead.

## Matching the mode to your rate plan, and revisiting it later

Tie the whole decision back to your utility, because the rate environment quietly does most of the deciding for you once you understand it. Under full retail net metering, a battery saves very little in any daily operating mode — the export credit already gives you full value for your surplus, so cycling the battery to "save" money mostly just wears it out for savings that aren't actually there. In that situation backup-only, or a high reserve, is often the most rational setting: you bought resilience whether you meant to or not, so keep the capacity in reserve rather than grinding through its cycles chasing a benefit net metering already handed you for free. Under net billing or poor export rates, the calculus flips entirely, and self-consumption turns the battery into a real financial asset by keeping your cheap solar for your own evening use instead of exporting it for pennies. And on a time-of-use plan with a wide peak-to-off-peak spread, time-of-use mode targets the most expensive hours specifically and usually edges out plain self-consumption. The single mistake to avoid across all of these is running an aggressive savings mode under full net metering, where the battery cycles itself — spending its limited lifetime cycles — for savings the export credit already provided at no cost to the battery's health. Match the mode to how your utility actually pays you, not to whichever setting happens to sound the most sophisticated in the app.

This is also why a single fixed setting rarely stays optimal all year, and why the smartest owners treat the mode and reserve as seasonal dials rather than a one-time choice. In the calm months, when the grid is stable and outages are rare, you can safely run a low reserve and let the battery work hard on savings. As storm season or wildfire season approaches — the weeks when your grid is most likely to fail and a failure is most disruptive — you raise the reserve deliberately, accepting thinner savings in exchange for a deeper cushion during exactly the stretch you're most likely to need it. The same instinct applies to your rate plan: if your utility's time-of-use pricing has a much wider peak-to-off-peak spread in summer than in winter, the case for time-of-use mode strengthens in summer and weakens in winter. None of this requires constant fiddling, but checking the setting two or three times a year, keyed to the seasons that actually change your risk and your rates, captures most of the benefit that a set-and-forget owner leaves on the table.

Knowing what your battery is actually doing is the other half of this. The app that lets you pick a mode also shows you the battery's daily behavior — when it charged, when it discharged, how low it went — and glancing at that occasionally is how you catch a mismatch between what you intended and what's happening. If you set a 40% reserve for backup but the graph shows the battery draining to near zero every night, something is configured wrong, and you'd never know without looking. Treat the monitoring as the feedback loop that tells you whether your chosen mode is behaving the way you wanted, rather than assuming the setting you picked is the setting the battery is honoring. The reassuring part is that none of this is a one-time decision locked in on installation day. The setting lives entirely in software, so you can change it in the app whenever your situation changes, and situations do change. A utility rolls out a new time-of-use rate; storm season arrives and you want a deeper reserve for a few months; your utility revises what it pays for exports and suddenly self-consumption is worth more than it was. Any of those is a reason to revisit the mode, and so is simply noticing that the battery is behaving in a way that doesn't match what you wanted from it — draining every night when you meant to keep a reserve, or sitting full and idle when you meant it to save you money. Treat the setting as something you tune rather than something you set once and forget.
