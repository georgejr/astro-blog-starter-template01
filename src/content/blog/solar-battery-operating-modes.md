Open your new battery's app for the first time and you're handed a decision the salesperson never mentioned: an operating mode, usually with a friendly name and a slider, set to whatever the installer left it on. That single setting decides whether your battery spends its life saving you money, standing guard for outages, or gaming your utility's rate schedule — and the default is frequently not the one that matches why you bought it.

The modes aren't complicated once you see what each is optimizing for. A battery can only be in one place at a time — charging, holding, or discharging — so every mode is really a rule for choosing among those three based on a different goal. Pick the goal, and the right mode mostly follows.

## The three jobs a battery can prioritize

Underneath the marketing names, home batteries do one of three jobs, and the modes are just those jobs made selectable. Understanding what the battery is physically doing in each — and it helps to be clear on [how a home battery moves energy in the first place](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/) — is what lets you match the setting to your situation instead of trusting the factory default.

### Backup-only: the battery as insurance

In backup-only mode, the battery charges up and then sits full, doing nothing day to day. It's holding its entire capacity in reserve for a grid outage, at which point it seamlessly keeps your home running. You get no bill savings from this mode — the battery isn't cycling to offset expensive power — but you get the maximum possible ride-through when the grid fails.

This is the honest choice for someone whose real reason for buying was resilience: frequent outages, medical equipment, a well pump, a home that can't sit dark. You're treating the battery as an insurance policy and accepting that insurance doesn't pay you monthly. What actually happens the moment the grid drops — and how long the battery lasts — is its own subject worth understanding before you rely on this mode, covered in [what happens when the grid goes down with a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/).

### Self-consumption: using your own solar after dark

In self-consumption mode, the battery's job is to keep your solar energy for yourself instead of exporting it. During the day, surplus production charges the battery; in the evening, the battery discharges to cover your home rather than pulling from the grid. The aim is to buy as little utility power as possible.

How much this saves depends entirely on your export rules. If your utility credits exports at the full retail rate, storing your own power for later saves almost nothing — a kWh exported at noon and credited in full offsets a kWh bought at night anyway, so the battery is cycling for no financial gain. Self-consumption earns its keep where exports pay poorly: under net billing or low export rates, every kWh you keep and use yourself is worth full retail, while the same kWh exported would have earned pennies. In that environment this mode is the money-maker.

### Time-of-use: arbitraging the rate schedule

Time-of-use mode is the most active. The battery watches the clock against your utility's pricing calendar, charges when power is cheap (from solar, or from the grid during off-peak hours), and discharges during the expensive peak window to spare you the highest rates. It's buying low and using high, in energy terms.

This mode only makes sense — and only makes money — if you're actually on a time-of-use rate plan with a meaningful gap between peak and off-peak prices. On such a plan it can outperform plain self-consumption, because it targets the specific hours where avoided power is worth the most. The interaction between storage and these rate structures is where batteries get genuinely clever, and it's worth seeing how [time-of-use rates reward shifting power](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/) to understand whether your plan has enough spread to bother.

## The backup reserve slider is the real decision

Most batteries let you blend goals through a single control: the backup reserve. It sets the floor the battery won't discharge below in daily operation, holding that percentage in reserve for outages while the rest works to save money. Set the reserve to 100% and you've effectively chosen backup-only. Set it to 0% and the battery will happily drain itself chasing savings, leaving nothing if the grid fails at a bad moment. Most people want something in between.

The slider is where your two goals — savings and resilience — trade off explicitly, and there's no universally correct setting. Someone in a stable grid area who bought mostly for bill savings might hold 20% in reserve. Someone in wildfire or storm country might hold 50% or more, accepting smaller savings for a deeper safety margin. The right number is a judgment about how often your grid actually fails and how much a failure costs you, not a default worth leaving untouched.

## Which mode matches which rate plan

Tie it back to your utility, because the rate environment does most of the deciding. Under full retail net metering, a battery saves little in any daily mode, so backup-only (or a high reserve) is often the most rational setting — you bought resilience, so keep it in reserve rather than cycling it for savings that aren't there. Under net billing or poor export rates, self-consumption turns the battery into a real financial asset by keeping your cheap solar for your own use. And on a time-of-use plan with a wide peak-to-off-peak spread, time-of-use mode targets the most expensive hours and usually edges out plain self-consumption.

The mistake to avoid is running an aggressive savings mode under full net metering, where it cycles the battery — using up its finite lifetime cycles — for savings the export credit already provided for free. Match the mode to how your utility actually pays you, not to whichever setting sounds most sophisticated.

## Modes aren't permanent

None of this is a one-time decision locked in at installation. The setting lives in software, and you can change it in the app whenever your situation does — a new time-of-use rate, a stretch of storm season, a change in how much your utility pays for exports. Revisit it when your rate plan changes or when you notice the battery behaving in a way that doesn't match what you wanted from it.
