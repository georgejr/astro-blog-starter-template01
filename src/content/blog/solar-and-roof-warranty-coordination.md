Picture a stain spreading across a bedroom ceiling two years after you went solar. The leak is somewhere under the array, and now you have a problem that's part detective work and part contract law. Was it a solar mounting bolt that wasn't flashed properly? Was it an aging roof that would have leaked anyway, panels or not? Was it a shingle that failed near — but not because of — a solar penetration? The answer decides who pays, and the uncomfortable truth is that a solar-related roof leak sits precisely on the boundary between two trades that each have an incentive to point at the other. Your roofer warranties the roof. Your solar installer warranties the array and the holes they drilled to attach it. When water comes through the roof near those holes, both can plausibly claim it's not their fault, and if you didn't set things up carefully before the panels went on, you can end up caught in the middle, paying to fix something you were told two different companies stood behind.

## Why a leak under panels turns into a finger-pointing problem

The root of the problem is that installing rooftop solar means putting holes in a surface whose entire job is to keep water out. Most residential arrays are attached by lag bolts driven through the roof into the rafters or trusses, and each of those penetrations has to be sealed and flashed so water can't get in around it. A competent installer does this well — modern flashing systems are designed to shed water reliably, and a properly flashed solar penetration is no more likely to leak than a plumbing vent or a chimney that's been on the roof for decades. The reality that solar panels, correctly installed, rarely damage a sound roof is well supported, and the evidence behind that reassurance is laid out in [will solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/). But "correctly installed" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and when a leak does appear near an array, the question of whether the installation caused it becomes a genuine dispute rather than an obvious call.

That dispute is hard to resolve because the two most likely culprits look similar from inside your house. A leak caused by a solar penetration — a bolt that missed the rafter, flashing that wasn't seated right, a sealant that failed — is the installer's responsibility. A leak caused by the roof itself reaching the end of its life, or by a defect unrelated to the panels, is the roofer's responsibility, or nobody's if the roof is simply old. From the ceiling stain, you can't tell which it is, and the two contractors involved have opposite incentives to characterize it. The roofer, if they're on the hook for the roof, wants it to be a solar problem. The installer, if they're on the hook for their penetrations, wants it to be a roof problem. Sorting real causation out often requires getting up on the roof, lifting panels, and inspecting the actual penetrations — work that itself costs money and that neither party is eager to volunteer to pay for. This is why solar roof leak responsibility is less a technical question than a coordination question, and why it's so much easier to settle before the panels go on than after the ceiling is stained.

Compounding the difficulty is a wrinkle specific to solar: getting to the leak means moving the array. If the water is coming in under a section of panels, diagnosing and repairing it may require removing and later reinstalling those panels, and that removal-and-reinstall labor is a real cost that has to land on someone. A roofer asked to fix a leak under an array will often, reasonably, decline to touch the solar equipment — they're not licensed or insured to handle it, and disturbing it could void the solar warranty. So even a straightforward roof repair under panels becomes a two-contractor job, with the solar company pulling and replacing the array around the roofer's work. Who pays for that panel handling is exactly the kind of thing that falls through the cracks when nobody agreed to it in advance. The deeper mechanics of how solar penetrations, flashing, and roof warranties interact — including how a solar install can affect a roofing manufacturer's warranty — are worked through in [will solar void your roof warranty](/blog/solar-roof-warranty-penetrations/), and it's foundational to understanding who owns what when water gets in.

## Which warranty owns which layer of the problem

To keep a leak from becoming a stalemate, you have to understand that there isn't one warranty on your roof-with-solar; there are several, they cover different things, and they come from different parties with different time spans. Untangling them is the key to knowing whom to call. The roofing side typically has two layers of its own: the roofing material manufacturer's warranty, which covers defects in the shingles or tiles themselves and often runs a long time on paper but with narrow terms, and the roofing contractor's workmanship warranty, which covers the quality of the installation and usually runs a shorter span — commonly a handful of years to a couple of decades depending on the roofer. If your roof leaks because a shingle was defective, that's the material warranty; if it leaks because the roofer installed it poorly, that's their workmanship warranty; and both can be jeopardized if someone other than an approved contractor later drills into the roof, which is precisely what a solar installer does.

The solar side mirrors this structure. The equipment — panels, inverters, racking — carries manufacturer warranties covering defects in those products. Separately, and more relevant to leaks, the solar installer provides a workmanship warranty covering the quality of their installation work, including the roof penetrations they made. This installer workmanship warranty is the one that should cover a leak caused by a solar bolt or flashing, and its length is the single most important number to check, because it varies enormously between companies — some offer coverage in the ten-year range, others up to twenty-five years, and a few offer very little. The distinction between an equipment warranty that covers a failed product and a workmanship warranty that covers a botched installation is fundamental and frequently confused, and it's spelled out in [workmanship versus equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/). For roof leaks specifically, the workmanship warranty is the one that matters, because a leak is an installation problem, not a defective-panel problem.

The dangerous gap opens between these two sets of warranties, and it's a gap of both scope and time. A solar installer's workmanship warranty covers their penetrations, but it doesn't cover the rest of your roof — if the leak is elsewhere, they'll say so, correctly. A roofer's warranty covers their work, but many roofers consider it void or at least contestable once a third party has drilled dozens of holes through their installation, which they never agreed to. So a leak that lands in the ambiguous zone — near a penetration but arguably caused by roof age, or under panels but not clearly from a bolt — can find both parties with a defensible reason to decline. Time makes it worse: solar workmanship warranties often outlast roofer workmanship warranties, so a leak that appears twelve years in might fall inside the solar warranty but outside the roofer's, or vice versa, leaving whichever party is still on the hook to argue the cause was the other's. The way to shrink this gap isn't to find a perfect warranty; it's to coordinate the two before either contractor starts work, so the boundary between their responsibilities is written down rather than fought over later.

## Coordinating the two trades before you sign

The best time to solve a solar roof leak dispute is years before it happens, at the point where you're choosing when to install solar and which installer to use. The most powerful move is a sequencing decision: if your roof is more than partway through its life, replacing it before or at the same time as the solar install eliminates the worst version of this problem, because you start with a fresh roof and a single coordinated set of warranties rather than an aging roof with solar bolted through it. Drilling an array into a roof with only a few years of life left is asking to remove that array in a few years to reroof — at your expense — and it maximizes the odds of a leak dispute in the interim. The full case for timing the roof and the solar together, and how to judge whether your roof needs replacing first, is made in [should you replace your roof before going solar](/blog/replace-roof-before-solar/), and for many homeowners it's the single most consequential decision in this whole area.

When the roof is sound and you're proceeding, the coordination happens in the contract and in the questions you ask before signing. Ask the solar installer directly how long their workmanship warranty runs, exactly what it covers with respect to roof penetrations and leaks, and what their process is when a leak appears under the array — specifically, whether they'll remove and reinstall panels to diagnose and repair at no charge if the cause is their penetration, and what happens if it turns out to be the roof. Get the answers in writing, because a verbal assurance is worthless when a ceiling is staining two years later. Ask whether they use a licensed roofer or roofing-certified crew for the penetrations, since some installers subcontract the roof-interface work and that adds another party to the chain. And confirm that installing solar won't void any active roofing warranty you currently hold — if your roof is new and under a manufacturer or contractor warranty, find out whether the solar penetrations affect it and get that reconciled before the drilling starts.

Documentation is the quiet hero of this whole process, and it costs nothing but a little diligence. Keep the roof's age and any roofing warranties on file, keep the solar installer's workmanship warranty and their leak-response commitment in writing, and photograph the roof and the penetrations at install so there's a record of the condition and the workmanship. If a leak ever appears, that paper trail is what lets you assign responsibility quickly instead of refereeing a standoff between two contractors. It's also worth folding the possibility of a future leak repair, and the panel-removal cost that comes with it, into how you think about the total lifetime cost of going solar — the sticker price of the install isn't the last dollar the roof interface will ever cost you, and grounding your budget with a tool like the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) helps set realistic expectations for the system while you keep a mental reserve for the roof-related contingencies. Leaks under solar panels are uncommon when the work is done well, and the broader reality is that panels don't cause roof leaks nearly as often as forum horror stories suggest — a myth examined in [do solar panels cause roof leaks](/blog/do-solar-panels-cause-roof-leaks/). But "uncommon" isn't "never," and the homeowners who handle a leak painlessly are the ones who decided, in writing, who owns which layer of the roof before anyone climbed up to drill the first hole.
