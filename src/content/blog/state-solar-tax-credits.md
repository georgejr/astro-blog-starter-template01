A lot of solar shoppers hear "30% tax credit" and assume that's the whole tax story. In some states it is. In others, there's a second, entirely separate credit waiting on your state income-tax return, and homeowners who don't know it exists leave real money unclaimed. The two credits come from different governments, follow different rules, and are claimed on different forms, but they can apply to the same solar purchase at the same time — the state credit stacking on top of the federal one rather than replacing it. Understanding how that stacking works, and the handful of ways it can trip you up, is the difference between capturing every dollar your purchase qualifies for and accidentally leaving a chunk of it behind.

The catch is that state solar credits are a moving, state-by-state patchwork. Some states have generous ones, many have none, and the specifics — the percentage, the cap, whether unused credit carries forward — change from state to state and over time as legislatures act. So the goal here isn't to hand you a list of which states offer what, because any such list goes stale; it's to explain how these credits work in general, how they interact with the federal credit, and what to verify for your own state so you claim everything you're owed and nothing you're not.

## Two governments, two credits, one solar system

The foundation is understanding that the federal and state credits are genuinely independent instruments, and their independence is what makes stacking possible. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is a credit against your federal income-tax liability, worth 30% of your qualifying solar costs, administered by the IRS, and available in every state — it doesn't care where you live. The mechanics of it, including that it's a nonrefundable credit you claim on your federal return and can carry forward if you can't use it all in one year, are covered in [how the solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/), and it's the anchor of almost every residential solar purchase's incentive picture. A state solar tax credit, where one exists, is a completely separate credit against your *state* income-tax liability, administered by your state's revenue department, defined by your state's law, and claimed on your state income-tax return. The two don't come from the same pot of money, they don't reduce each other, and qualifying for one has nothing to do with qualifying for the other. That separation is precisely why they stack: a dollar of federal credit reduces what you owe the IRS, and a dollar of state credit reduces what you owe your state, and there's no rule that says claiming one forfeits the other.

The stacking is powerful because the two credits attack your net cost from different directions, and together they can take a serious bite out of what you actually pay. Picture a state that offers, say, a 25% credit on solar costs with a cap — the exact numbers vary and you should treat these as an illustration rather than any specific state's current program. On a $20,000 system, the 30% federal credit is worth $6,000, and a 25% state credit, if uncapped, would be worth another $5,000, so the two together could in principle cover more than half the system's cost. In practice state credits are usually capped at a maximum dollar amount, so the state portion is smaller than the raw percentage suggests — a common structure is a percentage credit up to a ceiling of a few thousand dollars. Even so, a few thousand dollars of state credit stacked on top of the federal $6,000 is a meaningful improvement to your payback, and it's money that exists whether or not you know to claim it. The way multiple incentives combine — state credits, federal credit, rebates, property-tax and sales-tax exemptions — into a single stacked-up reduction of your net cost is the broader subject of [how credits, rebates and exemptions combine](/blog/stacking-solar-incentives/), and a state income-tax credit is one of the cleaner, more valuable pieces of that stack where it's offered.

There's a subtlety in how the two credits interact that's worth getting right, because it affects the exact dollars. The federal credit is calculated on your qualifying solar cost, and the question sometimes arises whether receiving a state tax credit reduces the cost basis you use for the federal calculation. The general treatment for a state *tax credit* — as opposed to a state rebate — is that it does not reduce the federal cost basis the way a rebate from a utility might, so you typically calculate the 30% federal credit on your full system cost and separately claim the state credit on its own terms. This distinction between a state tax credit and a cash rebate matters because they're treated differently for federal purposes, and it's exactly the kind of detail where a tax professional earns their fee. The safe posture is to not assume, and to confirm the interaction for your specific state's credit rather than guessing, because the difference between calculating the federal credit on the full cost versus a reduced basis is real money. This is a recurring theme with state credits: the general shape is simple, but the specifics are where the value and the pitfalls live.

Claiming the state credit is its own small project, separate from the federal paperwork, and the process varies by state. Where the federal credit is claimed on IRS Form 5695 — walked through step by step in [filing IRS Form 5695](/blog/irs-form-5695-solar/) — the state credit is claimed on whatever form your state's revenue department designates, often a specific renewable-energy or solar credit schedule attached to your state income-tax return. Some states require you to apply for or reserve the credit before or shortly after installation, sometimes through the state energy office, and some have annual funding caps that can run out, meaning the credit is available on a first-come basis until the year's allocation is exhausted. Missing an application deadline or a reservation step can cost you the credit even though your system qualifies, which is why the single most important thing to do is find your state's actual current rules — from the state revenue or energy department, not from a solar salesperson or an old blog post — before you install, so you don't discover a required step after the window has closed.

## What actually varies, and how to make sure you capture it

The reason state solar credits can't be summarized in a simple table is that nearly every parameter varies by state and changes over time, and each variation affects how much you actually get. The percentage varies — some states credit a modest slice of system cost, others a larger one. The cap varies enormously and is usually the binding constraint, because a generous-sounding percentage with a low dollar ceiling is really a small credit for any normal-sized system. Whether unused credit carries forward matters for anyone whose state tax liability is smaller than the credit: like the federal credit, a state credit is typically nonrefundable, meaning it can only reduce your state tax to zero and not below, so if your state credit exceeds your state tax bill for the year, the ability to carry the remainder into future years determines whether you capture the full value or forfeit the excess. States differ on whether and how long that carryforward is allowed. And the credit's very existence varies and expires — state solar credits get created, extended, reduced, and allowed to sunset by legislatures, so a program that existed when a neighbor installed may be gone or diminished by the time you do, and vice versa.

That volatility drives the one firm rule for state credits: verify the current program directly, for your state, at the time you're installing, and don't rely on any figure — including any in this article — as a live fact. The right sources are your state's revenue department for the tax mechanics and your state energy office for any application or reservation requirements. This matters both ways. On the upside, you don't want to miss a valuable credit because you assumed your state didn't offer one; on the downside, you don't want to bake a state credit into your budget that has expired or run out of funding for the year, then find your net cost is thousands higher than you planned. A solar salesperson has every incentive to quote you the most favorable incentive picture, so treat any state-credit figure in a sales pitch as something to independently confirm rather than as a settled number in your budget.

The interaction with your actual tax situation is the last piece, and it's the one most often glossed over. Both the federal and state credits are nonrefundable and depend on you having tax liability to offset — the federal credit against your federal liability, the state credit against your state liability. A homeowner with substantial income has plenty of both and captures both credits fully. A retiree living on modest income, someone whose withholding and deductions already zero out their state tax, or a household with little state tax liability may find they can't use the full state credit in the year they install, and whether they eventually capture it depends entirely on their state's carryforward rules. This is the same tax-liability constraint that governs the federal credit, applied separately to your state return, and it's worth thinking through before you count either credit as guaranteed savings. If your state and federal tax liabilities are both healthy, both credits are money in hand; if either is thin, the corresponding credit may be delayed or partially lost, and that changes the payback math.

To put the whole picture into a number you can act on, work it in the right order. Establish your qualifying system cost. Calculate the 30% federal credit on that cost — the [federal tax credit calculator](/federal-tax-credit-calculator/) does this cleanly and reminds you it's a credit against federal liability, not a rebate — and confirm you have the federal tax liability to use it. Then, separately, look up your state's current solar income-tax credit, if any: its percentage, its cap, its carryforward rules, and any application deadline, from your state's own agencies. Add the two credits together, subtract them from your gross cost, and you have a net cost that reflects the full stack rather than just the federal half. Feed that stacked net cost into the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) to see what the state credit does to your return, because a few thousand dollars of state credit stacked on the federal one can shorten payback by a year or more — a real improvement that's easy to leave on the table simply by not knowing the credit exists. The federal credit is the reliable anchor available everywhere; the state credit is the bonus that rewards homeowners who check. Check for yours, confirm it's current, claim it on the right form by the right deadline, and let it stack.
