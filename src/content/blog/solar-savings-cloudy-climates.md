A cloud doesn't switch a solar panel off. It dims it. Sunlight reaching the ground arrives in two forms: direct beam radiation, the sharp light that casts crisp shadows, and diffuse radiation, the softer light scattered by the atmosphere and by the clouds themselves, which fills a shadow with brightness even when the sun is nowhere to be seen. Panels convert both. On a heavily overcast day a panel might produce only 10 to 25 percent of its clear-sky output, but that floor sits well above zero, because the diffuse light still carries real energy and the cell still turns it into current. That one physical fact quietly decides the entire question of solar in cloudy places: reduced output is not the same as no output, and a great deal of cloudy-climate pessimism comes from confusing the two.

Whether that reduced output actually *pays off* is a separate question, and a financial one rather than a meteorological one. Cloudy regions do produce fewer kilowatt-hours per panel, which pushes payback longer, and there's no honest way around that. But payback depends on far more than sunshine, and in several of the cloudiest parts of the country the other factors push hard enough in solar's favor to more than make up the difference. The realistic answer is that cloudiness matters, and it matters in a direction that lengthens payback, but it rarely decides the outcome on its own.

## Why an overcast sky still produces

The reason panels keep working under cloud is that they respond to a broad slice of the light spectrum, not just to direct, unobstructed sunbeams. The mechanics of this — why a gray sky still drives meaningful production, and why the intuition that solar "needs constant sun" is a myth — are laid out in [the cloudy-day myth](/blog/do-solar-panels-work-on-cloudy-days/). The short version is that a panel's output tracks the total light energy landing on it, and even a fully overcast sky delivers a genuine fraction of that energy rather than cutting it off. The panel doesn't go dark when the sun ducks behind a cloud; it produces less, in proportion to how much light is getting through. Over a year, a cloudy climate simply accumulates fewer of those photons than a desert does, and the array makes correspondingly fewer kilowatt-hours. That production shortfall is the real, honest cost of a cloudy climate, and it's the number that has to be weighed against everything else in the financial picture.

There's a temperature twist worth knowing, because it complicates the tidy intuition that "sunny always beats cloudy." Solar cells lose efficiency as they heat up, so a cool, bright day in a cloudy northern climate can produce more per hour than a scorching one in the desert, where the panels bake and their voltage sags under the heat. Cool, cloudy regions don't get the desert's raw sun hours — nothing changes that — but the hours they do receive are often converted a little more efficiently, because the cells are running closer to their ideal temperature. It doesn't erase the gap between a gray climate and a sunny one, but it narrows it, and it's a reason the annual harvest in a cool cloudy place tends to come out somewhat better than a naive "they get half the sun, so they make half the power" estimate would suggest.

What actually determines a cloudy region's annual harvest is the total sun-hours it accumulates across a full year — the running sum of all that direct and diffuse light, cloudy days included. Cloudy places have fewer of them, so a given array makes fewer kilowatt-hours than the same array would in Arizona, and that's simply the starting condition you're working from. The mistake is treating that starting condition as the whole story. Because so much of the outcome depends on your specific latitude, your typical cloud cover, and how those combine over the seasons, the sensible move is to estimate your own location's annual production rather than reason from a general impression of how gray your winters feel. You can do exactly that with the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), which bakes regional sun data — cloud cover included — into its production figures, so the number it hands back already accounts for the overcast days rather than assuming a perpetual clear sky.

It's worth putting a rough scale on the penalty so the fear doesn't outrun the facts. A genuinely cloudy US region doesn't produce a tenth of what a desert does over a year; it more typically lands somewhere in the range of two-thirds to three-quarters of the sunniest states' annual yield per panel, because the many partly cloudy and bright-overcast days still contribute real energy and only the truly dark, storm-heavy days drop toward that 10-to-25-percent floor. That's a meaningful haircut, but it's a haircut, not a shutdown, and it's smaller than most people picture when they imagine a gray climate. The shape of the loss matters as much as its size, too. Cloudy-climate production is strongly seasonal — often quite good through long summer days when the sun is high and clouds are thinner, and weak through short, gray winters — so the annual figure is really an average of a generous season and a lean one rather than a steady trickle. That seasonal swing is exactly why the way surplus is handled becomes so important, and it's the reason two cloudy homes with identical annual production can end up with very different savings depending on whether their summer abundance is allowed to carry their winter shortfall. One more distinction is worth drawing: a persistent marine layer or morning fog that burns off by midday is a very different production story than a genuinely overcast storm belt, because the foggy climate often recovers its strongest sun in the afternoon when the loads and the rates are highest. The label "cloudy" hides a lot of variation, which is one more reason to estimate your specific location rather than lump yourself in with a reputation.

## Production is only one term in the equation

Here is where cloudy-climate pessimism usually goes wrong: it treats sunshine as the entire story, when payback is really a fraction with sunshine affecting only part of the top. On top of the fraction sits your annual savings — roughly the kilowatt-hours you produce multiplied by the price of the grid electricity they replace. On the bottom sits the system's net cost. Sunshine only touches the top term, and only one factor within it. The other factors can, and frequently do, more than compensate for a modest harvest.

The single most important of those other factors is your electricity rate. A kilowatt-hour of solar production is worth exactly as much as the grid power it displaces, so an expensive-electricity region rewards every single kilowatt-hour more richly than a cheap-electricity one does. This is the pivot the whole question turns on, because many cloudy areas — parts of the Northeast, for one — pair modest sunshine with high power prices, and a high rate can outweigh a low harvest entirely. A panel making fewer kilowatt-hours that are each worth a lot can easily beat a panel making more kilowatt-hours that are each worth little. The arithmetic makes the point more plainly than any argument. Picture two identical arrays, and assume — purely as illustration, not as quotes — a cloudy, high-rate home whose array makes 8,000 kWh a year at $0.28 per kWh, against a sunny, cheap-power home whose identical array makes 11,000 kWh at $0.11 per kWh. The cloudy roof avoids about $2,240 of grid cost a year ($8,000 × 0.28); the sunny roof avoids about $1,210 ($11,000 × 0.11). The cloudier roof produces nearly a third fewer kilowatt-hours and still saves almost twice as much, purely because each unit of energy it makes is worth more than double. Swap in your own rate and production and the same multiplication tells you where you actually stand — the number that matters is dollars saved, and dollars are kilowatt-hours times price, not kilowatt-hours alone. The mirror image is the genuinely hard case: a cloudy region with *cheap* electricity, where both terms work against you at once, few kilowatt-hours each worth little. That's the situation examined in [is solar worth the trouble in cloudy, cheap-power Washington](/blog/solar-panels-in-washington/), where it's the abundant low-cost hydropower, not the clouds alone, that stretches payback uncomfortably long. It's a useful reminder that the villain in a stubborn payback calculation is usually the rate, not the weather.

A second non-sunshine factor deserves as much attention as the rate: what your utility does with the power you send back. A cloudy climate produces unevenly — strong in summer, weak through a gray winter — which makes the treatment of surplus especially consequential. Under full net metering, summer overproduction banks as credit that offsets the winter shortfall, effectively letting the abundant months carry the lean ones, and that structure is remarkably forgiving of exactly the big seasonal swings that describe most cloudy regions. Where export credits fall below the retail rate, the seasonal mismatch bites harder, because the summer surplus you bank is worth less than the winter grid power it's meant to offset later. That's a structural policy variable rather than a weather one, and it can swing a cloudy region's economics as much as the cloud cover itself, so when you're weighing solar in a low-sun area, the export policy deserves to sit right alongside the sun-hours in your thinking — a generous one can rescue a cloudy climate, and a stingy one can compound its handicap. Sitting on top of all of this is the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, which reduces net cost by 30% regardless of how sunny your roof happens to be. Because it cuts the *bottom* term of the payback fraction rather than the top, it helps cloudy and sunny homes proportionally alike — a smaller absolute dollar amount against a cloudy region's typically smaller savings, but the same percentage improvement to the return. Rate escalation belongs in the same category of quiet helpers, because it compounds every one of those saved kilowatt-hours over the system's life. If your utility's prices tend to climb year after year — and in many high-rate regions they have — then each unit of solar production is worth a little more every year than it was the year before, which shortens payback regardless of how sunny the roof is. A cloudy home under expensive, rising rates is watching its savings grow over time even as its production stays flat, and that trajectory is easy to miss when you judge solar on a single year's snapshot. Whether all of this adds up to a system worth buying is the broader judgment walked through in [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/), which frames the harvest, the rate, and the policy as the three legs they really are.

## Running your own numbers

Because so many terms interact — production, rate, rate escalation, export treatment, and net cost after the credit — cloudy-climate solar simply can't be judged by a glance at the sky. It has to be calculated with your specific inputs, and the honest truth is that changing any one of those terms materially can flip the answer from "clearly worth it" to "barely" or the other way around. A home under expensive grid power with full net metering can pencil out beautifully in a climate that feels far too gray for solar, while a home under cheap power with poor export credits can struggle even somewhere sunnier. The forecast alone will not tell you which of those you are, and neither will your neighbor's opinion or a national average. Only the arithmetic run on your own figures will.

The practical move, then, is to plug real numbers in rather than reason from the weather. Estimate the production for your actual location, apply your true electricity rate along with a reasonable assumption about how fast it's rising, account for how your utility treats exported power, and let a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) combine all of it into a payback period you can actually trust. What people frequently find when they do this is that a cloudy roof sitting over expensive grid power with fair export rules pays back within a range that genuinely surprises them, because they had mentally written the system off on the strength of the climate before ever doing the multiplication. And occasionally they find the opposite — a gray sky over cheap, plentiful power that stretches payback long enough to give real pause. Either result is worth knowing, and both come from the same place. Gathering the inputs is less work than it sounds, and each one is something you can actually get rather than guess. Your electricity rate is printed on your utility bill — use your true all-in cost per kilowatt-hour, including delivery charges, not just the headline supply rate, since solar offsets the whole thing. Your production estimate comes from a location-aware tool that already folds in your regional cloud cover, so you're not the one having to judge how gray your winters are. Your export treatment takes a little digging into your utility's current rules — whether surplus is credited at the retail rate, a lower rate, or something tiered — but it's the single variable most likely to swing the answer in a seasonal climate, so it's worth the phone call or the search. And the net cost is simply the installed price after the 30% federal credit. Feed those four into the arithmetic and you get a payback period grounded in your reality rather than your region's reputation.

There's a design question that often follows once the numbers are in view. In a cloudy climate with poor export credits, a smaller array sized to cover the power you actually use in real time — rather than a large one that dumps summer surplus onto the grid for pennies — sometimes pays back faster, because every kilowatt-hour you self-consume is worth full retail while every exported one is worth much less. In a cloudy climate with full net metering, the opposite can hold, because the grid banks your summer surplus at full value to spend down in winter, which rewards building larger. The right size, in other words, is downstream of the same export policy that drives the savings, and it's another reason the calculation has to be yours rather than a rule of thumb.

None of this is theoretical, which is worth remembering when the gray sky outside makes solar feel like a bad bet. Some of the country's most established solar markets sit in decidedly cloudy regions, thriving on exactly the combination this piece has traced — high electricity prices and supportive export rules more than making up for a modest harvest. Homeowners in those places aren't defying physics; they're benefiting from the arithmetic that says a fewer-but-more-valuable kilowatt-hour can beat a more-but-cheaper one. The lesson isn't that cloudy climates are secretly great for solar everywhere, because the cheap-power cases are genuinely hard, but that the sky is a weak predictor of the outcome on its own. The forecast tells you how much energy you'll make. Your rate, your utility's export rules, and your system's cost tell you what that energy is worth, and those three, not the clouds, usually decide the answer. Clouds shave the top of the equation; whether that shave actually matters to the bottom line depends entirely on everything sitting underneath it. Run the numbers with your own inputs, and the sky stops being the thing you're guessing about and becomes just one term among several in a calculation you can finally see clearly.
