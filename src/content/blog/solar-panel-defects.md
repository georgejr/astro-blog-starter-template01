A solar panel loses output in two very different ways, and confusing them costs homeowners either money or peace of mind. The first is the slow, uniform fade every module experiences — a fraction of a percent a year, spread evenly across all 60 or 72 cells, entirely expected. The second is a localized defect: a fault in one cell, one region, or one panel that drags production down faster than the datasheet ever promised. Telling those two apart is the whole art of reading a struggling array, because the fixes, the costs, and the urgency behind them are nothing alike.

Normal degradation gets its own treatment in [what your system loses each year](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/); the number to hold in your head is roughly half a percent annually, give or take, applied evenly across the whole array. Anything steeper, patchier, or specific to one panel belongs to the defect category, and the three worth knowing by name are microcracks, hotspots, and potential induced degradation. They behave differently, they show up differently in your data, and they call for different responses.

## Cracks you can't see and the heat they cause

Silicon solar cells are brittle in a way their rugged aluminum frames disguise. The wafer inside is roughly the thickness of a few sheets of paper — manufacturers have shaved it thinner over the years to stretch expensive silicon further — and it tolerates flexing about as well as a saltine does. The forces that fracture it rarely leave a mark you could spot from the driveway. A hairline break in the crystalline wafer, a microcrack, hides under the glass and does its damage quietly, interrupting the fine metal fingers that are supposed to carry current off the cell.

Most microcracks are older than any electricity the panel has ever made. They get seeded before the first kilowatt-hour: a module set down hard on a pallet, a wafer stressed during soldering or lamination at the factory, a shipping container vibrating over a thousand miles of highway, a boot pressed on the glass during install, or a mounting clamp torqued a quarter-turn too far. Others arrive later, from hail strikes, heavy snow load, wind flexing the frame, and the daily expansion and contraction of a panel that heats up under the sun and cools off after dark. That last one — thermal cycling — is the reason a crack that looked harmless on day one tends to matter more with time. A fracture can be electrically "closed" on a warm afternoon, its two silicon faces still touching and current still crossing, then open as the cell contracts on a cold night. Season after season, that flexing can widen a hairline into an isolated fragment that no longer contributes anything, or worse, into a resistive bottleneck that starts to heat up.

The electrical consequence is subtle at first. Cells in a panel are wired in series, so they all carry the same current, which means the weakest cell sets a ceiling for its neighbors — the narrowest section of pipe throttling flow through the whole line. A single cracked cell doesn't kill the panel; it shaves a few percent off it, and that panel, or the string it sits in, drifts below the others. The gap is small and it grows slowly, which is exactly what makes it hard to notice without records to compare against. Modern half-cut cell designs blunt the impact by splitting the module into two halves wired so a problem on one side doesn't drag the whole panel down, but they don't make the crack itself disappear — they just contain how much it can cost you.

A hotspot is the more dangerous cousin, and it often begins where a microcrack ends. When a cell in a series string can't pass the current the others are pushing through it — because it's shaded, soiled, or cracked into a resistive island — it stops behaving like a generator and starts behaving like a resistor. The current forces its way through anyway, and that resistance converts electrical energy into heat concentrated in one small patch. A healthy cell turns sunlight into power; a reverse-biased one bakes. Left alone, a bad hotspot can discolor the backsheet brown, bubble or delaminate the encapsulant, and in rare cases scorch enough to raise a genuine fire concern.

Panel makers anticipate this with bypass diodes, usually three tucked into the junction box on the back, each guarding a group of about twenty cells. When one group underperforms, its diode opens a detour so current flows around the trouble instead of forcing through it — the module sacrifices roughly a third of its output to protect the rest from cooking. That design detail is also a diagnostic gift. A panel that abruptly drops about a third of its production, cleanly and suddenly, is showing you the fingerprint of one bypassed substring, whether from a failed diode or a hard-shaded third. A panel fading a few percent over months is telling a different story, one closer to the microcrack pattern. Persistent shade from a vent pipe, a lodged leaf, or droppings that never wash off can trigger the same reverse-bias heating, which is why hotspots overlap with the everyday [common panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) of soiling and shading rather than standing apart as some exotic failure. The through-line for both defects is that they announce themselves first as numbers, not as visible damage. One panel or string a few percent under its peers, or a sudden clean step down, is precisely the kind of pattern [your monitoring app can surface](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) — but only if it reports at the string or panel level rather than lumping everything into a single whole-system total.

## The fade that shows up at the end of a string

Potential induced degradation is the least intuitive of the three, because nothing is cracked, shaded, or physically broken. It comes down to voltage. String the panels together and the modules sit at different electrical potentials relative to the grounded metal all around them — the frames, the racking, the mounting rails bolted to your roof. In a typical residential string the total can run several hundred volts, and the modules at the ends sit at the greatest potential difference from ground. That standing voltage is the engine of the whole problem, which is why PID was rare in the small, low-voltage arrays of decades past and became a named concern as residential strings grew longer and higher in voltage.

Given enough voltage, heat, and humidity, a tiny leakage current flows between the cell and the frame, threading through the glass and the plastic encapsulant that are supposed to insulate them. That current drags charged particles — sodium ions liberated from the glass are the usual culprits — into the surface of the silicon, where they accumulate and create microscopic short circuits across the junction that's meant to separate charge and hold a voltage. The cell doesn't stop working, but it leaks. Its ability to keep the voltage it generates erodes, and the loss bites hardest in weak light, at the start and end of the day, when there was little power to spare in the first place. Hot, humid climates accelerate the whole process; a panel in coastal Florida heat runs a different risk profile than the same panel in a dry, mild valley.

Two features make PID distinctive enough to earn its own name. One is that it is systemic and gradual rather than local and sudden. It can affect whole modules at a time — often several of them clustered at the high-voltage end of a string — so it masquerades as accelerated degradation, an array quietly fading faster than the half-percent-a-year baseline with no single panel obviously at fault. The other is that, unusually for a solar defect, it is partly reversible. The same physics that drove the ions in can coax some of them back out. Specialized anti-PID equipment applies a corrective voltage to the string overnight, and over weeks it can recover a meaningful share of lost output; a number of modern inverters build that recovery function in and run it automatically. The caveat is that not every form recovers — the ion-shunting variety responds to treatment, while corrosion that has physically eaten at a cell's contacts is permanent — so early detection still matters more than any after-the-fact fix.

Whether an array is prone to PID at all is mostly decided before anything reaches the roof. Module construction matters — the specific glass, encapsulant, and cell chemistry a manufacturer chose — and so does the electrical design, particularly how the system is grounded and whether the inverter's topology holds the array at a potential that discourages ion migration. Manufacturers now sell modules tested and rated as PID-resistant, and a careful installer specs them alongside grounding and inverter choices that keep the risk low. As an owner your leverage is almost entirely at the buying stage: choosing components and an installer who take PID seriously, then watching production closely enough to catch a slow, string-end fade before it compounds silently for years. If a system that's several years old is fading faster than its degradation curve predicts, and the loss clusters where the string voltage is highest, PID is a suspect worth naming out loud to your installer rather than writing off as bad luck or a run of gray weeks.

## Reading the array before you pay for a diagnosis

None of the three defects announces itself cleanly from the ground, so finding them is a matter of working from cheap evidence toward expensive certainty. The cheapest tool is the one you already own: production data. A panel or string that sits persistently below its peers, or a whole system fading faster than its degradation curve should allow, is the first and often the only flag you need to start asking questions. This is where monitoring granularity pays for itself — a single system-level total can hide one sick panel in the noise, while panel-level or string-level reporting puts the culprit on a map. To judge whether a dip is even real, you need a sense of what a healthy array should be making in your location and season. Running your system size and sun hours through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you the ballpark baseline that lets you separate a genuine fault from an ordinary cloudy stretch, and that baseline is what turns a vague worry into a specific question worth chasing.

When the data says something is genuinely wrong, two imaging methods take over. Thermal imaging is the field standard: a handheld or drone-mounted infrared camera scans the array under load and shows hotspots as bright warm patches against the cooler working cells, and it flags many microcracks by the uneven heating they cause. Electroluminescence imaging is the definitive test for cracks — run current backward through a panel in a dark space and healthy silicon glows like a photographic negative while cracked or electrically dead regions stay black — but it usually means testing at night or pulling the panel to a lab, so it's a second step rather than a first look. You don't commission either one because production dipped two percent in a rainy month. You commission them when the numbers show a persistent, panel-specific, or accelerating loss that ordinary weather and soiling can't account for.

A few practical questions come up again and again, and the honest answers are reassuring more often than not. Not every microcrack is a countdown to failure; many stay electrically closed for the life of the panel and never measurably cut output, which is why a small, stable gap between two panels matters far less than a small gap that keeps widening season over season. Most hotspots can't be seen without an infrared camera, though a severe one leaves brown scorch marks or blistering on the backsheet that are visible from a ladder — and that discoloration is a reason to call your installer, not a reason to climb up and poke at a live roof. PID, for its part, isn't something you fix from the ground at all; your influence over it was spent back when you chose the equipment, and your job now is simply to notice the fade early enough to raise it.

The warranty question is where these defects get expensive, or don't. A manufacturing flaw generally falls under the product warranty that covers the module itself, often for twenty-five years, while a crack from rough handling or a botched install is a workmanship matter that sits with whoever mounted the panels. The catch is proving which is which years after the fact, when the installer may be long gone and the crack could plausibly have come from the factory floor or the rooftop. That ambiguity is the strongest practical argument for documenting your production from the day the system switches on. A clean record of what each string made when new is the evidence that converts a vague "it's underperforming" into a specific, defensible claim — and it's cheap insurance against the day you need it. The homeowner's real job here was never to diagnose anything from the roof. It's to keep an eye on the numbers, know roughly what healthy looks like, and recognize the difference between the gentle uniform fade every panel earns with age and the sharper, lopsided losses that mean something specific has gone wrong.
