A certification is a claim by an independent body that a person has met a defined standard — and the value of the claim depends entirely on how demanding the standard is and who it applies to. NABCEP badges appear on a lot of installer websites, so it's worth knowing exactly what the certification tests, who holds it, and where its usefulness stops. The short version: it's a meaningful signal about an individual's competence, and a weaker signal about the company as a whole, and keeping those two straight is the key to using it well.

## What NABCEP actually is

NABCEP stands for the North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners, an independent nonprofit that certifies individuals working in solar and related fields. It isn't a government license and it isn't a company accreditation — it's a professional credential earned by a person, similar in spirit to a certification an electrician or IT professional might hold.

Its flagship credential is the PV Installation Professional certification, aimed at experienced installers who lead system design and installation. Earning it isn't a matter of watching a video and clicking a button. A candidate has to document a combination of training and real field experience installing systems, then pass a rigorous exam covering system design, code compliance, safety, and installation practice. Certifications also require periodic recertification, so a holder has to keep current rather than earn the credential once and coast. NABCEP offers other, more specialized certifications too, but the PV Installation Professional is the one most homeowners will encounter.

That process is why the credential means something. It filters for people who have both done the work and demonstrated the underlying knowledge, which is not the same as merely having a business license.

## What the badge proves — and what it doesn't

Here's the distinction that trips people up. NABCEP certifies *individuals*, but you hire a *company*. A firm advertising "NABCEP certified" is telling you that at least one person on staff holds the credential. It is not telling you that the certified person will design your system, be on your roof, or supervise the crew that is.

So the badge proves:

- Someone in the organization has met a real, independent competence standard.
- The company cares enough about quality signaling to employ and retain that person.

And it does not prove:

- That your specific installation will be touched by that certified individual.
- That the company's workmanship, warranty, scheduling, or post-install support is good.
- That an installer *without* a badge is unqualified — plenty of excellent installers, especially small local shops, have skilled staff who simply never pursued the credential.

Read the certification as one input in a broader judgment, not a verdict. It raises the floor of your confidence; it doesn't settle the decision.

## Certification is not a license — and both are checkable

A point that causes real confusion: a NABCEP certification is not the same as the license that legally lets a company do the work. Installing solar in the US requires the appropriate state and local licensing — typically a contractor's license and licensed electrical work — and that licensing is mandatory, verifiable through your state's licensing board, and the true baseline. NABCEP is voluntary and sits on top of it. A firm can be fully licensed without any NABCEP-certified staff, and no amount of certification substitutes for the license and insurance you should confirm first.

Both claims are checkable, which is the practical takeaway. State licensing status can be looked up through the relevant state board. NABCEP maintains a public registry of current certificants, so if a company names a specific certified individual, you can confirm the credential is real and current rather than lapsed or invented. Verifying beats assuming: a badge on a website is a marketing asset, while a name you can look up is evidence. When a claim doesn't survive a thirty-second check, that itself is information about the company.

## How much weight to give it

Treat NABCEP as a tiebreaker and a conversation starter rather than a filter you screen on first. If two companies are otherwise comparable, the one with certified staff has demonstrated a commitment to the craft that's worth something. But a badge can't outweigh the fundamentals that actually determine whether your install goes well: licensing and insurance, a solid track record, clear warranties, and references you can check.

The productive move is to turn the badge into a question. Ask who specifically will design your system and who will lead the crew on your roof, and whether those people are certified — not just whether the company employs someone who is. An installer who answers plainly is giving you the real information the badge only hints at. Company structure changes how the badge reads, too. A large national firm almost certainly employs certified staff somewhere in the organization, which tells you little about the subcontracted crew that may actually do your job. A small local installer where the certified owner is also on the roof is a very different — and often more reassuring — situation. None of this bears on system design, which you can and should understand independently of who installs it.
