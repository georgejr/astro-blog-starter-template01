The question sounds simple until your project straddles a year-end. You signed the contract in November, paid a deposit in December, the crew installed in January, and the utility didn't grant permission to operate until February — so which tax year does the federal solar credit belong to? People assume it follows the money, that the year they wrote the big check is the year they claim the credit. Others assume it follows the contract date. Both are reasonable guesses and both are wrong. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, worth 30% of a qualifying solar system's cost, is governed by a single controlling concept: the date the system is *placed in service*. Get that date right and the timing question answers itself. Get it wrong and you risk claiming the credit in the wrong year, which at best means an amended return and at worst a disallowed claim. This is one of those rules where a little precision upfront saves a genuine headache later.

## Placed in service, not paid and not signed

The phrase that governs everything is "placed in service," and it has a specific meaning that doesn't line up with any of the intuitive alternatives. A solar system is placed in service in the tax year it's ready and available for its intended use — which, for a residential rooftop system, generally means installation is complete and the system is capable of operating. It is not the day you signed the contract. It is not the day you paid your deposit or even the day you paid in full. It is not the day the panels were delivered to your driveway. It's the point at which the installed system is finished and functional. That distinction is the entire answer to the straddling-the-year problem: if you paid in December but the system wasn't complete and operational until January, the credit belongs to the January tax year, because that's when it was placed in service — regardless of when the cash left your account.

This matters because solar projects routinely span a year-end, and the gap between paying and finishing can be weeks or months. Permitting, scheduling, the physical install, inspection, and the utility's grant of permission to operate all take time, and any of them can push a project that was paid for in one year into completion in the next. The IRS ties the credit to completion, not payment, precisely so that the timing is anchored to something concrete about the property rather than to the buyer's cash-flow choices. The federal credit's core rules — what it's worth, what qualifies, who's eligible — are laid out in [the solar tax credit, explained](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/), and the placed-in-service concept is the timing layer that sits on top of all of it. Everything else about the credit can be settled, but you can't claim it until the year the system is actually in service, and you claim it on the return for that year.

A subtlety that trips people up: "placed in service" for residential solar is generally read as the completion of installation, and there's a common question about whether you must have the utility's formal permission to operate — the final sign-off that lets you legally export to the grid — before the system counts as placed in service. The conservative and widely-followed reading is that the system should be installed and capable of operating for the credit to apply to that year, and many tax professionals treat substantial completion of the installation as the trigger rather than requiring the utility's paperwork to be in hand. But interconnection approval sometimes lags installation by weeks, and if that approval slips across a year-end while the physical install finished before it, you have a genuine judgment call about which year applies. This is exactly the kind of fact pattern where the right move is to consult a tax professional and to keep documentation — completion dates, inspection records, the installer's certification of substantial completion — rather than to guess. The general principle is stable and safe to state; the edge cases around interconnection timing are precisely where individualized advice earns its cost.

## The paid-in-one-year, finished-in-the-next scenario

Walk through the most common straddle, because it clarifies the whole rule. Suppose you sign a solar contract in October, pay a substantial deposit in November, and pay the balance in December when the panels go up — but the final electrical inspection and the system's completion slip into early January. You spent the money almost entirely in the earlier year. Instinct says claim the credit on that year's return, since that's when you paid. The rule says otherwise: the system was placed in service in January, so the credit belongs to the new year's return, filed the following spring. You'll claim 30% of the qualifying cost — including the money you paid in the prior calendar year — on the return for the year the system was completed. The cash timing doesn't split the credit; the whole credit lands in the placed-in-service year, drawing on the total qualifying cost regardless of when each installment was paid.

Now flip it. Suppose you sign and pay a deposit in December but the system was actually completed and operational in that same December — install finished, inspection done, system capable of running — even though you don't pay the final balance until January. Here the system was placed in service in December, so the credit belongs to that year's return, and it's based on the full qualifying cost even though part of the payment happened the following month. The lesson in both directions is the same: the credit follows the completion of the system, and the qualifying cost it's calculated on is the total project cost, not the portion you happened to pay in a given calendar year. The dollar amount is straightforward once you fix the year — 30% of what qualifies — and the [federal tax credit calculator](/federal-tax-credit-calculator/) is the quick way to see the figure for your specific system cost, so you know what number you're anchoring to whichever year turns out to be correct.

This has a real planning implication if you're installing near a year-end and your tax situations differ between the two years. Because the credit is a nonrefundable credit against your federal tax liability — it can reduce what you owe to zero but the mechanics of using it depend on having liability to offset — the year the credit lands can matter if your income or tax picture is very different across the boundary. You generally can't choose the year by manipulating when you pay, since it's the placed-in-service date that controls, but you can sometimes influence the completion timing itself by scheduling the install, and if there's a meaningful reason to land the credit in one year versus the other, that's a conversation to have with your installer and your tax advisor before the crew shows up rather than after. What you can't do is pay in December and simply elect to claim it that year if the system won't be finished until spring — the date is a fact about the property, not a choice on the form. The interaction between the credit and your actual tax liability, and what happens when the credit is larger than the tax you owe, is its own important topic covered next.

## What happens when the credit exceeds what you owe

Fixing the correct year raises the immediate follow-on question: what if, in that year, the 30% credit is larger than your total federal tax liability? This is common, because the credit on a typical residential system can run into five figures, which may exceed what a household owes in a single year. The Residential Clean Energy Credit is nonrefundable, meaning it can reduce your tax bill to zero but the IRS won't cut you a check for the excess in that year. The excess isn't lost, though — it carries forward to future tax years, where you can apply it against subsequent liability until it's used up. So a homeowner whose credit exceeds one year's tax doesn't forfeit the difference; they claim what they can this year and roll the rest forward. The mechanics of that carryforward — how you track the remaining amount and apply it in later years — are laid out in [solar tax credit carryover](/blog/solar-tax-credit-carryover/), and it's the reason the credit is usable even for people whose annual tax bill is smaller than the credit itself.

The practical consequence is that the placed-in-service year sets the *start* of the credit's life, not necessarily the year you extract all its value. You claim the full credit on the return for the placed-in-service year, and if your liability that year can't absorb the whole thing, the unused portion begins its carryforward journey from there. This is why getting the year right still matters even when carryforward exists: the credit has to be claimed on the correct year's return to begin with, and the carryforward flows from that starting point. It also means the timing question and the liability question are linked — the year you place the system in service determines both which return the credit goes on and the first year of tax liability it gets measured against. A homeowner who expects low liability in the placed-in-service year but higher liability soon after can take comfort that the carryforward will let them use the credit over time rather than losing it.

All of this lives on IRS Form 5695, the form where the Residential Clean Energy Credit is calculated and claimed, and where the carryforward from a prior year, if any, gets entered and applied. Walking through that form correctly is the mechanical end of the process, and the step-by-step is covered in [filing IRS Form 5695](/blog/irs-form-5695-solar/) — but the conceptual work is done once you've correctly identified the placed-in-service year and understood that the credit starts there and carries forward as needed. Two disciplines keep the whole thing clean. First, keep documentation of the completion date — the installer's certification, inspection records, the date the system became capable of operating — because that date is what substantiates the year you claimed. Second, because the specifics of eligibility, the current status of the 30% rate, and the treatment of edge cases can change, treat this article as the structure and confirm the current details against IRS guidance or a tax professional for your own return. The rules described here — placed-in-service timing, the 30% figure, nonrefundable-with-carryforward treatment — are the durable shape of the credit, and that shape is what answers the question of when you can claim it: the year your system is finished and ready to run, on the return you file for that year, with any excess rolling forward from there.
