One kilowatt of solar panels does not produce one kilowatt of energy over a year. It produces somewhere between roughly 1,200 and 1,600 kilowatt-hours across most of the populated United States — and that single ratio, kWh produced per kW installed, is the most useful number in solar sizing. Installers call it specific yield, and it's the quiet conversion factor that turns "how big a system" into "how much energy," or the reverse. Pin it down for your location and you can size an array, sanity-check a quote, or compare two regions on the back of an envelope.

The reason it's so useful is that it strips out system size entirely. A 5 kW array and a 12 kW array in the same town share the same specific yield, because the ratio normalizes for capacity. What's left is a clean measure of how productive each installed kilowatt is where you live — which is really a measure of your sun.

## Why the number lands where it does

Specific yield starts from peak sun-hours: the number of hours per day, averaged across the year, that sunlight hits the intensity used to rate panels. Multiply your daily peak sun-hours by 365 and you'd get a theoretical yield, but real systems never hit it, because energy is lost between the panels and the meter. Inverter conversion, wiring resistance, heat, soiling, and minor shading typically shave 15 to 25 percent off the ideal. That combined haircut is the system's derate.

So the working formula is straightforward: peak sun-hours per year, times the derate, gives specific yield. A location with 4.5 average daily sun-hours reaches about 1,640 hours a year; apply a ~20 percent loss and you land near 1,300 kWh per installed kW. A sunnier spot at 5.5 daily sun-hours pushes past 1,500. The two levers are your latitude-and-climate sun resource and how efficiently the specific system converts it. This is also where the difference between capacity and energy becomes concrete — the distinction that trips up so many shoppers, untangled in [kW versus kWh in solar](/blog/kw-vs-kwh-solar/).

## What a realistic figure looks like by region

The ranges below are rough, order-of-magnitude figures for a well-oriented, unshaded system in each area. Local climate, tilt, orientation, and shading move any individual roof up or down, so treat these as a starting sense of scale rather than a guarantee for your address.

| Region | Rough kWh per kW per year | What drives it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Desert Southwest (AZ, NV, NM) | 1,550–1,850 | Abundant clear-sky sun-hours |
| California, Mountain West | 1,450–1,650 | Strong sun; high-altitude clarity helps |
| Texas, Southern Plains | 1,350–1,550 | Good sun, some summer haze and heat losses |
| Southeast (FL, GA, NC) | 1,250–1,450 | Plenty of sun but frequent cloud and humidity |
| Mid-Atlantic, Midwest | 1,150–1,350 | Moderate sun, real winters |
| Northeast (NY, MA, New England) | 1,100–1,300 | Shorter winter days, more cloud |
| Pacific Northwest (coastal) | 1,000–1,200 | Persistent overcast trims the total |

Most homes sit in that broad 1,200-to-1,600 band, which is why it's a serviceable national rule of thumb. The extremes matter too: a shaded northern roof can dip below 1,100, while a clear high-desert array can top 1,700. ## Turning the ratio into a system size

The practical power of specific yield is that it works in both directions, which is exactly what sizing requires.

Going from usage to system size: divide your annual kilowatt-hours by the local specific yield. A home using 12,000 kWh a year in a region that yields 1,400 kWh per kW needs about 8.6 kW to cover its consumption on paper (12,000 ÷ 1,400). Going the other way, from a quoted system size to expected production: multiply. That same 8.6 kW system in that region should make roughly 12,000 kWh a year — and if a salesperson's estimate claims 15,000, the specific yield tells you the number is inflated for your climate.

That second use is where the ratio protects you. A production estimate is only as honest as the specific yield baked into it, so a quote promising far more kWh per installed kW than your region typically delivers deserves a hard question. ## The assumptions that move your real number

A regional average is a fine starting point, but four factors push a specific roof off it, and knowing them keeps expectations honest:

- Orientation and tilt. A south-facing array at a good pitch hits the regional figure; east, west, or north-facing planes give up a meaningful slice, and a poor tilt costs a little more.
- Shading. Even part-time shade from a tree or chimney pulls the effective yield down, sometimes well below the clear-roof number.
- Temperature. Hot climates lose a few percent to heat on scorching afternoons, which is why a blazing-hot location doesn't always out-yield a cooler, clear one by as much as its sun-hours suggest.
- Snow and soiling. Regions with heavy snow or dust lose a small annual slice while the glass is covered.

Because these stack, two houses on the same street can post different specific yields. That's why the ratio is best used as a check and a first estimate, then refined with your roof's actual orientation and shading.
