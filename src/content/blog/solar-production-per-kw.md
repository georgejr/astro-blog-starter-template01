One kilowatt of solar panels does not produce one kilowatt-hour of energy over a year. It produces somewhere between roughly 1,200 and 1,600 kilowatt-hours across most of the populated United States — and that single ratio, kilowatt-hours produced per kilowatt installed, is quietly the most useful number in all of solar sizing. Installers call it specific yield, and it's the conversion factor that turns "how big a system do I need" into "how much energy will it make," or runs the calculation in reverse. Pin it down for your location and you can size an array, sanity-check a salesperson's production estimate, or compare two regions on the back of an envelope, all without any software.

The reason specific yield is so useful is that it strips system size out of the picture entirely. A 5 kW array and a 12 kW array in the same town share the same specific yield, because the ratio normalizes for capacity — divide production by installed kilowatts and the size cancels out. What's left is a clean measure of how productive each installed kilowatt is where you happen to live, which is really just a measure of your sun. That's why it travels so well between quotes and between regions: it isolates the one variable that actually differs from place to place, your solar resource, from the one that differs from house to house, how many panels you bought.

## Where the number comes from

Specific yield starts from peak sun-hours, which is a slightly misleading name for a genuinely useful concept. It's the number of hours per day, averaged across the whole year, that sunlight hits your location at the intensity panels are rated against. A place with 4.5 peak sun-hours isn't sunny for only four and a half hours; it collects, over a full day of varying-intensity light, the equivalent of four and a half hours at full rated intensity. Multiply your daily peak sun-hours by 365 and you'd get a theoretical annual yield — but no real system ever reaches that theoretical figure, because energy leaks away at every step between the panels and your meter. Inverter conversion loses a bit, wiring resistance loses a bit, heat costs a few percent on hot afternoons, and soiling and minor shading nibble at the rest. Added together, those losses typically shave 15 to 25 percent off the ideal, and that combined haircut is what installers call the system's derate.

So the working formula is refreshingly simple: peak sun-hours per year, multiplied by the derate factor, gives specific yield. A location averaging 4.5 daily peak sun-hours reaches about 1,640 sun-hours a year; apply a roughly 20 percent loss and you land near 1,300 kWh per installed kilowatt. A sunnier spot at 5.5 daily sun-hours pushes past 1,500. The two levers that set your number are therefore your sun resource, which is a function of latitude and climate, and how efficiently your specific system converts that resource, which is a function of equipment quality, orientation, temperature, and cleanliness. Everything that moves specific yield moves one of those two things.

It helps to walk one calculation all the way through with its assumptions stated, because the formula is easier to trust once you've watched it produce a familiar number. Assume a location averaging 4.2 peak sun-hours a day and a system that loses about 18 percent between the panels and the meter — a middle-of-the-road derate for a decent modern install. Annual sun-hours come to roughly 1,530, which is 4.2 times 365, and applying the 18 percent loss leaves about 1,255 kWh per installed kilowatt over the year. That lands squarely in the national band and would be an unremarkable figure for much of the middle of the country. Nudge the sun-hours up to 5.5 for a sunnier climate and hold the same derate, and you're near 1,650. Push the losses up toward 25 percent for a hot, slightly shaded roof and you slide back to around 1,150. None of these need special software — they're a multiplication and a subtraction — and the exercise shows you exactly which lever is moving your number and by how much. The derate isn't mysterious either once you break it apart: the inverter gives up a few percent turning DC into AC, wiring resistance costs a little more, heat costs a few percent on the hottest afternoons, and soiling and minor shading take the rest. Add those small, individually forgivable losses together and you get the 15-to-25 percent haircut that separates the theoretical figure from what the meter actually records at the end of the year.

This is also the point where the difference between capacity and energy stops being abstract and becomes concrete, because specific yield is literally the bridge between them. A kilowatt is a rate — how fast the system can produce at a given instant, its size. A kilowatt-hour is an amount — how much energy actually accumulated over time. Specific yield is the exchange rate between the two over a year, and confusing the two is the single most common mistake solar shoppers make. Someone hears "I have a 10 kW system" and pictures a fixed quantity of energy, when what they actually have is a fixed capacity that produces wildly different amounts of energy in July versus December, in Arizona versus Seattle. The distinction that trips up so many buyers is untangled in [kW versus kWh in solar](/blog/kw-vs-kwh-solar/), and specific yield is exactly the tool that resolves the confusion: it's the number that tells you how many kilowatt-hours a kilowatt of capacity will hand you over the course of a year in your particular spot on the map. Once you internalize that a kilowatt of panels is worth about 1,200 to 1,600 kilowatt-hours a year depending on where it sits, the whole vocabulary of solar starts to make sense.

## What specific yield looks like across the country

The ranges in the table below are rough, order-of-magnitude figures for a well-oriented, unshaded system in each area. Local climate, tilt, orientation, and shading push any individual roof up or down from these, so treat them as a starting sense of scale rather than a guarantee for your address. A south-facing array at a good pitch in clear high desert will sit near the top of its band or above it; a compromised roof in the same region can fall well below.

| Region | Rough kWh per kW per year | What drives it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Desert Southwest (AZ, NV, NM) | 1,550–1,850 | Abundant clear-sky sun-hours |
| California, Mountain West | 1,450–1,650 | Strong sun, high-altitude clarity helps |
| Texas, Southern Plains | 1,350–1,550 | Good sun, some summer haze and heat losses |
| Southeast (FL, GA, NC) | 1,250–1,450 | Plenty of sun but frequent cloud and humidity |
| Mid-Atlantic, Midwest | 1,150–1,350 | Moderate sun, real winters |
| Northeast (NY, MA, New England) | 1,100–1,300 | Shorter winter days, more cloud |
| Pacific Northwest (coastal) | 1,000–1,200 | Persistent overcast trims the total |

Most American homes sit somewhere in that broad 1,200-to-1,600 band, which is exactly why it works as a serviceable national rule of thumb — reach for 1,400 when you know nothing else about a location and you'll rarely be far wrong. The extremes matter too, and they explain why blanket claims about solar payback fall apart. A heavily shaded northern roof can dip below 1,100 kWh per kilowatt, meaning every panel there earns noticeably less than the same panel would in Phoenix, while a clear high-desert array can top 1,700 and make each panel a workhorse. That's a spread of more than 50 percent in what an identical kilowatt of hardware produces, driven entirely by geography.

Finding your own location's figure doesn't require guessing which row of the table you fall in. Public solar-resource maps and modeling tools published by national labs will give peak sun-hours or annual production estimates for a specific address, and a good installer's proposal should state the specific yield it assumes so you can check that assumption against the regional band. The seasonal spread hiding behind the annual average is worth picturing too, because the yearly number conceals a lot of variation: a northern location might see six or more peak sun-hours on a long clear June day and barely two on a short overcast December one, and the annual figure is simply the average of that wide swing. That's exactly why an array is sized around the yearly total rather than any single month — the strong months carry the weak ones, and judging a system by December alone would badly mislead you. Two homes in the same metro area can also land on different rows of the practical table despite sharing a climate, because one has a clean south-facing roof at a good pitch while the other is stuck with a shaded east-west split. The regional band tells you what your climate makes possible; your specific roof decides where within that band, or below it, you actually end up. Treat the table as the ceiling your geography allows and your roof's orientation and shading as how much of that ceiling you get to keep.

Because production per kilowatt varies this much, the economics of solar vary right along with it, and specific yield is the hidden variable behind why the same system pays off quickly in one state and barely breaks even in another. A kilowatt that makes 1,700 kWh a year in the Southwest offsets far more of an electric bill than a kilowatt making 1,100 kWh in a cloudy northern market — and that's before you layer on the wide differences in electricity prices and export compensation that also swing the math. The full state-by-state picture, and how specific yield combines with local rates and incentives to shape the return, is laid out in [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/). The yardstick here — kilowatt-hours per kilowatt — is the production half of that story, the piece that tells you how much energy your climate will actually let the hardware make before any dollars enter the conversation.

## Using the ratio both ways

The practical power of specific yield is that it runs in both directions, which is precisely what real sizing decisions require. Going from usage to system size, you divide your annual kilowatt-hours by the local specific yield. A home using 12,000 kWh a year in a region that yields 1,400 kWh per kilowatt needs about 8.6 kW to cover its consumption on paper — 12,000 divided by 1,400. Going the other way, from a quoted system size to expected production, you multiply. That same 8.6 kW system in that region should make roughly 12,000 kWh a year, closing the loop. Being able to run the arithmetic both ways means you can start from either end — from the bill you want to erase or from the system a salesperson is pitching — and check that the two agree.

That second direction is where the ratio quietly protects your wallet. A production estimate is only ever as honest as the specific yield baked into it, so a quote promising far more kilowatt-hours per installed kilowatt than your region typically delivers deserves a hard, direct question. If a salesperson's estimate for that 8.6 kW system in a 1,400-yield region claims 15,000 kWh a year, the specific yield tells you the number is inflated for your climate by a wide margin, and the savings projection built on top of it is inflated too. Inflated production estimates are one of the most common ways an optimistic quote oversells the payback, and knowing your regional yield is the cheapest defense against them. That gives you a thirty-second test to run on any proposal that lands on your kitchen table. Take the system size in kilowatts, multiply by the specific yield for your area, and see whether the result is close to the annual production the salesperson promised. If a 7 kW system in a 1,300-yield region is quoted at 9,100 kWh a year, the numbers agree and you can trust the rest of the estimate a little more. If that same system is quoted at 12,000 kWh, someone has assumed a specific yield your climate simply doesn't deliver, and every savings figure built on that production is inflated by the same margin. You don't need to pin down the exact right number to know a claimed one is impossible for your latitude — the ratio alone catches the overpromise. Inflated production estimates are among the most common ways an optimistic quote manufactures an attractive payback, and this single multiplication is the cheapest defense a shopper has against them.

A regional average is a fine starting point, but four factors push a specific roof off it, and knowing them keeps your expectations honest. Orientation and tilt come first: a south-facing array at a good pitch hits the regional figure, while east, west, or especially north-facing planes give up a meaningful slice, and a badly wrong tilt costs a little more on top. Shading comes second, and even part-time shade from a single tree or chimney can pull effective yield well below the clear-roof number, because of how it drags on whole strings. Temperature comes third: hot climates lose a few percent to heat on scorching afternoons, which is why a blazing-hot location doesn't always out-yield a cooler, clear one by as much as its raw sun-hours suggest. Snow and soiling round it out, costing a small annual slice in regions where the glass spends time covered. Because these effects stack, two houses on the same street can post noticeably different specific yields from identical panels. That's exactly why the ratio is best used as a first estimate and a sanity check, then refined with your roof's real orientation and shading rather than trusted as gospel. The same logic scales all the way down to a single module — what one panel makes on a given day is just specific yield expressed per panel and per day, worked through concretely in [what one solar panel really produces in a day](/blog/solar-panel-output-per-day/). Sizing a whole array or sanity-checking a single panel, it's the same yardstick doing the work either way.
