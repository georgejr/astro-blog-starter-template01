Solar and a heat pump are each worthwhile on their own. Put together, they do something neither does alone: they turn your roof into a heating and cooling source, not just a way to shave the lighting-and-appliances part of the bill. The reason the combination punches above the sum of its parts comes down to a single number that describes how a heat pump uses electricity — and once you see that number, the case for pairing the two becomes hard to unsee.

## Why a heat pump changes the value of every solar kWh

A resistance heater — the coils in an electric furnace or baseboard — converts one unit of electricity into one unit of heat. A heat pump doesn't make heat; it moves it, pulling warmth from the outside air (even cold air) into the house. That's why it can deliver roughly three units of heat for every unit of electricity it draws, a ratio expressed as a coefficient of performance, or **COP, of about 3** in mild conditions. Efficiency drops as it gets colder, and modern cold-climate units hold up far better than older ones, but the headline stays: a heat pump is a multiplier on electricity, not a one-to-one converter.

Now layer solar underneath it. A solar kilowatt-hour that would have offset a $0.17 grid kWh is already worth $0.17. Feed that same kWh into a heat pump running at a COP of 3, and it delivers heat that a resistance heater would have needed three kWh to match. You haven't changed what the solar produces — you've changed what each kilowatt-hour *accomplishes*. The panels don't get more valuable; the load they're feeding got three times more efficient.

### The comparison that matters: heat pump vs. gas furnace under solar

The honest baseline for most homeowners weighing this isn't resistance heat — it's a gas furnace. Here the combo's advantage is real but conditional, and it's worth being straight about.

A gas furnace burns fuel your solar panels can't touch. No matter how large your array, it does nothing for a therm of natural gas. Heat with gas and your winter heating bill sits entirely outside the reach of your roof. Swap to a heat pump and that heating load moves onto electricity — where your panels *can* reach it. The combo's savings come from relocating your single largest seasonal load onto the one energy source you're generating for free at the margin.

Whether that beats gas on pure operating cost depends on the local price of gas versus electricity and how cold your winters run. Where electricity is expensive and gas is cheap, a heat pump on grid power can cost more to run than the furnace it replaced — but solar changes that calculation by lowering the effective price of the electricity feeding it. The pairing is strongest precisely where each piece is weakest alone: solar covers the heat pump's appetite for power, and the heat pump gives the solar a large, valuable load to offset.

## The timing mismatch you have to plan around

There's a catch the enthusiasts skip: solar and heating peak at opposite times. Your panels produce most in summer and least in the short, dim days of December — exactly when a heating load spikes. The [winter usage spike from electric heat](/blog/electric-heating-usage/) lands when your roof is least able to cover it, so a naive "the solar powers the heat pump" story oversells the direct match.

What actually happens on an annual basis is that summer overproduction offsets winter shortfall through your billing arrangement. Under net metering, the surplus kilowatt-hours you bank in July help pay for the grid power your heat pump draws in January. Under a net-billing or inflow-outflow structure where exports pay less than retail, that summer-to-winter transfer is less generous, and more of your winter heat-pump electricity comes at full retail price. This is why the compensation rules in your area shape the combo's economics as much as the hardware does.

The practical implication is about sizing. Adding a heat pump can substantially raise your annual electricity usage, so a solar array sized for your pre-heat-pump bills will fall short once the heat pump is running. [Resize your array before adding a heat pump](/blog/solar-sizing-for-heat-pump/) rather than after — retrofitting extra panels later means paying fixed permit and mobilization costs a second time.

## Putting rough numbers on it

Assume, purely to illustrate the mechanics, a home that heats with a heat pump adding 4,000 kWh a year of heating and cooling load, in a region where solar yields about 1,400 kWh per installed kW annually. Covering that added load takes roughly 3 kW of extra panels (4,000 ÷ 1,400 ≈ 2.9). At an assumed retail rate of $0.17/kWh, those 4,000 kWh would cost about $680 a year from the grid — and that's the load your incremental panels are canceling.

Against a resistance heater doing the same job, the heat pump itself already cut the electricity needed by roughly two-thirds before the panels entered the picture. Stack the two savings — efficient conversion, then free-at-the-margin generation — and you can see why the combo gets described as a multiplier. The exact figure for your home turns on your climate, your rates, and your export terms, which is what the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) is for; set the added heating load and the retail rate, and it returns a payback that reflects the combined system rather than solar alone.

## Who the pairing actually rewards

The combination shines brightest for a specific profile: a home currently heating with expensive electric resistance or pricey delivered fuel, in a climate where a modern heat pump holds decent efficiency, with net metering or a reasonable export credit and enough roof to size up. There, you're replacing an expensive heating source with an efficient one and feeding it from a roof you'd have wanted anyway.

The case softens where gas is very cheap, where winters are brutal enough to lean hard on backup resistance heat, or where exports pay so little that winter heat-pump power all comes at full retail. Even then the pairing usually still saves money — it just saves less, and the payback lengthens. The way to know your own answer is to size the whole electric load first, then run it against your real rates and export rules. What solar saves in general is covered in [how much solar actually saves](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/); the heat pump's contribution is to make each of those saved kilowatt-hours do the work of three.
