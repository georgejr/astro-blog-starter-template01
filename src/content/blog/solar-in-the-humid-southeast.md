The humid Southeast — Georgia, the Carolinas, Alabama, Mississippi, Tennessee, and the humid stretches of neighboring states — is a solar market defined less by how much sun it gets than by what that sun has to do. It gets a lot of sun, but hazier and stormier than the desert Southwest, and it gets a cooling load that runs eight or nine months a year and dominates the electric bill. The interesting thing about pairing solar with a Southeastern home is how well those two facts fit together: the season when the sun is strongest is the same season the air conditioner is running hardest, so solar production and peak demand line up in a way they don't in a heating-dominated northern climate. That alignment is the quiet strength of Southeastern solar, and it shapes how you should size and think about a system.

The complications are equally specific to the region. Afternoon thunderstorms clip the brightest hours of summer days, humidity and haze soften the sunlight compared to a clear desert sky, and the export rules that decide how much your surplus is worth vary sharply from one Southeastern utility to the next — often in a stingy direction. Understanding the region means holding the favorable alignment and the local frictions in the same frame.

## Cooling that runs most of the year, and sun that mostly keeps up

The defining load in a Southeastern home is air conditioning, and it's worth appreciating just how large and how long-running that load is before sizing anything. In much of the region the cooling season stretches from spring into late fall — realistically eight to nine months when the AC runs at least some days, and a solid four or five months when it runs hard every day. Summer humidity makes it worse in a way a dry-heat climate doesn't: an air conditioner in the humid Southeast isn't just lowering the temperature, it's wringing moisture out of the air, and that latent cooling load means the system runs longer and works harder to hit the same thermostat setting than the same unit would in Arizona. The upshot is that a Southeastern home's electricity usage is dominated by summer cooling, with a big midyear hump on the bill and comparatively modest shoulder and winter months, especially in homes that heat with gas. Exactly how much a summer of cooling adds to usage — and it's often the largest single line on the annual bill — is the subject of [air conditioning and your kWh](/blog/air-conditioner-electricity-usage/), and getting that number right for your own home is the foundation of sizing solar here.

The reason this matters for solar is timing. In a northern, heating-dominated home, the biggest energy demand comes in the dead of winter, precisely when solar produces the least, so the array and the load are out of phase and the system leans hard on banked summer credits to cover the winter gap. In the cooling-dominated Southeast, the opposite is true: the biggest demand comes in summer, when the days are longest and the sun is highest, so solar production peaks at roughly the same time the air conditioning load peaks. A Southeastern array is making its most power on the hot, bright afternoons when the AC is running flat out, which means a large share of that production is consumed on-site in real time rather than exported. That's the ideal case for solar economics, because power you use as you make it is worth your full retail rate, while power you export is worth only whatever your utility pays for it — and in this region, that export rate is often low. The alignment of cooling demand and solar supply is the single biggest reason solar works well in the Southeast despite the region's modest electricity rates and weak export rules.

The alignment isn't perfect, and the imperfections are worth planning around rather than being surprised by. The first is the afternoon thunderstorm, a near-daily feature of Southeastern summers. On a typical July afternoon, clouds build through the day and a storm rolls through in the late afternoon, clipping production during hours that would otherwise be strong. The array still produces well in the morning and midday, but the late-afternoon peak — which in a hot climate is also when cooling demand is highest as the day's heat accumulates — can get cut short by clouds. The second is haze and humidity, which scatter and soften sunlight so that even a "clear" Southeastern summer day delivers somewhat less intense sun than a clear day at the same latitude in a drier climate. Neither of these is a dealbreaker; they're the reason a Southeastern array's production estimate should use a regionally appropriate figure rather than an optimistic clear-sky number. As a planning range, well-oriented Southeastern arrays commonly produce somewhere around 1,300 to 1,500 kWh a year per kW of panels — very respectable, a notch below the desert Southwest, and comfortably ahead of the cloudy North. Run your own roof through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) with a figure in that range rather than a national default, and the storm-and-haze reality is baked into your estimate from the start.

The cooling load also creates an opportunity that's specific to hot climates: the biggest load in the house runs during the sunniest hours, so modest efficiency and timing moves compound with solar unusually well. Pre-cooling the house in the early afternoon when the panels are producing hard, then letting the thermostat drift up slightly during the late-afternoon storm-and-peak window, shifts cooling energy into the solar-rich part of the day and reduces the evening draw the array can't cover. A well-sealed, well-insulated house holds that pre-cooled comfort longer, stretching the benefit. None of this is unique to the Southeast, but it pays off more here because the cooling load is so dominant and so well-aligned with production — every bit of cooling you can move into daylight is cooling powered by your own roof rather than bought from the grid on an evening when the panels are winding down.

## Rates, exports, and storms: where the Southeast gets its own math

If the sun-and-cooling alignment is the region's strength, its electricity rates and export policies are where the economics get more complicated, and they're the numbers that most separate a good Southeastern solar deal from a mediocre one. Residential electricity rates across much of the Southeast have historically run near or somewhat below the national average — a fact rooted in the region's mix of generation and its regulated utility structure. Cheaper grid power is pleasant to pay and frustrating to go solar against, because solar savings are denominated in the rate you avoid: every kilowatt-hour your array produces and you consume is worth exactly what you'd otherwise have paid for it, so a lower rate means each solar kilowatt-hour saves you a little less than the same kilowatt-hour would in a high-rate market like the Northeast or California. This is the main reason Southeastern paybacks tend to be moderate rather than spectacular despite the good sun — the region is strong on production and weaker on rate. To see where you actually stand, calculate your effective rate honestly from a full year of bills, delivery charges included, using the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/), because the advertised supply rate usually understates what you really pay and therefore understates your solar savings.

The export tariff is the other decisive number, and it's the one that varies most within the region. The Southeast is served by a patchwork of large investor-owned utilities, municipal utilities, and rural electric cooperatives, and their policies for compensating rooftop-solar exports range from reasonable to quite stingy — with a general regional trend toward crediting exported power well below the retail rate rather than at full net metering. This is where the sun-and-cooling alignment pays off twice: because so much of a Southeastern array's summer production is consumed on-site running the air conditioner, the home is less exposed to a weak export rate than a home whose production and usage are out of phase. A system sized to cover the daytime cooling load, exporting relatively little, is insulated from a poor export tariff, while an oversized system betting on generous export credits is exposed to every unfavorable policy decision. The practical guidance follows directly: in a weak-export utility, size for self-consumption of your cooling load rather than for maximum production, and get your utility's current export terms in writing before you sign, including whether existing customers are grandfathered if the rules change. How different states in the region handle this varies enough to matter — [Georgia's great sun and stingy export rules](/blog/solar-panels-in-georgia/) and [North Carolina's quieter but stronger solar program](/blog/solar-panels-in-north-carolina/) sit at noticeably different points on that spectrum, and your own utility's policy is what actually governs your result.

Storm season adds the region's last distinctive layer, and it's more about resilience and roof condition than about production. The Southeast sees severe thunderstorms, tropical systems that push inland from the Gulf and Atlantic, and the occasional ice storm at the northern edge of the region, and all of that argues for two things. First, roof condition matters more here because roofs take a beating and get replaced more often; installing an array on a roof with only a few years of life left means paying to remove and reinstall it when the roof is redone, so starting from a roof with a decade or more left is standard advice. Second, the frequency of storm-driven outages makes battery backup a more compelling proposition than the bill-savings math alone would suggest, because the value of a battery in this region includes riding out the outages that follow severe weather — a value that doesn't show up on an electric bill but is real to anyone who's lost power for days after a storm. Whether that backup value justifies a battery is a personal calculation, but it's a more live question in the storm-prone Southeast than in a placid-grid region, and it belongs in the decision alongside the rate and export numbers.

The Southeastern solar picture, condensed: the sun is good if not desert-elite, and crucially it peaks when your air conditioning does, so a well-sized array covers a large, well-aligned cooling load in real time. The rates are moderate and the export rules are often weak, which caps how fast the system pays back and argues for sizing to your daytime cooling consumption rather than for maximum export. And storm season makes roof condition and backup power live considerations rather than afterthoughts. Get your cooling load and your effective rate right, confirm your export tariff in writing, size for self-consumption of that summer peak, and Southeastern solar rewards you with a system whose production shows up exactly when your house needs it most.
