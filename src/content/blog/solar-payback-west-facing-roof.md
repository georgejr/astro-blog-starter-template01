The advice that solar "needs a south-facing roof" has scared off more good candidates than almost any other myth in the business. It's true that in the northern hemisphere a south-facing array captures the most annual sunlight, and if you're lucky enough to have one, it's the orientation to use. But the majority of American homes don't offer a clean south face, and plenty of them go solar anyway on east- and west-facing roofs that produce nearly as much and pay back only a little slower. The question that actually matters isn't whether your roof faces due south — it's how much production you give up by facing east or west instead, and how much that lost production stretches your break-even.

Both answers turn out to be smaller than the myth implies. A west- or east-facing array typically produces somewhere in the range of 10 to 20 percent less than the same array facing south, and because payback scales roughly with production, that translates to a payback period perhaps a year or two longer — not a decade, not a deal-breaker. Whether that trade is worth making depends less on the compass than on the two variables that dominate every solar payback: what you pay for the system and what you pay for grid electricity.

## How much orientation actually costs, and why it's less than you'd fear

Solar panels generate the most when sunlight strikes them most directly, and in the northern hemisphere the sun spends the day arcing across the southern sky. A south-facing array at a tilt near your latitude therefore gets the best average angle on the sun across the whole year and produces the most annual energy. That's the benchmark everything else is measured against. But "less than the maximum" is not the same as "not worth it," and the size of the shortfall is where the myth falls apart. A due-west or due-east roof captures strong sun for half the day — the afternoon for west, the morning for east — and decent oblique sun for much of the rest, so it typically lands only 10 to 20 percent below the south-facing ideal on annual production. The exact figure depends on latitude, tilt, and local weather, but for most US homes an east or west roof gives up something in that band, not the catastrophic loss homeowners imagine. Our side-by-side look at [south-facing versus east-west arrays](/blog/south-facing-vs-east-west-solar-panels/) works through the geometry in more detail, but the headline is that the penalty is a haircut, not a beheading.

Put that penalty into payback terms with a labeled example. Assume a 7 kW system that, facing south in your area, would produce about 10,500 kWh a year and, at an effective electricity rate of $0.18/kWh, save roughly $1,890 annually. Suppose it costs $18,200 installed, or about $12,740 net after the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit for a homeowner who can use it. Facing south, that's a payback of about 6.7 years. Now face the same system west and knock 15 percent off production: output falls to about 8,900 kWh, annual savings to roughly $1,600, and payback stretches to about 8.0 years. A 15 percent production cut moved the break-even by a little over a year. That's the whole story of orientation and payback in one comparison — real, measurable, but modest, and nowhere near enough to disqualify a west roof on its own. If the payback concept itself is new to you, [solar payback period explained](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/) covers the basic net-cost-over-annual-savings math this example is built on.

What makes the orientation penalty shrink or grow is the same thing that governs payback generally: your electricity rate and, increasingly, when you're billed for it. In a high-rate market the extra kilowatt-hours a south roof would have produced are worth more, so the dollar gap between south and west widens — but the absolute payback on both is short, so both are attractive. In a low-rate market the dollar gap narrows, but payback on everything is longer. Either way, the rate you pay for grid power swings your payback far harder than the compass direction of your roof does, which is exactly the point our full breakdown of [what moves the payback number](/blog/solar-payback-factors/) keeps returning to: price paid and rate avoided are the heavyweights, and orientation is a second-tier adjustment riding on top of them. A homeowner with a west roof in an expensive-electricity state will beat a homeowner with a south roof in a cheap-electricity state on payback every time. The roof direction is real, but it's not the variable that decides the outcome.

There's a twist that can actually make a west roof more valuable than its raw production suggests, and it depends on how your utility bills you. Under old-fashioned flat-rate net metering, every kilowatt-hour is worth the same regardless of when it's produced, so a south roof's extra annual energy is simply extra credit and orientation is a pure production question. But under time-of-use rates — increasingly common, and the direction utilities are moving — electricity costs the most during the late-afternoon and early-evening peak, precisely when a west-facing array is still cranking and a south-facing one has begun to fade. A west roof produces later in the day, better matching that expensive peak window, so each of its kilowatt-hours can be worth more than a south roof's midday kilowatt-hours that get exported at a lower rate. Under the right time-of-use plan, a west array's slightly lower total production can be partly or even fully offset by the higher value of when it produces, narrowing or closing the payback gap with south. That reversal is worth checking against your own utility's rate schedule, because it turns west from a compromise into a deliberate choice.

## When the trade is worth it, and how to run your own numbers

East and west are close cousins in production terms but differ in timing, and the difference occasionally matters. An east-facing array produces in the morning, ramping up early and tapering by afternoon; a west-facing array does the reverse. On a flat-rate bill they pay back almost identically, since annual totals are similar. On a time-of-use bill, west usually has the edge because its afternoon production lines up with the pricey evening peak, while east's morning production lands in cheaper hours. And for a household that runs heavy loads at a particular time — an EV that charges in the evening, air conditioning that peaks in late afternoon, a work-from-home schedule — matching the array's production curve to your consumption curve raises the share of solar you use directly rather than exporting at a discount, which quietly improves the economics. So if you have a choice between an east and a west plane of equal size and sun, west is often marginally the better payback bet, especially anywhere exports are worth less than retail.

The orientation question rarely stands alone, and it's a mistake to obsess over the compass while ignoring the factors sitting right next to it. Tilt interacts with direction — a shallower pitch softens the penalty of a non-south orientation, since a nearly flat panel cares less about which way the roof points — and shading can dwarf orientation entirely, because a west roof in full sun will crush a south roof shadowed by a tree every afternoon. The condition and size of the roof plane matter too: a large, unshaded west face that fits your whole array is a far better foundation than a small, cluttered south face that only holds half the panels you need. Judge the roof as a whole, not by direction alone, and a west or east orientation that looked like a strike against the project often turns out to be a minor adjustment among bigger factors.

One consequence of the orientation penalty is that it slightly changes how you size the system, and ignoring that is a common mistake. If a west roof produces 15 percent less per panel than a south roof would, then hitting the same annual offset takes about 15 percent more capacity — a few extra panels, if the roof has room for them. On a large, unshaded west plane that's no problem; you simply install a somewhat bigger array to reach the same production, and because the marginal panels are cheaper per watt than the first ones, the cost penalty of orientation is smaller than the production penalty suggests. Where it bites is on a space-constrained roof that can't fit the extra panels, in which case the west orientation caps your achievable offset rather than just lengthening payback. So the practical question a non-south roof poses is really two questions: how much longer is payback per panel, and is there room to add the panels that close the production gap? On most roofs the answer to the second is yes, which keeps orientation firmly in the category of a manageable adjustment.

Two roofs are worth building an array across when one direction won't hold enough panels, and this is where non-south homes often land their best answer. Splitting panels between an east plane and a west plane gives you a broad, flat production curve — energy in the morning and energy in the afternoon — which can match a typical household's usage better than a south array's midday spike, and lets you install more total capacity than either single plane would allow. The combined output of an east-west split is usually within a modest margin of what a single south array of the same panel count would make, and the smoother curve is a genuine advantage on time-of-use rates. So a home with no usable south roof but good east and west planes isn't settling; it's building a perfectly sensible system that many installers would recommend even where south was available.

The south-facing roof is the best case. It was never the only case worth building.
