The worry is understandable the moment you picture it: a crew is about to drill dozens of holes through the roof that keeps rain out of your house, then bolt heavy panels on top of it for the next twenty-five years. Stated that plainly, it sounds like an invitation to a leak. The reassuring reality is that a correctly installed rooftop array almost never causes a leak, and the reason isn't luck or good sealant. It's that the entire mounting method was engineered around this exact fear, using the same waterproofing logic that has kept plumbing vents and chimneys from leaking on ordinary roofs for a century. Leaks do happen on a small fraction of installs, but when they do, the cause is almost always a specific, avoidable mistake rather than the mere presence of holes. Understanding the difference between the myth and the genuine risk is what lets you judge whether your own project is being done right.

## Why a hole in the roof doesn't mean water in the house

Start with what a mounting penetration actually is, because the image of "drilling holes in the roof" skips the part that matters. To anchor an array, the crew locates the roof rafters and drives a lag bolt through the decking into solid framing at each mounting point. That bolt does carry a stainless steel foot or bracket that the panel rails attach to. But the bolt itself is not what keeps water out, and treating it as the waterproofing is exactly the mistake that causes the rare leak. The waterproofing comes from flashing — a piece of metal, or a metal-and-membrane assembly, that slides up underneath the course of shingles above the penetration and extends down over the shingles below it. Water running down the roof hits the top edge of the flashing, is carried over and around the bolt, and continues down the roof surface without ever reaching the hole. The bolt passes through a sealed opening in the flashing, and the shingle above hides and protects the whole assembly.

This is the identical principle that waterproofs every other thing that pokes through a roof. Plumbing vent pipes, bathroom exhaust fans, chimneys, and skylights all breach the roof surface, and none of them leak when they're flashed correctly, because the flashing — not the pipe or the sealant — is doing the work. Solar mounts borrow that proven method wholesale. On a standard asphalt shingle roof, which is the surface most American homes bring to a solar project, the flashing tucks cleanly under the shingle above and the layered, overlapping design of the roof does the rest. The particular way solar mounts integrate with a shingle roof, and why that surface is so forgiving of penetrations, is covered in more detail in [this look at solar on asphalt shingles](/blog/solar-panels-asphalt-shingle-roof/). The takeaway is that a flashed penetration is not an open wound in your roof; it's a managed, shingled-over detail that sheds water the way the rest of the roof does.

There's a second layer of protection on top of the flashing, and it's worth understanding so you can recognize good practice from bad. Reputable installers also apply a sealant — typically a butyl or polyurethane compound rated for roofing — around the bolt penetration beneath the flashing, and often set the mounting foot in a bed of that sealant as well. That sealant is a backup, not the primary defense. A well-built mount would keep water out on flashing alone; the sealant is there to handle the small imperfections and the decades of thermal expansion and contraction that eventually work on any assembly. The problem arises when an installer skips or rushes the flashing and relies on a smear of sealant alone to plug the hole. Sealant used as the only barrier will hold for a few years, then crack, shrink, and pull away as it ages and as the roof heats and cools through thousands of cycles. That's not a solar problem; it's a workmanship problem, and it would fail the same way on any roof penetration done that carelessly. The presence of proper flashing is the single clearest sign that a mount was built to last, which is why it's worth asking an installer to describe exactly how they flash their penetrations before you sign anything.

The broader evidence that panels don't wreck roofs, drawn from how these systems actually perform over their lifespan rather than from first-day fears, is laid out in [this examination of whether solar damages your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/). What consistently emerges is that the panels themselves tend to protect the roof area beneath them from sun, hail, and weather, and that the mounting hardware, done properly, is a non-event. The array outlives the fear. Where trouble appears, it clusters around a handful of specific mistakes and conditions, and those are worth knowing in detail, because they're the difference between the myth and the actual risk.

## Where leaks genuinely come from

Real solar-related leaks are not random, and they're not caused by the holes as such. They trace back to a short list of identifiable failures, and nearly every one is preventable. The most common by far is a missed or misaligned mount. If a crew drives a lag bolt hunting for a rafter, misses it, and moves over to try again, that first abandoned hole has to be flashed and sealed just as carefully as a real one — and a rushed crew sometimes leaves it inadequately covered. A bolt that goes into decking but misses the rafter also fails to grip properly and can loosen over time, working the flashing and opening a path for water. Careful installers locate rafters precisely before drilling, and a good site survey plus a clean layout dramatically reduces the number of exploratory holes. The number of penetrations isn't the risk; the number of poorly executed penetrations is.

The second major source is an old roof that shouldn't have had panels put on it yet. Flashing integrates with the shingles around it, so if those shingles are already brittle, curling, or near the end of their service life, the whole waterproofing system underneath the array is compromised from day one — and the panels make that aging surface much harder and more expensive to fix later. A roof with only a few years of life left is a roof that will need replacing under a live array, which means paying to remove and reinstall the panels on top of the reroof cost. This is why the timing question deserves real thought before a solar project, and why [replacing the roof before going solar](/blog/replace-roof-before-solar/) is often the cheaper path even though it feels like an unwelcome extra expense up front. A leak that appears three years after installation on a fifteen-year-old roof usually isn't the solar array's fault in any meaningful sense; it's the roof reaching the end it was already headed toward, now with panels in the way.

Improper flashing technique on unusual roof types is the third cluster, and it's where installer experience matters most. Asphalt shingles are forgiving, but tile and metal roofs demand entirely different flashing methods, and a crew that mostly works on shingles can get into trouble on a clay tile roof they don't know how to handle. Tile requires either specialized tile-replacement flashing or careful tile removal and reinstatement around each mount; done wrong, cracked tiles and poorly sealed penetrations invite water. Metal roofs, especially standing-seam metal, can often be clamped without any penetration at all, but a crew that penetrates a metal roof and flashes it poorly creates a leak path that's hard to trace. If your roof isn't standard shingle, the single most important vetting question is whether the installer has genuine, frequent experience with your specific roofing material — not whether they've done solar, but whether they've done solar on your kind of roof.

A few smaller causes round out the list. Sealant-only mounts, described earlier, fail with age. Penetrations placed too low on the roof, in valleys, or too close to other roof features can concentrate water in ways a flat field of the roof wouldn't. And very occasionally, foot traffic during installation cracks or dislodges existing shingles or tiles that then leak independently of the mounts — which is one reason a good crew walks the roof carefully and repairs anything they damage. What unites the entire list is that none of these are inherent to putting solar on a roof. They're failures of assessment, workmanship, or material knowledge, every one of which a careful homeowner and a competent installer can avoid. The myth says holes cause leaks; the evidence says specific mistakes cause leaks, and holes done right cause nothing at all.

## Protecting your roof, and your recourse if something goes wrong

Since the risk lives in workmanship rather than in the concept, protecting yourself is mostly about choosing and holding the installer accountable rather than second-guessing the physics. The strongest single protection is the workmanship warranty. Reputable solar installers back their roof penetrations with a warranty covering leaks caused by their work, and these commonly run ten years, with some extending longer. Read exactly what it covers and for how long, because this is the document that turns "trust me" into an enforceable promise. A company confident in its flashing will stand behind it in writing; one that hedges or offers only a token warranty on the roof penetrations is telling you something. Confirm the warranty is with a company likely to still exist when you might need it, which is a real consideration given how many solar firms come and go — a warranty from a long-established local roofer-installer can be worth more than a longer one from a company you've never heard of.

Timing and roof condition are the other half. Have the roof honestly assessed before the project, and if it's within several years of needing replacement, seriously weigh reroofing first. The math is not subtle: removing and reinstalling an array to reroof underneath it later is a substantial, avoidable cost, and factoring the roof's remaining life into the decision up front protects both your roof and your solar investment. If you're weighing a reroof against the cost of the solar project as a whole, the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) can help you frame the system's price so you can see how a roof replacement fits into the total, and whether doing both together changes the picture. Bundling the two jobs sometimes lets you avoid paying twice for scaffolding and mobilization, and it guarantees the panels sit on a surface with a full life ahead of it.

If a leak does appear after installation, the response matters as much as the prevention. Document it immediately — photos of the water intrusion, the date, and the conditions — and contact the installer under the workmanship warranty before anyone else touches the roof, because a third party working on it can muddy the question of responsibility. Most post-install leaks are small and traceable to a single penetration, and a competent installer will locate and reflash it. The frustrating cases are the ones where the installer has vanished or disputes the claim, which loops back to why the choice of company and the strength of the written warranty are the real safeguards, made long before any water appears. Judged fairly, the honest answer to whether solar panels cause roof leaks is that the panels and their properly flashed mounts almost never do, and that the small residual risk is concentrated in aging roofs and careless crews — both of which you have the power to screen out before the first hole is ever drilled.
