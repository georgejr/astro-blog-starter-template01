Two home batteries can advertise "10-year warranty" and mean very different things. One might guarantee the battery still holds most of its capacity after a decade of daily cycling; the other might quietly cap the total energy it will ever move, and hit that cap in year six if you use it hard. The word "warranty" hides the terms that actually matter. Learning to read three numbers — years, cycles, and throughput — tells you far more than the headline ever will.

## Warranties expire three ways, whichever comes first

Almost every home battery warranty is bounded by multiple limits at once, and coverage ends when *any one* of them is reached. Miss this and you'll overestimate how long you're protected.

**Time.** A fixed number of years, commonly ten, from installation. Simple, but rarely the whole story.

**Cycles.** A count of full charge-and-discharge cycles, sometimes stated as a number like "one cycle per day for the warranty term." If you cycle the battery more aggressively than that — say, charging and discharging twice a day for time-of-use arbitrage — you can burn through the cycle allowance before the years run out.

**Throughput.** A total amount of energy the battery is guaranteed to move over its life, measured in megawatt-hours. This is the sneakiest limit because it's the one buyers ignore. A battery rated for, say, a fixed lifetime throughput will hit that ceiling faster the harder you work it.

Whichever of the three you reach first ends the warranty. A battery cycled gently might age out on the ten-year clock; one worked hard for daily arbitrage might exhaust its cycles or throughput years earlier. How these limits translate into real-world lifespan is the subject of [solar battery lifespan: cycles, years, and what wears them out](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/).

## The number that matters most: guaranteed end capacity

Batteries don't die on their warranty date — they fade. So the most revealing line in the document isn't a limit, it's a promise: the *guaranteed capacity retention* at the end of the term. A common structure guarantees the battery will still hold roughly 70% of its original usable capacity when the warranty expires. That means a battery that started at 10 kWh usable is only promised to still deliver about 7 kWh after the full term.

That figure reframes what you're buying. You're not paying for a battery that stays full-size for a decade; you're paying for one that degrades along a guaranteed floor. Two batteries with identical price and capacity can promise different end-of-warranty retention, and the one guaranteeing more usable energy at year ten is quietly the better deal even if the sticker specs look the same.

Here's how the pieces line up when you compare products:

| Warranty term | What it tells you | What to watch for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Years | Calendar coverage window | Whether another limit ends coverage sooner |
| Cycle count | How many charge/discharge cycles are covered | Assumes a usage pattern — check it against yours |
| Throughput (MWh) | Total lifetime energy covered | The hidden ceiling for heavy cyclers |
| End capacity % | Guaranteed usable capacity at expiry | Higher is better; compare like for like |

## The conditions that quietly void coverage

A warranty is only as good as the conditions attached to it, and batteries carry more of these than panels do. Read the fine print for:

- **Temperature limits.** Batteries have an operating temperature range, and installing one where it bakes or freezes can void coverage. Where the battery physically lives matters to both performance and the warranty.
- **Depth-of-discharge rules.** Some warranties assume you don't routinely drain the battery past a certain point. Regularly running it deeper than specified can affect coverage, which is why [depth of discharge](/blog/battery-depth-of-discharge/) is worth understanding before you set an aggressive operating mode.
- **Approved installation and use.** Non-professional installation, unapproved pairings, or off-label uses can all be grounds for denial.
- **Registration and maintenance.** Some manufacturers require you to register the product or keep the system connected to monitoring for the warranty to stay valid.

There's also the question of *who* honors what. The battery manufacturer covers the hardware; your installer typically covers the labor to diagnose and swap it. When a battery fails in year seven, you don't want to discover the module is covered but the truck roll and labor aren't. The split between equipment and workmanship coverage — and why you want both — is spelled out in [workmanship versus equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/).

## Comparing warranties without getting fooled

Line two products up and normalize them. Convert everything to the same footing: usable capacity, guaranteed end capacity, and whichever of cycles or throughput is likely to bind first *given how you'll actually use the battery*. A battery destined for occasional backup faces a very different limiting factor than one cycled twice daily for rate arbitrage.

Before you sign, model your real usage pattern — how often you'll cycle, how deep, and for what purpose — in the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/). That usage profile is what tells you which warranty limit you'll hit first, and therefore which product's fine print is actually protecting you rather than just sounding reassuring. The headline number on the brochure is the least informative part of the whole document.
