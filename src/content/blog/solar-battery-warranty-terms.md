Two home batteries can both advertise a "10-year warranty" and mean strikingly different things by it. One might guarantee the battery still holds most of its capacity after a decade of daily cycling. The other might quietly cap the total amount of energy it will ever move and hit that cap in year six if you work it hard, at which point the warranty is spent no matter what the calendar says. The word "warranty" is doing a lot of concealing in that comparison, hiding the terms that actually determine what you're protected against and for how long. Learning to read three numbers — years, cycles, and throughput — plus one promise about capacity tells you far more than the reassuring headline on the datasheet ever will.

## Warranties expire three ways, whichever comes first — and one number tells you what you're really buying

Almost every home battery warranty is bounded by several limits at once, and coverage ends the moment any single one of them is reached. Miss that "whichever comes first" logic and you'll badly overestimate how long you're actually protected. The first limit is time — a fixed number of years, commonly ten, running from installation. It's simple and it's the one everyone notices, but it's rarely the whole story. The second is cycles — a count of full charge-and-discharge cycles, sometimes expressed indirectly as an assumption like "one cycle per day across the warranty term." If you cycle the battery harder than that assumption, say by charging and discharging it twice daily to arbitrage time-of-use rates, you can burn through the cycle allowance well before the years run out. The third is throughput — a total quantity of energy the battery is guaranteed to move over its lifetime, usually measured in megawatt-hours. Throughput is the sneakiest of the three because it's the one buyers routinely ignore, and a battery rated for some fixed lifetime throughput will reach that ceiling faster the harder it's worked, quietly ending coverage on a battery that still looks and feels fine.

Which of the three you hit first depends entirely on how you use the battery, and that's the part worth internalizing. A battery cycled gently for occasional backup will most likely age out on the ten-year clock, never coming close to its cycle or throughput limits. A battery worked hard every day for rate arbitrage might exhaust its cycles or throughput years before the calendar limit, and the owner who assumed "10-year warranty" meant ten years of coverage gets an unwelcome surprise. The way these limits translate into real-world lifespan — how many years of actual use they imply under different usage patterns — is the subject of [solar battery lifespan: cycles, years, and what wears them out](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/), and it's worth reading alongside any warranty you're evaluating, because the warranty describes the coverage while the lifespan describes what you'll actually get.

The most revealing line in the entire document, though, isn't any of those three limits. It's a promise about how much capacity survives to the end. Batteries don't drop dead on their warranty date — they fade, losing a little usable capacity with every cycle and every year. So the number that tells you what you're really buying is the guaranteed capacity retention at the end of the term. A common structure guarantees the battery will still hold roughly 70% of its original usable capacity when the warranty expires. Read that carefully: a battery that started with 10 kWh of usable capacity is only promised to still deliver about 7 kWh after the full term, and anything above that floor is upside the manufacturer isn't committing to. That figure reframes the whole purchase. You're not paying for a battery that stays full-size for a decade and then stops; you're paying for one that degrades along a guaranteed floor, and the floor is the actual product. Two batteries with identical price and identical nameplate capacity can promise different end-of-warranty retention, and the one guaranteeing more usable energy at year ten is quietly the better deal even when the sticker specs look the same.

Here's how the pieces line up when you're comparing products side by side:

| Warranty term | What it tells you | What to watch for |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Years | Calendar coverage window | Whether another limit ends coverage sooner |
| Cycle count | How many charge/discharge cycles are covered | Assumes a usage pattern — check it against yours |
| Throughput (MWh) | Total lifetime energy covered | The hidden ceiling for heavy cyclers |
| End capacity % | Guaranteed usable capacity at expiry | Higher is better; compare like for like |

## The conditions that quietly void coverage

A warranty is only as good as the conditions attached to it, and batteries carry considerably more fine print of this kind than panels do — a solar module bolted to a rack has far fewer ways to run afoul of its terms than a battery managing charge, temperature, and depth of discharge every day. The conditions worth reading closely start with temperature. Batteries have a specified operating temperature range, and installing one somewhere it bakes in summer heat or freezes in winter can void coverage outright, which is why where the battery physically lives matters to both its performance and its warranty standing. A garage that hits extreme temperatures, an unshaded outdoor wall in a hot climate, or an uninsulated space in a cold one can all put a battery outside its rated envelope, and the manufacturer will point to that if a claim comes in.

Depth-of-discharge rules are the next thing to check. Some warranties assume you won't routinely drain the battery past a certain point, and regularly running it deeper than specified can affect coverage even if the battery seems to tolerate it fine day to day. This is why [depth of discharge](/blog/battery-depth-of-discharge/) is worth genuinely understanding before you set an aggressive operating mode, because the setting that squeezes a little more usable energy out of the pack each day can, in some warranties, be the setting that undermines the coverage you paid for. Beyond temperature and depth of discharge, watch for the approved-installation and approved-use clauses: non-professional installation, pairing the battery with unapproved inverters or components, or using it in an application the manufacturer never sanctioned can all become grounds for denying a claim. And several manufacturers bury procedural requirements in the fine print — registering the product within a window after installation, or keeping the system connected to monitoring — that must be satisfied for the warranty to stay valid at all. Skip the registration email and you may find, years later, that coverage lapsed before you ever needed it.

A quieter risk sits behind all these conditions: the manufacturer has to still be around to honor them. A battery warranty is a promise stretching a decade or more into the future, and the company making it is betting its own survival on a fast-moving industry. A long, generous warranty from a manufacturer with shaky finances or a short track record is worth considerably less than a slightly shorter one from a company likely to still exist when a claim comes due. This doesn't mean chasing only the biggest names, but it does mean weighing a warranty's terms against the odds that the entity behind it will be reachable, solvent, and shipping replacement modules in year eight or nine. A warranty is only ever as strong as the company standing behind it, and that's a factor no spec sheet lists.

There's also the question of who honors what, which sounds like a technicality until a battery fails in year seven. The battery manufacturer covers the hardware — the module itself — while your installer typically covers the labor to diagnose the fault and physically swap the unit. Those are two different warranties from two different parties, and the gap between them is exactly where an unpleasant surprise lives: you don't want to learn, mid-failure, that the module is covered but the truck roll and the labor to replace it are not, leaving you with a "free" battery and a substantial bill to get it installed. The split between equipment coverage and workmanship coverage, and why you want both clearly documented rather than assuming one implies the other, is spelled out in [workmanship versus equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/), and it applies to batteries every bit as much as to the rest of a solar system.

## Comparing warranties without getting fooled

The way to compare two products fairly is to normalize them onto the same footing before you look at the headline numbers at all. Convert everything to comparable terms — usable capacity, guaranteed end capacity, and whichever of cycles or throughput is likely to bind first given how you'll actually use the battery — because a comparison that ignores your usage pattern compares the wrong things. A battery destined for occasional backup during outages faces an entirely different limiting factor than one cycled twice daily for rate arbitrage: the backup battery will almost certainly age out on the calendar, so its end-capacity guarantee is what matters most, while the arbitrage battery will likely hit its cycle or throughput ceiling first, making those limits the ones to scrutinize. Comparing the two on calendar years alone would tell you almost nothing useful.

That's why the honest first step is to model your real usage pattern rather than the manufacturer's convenient assumption — how often you'll cycle the battery, how deeply, and for what purpose. Running your actual pattern through the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) shows you which warranty limit you're on track to reach first, and therefore which product's fine print is genuinely protecting you rather than just sounding protective. Once you know you're a heavy daily cycler, you read the throughput and cycle limits with new eyes; once you know you're an occasional-backup user, the end-capacity guarantee becomes the number that decides between two batteries. Either way, the headline "10-year warranty" turns out to be the least informative line in the whole document — a starting point for questions, not an answer to them, and a poor basis for choosing between two batteries that might behave very differently under the load you're actually going to put on them.
