The number stamped on the back of an electric range — often something like 4,000 or 5,000 watts for the oven — makes cooking look like a monster on your electric bill. It isn't, and the gap between that nameplate figure and what the oven actually consumes is the whole story of kitchen energy use. An electric oven does draw a lot of power, but only in bursts, and only when the heating element is actively on. Once you understand that the element cycles rather than running continuously, the scary wattage on the spec plate turns into a much more modest figure on your monthly statement, and you can start estimating your own cooking usage with reasonable confidence.

The reason the nameplate overstates real consumption comes down to the difference between watts and watt-hours. Watts measure the rate of draw at the instant the element is energized; watt-hours, which roll up into the kilowatt-hours your utility bills, measure draw multiplied by time. A 4,000-watt oven element pulls 4,000 watts *while it's heating*, but a thermostat switches it off the moment the cavity reaches your set temperature and only kicks it back on when the temperature drifts down. During a bake at 350°F, the element might be actively on maybe 30% to 50% of the time after the initial preheat, cycling on and off to hold temperature. So an oven rated at 4,000 watts doesn't consume 4 kWh in an hour — it consumes something closer to 2 kWh, sometimes less in a well-insulated modern oven, because it spends much of that hour coasting with the element off.

## What an hour of cooking actually costs in kilowatt-hours

Put a real hour of cooking under the microscope and the picture gets concrete. A typical electric oven, counting the energy-hungry preheat and then a bake that holds temperature, lands somewhere around 2 to 5 kWh for an hour of oven cooking, with most everyday baking closer to the low end of that band and long, high-temperature roasting closer to the top. Preheat is the most intensive stretch, because the element runs nearly continuously to drive the empty cavity from room temperature up to setpoint; after that, consumption drops sharply into the cycling phase. This is why a quick 400°F preheat followed by ten minutes of baking can use nearly as much energy as the ten minutes of baking themselves — the preheat did most of the work.

The stovetop tells a similar story, scaled by burner size. Electric coil and radiant burners come in a range of wattages, and the burner you choose matters as much as how long you cook. Here's a rough guide to burner draw and what a typical cooking session consumes, offered as labeled illustrations rather than exact readings for your specific range:

| Burner or mode | Typical power draw | Energy for a common use |
|---|---|---|
| Small burner (simmer) | ~1,200 W | ~0.3 kWh for 15 min at moderate heat |
| Large burner (high) | ~2,500-3,000 W | ~0.7-1.0 kWh to boil and cook pasta |
| Oven bake (after preheat) | ~2,000-4,000 W element, cycling | ~2 kWh for an hour at 350°F |
| Self-cleaning cycle | ~3,000-4,000 W, mostly on | ~4-6 kWh for a 3-4 hour cycle |

A few patterns fall out of that table. Boiling water is the stovetop's biggest single draw, because a large burner runs at or near full power until the pot reaches a boil, and covering the pot or matching the burner to the pot's diameter genuinely trims the energy by getting to the boil faster. Simmering, by contrast, sips power, since a small burner on low draws a fraction of its rated wattage. And the self-cleaning cycle is quietly the most energy-intensive thing your range does — it heats the oven to 800°F or more and holds it there for hours with the element running almost the whole time, which is why a single self-clean can consume more electricity than a week of ordinary baking. Using it a couple of times a year is fine; running it monthly is a small but real line on the bill.

Convection ovens deserve a note, because they cut cooking energy in a way that's easy to miss. A convection fan circulates hot air, which cooks food faster and often at a lower temperature than conventional baking. Faster cooking and lower setpoints mean the element runs less, so convection can shave a meaningful slice off oven energy for the same meal — one of the few "free" efficiency wins in the kitchen, since it's already built into most modern ovens if you bother to select the mode. Air fryers and countertop toaster ovens push this further for small portions: heating a tiny insulated cavity to cook a single tray of food uses a fraction of the energy of heating the full oven cavity, which is why reheating leftovers in the big oven is one of the more wasteful habits going.

To translate any of these kWh figures into dollars, multiply by your electricity rate. Assume, as a labeled example, a rate of $0.17 per kWh: an hour of oven baking at 2 kWh costs about 34 cents, boiling a pot of pasta at 0.8 kWh costs about 14 cents, and a self-cleaning cycle at 5 kWh costs about 85 cents. Those are small numbers per event, which is the honest headline — individual cooking sessions barely register. If you want to run your own rate against your own cooking habits, the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) lets you plug in kWh and rate to see the cost, and it's a useful way to check whether your intuition about kitchen expense matches reality. Most people are surprised to find it's lower than they feared.

## Coil, radiant, and induction: does the stovetop type change the number?

Not all electric cooktops turn electricity into cooked food with equal efficiency, and the differences are worth knowing if you're comparing ranges or trying to understand why two households cook the same meals for different amounts of energy. A traditional coil or smooth-top radiant burner heats an element red-hot and then transfers that heat to the pan by conduction and radiation. It works, but a lot of the heat goes into warming the burner, the surrounding cooktop, and the kitchen air rather than the food, so a meaningful share of the electricity you pay for never reaches the pot. That waste is why the burner and cooktop stay hot long after you've turned them off — that residual heat is energy you bought and didn't cook with.

Induction changes the mechanism entirely. An induction burner uses a magnetic field to generate heat directly in the cookware itself, so the pan becomes the heating element and the cooktop surface stays relatively cool. Because the heat is created in the pot rather than transferred to it, far less energy is lost to the surroundings, and induction typically delivers a larger fraction of its drawn electricity into the food. In practice that means an induction burner boils water faster and uses somewhat less electricity to accomplish the same cooking than a coil or radiant burner drawing similar wattage, since it isn't wasting energy heating a glowing element and the air around it. The catch is that induction requires magnetic cookware — cast iron and most stainless steel work, aluminum and copper generally don't — and induction ranges cost more upfront. The energy saving on cooking alone is real but modest in absolute dollars, given how small cooking is as a share of the bill, so induction is usually chosen for its speed and control as much as for efficiency.

The comparison people most often ask about is electric versus gas, and there the honest answer is that it's not really an electricity question at all. A gas range burns natural gas for the stovetop and often for the oven, so switching from gas to electric cooking *adds* to your electric bill while removing a piece of your gas bill — it moves the energy from one utility to another rather than simply saving. Whether that swap saves money overall depends on the relative price of gas and electricity where you live, and on the higher end-use efficiency of induction, which can partly offset electricity's typically higher per-unit cost. For anyone electrifying a kitchen as part of a broader move off gas, the cooking load is one of the smaller pieces of that transition, and it shouldn't be the deciding factor — the big electric loads that follow a full electrification are heating and water heating, not the stove.

## Where cooking fits in the whole household picture

Add up a month of typical cooking and the total stays modest. A household that bakes a few times a week, uses the stovetop daily for ordinary meals, and runs the self-clean occasionally might spend somewhere in the range of 30 to 60 kWh a month on the oven and stovetop combined — call it a few dollars to maybe ten dollars, depending on your rate and how much you actually cook. Against a whole-home usage that for many families runs 800 to 1,200 kWh a month, cooking is a single-digit percentage of the bill. It's real, but it's not where the money goes, and anyone hunting for savings on their electric bill will find far bigger game elsewhere. The appliances that quietly dominate a home's consumption — heating, cooling, water heating, and the always-on background loads — are laid out in our rundown of [what uses the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), and cooking sits well down that list for the great majority of households.

That said, there are a couple of situations where cooking climbs high enough to notice. Someone who bakes daily — a home baker, a big family that cooks every meal from scratch, a holiday stretch with the oven running for hours on end — can push cooking usage several times higher than the casual figure above, and at that point it starts to show up as a visible bump in the month's kWh. If your December bill spikes and you spent the month roasting, baking, and hosting, the oven is a genuine part of the explanation, not just the extra guests running the heat. This is where reading your own bill month over month pays off; our guide to [reading an electric bill line by line](/blog/how-to-read-electric-bill/) helps you separate a cooking-driven bump from a heating-driven one, since both tend to land in the same cold-weather months and get blamed on each other.

The other place cooking matters is in the broader question of how a household's total usage is built up, load by load. Knowing that your oven runs around 2 kWh an hour and your large burner around 1 kWh a session lets you assemble a rough model of your kitchen's contribution and slot it into the larger estimate of your home's demand. That kind of bottom-up accounting — tallying each major load to reconstruct your monthly total — is exactly how you'd estimate usage without a year of bills in hand, and it's the approach we walk through in our look at [how much electricity the average American home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/). Cooking is one of the easier pieces to estimate, because the wattages are printed on the appliance and the cycling behavior is predictable once you know to expect it.

The practical upshot for anyone worried about cooking costs is reassuring and a little anticlimactic. Switching from your oven to a countertop appliance for small meals, using convection when it's available, covering pots to boil faster, and saving the self-clean for when it's genuinely needed will each trim a bit, and together they add up to a modest saving over a year. But nobody should reorganize their kitchen habits expecting a dramatic drop in the electric bill, because cooking simply isn't a large enough share to deliver one. The value in understanding oven and stove energy use isn't a big saving — it's the ability to look at a surprising bill and correctly identify whether the kitchen was the culprit or whether the real story was the furnace, the air conditioner, or the water heater working overtime out of sight. Once you can put a defensible kWh figure on an hour of cooking, you stop guessing and start knowing which part of the house actually earned that month's charge.
