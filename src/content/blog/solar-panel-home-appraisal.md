You've read that solar adds value to a home. Then an appraisal comes back and the panels barely move the number, or a lender's form seems to skip over them entirely. Both of those things can be true at the same time, and the gap between them is almost never a verdict that solar has no value. It's a gap about information, method, and evidence — whether the appraiser had the documents, used an approach suited to solar, and could find comparable sales to hang a defensible dollar figure on. Underneath all of that sits a more basic question that decides whether the conversation even starts: who actually owns the equipment bolted to the roof. Answer that wrong and nothing else in the appraisal matters, because there may be nothing there for the appraiser to value in the first place.

## What the appraiser can and can't count

An appraiser assigns value to what the homeowner owns and can transfer to a buyer. That single principle makes ownership structure the first fork in the road, and it changes everything downstream of it. A system you bought outright, or financed with a loan secured against the equipment rather than clouding the home's title, is real property. It conveys with the house, it can be appraised, and it can add value. A leased system, or one running under a power purchase agreement, is a genuinely different animal. The panels belong to a third party. What transfers to the buyer isn't an asset so much as an obligation — a contract they have to be willing to assume, with monthly payments and terms attached. Appraisers generally can't add value for equipment the seller doesn't own, and an unattractive lease can actively complicate a sale rather than sweeten it, because now the buyer is being asked to take over someone else's agreement as a condition of closing. If you're thinking through what happens to your system when you sell, that ownership distinction runs through the entire discussion in [what a move does to your solar payback](/blog/solar-payback-if-you-move/), and it's worth settling in your own mind before an appraiser ever walks the property.

The second question is quieter but nearly as decisive: does the appraiser even know the system is there, and know how it performs? Solar doesn't announce itself the way a finished basement or a renovated kitchen does. It won't show up in tax records or the public listing history in any usable form, and an appraiser working from a desk has no way to conjure production data out of thin air. The system size, the ownership documents, a real record of the kilowatt-hours it has generated, and any warranties that survive a sale all have to be handed over deliberately, or they simply don't factor into the number. This is the part homeowners most often leave on the table. A tidy folder — the interconnection agreement, a year or two of production history, proof that the system is owned free and clear, and the transferable warranty paperwork — does more for an appraised value than the panels themselves do sitting silently on the roof. Value that an appraiser can see and document is value that can make it into the report. Value they'd have to assume or infer tends to vanish, not because anyone doubts it exists, but because a defensible appraisal can't rest on a guess. The homeowner who treats the paperwork as an afterthought is, in effect, asking the appraiser to undervalue the system, and appraisers oblige that request by default.

## The income approach, and the depreciation trap that competes with it

The method most associated with credible solar valuation treats the array as an income-producing asset rather than a piece of hardware. Instead of asking what the panels cost to buy, or what they'd cost to replace today, it asks a different question: what is the electricity they'll produce worth over the years of life they have left, and what is that future stream of savings worth in today's dollars? You forecast the annual bill savings, let them erode gently as the panels degrade, project them across the system's remaining useful life, and discount them back to a present value. That's the logic behind the PV Value approach that many appraisers and lenders reference, and it mirrors exactly how an investor would size up any asset that throws off cash. The virtue of it is that it values what the system does, not what it's made of.

The inputs are the same ones that make solar economics stubbornly local. Annual production depends on the system size and the climate it sits in. The value of each kilowatt-hour depends on the local electricity rate and the net metering or export rules in that specific utility territory. The discount rate and the remaining useful life together decide how heavily those future savings get weighted against the present. Change the state, the utility, or the rate structure and the identical physical array lands on a different appraised value — which is precisely why a solar home in a high-rate market with full-retail net metering appraises differently than the same home in a territory where exports earn a fraction of retail. A brief, labeled illustration shows the shape without pretending to be a quote: assume a system saving a household on the order of $1,200 a year at today's rates, savings that fade slowly as the panels age, running across a remaining useful life of roughly two decades, discounted at a rate a lender would find reasonable. The present value of that stream lands somewhere in the low-to-mid five figures — not the full original install price, and emphatically not zero. Every one of those assumptions moves the figure, which is the whole point: value tracks the savings, and the savings are regional. The mechanics of estimating that annual savings number are the same ones behind [how much solar actually saves](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/), and you can sketch your own version of the present-value math with the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) before an appraiser ever runs their version.

Competing with that method is the cost approach, which estimates value as replacement cost minus depreciation. It tends to surface when an appraiser lacks the production data or the comparable sales to run a full income analysis, and it carries a specific hazard. Depreciation can be applied aggressively, and a system valued mostly on "what's left of the aging hardware" comes in low almost by construction — the approach quietly ignores that a paid-off array keeps cutting the electric bill regardless of its accounting age. A ten-year-old system that still offsets most of a household's usage is worth far more than its depreciated hardware value suggests, but the cost approach isn't built to see that. The deeper reason both methods matter is that appraisers lean hardest on a third leg entirely: recent sales of comparable homes. For most features, matched-pair sales work beautifully. For solar they often break down, because in a neighborhood where few solar-equipped homes have sold recently, there simply aren't comparable transactions to isolate what the panels added. When those comparables are thin, the appraiser falls back on cost-based reasoning or, sometimes, assigns little value at all — not out of any conviction that solar is worthless, but because the local market hasn't yet generated the sales evidence to price it with confidence. This is where regional maturity outweighs physics. In markets with years of solar adoption and a steady flow of solar-equipped sales, the paired data exists to support a value adjustment. In markets where rooftop solar is still a novelty, an identical system may struggle to appraise for much at all, purely because the evidence base isn't there yet. The panels are the same; the surrounding sales history is not.

## Making the value survive to the closing table

A handful of patterns show up again and again in systems that appraise well, and none of them are exotic. Clear ownership comes first — an owned system, or one financed with debt tied to the equipment rather than a lien clouding the home's title, is the cleanest thing an appraiser can value. Documented production comes next, because a real record of kilowatt-hours generated is what lets an appraiser run the income approach instead of retreating to a depreciated hardware guess. Transferable warranties and a reputable installation help, since coverage that survives the sale reduces the buyer's risk, and reduced risk supports value on the appraisal. And a system sized sensibly to the home rather than wildly oversized reads as a rational improvement; an array whose surplus earns almost nothing under local export rules is harder to justify on paper than one roughly matched to the household's usage. Strip away the jargon and these amount to the same thing: hand the appraiser a coherent story backed by documents, and the value has somewhere to land.

That's also how you reconcile the two facts this article opened with. The broad finding that solar-equipped homes tend to command a modest premium comes from market studies that look across large numbers of sales — the terrain covered in [whether solar raises your home's value](/blog/solar-panels-home-value/). Those studies describe a tendency across many transactions. An individual appraisal is a far narrower exercise, boxed in by one appraiser's data, the method they chose, and whatever comparable sales happened to exist in that market on that particular day. A flat appraisal usually reflects one of those constraints — missing production documents, an ownership structure that gave the appraiser nothing to count, or a thin local sales record — rather than a considered judgment that the panels are worthless. The two findings aren't in conflict; they're answering different questions at different scales.

Which points to what a seller should actually do, and it's unglamorous. Assemble the ownership proof, the production history, and the warranty documents before the appraiser arrives, not after the report comes back disappointing. Be ready to note whether the local market has recent solar sales the appraiser can draw on, since that context can nudge which method they lean on. If the system is leased, understand early that the lease is a transfer obligation rather than an asset, and get the assumption terms in front of any buyer sooner rather than later. Value that can be documented and defended is value that survives all the way to the closing table. Value the appraiser is left to infer from nothing is exactly the value most likely to quietly disappear between the walk-through and the final number — and the difference between those two outcomes is mostly a folder of paperwork the homeowner either did or didn't prepare.
