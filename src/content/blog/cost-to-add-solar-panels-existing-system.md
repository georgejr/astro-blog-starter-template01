The panels you add to an existing system are physically identical to the ones already on your roof, and often cheaper per module than they were at your first install. Yet the *project* costs more per watt — sometimes dramatically more. A homeowner who paid a competitive rate for a 7 kW system is routinely surprised to find that bolting on another 2 kW costs far more than 2/7 of the original bill. The reason isn't the hardware. It's that a solar install has a large fixed cost that a big first system spreads across many panels and a small expansion spreads across few.

That's the whole story of expansion pricing, and it's a story about arithmetic, not greed. Understanding it tells you when adding panels is a reasonable buy and when you'd have been far better off building the bigger system the first time.

## The fixed costs don't get smaller

Every solar project carries a block of costs that barely changes with system size. A crew still has to mobilize a truck and travel to your house. A designer still has to draw the plans. The permit office still charges its fee, and someone still has to prepare the paperwork. The utility still runs its interconnection review. These are the [soft costs](/blog/solar-soft-costs/) that already make up a large share of any solar bill — and on an expansion, they land on a handful of panels instead of a whole roof's worth.

Think of it as two buckets. One bucket scales with size: the panels themselves, the racking, the per-watt labor of mounting and wiring. The other bucket is nearly flat regardless of size: mobilization, design, permitting, inspection, and interconnection. On a large first install, the flat bucket is diluted across so many watts that it barely moves the per-watt price. On a small expansion, that same flat bucket is divided by a small number, and the per-watt figure balloons.

| Cost driver | Behavior on a large first install | Behavior on a small expansion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Panels and racking | Scales with size; volume helps | Scales with size; too few to earn volume pricing |
| Mounting and wiring labor | Spread over many panels | Spread over few panels |
| Truck roll and mobilization | Diluted across the whole system | Charged nearly in full to a small job |
| Design and permitting | A small slice per watt | A large slice per watt |
| Interconnection review | One application, many watts | A fresh application for a few watts |

*Illustrative structure, not quoted prices — the point is which rows shrink with scale and which don't.*

The result is a per-watt premium on expansions that commonly lands somewhere around $0.75 to $1.50 per watt above what the same panels cost inside a larger original project. Treat that as a planning assumption rather than a quote; the exact figure depends on how small the addition is and how much of the old design can be reused. The smaller the expansion, the worse the per-watt penalty, because the fixed bucket has fewer watts to hide behind. Comparing any expansion quote against your original [cost per watt](/blog/solar-cost-per-watt/) makes the premium visible immediately.

## When the existing equipment fights back

The soft-cost math is the baseline penalty. On top of it sit the hardware constraints, and these are where an expansion occasionally jumps from "pricier per watt" to "surprisingly expensive."

The inverter is the usual culprit. If your original inverter was sized tightly to the original array, added panels have nowhere to send their power, and the expansion drags in either a larger inverter or a second one — a cost that can rival the panels themselves. The electrical panel is the second constraint: if its busbar is already near the limit for back-fed solar current, the expansion can trigger a main panel upgrade. Neither cost has anything to do with the modules; both are consequences of how the *first* system was designed, which is exactly why sizing the supporting equipment for growth up front — the case made in [planning to add panels later](/blog/adding-solar-panels-later/) — is worth so much. An expansion onto a system that left headroom is a modest job. An expansion onto a system built to the exact watt can turn into a partial rebuild.

A quieter cost is re-permitting and re-interconnection. Adding capacity generally means a new permit and a fresh utility application, and in some territories it can even prompt a review of the terms your original system was interconnected under. That's a paperwork cost in dollars and a risk in kind — worth confirming before you commit, so an expansion doesn't accidentally disturb a favorable arrangement you already hold.

## The comparison that actually decides it

Set an expansion against its real alternative and the decision clarifies. The alternative to adding 2 kW later is almost never "do nothing" — it's "would I have been better off installing the larger system the first time?" Run against that benchmark, the honest answer is usually yes: the same panels inside the original project would have carried a much lower per-watt cost, because they'd have shared the fixed bucket with everyone else.

That doesn't make expansion a mistake. It makes it a specific tool for a specific situation — one where your needs genuinely grew *after* the first install, for reasons you couldn't have priced in earlier:

- You added an EV, a heat pump, or an addition that raised your usage in a way you couldn't have justified sizing for originally.
- Your first system was capped by budget or by a program limit, and you always intended to grow it.
- Rates or export rules shifted enough that more self-consumption now pays where it didn't before.

In those cases the per-watt premium is simply the price of not having known the future — and it's often still cheaper than the years of higher bills you'd pay by leaving your grown usage un-offset. What doesn't make sense is expanding as a substitute for right-sizing when you *could* have sized correctly from the start.

Before committing to an addition, price it honestly against a from-scratch larger system on paper. Feed both into the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) — the existing array plus the addition versus the single larger system you might have built — and let the per-watt numbers speak. If the expansion premium is modest and your usage genuinely climbed, add the panels. If the premium is steep and the underlying need was foreseeable, the lesson is one to carry into the next system: the cheapest kilowatt is almost always the one you size for the first time.
