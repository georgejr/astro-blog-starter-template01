The panels you add to an existing system are physically identical to the ones already on your roof, and often cheaper per module than they were at your first install — panel prices have kept drifting down for years. Yet the project as a whole costs more per watt the second time, sometimes dramatically more. A homeowner who paid a competitive rate for a 7 kW system is routinely surprised to find that bolting on another 2 kW costs far more than 2/7 of the original bill. The reason has nothing to do with the hardware and everything to do with structure: a solar install carries a large block of fixed cost, and a big first system spreads that block across many panels while a small expansion spreads it across few.

That is the whole story of expansion pricing, and it is a story about arithmetic, not greed. No installer is inventing a surcharge to punish you for coming back. The fixed costs are simply real, they don't shrink for a small job, and they land on a handful of panels instead of a whole roof's worth. Once you see how those costs behave, you can tell the difference between an expansion that is a reasonable buy and one where you would have been far better off building the bigger system the first time around.

## The fixed costs don't get smaller

Every solar project carries a set of costs that barely changes with system size. A crew still has to load a truck and drive to your house. A designer still has to draw the plans and pull the roof measurements. The permit office still charges its fee, and someone still has to prepare and submit the paperwork. The utility still runs its interconnection review on its own timeline. These are the [soft costs](/blog/solar-soft-costs/) that already make up a large share of any solar bill — and on an expansion, that same block of work lands on a few panels instead of dozens.

The cleanest way to picture it is two buckets. One bucket scales with size: the panels themselves, the racking, and the per-watt labor of mounting and wiring each module. The other bucket is nearly flat regardless of size: mobilization, design, permitting, inspection, and interconnection. On a large first install, the flat bucket gets diluted across so many watts that it barely nudges the per-watt price. On a small expansion, that same flat bucket is divided by a small number of watts, and the per-watt figure balloons. Nothing in the flat bucket cares whether you are adding two panels or twenty — the truck roll costs the same, the permit costs roughly the same, the design review costs the same.

| Cost driver | Behavior on a large first install | Behavior on a small expansion |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Panels and racking | Scales with size; volume helps | Scales with size; too few to earn volume pricing |
| Mounting and wiring labor | Spread over many panels | Spread over few panels |
| Truck roll and mobilization | Diluted across the whole system | Charged nearly in full to a small job |
| Design and permitting | A small slice per watt | A large slice per watt |
| Interconnection review | One application, many watts | A fresh application for a few watts |

*Illustrative structure, not quoted prices — the point is which rows shrink with scale and which don't.*

The result is a per-watt premium on expansions that commonly lands somewhere around $0.75 to $1.50 per watt above what the same panels would have cost inside a larger original project. Treat that as a planning assumption rather than a quote; the exact figure depends on how small the addition is and how much of the old design and equipment can be reused. The pattern is consistent, though: the smaller the expansion, the worse the per-watt penalty, because the fixed bucket has fewer watts to hide behind. A two-panel add-on is almost always a poor value per watt, while a ten-panel expansion starts to look more reasonable. Pulling your original contract and comparing its [cost per watt](/blog/solar-cost-per-watt/) against an expansion quote makes the premium visible immediately, and it is the single most useful check you can run before deciding.

To make the premium concrete, picture the same twelve panels in two settings. Bundled into a large first install, they share the truck roll, the design fee, and the interconnection application with thirty other panels, so each one carries only a sliver of that fixed cost. Installed on their own as an expansion, those same twelve panels shoulder the entire truck roll, a fresh design, and a new application by themselves. The panels cost the same in both cases; the overhead per panel is several times higher in the second. That is why an expansion quote can look startlingly close in total dollars to what a much larger original job would have run — you are paying nearly the full fixed costs for a fraction of the hardware, which is the whole reason the per-watt figure balloons on a small job.

## When the existing equipment fights back

The soft-cost math is only the baseline penalty. On top of it sit the hardware constraints, and these are where an expansion occasionally jumps from merely pricier-per-watt to genuinely, unpleasantly expensive.

The inverter is the usual culprit. If your original inverter was sized tightly to the original array — which is common, because a snug inverter costs a little less and clips a little more efficiently — then added panels have nowhere to send their power. The expansion drags in either a larger replacement inverter or a second unit alongside the first, and either one can rival the cost of the panels themselves. The electrical panel is the second constraint. If its busbar is already near the code limit for back-fed solar current, adding capacity can trigger a main panel upgrade, a job that has nothing to do with the modules and everything to do with the wiring behind them. Neither of these costs is about the panels at all. Both are consequences of how the first system was designed — which is exactly why leaving headroom in the supporting equipment at the first install is worth so much. An expansion onto a system that was built with room to grow is a modest job. An expansion onto a system built to the exact watt can turn into a partial rebuild, where you pay to replace working equipment just to make space for a few new panels.

A quieter cost is re-permitting and re-interconnection. Adding capacity generally means a new permit and a fresh utility application, and in some territories it can even prompt a review of the terms your original system was interconnected under. That is a paperwork cost in dollars and a risk in kind. If your original system enjoys a favorable net metering arrangement, it is worth confirming in advance that an expansion won't disturb it — you do not want to trade a small production gain for a downgrade to your export rate on the whole system. Ask the question before you commit, not after.

## The comparison that actually decides it

Set an expansion against its real alternative and the decision clarifies quickly. The alternative to adding 2 kW later is almost never "do nothing." It is "would I have been better off installing the larger system the first time?" Measured against that benchmark, the honest answer is usually yes: the same panels inside the original project would have carried a much lower per-watt cost, because they would have shared the fixed bucket with everyone else. This is the core case for getting the size right up front, and it is the same logic that runs through [oversizing vs. undersizing](/blog/oversizing-vs-undersizing-solar/) — the cheapest kilowatt is almost always the one you plan for on day one.

That does not make expansion a mistake. It makes it a specific tool for a specific situation: one where your needs genuinely grew after the first install, for reasons you could not reasonably have priced in earlier. You added an EV, a heat pump, or an addition that raised your usage in a way you could not have justified sizing for originally. Your first system was capped by budget or by a program limit, and you always intended to grow it when you could. Or rates and export rules shifted enough that more self-consumption now pays where it did not before. In each of those cases the per-watt premium is simply the price of not having known the future, and it is often still cheaper than the years of higher bills you would pay by leaving your grown usage un-offset. Paying a premium to cover a real new load beats paying full retail to the utility indefinitely.

What does not make sense is treating expansion as a substitute for right-sizing when you could have sized correctly from the start. If you knew the EV was coming and chose the smaller system anyway to save on the first invoice, the expansion premium is a self-inflicted cost, not an unavoidable one. Before committing to an addition, price it honestly against a from-scratch larger system on paper. Feed both scenarios into the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) — the existing array plus the addition versus the single larger system you might have built — and let the per-watt numbers speak plainly. If the expansion premium is modest and your usage genuinely climbed for reasons you could not have foreseen, add the panels with a clear conscience. If the premium is steep and the underlying need was foreseeable all along, take it as the lesson to carry into your next system rather than a reason to abandon this one: the second batch of panels is the expensive way to buy capacity, so buy as much of it as you can justify the first time.
