A flyer shows up under the wiper or wedged in the door: professional solar panel cleaning, book now, restore your production. It plants a reasonable-sounding worry — are dirty panels quietly bleeding money off your roof? — and then offers to make that worry go away for a fee. Before you write the check, or haul out the ladder to do it yourself, it's worth separating the real question underneath from the sales pitch wrapped around it. In most climates the honest answer to "how much is a cleaning actually worth" is "less than the flyer wants you to think," and whether a paid service genuinely beats a garden hose depends far more on where you live and what your roof looks like than on any cleaning technique. The decision isn't really pro-versus-DIY at all until you've first answered whether the panels need cleaning in the first place — and for a lot of homes, most of the time, they don't.

## What dirty panels actually cost you

Panels lose output when dust, pollen, bird droppings, and general grime settle on the glass and block light from reaching the cells underneath. That loss is real and physically straightforward — less light in means less power out. But for most homes it's modest, commonly landing on the order of **2–5%** of annual production in ordinary conditions, with rain doing a great deal of the cleaning for free. This is the part the flyer never mentions. Solar panels are tilted and their glass is smooth, so rainfall rinses away a good share of loose soiling on its own, the same way a windshield sheds dust in a storm. A system in a genuinely rainy climate may never need a deliberate wash across its entire life, because the weather handles the routine soiling before it ever accumulates enough to matter. The full picture of when dirt genuinely crosses from cosmetic to costly is laid out in [do solar panels need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/), and the short version is that the answer is "rarely, and less than you'd guess" for the average roof under an average sky.

The exceptions are what change the math, and they're worth knowing because they're the only times cleaning reliably pays for itself. A long dry season with no rinsing rain lets soiling build steadily with nothing to reset it, so arid regions see more genuine loss than wet ones. Heavy pollen in spring, agricultural dust from nearby fields, wildfire ash, or the fine grit thrown off by a nearby construction site can all pile up faster than occasional rain resolves. Panel geometry matters too: flat or very low-tilt panels shed dirt poorly and tend to hold puddled grime along their lower edge, where rainwater pools and dries into a stubborn band rather than sheeting off cleanly. And then there are localized messes, which punch above their weight — a concentrated cluster of bird droppings shading part of a single module can cost more output than its small physical footprint suggests, because of the way shading on even a few cells drags down the performance of the string they're wired into. In those specific situations, a cleaning recovers meaningful, measurable output, and paying for one makes sense. In a temperate, rainy climate on a well-tilted roof, the same cleaning recovers very little, because rain was already doing the job for free and there was never much loss to reclaim. The lesson isn't that cleaning is worthless — it's that its value is highly conditional, concentrated in dry, dusty, or low-tilt situations and close to negligible in wet, steep ones. Which category your roof falls into determines everything about whether the flyer is describing a real problem or manufacturing one.

## Weighing the two options, and why safety decides it

Once you've established that a cleaning is actually warranted, the choice between hiring it out and doing it yourself comes down to three things: what it costs, how safe it is, and whether the results genuinely differ. Laid out honestly, the comparison looks like this:

| Factor | Professional service | Doing it yourself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Out-of-pocket cost | A service fee each visit | Mostly your time; basic supplies |
| Safety | Crew carries the roof risk and equipment | You take on ladder and roof-height risk |
| Reach and results | Equipped for high, steep, or large arrays | Fine for accessible, low-tilt panels |
| Convenience | Booked and done | On your schedule, but it's your labor |

Read across the rows and the split becomes clear. The professional's core value is not a secret cleaning method or a proprietary solution — panels get cleaned with water and a soft brush either way, and there's no trade technique that a homeowner with a hose can't replicate on an accessible panel. What the fee actually buys you is the risk and the reach: the crew brings the equipment and, more importantly, takes on the danger of the job so you don't have to. In plain terms, the service fee mostly buys you off a ladder. That framing matters because it tells you exactly when paying is worth it and when it isn't. If the job is easy and safe to reach, you're paying someone to do something you could do yourself with a bucket. If the job is high, steep, and genuinely dangerous, you're paying them to absorb a risk that's well worth transferring.

Which is why the single most important input in the whole decision isn't cost at all — it's height and pitch. From the ground, cleaning a panel looks trivial: some water, a soft brush, a gentle rinse. But the job doesn't happen on the ground. It happens on a wet, slick roof, often two stories up, where the same water that cleans the panel makes the footing treacherous. Falls from roof height are serious and sometimes fatal, and no amount of recovered solar production justifies one. If your panels sit on a steep or high roof, that fact alone can and should settle the question in favor of a professional or a ground-based approach, regardless of what the service fee is — the money saved by climbing up there yourself is nothing against the downside if you slip. If instead the panels are on a low, walkable, or single-story roof reachable from a stable ladder, DIY becomes genuinely reasonable, because the risk that made the professional worth paying has mostly evaporated. There's also a sensible middle path that dodges the whole roof-risk question: cleaning accessible panels from the ground using a soft brush on an extension pole and a gentle hose, never climbing at all. Where the layout makes it feasible, that approach captures most of the DIY savings without putting you on a wet roof. The safe method for whichever route you take — along with its hard limits about when to simply stop and call someone — is spelled out in [cleaning solar panels safely](/blog/how-to-clean-solar-panels/), and the honest boundary it draws is that some roofs a homeowner just shouldn't be on.

## Doing the math before you book

Because the recoverable loss on a typical system is usually small, the economics of paid cleaning are tighter than the flyer's confident tone implies, and running the actual numbers is what protects you from overpaying for reassurance. The value of a cleaning is simple to frame: it's the production the cleaning restores, priced at your electricity rate, weighed against the fee you pay for it. When soiling losses are sitting in the low single digits and rain periodically resets them for free, a recurring paid service can easily cost more than the power it recovers, at which point it stops being maintenance and becomes a line item that quietly drags on your returns rather than protecting them. That's the same category of ongoing expense examined in [maintenance and inverter costs in payback math](/blog/maintenance-costs-solar-payback/) — the small, recurring costs that individually seem trivial but collectively erode the returns a system was supposed to deliver. Cleaning is one of the easiest of these to overspend on, precisely because it's driven by a vague sense that panels "should" be cleaned rather than by any evidence that yours specifically need it. A homeowner who books a quarterly cleaning out of diligence, on a rainy-climate roof that rain already handles, is paying real money to recover almost nothing.

The way to check, rather than guess, is to estimate the recoverable output and put a price on it. Your monitoring data is the tell. If production is sagging noticeably beyond what the season and recent weather would explain, and it doesn't bounce back after a good rain, then there's genuine value sitting on those panels waiting to be reclaimed, and a cleaning is justified. If production is tracking right where it should for the time of year, a cleaning is mostly cosmetic — the panels might look a little dusty from the driveway, but they're doing their job, and washing them buys you a shinier roof rather than more electricity. Running the restored kilowatt-hours and their dollar value through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) turns "should I pay for this" from a vague worry into an actual number, and in many climates that number quietly argues for letting the rain do the work it was already doing. For most homeowners, then, the reasonable policy writes itself: watch your production data rather than a calendar, let rain handle the routine soiling, and clean only when the numbers show a real, persistent loss that a wash would actually fix. When a cleaning is warranted, let the roof decide the method — accessible and low-tilt favors a careful DIY or ground-pole approach, while steep, high, or large arrays justify paying a professional to take on the risk you shouldn't. The technique was never what you were paying for. Safety and reach were. Match the approach to your roof and your climate, and cleaning stops being a reflexive expense chasing a problem you might not have, and becomes what it should be: an occasional, evidence-based fix for the specific times your panels genuinely need one.
