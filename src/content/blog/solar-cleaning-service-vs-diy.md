A flyer shows up under the wiper or on the door: professional solar panel cleaning, book now, restore your production. It plants a reasonable-sounding worry — are dirty panels quietly costing you money? — and offers to solve it for a fee. Before you write the check or climb the ladder yourself, it's worth separating the real question from the sales pitch. In most climates the honest answer to "how much is cleaning worth" is "less than people think," and whether a paid service beats a garden hose depends heavily on where you live and what your roof looks like.

## What dirty panels actually cost you

Panels lose output when dust, pollen, bird droppings, and grime block light from reaching the cells. That loss is real, but for most homes it's modest — commonly on the order of **2–5%** of annual production in ordinary conditions, with rain doing much of the cleaning for free. Panels are tilted and smooth, so rainfall rinses away a good deal of loose soiling on its own, which is why a system in a rainy climate may never need a deliberate wash at all. The full picture of when dirt genuinely matters is laid out in [do solar panels need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/).

The exceptions are what change the math. A long dry season with no rinsing rain, heavy pollen, agricultural dust, wildfire ash, or a nearby construction site can let soiling build past the point rain resolves. Flat or low-tilt panels shed dirt poorly and hold puddled grime at their lower edge. And localized messes — a stubborn bird-dropping cluster shading part of a module — can cost more than their small footprint suggests, because of how shading on a few cells drags on the rest. In those cases cleaning recovers meaningful output. In a temperate, rainy climate on a well-tilted roof, it often recovers very little.

## Weighing the two options

The choice between hiring out and doing it yourself comes down to three things: what it costs, how safe it is, and whether the results differ. Here's the honest comparison.

| Factor | Professional service | Doing it yourself |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Out-of-pocket cost | A service fee each visit | Mostly your time; basic supplies |
| Safety | Crew carries the roof risk and equipment | You take on ladder and roof-height risk |
| Reach and results | Equipped for high, steep, or large arrays | Fine for accessible, low-tilt panels |
| Convenience | Booked and done | On your schedule, but it's your labor |

Read across the rows and the split is clear. A professional's core value isn't a secret cleaning technique — panels get cleaned with water and a soft brush either way — it's carrying the risk and reach that make the job hard for a homeowner. The service fee mostly buys you off a ladder.

### Safety is the real deciding factor

The single most important input isn't cost — it's height and pitch. Water, a hose, and a soft brush make cleaning look trivial from the ground, but the job happens on a wet, slick roof, often two stories up. Falls from roof height are serious, and no production gain justifies one. If your panels are on a steep or high roof, that fact alone can settle the question in favor of a professional or a ground-based approach, regardless of the fee. If they're on a low, walkable, or single-story roof reachable from a stable ladder, DIY becomes reasonable. The safe method — and its hard limits — is spelled out in [cleaning solar panels safely](/blog/how-to-clean-solar-panels/).

A middle path exists: cleaning accessible panels from the ground with a soft brush on an extension pole and a gentle hose, never climbing at all. Where it's feasible, it captures most of the DIY savings without the roof risk.

## Doing the math before you book

Because the recoverable loss is usually small, the economics of paid cleaning are tighter than the flyer implies. The value of a cleaning is the production it restores, priced at your electricity rate, against the fee. When soiling losses are in the low single digits and rain resets them periodically, a recurring paid service can cost more than the power it recovers — it becomes a line item that quietly drags on your returns rather than protecting them. That's the same category of ongoing expense examined in [maintenance and inverter costs in payback math](/blog/maintenance-costs-solar-payback/), and it's easy to overspend on out of a vague sense that panels "should" be cleaned.

The way to check is to estimate the recoverable output and price it. If your monitoring shows production sagging beyond what season and weather explain, and it doesn't recover after rain, there's genuine value to reclaim. If production is tracking normally, a cleaning is mostly cosmetic. Running the restored kilowatt-hours and their value through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) turns "should I pay for this" from a guess into a number — and in many climates that number argues for letting the rain do the work.

## A sensible default

For most homeowners the reasonable policy is: watch your production data rather than a calendar, let rain handle routine soiling, and clean only when the numbers show a real, persistent loss that a wash would fix. When cleaning is warranted, let the roof decide the method — accessible and low-tilt favors a careful DIY or ground-pole approach, while steep, high, or large arrays justify paying a professional to take on the risk. The technique isn't what you're paying for. Safety and reach are. Match the approach to your roof and your climate, and cleaning stops being a reflexive expense and becomes what it should be: an occasional, evidence-based fix.
