Homeowners across the fire-prone West often buy solar partly for resilience — the reasonable-sounding idea that if the grid goes down, the panels on the roof will keep the lights on. Then the first red-flag warning arrives, the utility cuts power to the whole area as a precaution, thick smoke rolls in and turns the afternoon an eerie orange, and they discover two hard truths at once: the panels aren't producing much under all that smoke, and even if they were, a standard grid-tied system shuts itself off the instant the utility power disappears. The resilience they thought they'd bought isn't there. It can be there — very much so — but only if the system was designed for the specific conditions that wildfire country throws at it, and those conditions are unusual enough that a generic install quietly fails at them.

Wildfire-prone regions stress a solar system in two distinct ways that milder climates never do, and confusing them leads to the wrong fixes. The first is smoke haze, which temporarily cuts how much energy the panels produce while the sky is choked. The second is the deliberate power shutoff, where the utility itself de-energizes lines to prevent ignitions, taking the grid down for hours or days at a stretch. Neither of these makes solar a poor choice — the West still gets abundant sun the rest of the year, and the fundamentals that make solar pay off there remain intact. But both change how you should design and equip a system, and a homeowner who understands them going in ends up with real resilience instead of a roof full of panels that go dark exactly when they're needed most.

## What smoke actually does to production, and what it doesn't

Wildfire smoke is essentially a very heavy, high-altitude haze, and it affects panels the way any thick atmospheric obstruction does: it scatters and blocks a portion of the sunlight before it reaches the glass. The key thing to understand is that panels don't need direct, unobstructed sun to work at all — they respond to the total light energy landing on them, and even on a smoky day a meaningful amount of diffuse light still filters through. This is the same physics that lets panels keep producing on an overcast day, just pushed toward the severe end of the scale, and the honest baseline for how solar behaves without direct sun is worth having in mind, which our [look at what panels really do on cloudy days](/blog/do-solar-panels-work-on-cloudy-days/) lays out. Smoke is that effect turned up: a thin haze from a distant fire might trim output only slightly, while a dense, sun-blotting pall from a nearby blaze can knock production down substantially for the days it lingers.

Putting a range on it is fair as long as it's clearly a range rather than a promise, because the loss depends entirely on how thick the smoke is and how long it sits. Light-to-moderate smoke haze commonly costs something in the neighborhood of 10 to 30 percent of a clear-day's output, and a truly severe, orange-sky event — the kind where streetlights come on at noon — can cut production much more sharply, occasionally past half, for as long as the worst of it persists. The critical qualifier is that this is temporary. Smoke events last days to a few weeks in a bad season, not months, and the panels return to full output the moment the air clears. Weighed against a full year of production, even a rough fire season takes a modest bite out of the annual total that actually drives your bill, rather than gutting it. The right way to describe smoke haze and solar output is not "smoke ruins solar" but "smoke takes a real, temporary bite out of the worst days, and the year mostly recovers around it."

Ash is the second-order problem, and it's the one people forget until they see it. Wildfire smoke often deposits a fine layer of ash and soot on the panels, and unlike ordinary dust, ash can be greasy and stubborn, clinging to the glass and blocking light even after the air itself has cleared. A system that's back to clear skies can still underproduce for a while simply because its panels are dirty, and in a region where rain may not arrive for months to wash them, that film sits there costing you output. The fix is straightforward — the panels can be cleaned, gently and safely, once conditions allow — but it's a maintenance reality specific to fire country that a homeowner elsewhere never has to think about. Checking your monitoring after a smoke event, and arranging a cleaning if production hasn't bounced back with the clear sky, is part of owning solar where the air gets ashy. ## The shutoff problem is bigger than the smoke problem

Smoke is a nuisance that trims production; the deliberate power shutoff is the event that actually tests whether your system delivers resilience, and it's where most grid-tied installs quietly fail. When fire risk spikes, utilities across the West increasingly de-energize distribution lines on purpose to keep their own equipment from sparking a blaze — a public-safety power shutoff that can take the grid down across whole communities for hours or, in a serious event, days. This is precisely the scenario a resilience-minded homeowner bought solar to ride out, and it's precisely the scenario where a standard grid-tied system does nothing, because of a safety feature that surprises almost everyone who hasn't been told about it.

A grid-tied solar system without storage is required to shut itself off the moment the grid goes down, even in bright sunshine, even during a shutoff that has nothing to do with a fault on your own roof. The reason is anti-islanding protection: if your panels kept pushing power onto the lines during an outage, they could energize wires that utility crews believe are dead, creating a lethal hazard for the people working to restore service. So the inverter senses the grid is gone and stops producing, full stop. This is the mechanism that leaves a house full of solar panels sitting dark during a daytime outage, and it catches people completely off guard — the whole logic of it, and why it's not a flaw but a safety necessity, is worth understanding through our explainer on [why grid-tied solar shuts off in a blackout](/blog/anti-islanding-solar-shutoff/). The practical upshot for wildfire country is blunt: if your only goal for solar is lower bills, a standard grid-tied system is fine, but if resilience during shutoffs is part of why you're buying it, panels alone will not deliver it, no matter how much sun is falling on them.

What restores the resilience is storage, and this is where the whole design changes. A solar-plus-battery system can island — safely disconnect from the grid and run as its own little power system — so when the utility cuts power, the battery seamlessly carries your essential loads and, critically, the panels can keep charging that battery through the day rather than sitting idle. That combination is what turns solar from a bill-reduction tool into genuine wildfire-season resilience: the battery covers the house overnight and through the smoky, low-production hours, and the panels refill it whenever the sun breaks through, stretching a multi-day shutoff far longer than the battery's raw capacity alone would manage. How that islanding switchover actually works, and why not every system can recharge from the roof while islanded, is worth reading before you assume it's automatic, and our [walkthrough of what happens when the grid goes down and you have a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/) covers the details you'll want to confirm with an installer.

The design has to reckon honestly with the fact that the two hazards can arrive together, which is the hard part of engineering resilience here. A shutoff and heavy smoke often coincide — the same dangerous fire weather that triggers the de-energization is producing the smoke that dims your panels — so the worst case is a multi-day grid outage during which your solar is also producing well below normal. That's exactly the scenario to size for, and it argues for a more conservative approach than a homeowner in a mild climate would take: enough battery capacity to carry the essentials through the low-production smoke days without leaning on full solar recharge, and a realistic, trimmed list of what truly must stay powered — the refrigerator, some lights, internet and phone charging, a well pump, and any medical equipment — rather than an attempt to run the whole house. Sizing storage against that specific worst-case load and duration, instead of against a sunny-day assumption, is what separates a system that actually carries you through a shutoff from one that runs dry on the second smoky evening. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you match capacity to a concrete essential-loads list and a target number of days, which is far more useful here than a generic figure, because in fire country the number of low-sun days you need to bridge is the whole design question.

California has been the proving ground for all of this, both because it sees the most aggressive shutoff programs and because it overhauled how it credits solar in ways that already push new systems toward pairing panels with storage. That combination — frequent shutoffs plus a rate structure that rewards self-consumption over export — points every new fire-country system in the same direction, and the states that see the most wildfire risk are increasingly the ones where a battery is treated as part of the system rather than an optional add-on. It's worth budgeting for storage from the outset in these regions rather than discovering after the fact that the resilience you wanted requires a second purchase.

There's a permitting and safety layer worth flagging too, because it interacts with the hardware choices. Battery systems in fire-prone jurisdictions often face specific siting and code requirements — clearances, ventilation, and placement rules meant to keep the storage itself from becoming a hazard — and installers who work in these regions build those requirements into the design as a matter of routine. Ask an installer how their proposed battery placement satisfies the local fire code, because a system that can't be permitted is a system that can't be built, and in fire country those rules are stricter and more actively enforced than in most of the country.

The broader lesson for the fire-prone West is consistent regardless of which state you're in: solar remains a strong choice on the fundamentals, smoke is a real but temporary and largely recoverable drag on production, and the resilience people most want from solar in these regions comes specifically from adding a battery and designing the system to island through the shutoffs that a standard grid-tied install is required to sit out. Buy solar there for the bill savings and you'll get them; buy it for resilience and skip the storage, and the first red-flag shutoff will teach you, in the dark, exactly what was missing.
