A "solar generator" is really just a big rechargeable battery in a box, paired with the electronics to charge it from solar panels and to hand power back out through regular outlets. There's no engine, no fuel, and no fumes — which is exactly why they've become popular for RVs, van life, and tent camping where a gas generator would be loud, smelly, or banned outright. The catch is that the marketing sells them on their peak wattage and their sleek looks, while the thing that actually determines whether one gets you through a weekend is a less glamorous number: watt-hours of capacity, matched against how much energy you burn in a day and how fast you can put energy back in. Get that match right and a modest unit runs a comfortable camp indefinitely. Get it wrong and an expensive one dies overnight. Sizing it is arithmetic, not guesswork, and it starts with your own load list.

## Watt-hours in, watt-hours out: building your daily number

Everything about sizing a solar generator flows from one figure — how many watt-hours you consume in a typical day of the kind of trip you actually take. A watt-hour is simply a watt drawn for an hour, and it's the unit that lets you compare a phone charger to a fridge to a coffee maker on the same scale. The reason capacity is quoted in watt-hours (Wh) rather than just watts is that it captures both how hard a device pulls and how long it runs, which is what actually drains the battery.

Build the number from your real loads rather than a guess. Walk through a day and list what you'll run, each device's wattage, and how many hours it's on, then multiply and add it up. A rough camping example, all figures assumed: a 12V RV fridge averaging maybe 40 watts but cycling on and off might work out to around 500 Wh over a full day; LED lights and phone or tablet charging, perhaps 150 Wh; a laptop for a few hours, 200 Wh; a water pump and a few small odds and ends, 150 Wh. That's roughly 1,000 Wh a day for a fairly light setup. Add a coffee maker, an induction burner, or a CPAP machine and the number climbs fast — heating and cooking appliances are watt-hogs, a single 1,000-watt kettle running ten minutes eats about 170 Wh, and a CPAP can pull 200 to 400 Wh a night depending on whether you run its humidifier. The point of the exercise isn't precision to the last watt-hour; it's to land in the right neighborhood, because that daily total is the anchor for everything else. Working from an actual load list rather than a "what size do I need" rule of thumb is the same discipline that governs any off-grid sizing, and the general method — including how to handle intermittent and seasonal loads — is laid out in [sizing an RV solar starter setup](/blog/rv-solar-starter-sizing/).

Once you have a daily watt-hour figure, translate it into a capacity target by accounting for two haircuts the spec sheet won't volunteer. First, usable capacity is less than rated capacity: like any lithium battery, a solar generator reserves some capacity to protect its cells, and its inverter loses a bit of energy converting the battery's DC into the AC your appliances use. A realistic rule is to plan on getting perhaps 85 to 90% of the rated watt-hours as actual delivered energy. Second, you rarely want to run a battery to zero and refill it to full every single day, both because it's stressful on the cells and because it leaves no margin for a cloudy day. So a unit *rated* around 1,200 to 1,500 Wh is a sensible match for a genuine 1,000 Wh daily draw — it covers the day with a cushion and doesn't have to be flogged to empty. If your load list came out closer to 2,000 Wh a day, you're looking at a 2,500 Wh-plus unit, or a base unit with an add-on expansion battery, which many larger models support. The same usable-versus-rated logic that governs home storage applies here, and you can pressure-test a candidate unit's real runtime against your load list with the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) rather than trusting the headline capacity.

## The input side: how fast can you actually refill it?

Capacity is only half the equation, and it's the half people fixate on while neglecting the half that actually strands them: recharge. A solar generator with a big battery but a weak solar input is like a large fuel tank with a tiny filler neck — impressive on paper, frustrating in the field. If you're off-grid for more than a day or two, your generator isn't really "1,500 Wh of power." It's "1,500 Wh of buffer plus however much solar you can pour back in each day," and that second term is what determines whether you can stay out indefinitely or have to drive somewhere to recharge.

Two input specs matter. The first is the generator's maximum solar input, given in watts — the ceiling on how much panel power it can accept at once. A unit that accepts only 200 watts of solar can't be rescued by hanging 600 watts of panels on it; it'll cap the input and waste the excess. The second is how much panel you actually deploy and how much sun it sees. Here the honest gap between a panel's rating and its real output is everything. A "200-watt" folding panel produces its rated 200 watts only in ideal, perpendicular, full sun; in the real world of imperfect angles, heat, haze, and a sun that moves across the sky, a good day might deliver the equivalent of four to five peak hours, so that 200-watt panel might return something like 700 to 900 Wh over a day rather than a theoretical 200 times the hours of daylight. Match that against a 1,000 Wh daily draw and you can see the problem: a single 200-watt panel roughly keeps pace on a sunny day but falls behind under clouds or shade, so a setup meant for real independence usually wants more panel than the daily math naively suggests — often 300 to 400 watts of panel feeding a 1,000 Wh-a-day habit, to bank a surplus on good days against the bad ones. Estimating a panel's true daily yield from its rating and your location's sun hours is the same calculation used for rooftop arrays, and the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) applies it to whatever wattage of portable panel you're considering.

Recharge speed also depends on your alternatives to the sun, and a well-planned mobile setup usually has more than one. Most solar generators can also charge from a wall outlet when you have hookups, and many can charge from a vehicle's 12V outlet or a DC-to-DC charger while you drive — which for a road-tripping RV is a genuinely important input, because a few hours of driving between campsites can meaningfully top up the battery independent of the weather. The realistic way to plan is to treat solar as your primary daytime refill, driving as a useful supplement, and shore power as the backstop for a stretch of bad weather. If your trips are the kind where you park in one shady spot for a week, solar alone may not keep up and you'll want either much more panel or a plan to relocate the panels into the sun even when the rig is parked in shade — which raises the roof-mounted-versus-portable question that [RV roof panels versus portable panels](/blog/rv-roof-vs-portable-solar-panels/) works through in detail, since a fixed roof array can't chase the sun the way a portable panel on a long cable can.

## Matching the unit to how you actually camp

The right size ultimately depends less on a universal formula than on what kind of camper you are, and being honest about that saves you from both overspending and getting stranded. A weekend tent camper who wants to keep phones, a light, and maybe a small fan going needs almost nothing — a compact unit in the few-hundred-watt-hour range, topped by a small folding panel, covers it easily and packs away small. That same person does *not* need the giant unit the forums rave about, and buying one means hauling around weight and cost they'll never use. At the other extreme, a full-time van dweller running a fridge, laptop, lights, water pump, and occasional cooking is really building a small off-grid power system, and an all-in-one solar generator may or may not be the most cost-effective way to do it. Once your needs get large and permanent, a component-based system — separate battery, charge controller, inverter, and panels sized independently — often delivers more capacity per dollar and more flexibility than a sealed box, and the tradeoff between the two approaches is exactly what [solar generators versus building your own system](/blog/solar-generators-vs-panel-systems/) weighs. The convenience of an all-in-one is real; so is the premium you pay for it.

For the broad middle — RVers and van campers who want to boondock for several days at a stretch, run a fridge and normal electronics, and occasionally use a modest appliance — the sizing usually lands in the same territory: a unit rated somewhere around 1,000 to 2,000 Wh, fed by 200 to 400 watts of solar, with vehicle charging as a supplement and the option to expand the battery if your needs grow. Within that range, let your real load list nudge you, not the marketing. If your daily number is genuinely 800 Wh and you drive most days, you can size down and save money and weight. If you run a CPAP, cook electrically, or camp in cloudy country, size up on both battery and panel, because the failure mode of an undersized system — waking up to a dead unit and a warm fridge — is far more painful than the mild cost of a little extra headroom. The whole exercise comes back to two numbers you can actually calculate: the watt-hours you'll use in a day, and the watt-hours you can realistically put back. Get an honest handle on both, size the battery to the first with a cushion and the panel to the second with margin for bad weather, and the generator stops being a gamble and becomes a tool you can count on.
