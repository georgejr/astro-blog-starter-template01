The whole challenge of powering a tiny home with solar sits in one sentence: the roof is small, but the appliances are not. A 20-foot tiny house might have room for four to eight panels once you subtract vents, edges, and a walking path, yet the people inside still expect a refrigerator that runs around the clock, laundry that actually cleans clothes, and heat that keeps the place livable through a cold week. A full-size energy appetite meeting a doll-size roof is the tension every tiny-home solar design has to resolve, and the resolution is almost always the same. You decide what you are willing to run before you decide how many panels to buy. Get that order right and a small array carries a comfortable life; get it backward and you spend the first winter rationing electricity you were sure you had paid for.

## Start with the roof, because it sets the ceiling

On a conventional house, roof space is rarely the binding constraint. Usage is, and the roof is big enough to accommodate whatever the household needs. On a tiny home the situation flips completely, and that inversion is the single most important thing to internalize before you spend a dollar. A typical tiny-house roof plane offers maybe 100 to 200 usable square feet, and standard residential panels run around 18 square feet each. That math caps you at roughly four to eight panels, or somewhere in the neighborhood of 1.5 to 3 kilowatts of capacity, before you have considered a single appliance. Everything downstream of that number has to fit inside it, which makes tiny-home solar a subtractive exercise rather than an additive one.

The distinction is not just semantic. A suburban homeowner asks, "How much do I use, and how many panels cover it?" and then buys that many panels. A tiny-home owner has to ask the harder question in reverse: "Given the panels I can physically fit, which loads survive the cut?" The usual sizing logic that starts from consumption and works up to an array, the approach we walk through in [sizing an off-grid system from your actual load list](/blog/off-grid-solar-system-sizing/), still applies, but for a tiny home the roof answers first and the load list has to bend to fit the answer. That reordering feels uncomfortable to people used to the idea that you simply buy enough solar to cover your needs. On a tiny roof there may not be enough roof to cover every need, and pretending otherwise is how the design goes wrong.

Whether you can spill beyond the roof limit depends entirely on your setup, and it is worth figuring out early. A tiny house on a permanent foundation with a bit of yard has options: a small ground-mounted array or a tilt rack set up nearby can catch low winter sun at a better angle than a flat roof ever will, and ground mounts are not constrained by the footprint of the building. A tiny house on wheels usually cannot do any of that, and for those builds portable panels you set out in the morning and stow before driving become the pressure-relief valve when the roof alone falls short. Either way, the discipline is the same: establish the roof number first, treat any ground or portable capacity as a known supplement rather than a vague hope, and design the rest of the system against that total.

Sitting underneath all of this is a decision that reshapes everything else, and it should be settled before you size anything: is the tiny home connected to a utility or not? The answer changes the entire design, the same way it does for any building, because grid-tied and off-grid solar are genuinely different machines, as we lay out in [grid-tied versus off-grid solar](/blog/grid-tied-vs-off-grid-solar/). A grid-tied tiny home treats the utility as its buffer. The panels shave the bill, the grid quietly covers whatever the small roof cannot, and you never have to size for the worst week of the year. Under that arrangement even a modest 2-kilowatt array is a real help, because it does not have to carry the whole load alone. An off-grid tiny home has no such safety net. The panels and a battery bank must carry the entire load through cloudy stretches, which means the design is ruled by the days that produce the least, not the average. Off-grid is where the small-roof problem turns genuinely hard, because there is no grid standing by to absorb an ambitious appliance choice.

## Prioritizing loads on a tight power budget

With only a couple of kilowatts to work with, every watt has to justify itself, and the productive way to think about it is a tiered list rather than a single yes-or-no cut. Some loads must run continuously and are effectively non-negotiable. Some you will run occasionally and can schedule around the sun. And some are so hungry that, on a tiny roof, they are better served by fuel than by electrons no matter how much you would prefer otherwise. The table below is an illustrative ordering to make the tiers concrete; treat the wattages as round planning figures, not measurements of your specific gear, since real appliances vary widely.

| Load | Rough daily energy | Priority on a tiny-home budget |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Efficient 12V or upright fridge | ~0.5–1 kWh | Non-negotiable; runs all day |
| LED lighting, phone and laptop charging | ~0.2–0.5 kWh | Cheap to keep; low draw |
| Water pump, ventilation fan | ~0.2–0.4 kWh | Small and essential |
| Induction cooktop (occasional) | ~0.5–1 kWh per use | Manageable if used briefly |
| Washer or compact combo unit | ~0.3–0.7 kWh per load | Time it for sunny midday hours |
| Electric resistance heat | 3–10+ kWh/day | Usually too heavy; use another fuel |
| Air conditioning | 2–6+ kWh/day | The hardest load; often needs the grid |

The two loads that break tiny-home solar budgets are the same two that break off-grid budgets everywhere: resistive electric heat and air conditioning. Heating a space with electric resistance is enormously energy-hungry, and a tiny roof simply cannot generate enough to do it through a cloudy winter week when production is already at its annual low and heating demand is at its peak. The two curves work against each other at exactly the wrong time. This is why most successful off-grid tiny homes heat with propane, wood, or a diesel heater and reserve electricity for everything else. It is not a failure of the solar design; it is the design recognizing what a small array can and cannot do. Cooling is the other stubborn case. A high-efficiency mini-split run during peak sun hours is far kinder to the power budget than a window unit grinding away into the night, because it lets the array feed the load directly instead of draining the battery after dark. The single most effective habit for living within a small array is shifting the heavy discretionary loads, laundry and cooking and cooling, into the midday production window when the panels are working hardest. Run the washer at noon rather than 9 p.m. and it draws from sunlight instead of storage. That one scheduling change can be the difference between a system that feels generous and one that feels perpetually strained, without adding a single panel.

## The battery does more work than the panels

On a tiny home, the battery is arguably the more important half of the system, which surprises people who fixate on panel count. The fridge runs overnight, lights come on after dark, and any off-grid setup has to bridge from sunset all the way to sunrise on stored energy alone. A well-matched battery lets a small array punch well above its weight, banking the midday surplus and paying it back through the evening. An undersized one leaves you rationing power at 9 p.m. even though the panels did their job at noon. The panels determine how much energy you can harvest; the battery determines whether that energy is available when you actually want it, which in a home is mostly after dark.

Sizing the battery follows the same essential-loads logic used in any storage design. You total the energy your must-run loads consume overnight, then add margin for cloudy days when the panels cannot fully refill it, a method we detail in [sizing a battery for essential loads](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/). For a tiny home the honest move is to size the battery against the short priority list from the last section, not the full wish list, because sizing to the wish list produces a battery bank that will not fit in the home and cannot be refilled by the roof anyway. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) helps you turn your overnight load total into a target capacity, and the [how-many-solar-panels tool](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) gives a reality check on whether your roof can actually refill that battery each day. Those two numbers, the battery you need and the panels that can recharge it, have to agree, and on a tiny roof they often argue. Where they conflict, the roof wins and the load list shrinks again.

A constraint tiny-home owners consistently underestimate is that the electronics need space too. A solar setup is not just panels on the roof. There is a charge controller or inverter, the battery itself, wiring, and often a small distribution panel, and all of it has to live somewhere inside a home already fighting for every cubic foot. Batteries in particular want a spot that stays within a reasonable temperature range and stays accessible for the rare service call, which in a tiny house usually means a bench seat, a stair riser, or a dedicated cabinet planned into the build from the start rather than bolted on as an afterthought. Retrofitting storage into a finished tiny home is far harder than designing the cavity for it up front, so this belongs in the floor plan, not the punch list. System voltage is the related early decision that people put off and later regret. Small setups are often wired at 12 volts for simplicity and compatibility with RV-style appliances, but as loads grow, a 24- or 48-volt architecture moves the same power through thinner, cheaper wiring with lower losses. A tiny home sits right at the boundary where those choices diverge, so it pays to settle the voltage before buying components, since the battery, inverter, and charge controller all have to agree on it and swapping later means replacing hardware.

A tiny home will not run like a four-bedroom house on solar, and pretending otherwise is precisely how people end up disappointed. But a clear-eyed design, a fridge and lights and pumps and light cooking on electricity, with heat handled by fuel and the heavy loads timed for the sun, runs comfortably on a roof most people would dismiss as too small to bother with. The constraint is not a flaw the plan has to overcome. It *is* the plan.
