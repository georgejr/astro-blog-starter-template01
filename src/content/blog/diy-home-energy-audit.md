A professional home energy audit usually runs a few hundred dollars, and the good ones earn it: a certified auditor shows up with a blower door, an infrared camera, and combustion-safety gear that finds problems you can't see or feel. But strip a typical audit report down to the findings that actually change your bill, and most of them come from observations you can make yourself in an afternoon with a notebook and a year of utility statements. A self-audit won't replace the instruments. It will, however, tell you whether you need them yet.

The point of doing it yourself first is sequencing. Most homes have two or three obvious drains hiding behind a dozen small ones, and you don't need a thermal camera to find the obvious ones. Chase those, then decide whether a paid audit is worth it for whatever's left.

## What a walkthrough can and can't tell you

Set expectations before you start, because a self-audit has hard limits. Without a blower door you can't quantify how leaky the house is, only find individual drafts. Without an infrared camera you can't see missing insulation inside a wall. And you should not poke around gas appliances checking for combustion problems — that is genuinely a job for someone with a meter. If your bills are high and this walkthrough turns up nothing, that absence of findings is itself the signal to bring in a pro, because the waste is probably inside the building's shell where your eyes can't reach.

What a walkthrough does well is inventory. It tells you what you own, how much of it runs, and how long. That inventory is where the largest, cheapest wins almost always live.

## Start with twelve months of bills, not the house

Resist the urge to walk around first. The most useful hour of a DIY audit happens at the kitchen table with a full year of electric bills lined up. You're looking for the shape of your usage, not a single month's total.

Two patterns matter. The first is your baseline — the lowest month of the year, usually a mild spring or fall month when you're barely heating or cooling. That baseline is what your home draws just to exist: refrigeration, standby electronics, water heating, the always-on layer. The second is the swing between that baseline and your peak month. A big summer spike points at air conditioning; a big winter spike on an all-electric home points at heating; a flat line all year means the fix is in your baseline, not your thermostat. If your bill seems high but you can't say which of these describes you, working through [a bill-by-bill diagnostic](/blog/why-is-my-electric-bill-so-high/) first will save you from chasing the wrong room.

Write down your annual total in kilowatt-hours. You'll need it at the end, and most people have never once looked at it.

## The room-by-room pass

Now walk the house, roughly in order of how much energy each system moves. Carry the notebook. You're recording nameplate wattages, ages, and run-times, not fixing anything yet.

### Heating, cooling, and hot water first

These three systems dominate almost every home's usage, so they deserve the first and most careful look. Note the age of your furnace or heat pump, your air conditioner, and your water heater — equipment past roughly fifteen years is often far less efficient than a modern replacement, and knowing the age tells you whether efficiency upgrades belong on your list at all.

Check the obvious operational stuff a technician would flag: a clogged furnace filter, supply registers blocked by furniture, an attic hatch that doesn't seal, a water heater set above 120°F, and the thermostat schedule. A programmable or smart thermostat left on "hold" at a comfortable temperature around the clock quietly erases its own savings. These systems are where [most of a home's electricity actually goes](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), which is why a single correct setting here outweighs a whole afternoon of unplugging chargers.

### Plug loads and the always-on drain

Next, hunt the loads that never turn off. Walk the house at night, when it's easier to spot the glow of standby lights. Cable boxes, gaming consoles left in instant-on, a second refrigerator or chest freezer in the garage, aquariums, well pumps, and old power strips feeding entertainment centers all draw around the clock.

An old garage fridge kept for a few cases of drinks can cost more per year than it would take to replace, and a game console in the wrong power mode can draw nearly as much idle as in use. These [phantom loads](/blog/phantom-loads-electricity/) rarely show up as any single scary number — the problem is that a dozen of them run 8,760 hours a year while you're asleep or away. A cheap plug-in watt meter, left on a suspicious device for a day, turns guesses into figures.

### Lighting and the leftovers

Lighting is usually a smaller slice than people expect, but it's also the easiest to fix, so note any remaining incandescent or halogen bulbs — especially in fixtures that stay on for hours. Bathroom vanity strips, kitchens, and porch lights left on overnight are the ones worth converting first. Everything else — the toaster, the coffee maker, the occasional vacuum — is genuinely rounding error, and you should feel free to ignore it rather than pad your list with items that will never move the bill.

## The envelope: finding where conditioned air escapes

If heating or cooling drives your bill, the building shell decides how hard those systems have to work. You can't measure leakage without a blower door, but you can find the leaks that matter with the back of your hand and a lit incense stick on a windy day.

Move slowly along the usual suspects: the bottoms of exterior doors, window frames, electrical outlets on exterior walls, recessed lights punching into the attic, the rim joist in the basement, and any place a pipe or wire passes through the shell. Smoke that bends means air is moving. In the attic, look at insulation depth against the joists — if you can see the tops of the joists, you're short. These are findings you can act on cheaply with weatherstripping, gaskets, and caulk, and they're also exactly the findings a paid auditor would confirm with instruments if you wanted proof before a bigger insulation job.

## Turning a walkthrough into a short list

By now you have pages of notes and a temptation to fix everything. Don't. Rank the list by two questions: how many hours a year does this run, and how cheaply can I change it? A setting change on your largest system, a garage freezer you didn't need, and an hour of caulk usually sit at the top. A phone charger left plugged in sits at the bottom, no matter how often the internet tells you to unplug it.

The exercise also produces one number worth keeping: your real annual kilowatt-hour usage, ideally after you've trimmed the easy waste. That figure is the honest input for any bigger decision — including solar, where sizing a system against an inflated, pre-audit bill means paying for panels to power waste you could have deleted for free. Once you've settled your usage, you can size an array against it with the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) and see what's actually left to offset.

An afternoon won't make your house airtight or tell you which wall is under-insulated. It will tell you where your money is going, and that alone puts you ahead of most homeowners, who pay every month without ever asking the question.
