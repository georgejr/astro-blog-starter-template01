The fear that drives a lot of battery purchases is specific and concrete: the power goes out for a day or two, and a freezer full of meat and a refrigerator full of groceries slowly warm up and spoil. It's a rational worry — that food can add up to real money, and losing it during an outage feels like insult on top of injury. The good news, and it genuinely is good news, is that keeping a refrigerator and a freezer cold is one of the easiest backup jobs there is. These are small, steady loads, and the battery it takes to carry them through a normal outage is far smaller and cheaper than the whole-home backup a salesperson might steer you toward. The trick is knowing how much energy the appliances actually use, which is almost always less than their labels or your instincts suggest.

## What a fridge and freezer really draw

The number that trips people up is the nameplate. A refrigerator might list a running wattage that makes it look like a serious draw, but that figure describes the appliance when its compressor is actively running — and the compressor isn't running most of the time. A refrigerator cycles: the compressor kicks on to pull the temperature back down, runs for a stretch, then shuts off and coasts while the insulation holds the cold. Over a full day, a modern refrigerator's compressor is only actually running for a fraction of the hours, which is why what matters for battery sizing isn't the running watts but the total energy used across the day, measured in kilowatt-hours. A typical modern refrigerator uses somewhere in the neighborhood of 1 to 2 kilowatt-hours a day, and a separate chest or upright freezer adds roughly another 1 to 2, so the two together commonly land around 2 to 4 kWh per day. That's the number that should anchor everything else, and it's dramatically smaller than the running-wattage figure would lead you to fear.

Age and type push you around within that range. An older refrigerator from before efficiency standards tightened can use two or three times what a new one does, and a garage freezer working against summer heat runs its compressor harder and longer than one in a conditioned basement. A modern, well-sealed unit in a cool room sits at the low end; an old second fridge in a hot garage sits at the high end. If you want to stop guessing, the cleanest move is to put an inexpensive plug-in energy meter on each appliance for a few days and read the actual kilowatt-hours it accumulates. That measured number, extrapolated to a full day, beats any table because it captures your specific appliance, your specific room temperature, and your specific habits of opening the door. For sizing a battery you're betting real money on, an afternoon with a meter is worth more than any estimate, including this one.

There's a second number that matters less for total energy but very much for whether the battery will start the appliance at all: the surge. When a compressor motor first kicks on, it briefly pulls several times its running wattage for a fraction of a second — an inrush current that can be a large multiple of the steady draw. The total energy in that surge is trivial, so it barely affects how many kilowatt-hours you need, but it does affect the battery and inverter's instantaneous power rating. A battery system has to be able to deliver that momentary spike without stumbling, which is why battery specs quote both a continuous power rating and a higher surge rating. For a single fridge and a single freezer this is almost never a problem — their combined surge is well within what even a modest home battery inverter can deliver. It becomes something to check only if you're stacking several motor loads onto a small unit, or running two refrigerators plus a freezer plus a sump pump off one modest inverter. Understanding the difference between the energy a device uses and the instantaneous power it demands is the same distinction that separates a battery's kilowatt-hour rating from its kilowatt rating, and getting it straight prevents both over- and under-buying.

## Sizing the battery for a real outage

Once you have the daily energy figure, sizing becomes arithmetic with a couple of honest adjustments. Say your fridge and freezer together use 3 kWh a day — a reasonable midpoint. If you want to carry them through a two-day outage, that's 6 kWh of energy the battery has to deliver. But you can't count on 100% of a battery's rated capacity, for two reasons. First, most home batteries have a usable depth of discharge below their full nameplate — you're advised not to run them all the way to empty, and the specs reflect a usable capacity that's a bit less than the headline number. Second, converting stored DC energy to the AC your appliances use loses a few percent to inverter and round-trip inefficiency. So the practical rule is to size the battery somewhat larger than the raw energy math suggests. To reliably deliver 6 kWh to the appliances, you'd want a battery with usable capacity comfortably above that — call it around 7 to 8 kWh of usable storage to leave a sensible margin. That's a modest battery by home-storage standards, and it's the entire reason refrigeration-only backup is such an accessible goal.

The bigger lever on sizing isn't the appliances at all — it's how long an outage you're planning for and whether the sun is going to help. If your realistic worst case is an overnight or one-day outage, a small battery covers it easily. If you're in a region prone to multi-day outages after storms, the math changes, and this is where solar transforms the picture. A battery paired with a solar array isn't a fixed bucket that drains and stays empty; it's a bucket that refills every sunny day. A fridge and freezer needing 3 kWh a day can, in many cases, be carried indefinitely by even a modest solar array that recharges the battery during daylight while the appliances sip from it around the clock. In that scenario you're not sizing the battery to hold the entire outage's worth of energy — you're sizing it to bridge from one day's solar recharge to the next, which for refrigeration might be just a single day of capacity plus margin. The distinction between a standalone battery that must hold the whole outage and a solar-charged battery that only has to bridge overnight is the single biggest factor in how much storage you actually need, and it's why our walkthrough of [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/) keeps coming back to recharge, not just capacity.

That framing also tells you what not to buy. Backing up only the refrigeration — rather than the whole house — is what keeps this cheap, and it's usually the smart call for the food-spoilage worry specifically. A refrigerator and freezer on a small dedicated battery, ideally recharged by solar, sidesteps the expense of a whole-home system sized to run air conditioning, electric heat, and a dozen other loads you don't actually need during an outage. The general principle of sizing storage around only the loads that truly matter is laid out in our guide to [sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/), and refrigeration is close to the purest example: a couple of appliances, a few kilowatt-hours a day, and a battery that costs a fraction of the whole-home alternative. If your goal is genuinely "don't let the food spoil," resist the upsell to a system built for a very different goal.

## From estimate to a system that fits

Turning the numbers into a purchase means being clear about your own worst case and your own appliances rather than a generic scenario. Start by measuring or estimating the daily kilowatt-hours for each unit — the fridge, the freezer, and any second refrigerator in a garage or basement, since "battery for two refrigerators" is a common enough setup that it's worth adding the third appliance into the total honestly rather than hoping it disappears. Add them for your combined daily draw, decide how many days of autonomy you want the battery alone to provide, and multiply. Then inflate the result to account for usable depth of discharge and conversion losses, landing on a usable-capacity target with real margin. Running those inputs through a [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you test different outage lengths against different battery sizes quickly, and it makes the effect of adding solar recharge visible — you'll see the required capacity drop sharply the moment you assume the sun helps refill the battery each day.

A few practical details separate a system that works from one that disappoints when you need it. Confirm the inverter's continuous and surge ratings comfortably exceed your appliances' combined running and startup draw, so a compressor kicking on doesn't trip the system — for a fridge and a freezer this is nearly always fine, but it's a five-minute check worth doing. Make sure the appliances you care about are actually wired to the backed-up circuits; a battery does nothing for a freezer on a circuit it isn't connected to, which is a surprisingly common oversight in partial-backup installs. And think about placement and temperature, since both the battery and the freezer perform differently in a hot garage than in a conditioned space, and a garage freezer's higher summer draw should be reflected in the number you size around. Get those details right and a small, sensible battery does exactly the job people are most afraid of losing — it keeps the food cold. The broader mechanics of matching storage to your real needs are covered in our [solar battery sizing walkthrough](/blog/solar-battery-sizing-guide/), but for the narrow, common goal of protecting a refrigerator and a freezer, the answer is reassuring: you need far less battery than the fear suggests, and pairing even a small one with solar can stretch a day of food-saving into as many days as the sun keeps shining.
