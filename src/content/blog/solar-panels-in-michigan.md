Michigan is a useful place to understand residential solar precisely because it doesn't offer the deal most people picture. The mental model almost everyone carries — every kilowatt-hour you export spins the meter backward at full retail value — describes classic net metering, and Michigan walked away from that years ago. What replaced it is an inflow-outflow structure that credits the power you export at a lower rate than you pay for the power you import. Get that single distinction right and the rest of Michigan solar follows logically. Miss it, and every savings number you sketch will run high.

## Inflow and outflow, not a backward-spinning meter

Under Michigan's distributed generation program, your utility meters two separate streams instead of netting them into one. Inflow is the electricity you pull from the grid whenever your panels aren't keeping up — overnight, in the dim stretch of winter, on a heavily overcast afternoon. You pay full retail for it, the same rate as any other customer. Outflow is the surplus your array pushes back onto the grid when it is making more than the house is drawing at that moment, typically midday in the shoulder seasons. That outflow is credited, but at a rate set closer to what the utility pays to generate power than to the full retail price you are charged on inflow.

The gap between those two rates is the whole story of Michigan solar. A retail rate you pay bundles two very different things: the cost of generating the electricity, and the cost of the poles, wires, transformers, metering, and grid upkeep that deliver it to your door. Outflow credits generally compensate you for the generation slice and little of the delivery slice — the utility's argument being that your exported power still travels over infrastructure it has to build and maintain. Whether that strikes you as fair or not, it is the structure, and its practical meaning is blunt: a kilowatt-hour you export is worth noticeably less to you than a kilowatt-hour you consume on the spot, in your own house, the instant a panel makes it.

To feel the mechanic, put illustrative numbers on it — say you pay about 18 cents per kilowatt-hour on inflow and earn about 9 cents on outflow. Those figures are stand-ins to show the shape of the problem, not a quote of any Michigan utility's current tariff, which you will need to confirm directly. With that shape, a kilowatt-hour you use as you make it saves you the full 18 cents, while the same kilowatt-hour exported at midday and bought back that evening nets you 9 cents out and 18 cents in — a 9-cent round-trip loss on power you generated yourself. The wider the real gap on your bill, the more it pays to use your own electricity rather than sell it cheap and rebuy it dear.

That places Michigan in the broad family of net-billing states rather than net-metering ones, and the distinction reshapes how a system should be sized and whether storage makes sense. The mechanics of that shift, and why it changes the math, are the subject of [net billing vs. net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/); everything there applies directly here.

Because exports pay less than imports, the sizing logic inverts from the net-metering playbook. Under full net metering you can happily oversize, banking summer surplus at retail value to draw down against winter shortfalls. Under inflow-outflow, every panel beyond what your home actually consumes during daylight produces power that is mostly exported at the discounted rate — so the first panels on your roof are the most valuable, offsetting full-retail inflow, and each additional panel past your daytime need earns less while costing the same to install. Payback lengthens on that final tranche rather than holding steady.

The productive response is to bias the design toward self-consumption instead of maximum production. That means sizing closer to your daytime usage than to your total annual usage, unless you are willing to move load into daylight — and much of it you can. Running the dishwasher and laundry midday, preconditioning the house before the evening rate, and scheduling an EV to charge in the early afternoon all convert would-be cheap exports into offsets of full-price inflow. Storage enters the calculation differently than it would for a net-metering customer, too: a battery lets you hold midday surplus for the evening instead of exporting it at the low outflow rate, and that stored kilowatt-hour is then worth the full retail spread rather than the discounted credit. Whether it pencils out still depends on the size of the inflow-outflow gap and the battery's cost, but the case is stronger here than in a true net-metering state, where a battery mostly duplicates what the grid already does for free.

Modeling any of this honestly means entering the export credit separately from the retail rate — most quick payback estimates quietly assume the two are equal, which is exactly the net-metering assumption Michigan does not grant. ## What snow and cold actually cost you

Michigan's climate pulls in two opposite directions at once, and the honest way to estimate production is to separate them, because one helps and one hurts.

Cold, by itself, is quietly good for solar. Panels are semiconductors, and like most semiconductors they operate a little more efficiently when they are cold than when they are hot. Their nameplate rating is set at a cell temperature of 25°C, and for many crystalline-silicon modules output rises by roughly 0.3 to 0.4% for every degree Celsius the cells sit below that mark. A crisp, clear February afternoon — bright sun, freezing air — can push panels to briefly outproduce their nameplate expectation per hour of sun, exactly the opposite of what happens on a blazing July roof where heat drags output down. That counterintuitive edge is the theme of [why cold climates help solar](/blog/solar-panels-in-cold-climates/), and it partially offsets the shorter, lower-angle days of a Michigan winter.

Snow is the complication, and lake-effect snow off the Great Lakes especially so. The mechanism is simple and total: when a panel is covered, it produces nothing, because snow blocks the light completely — there is no partial credit for a coating that shades half the cells, since a covered string is a dark string. What softens the blow is timing. Those losses land in December and January, the darkest, lowest-production months of the year, when the array was only ever going to contribute a small slice of its annual total. A panel buried for a week in December costs you far less absolute energy than the same week of coverage would in June, because June is doing the heavy lifting and December barely moves the yearly needle. Two physical facts help further: panels are dark glass mounted at a tilt, so they absorb sunlight, warm above the surrounding roof, and shed snow noticeably faster than the shingles around them — often sliding clear within a day or two of a storm while the roof stays white. And Michigan's annual production is dominated by the long spring-through-fall stretch when snow simply is not a factor.

Roof pitch feeds directly into that shedding. A steeper array sheds snow faster than a shallow one, so a low-slope roof holds a blanket longer and loses a bit more winter production; it is worth asking an installer how tilt will affect shedding on your specific roof before you settle a design. What almost never makes sense is climbing up to clear the panels yourself. You would be standing on an icy roof, or raking tempered glass with a long pole, to recover a handful of low-value winter kilowatt-hours — risking scratched panels and a fall for pennies of production. Let the snow slide off on its own. One small effect cuts the other way and is worth knowing: fresh snow on the ground is highly reflective, and on a clear day some of that bounced light reaches a tilted array, nudging production up slightly. It is minor, but it is real, and it partly counters the coverage losses.

It helps to put rough shares on the seasons. In a Michigan-like climate a single deep-winter month such as December might contribute only a low-single-digit percentage of the year's production, while a peak summer month contributes several times that — figures that vary by site but always point the same direction. Illustratively, if December is worth around 3% of your annual harvest and June around 13%, then losing half of December to snow costs you something like 1.5% of the year's total, not 15%. Across a system's twenty-five-year-plus life, a handful of snowy weeks each winter is a rounding error against the spring, summer, and fall production that dominates every year. That is why snow rarely swings a Michigan payback decision, even though it is the first objection almost everyone raises.

None of that makes winter loss zero, and the mistake to avoid is modeling the array as if it runs clear all year. A realistic annual estimate bakes in some snow-related winter loss rather than assuming a spotless array every day — a modest haircut on the December and January contribution, which is small to begin with. The opposite mistake is just as costly: letting a fear of snow talk you into believing a Michigan array is a poor investment, when the winter months you are worried about are a minor share of the yearly harvest. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) spreads expected production across the seasons so you can see for yourself how little the deep-winter months add to the annual total — and therefore how little a few snow-covered days actually cost.

## Incentives and where Michigan sits on the national map

Michigan has no state income-tax credit for residential solar, so the incentive carrying the most weight for most homeowners is the federal one. The Residential Clean Energy Credit is worth 30% of a qualifying system's cost, claimed against your federal income tax, subject to the eligibility rules in current IRS guidance — the specifics of what qualifies and how to claim it are worth confirming with the IRS instructions or a tax professional rather than taking a salesperson's summary at face value. It is a credit, not a rebate check: it reduces the federal tax you owe, and under current rules an amount larger than your liability in the install year generally carries forward to offset tax in later years. Beyond that federal 30%, incentives in Michigan tend to be utility-specific and subject to change; a given utility may run a rebate or a program in one year and not the next. Treat any rebate, bonus, or "limited-time" incentive a salesperson quotes as something to verify directly with your utility before it factors into your math, not a statewide guarantee you can bank on.

How those pieces stack matters if a verified utility rebate does exist. As a structural rule, a rebate that lowers what you actually pay for the system can also lower the cost basis the 30% federal credit is calculated on, so two incentives rarely add at full face value — the sequencing changes the total, which is one more reason to run the specifics past a tax professional. Storage is worth flagging in the same breath: under current federal rules a home battery of sufficient capacity qualifies for that same 30% credit, which improves the math for exactly the self-consumption strategy Michigan's export gap rewards.

Property taxes are a separate local question worth raising with your assessor. Some jurisdictions exempt the value that solar adds from reassessment so that a system does not raise your annual property-tax bill; whether and how that applies where you live varies, and it is the kind of detail worth confirming rather than assuming in either direction.

One procedural step is easy to overlook and non-negotiable: to be credited for outflow at all, your system has to be formally enrolled in the utility's distributed generation program and interconnected under its rules. That is the paperwork gate between panels physically sitting on your roof and credits actually appearing on your bill. Your installer normally handles the interconnection application, the required equipment specs, and the utility's inspection, but confirm it is written into the project scope — an array that generates power without being enrolled earns you nothing for what it exports.

Step back to the national picture and Michigan sits in an instructive middle. It lacks the punishing summer heat that suppresses panel output across the Southwest, and it also lacks the strong retail net metering that still sweetens the deal in a shrinking handful of states. What it offers instead is decent if seasonal production, genuine cold-weather efficiency, moderate retail electricity rates, and an export credit that rewards self-consumption over oversizing. That combination is neither the best nor the worst case in the country — it is a solidly middle-of-the-pack setup where solar can pay off, but only if you model it with Michigan's own numbers. Where any given state lands on that spectrum of sun, rates, and export rules is exactly what [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/) lays out.

Put concretely, three levers decide any state's solar case: sun, retail rates, and export rules. Arizona wins on sun, but its heat and shifting export terms complicate the rate side; a high-rate Northeast state can pay off on expensive imports despite weaker sun; Michigan's moderate rates and discounted exports mean the deciding move is how much of your own power you keep rather than sell. None of those states is sized the same way, and Michigan's version specifically rewards restraint and self-use over a maximal roof.

For a Michigan homeowner, the takeaway is narrow and concrete. Pull your twelve months of usage in kilowatt-hours. Find out your utility's current inflow and outflow rates — the actual numbers, not the retail-only assumption a quick estimate defaults to. Size the system toward the power you will genuinely use during daylight, leaning on load-shifting or storage to keep more of your production in-house. And discount winter production for snow before you sign anything. A system that looks marginal when someone models it at full retail export can look perfectly reasonable once it is modeled honestly at Michigan's real outflow rate — and the reverse is true too, which is the whole reason the modeling has to run on Michigan's numbers instead of a net-metering fantasy borrowed from another state.
