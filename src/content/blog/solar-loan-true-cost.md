Two quotes land for the same 8 kW system. One says $24,000, cash. The other says "$0 down, low monthly payment" and somehow never prints a total anywhere on the page. When you finally sit down and add up the payments on that second quote, the system turns out to cost closer to $31,000 — and that's before a single dollar of interest enters the picture. Nothing was stolen and no rule was broken. You simply ran into a dealer fee, the single most misunderstood number in residential solar financing, and the reason a solar loan cost is almost never just the price stretched over time.

The gap between those two figures is the real subject worth understanding. A solar loan doesn't merely spread a fixed price across a series of payments; the financed price itself is usually higher than the cash price, and the interest then sits on top of that already-inflated base. There are two layers to the cost, not one, and the layer most people obsess over — the interest rate — is the second and smaller of them. Separate the two and a financing decision that felt like a leap of faith becomes something you can actually price.

## The dealer fee: the markup you rarely see itemized

When a lender offers a low-interest or zero-down solar loan, someone has to pay for that cheap money, and it isn't the lender. That cost gets bundled into a dealer fee — a percentage the installer pays the lender to originate the loan, which the installer then folds back into the price you finance. These fees commonly run somewhere between 15 and 30 percent of the system price, and they are almost never shown as a separate line item. You don't see a row on the quote that says "dealer fee." You see a slightly larger total and an attractive interest rate, and the fee lives silently inside the former.

The mechanics are quietly clever once you see them. On a $24,000 cash system carrying a 25 percent dealer fee, the installer needs the financed amount to net them the same $24,000 after the lender takes its cut. So the financed "price" gets set around $32,000, the lender keeps roughly $8,000, and you're presented with a pleasant-looking interest rate on a number that was inflated specifically to make that rate possible. A lower advertised rate, in other words, often just means a bigger hidden fee doing the work behind the scenes. The two move together, which is why chasing the lowest rate without ever asking about the fee is how buyers end up paying the most.

That's why the most useful question you can put to any financed quote is blunt and specific: "What is the cash price for this exact system?" The difference between that cash number and the financed total is the dealer fee, laid bare. If a company can't or won't state a cash price, that refusal is itself a piece of information about how the deal is structured. This same dynamic — invisible markups baked into the presentation rather than the hardware — drives a large share of [why solar quotes for the same house differ by thousands](/blog/why-solar-quotes-vary/), and financing is one of the biggest single contributors to that spread.

The clearest way to see all three layers at once is to price the same system three ways. Assume a system that genuinely costs $24,000 as a true cash price, a 25 percent dealer fee on the branded loan, and simple illustrative interest terms — these are labeled assumptions chosen to show the structure, not current market rates.

| Path | Amount financed | Rough total before credit | What drives the number |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cash | $0 | ~$24,000 | No fee, no interest |
| Own loan (HELOC / credit union) | $24,000 | $24,000 + your interest | Interest only, on the real price |
| Dealer-financed | ~$32,000 | $32,000 + interest on $32,000 | Dealer fee, then interest on top |

The row that surprises people is the middle one. Bringing your own financing — a home equity line, a credit union personal loan — lets you finance the honest $24,000 rather than the inflated $32,000. You still pay interest, but on the real number, and often at a rate you can shop and compare in the open. There's a tax wrinkle worth naming here too: the 30 percent federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, subject to current IRS rules and your own tax situation, applies to what you actually paid for the qualified system, so an inflated financed price doesn't inflate your credit. It just enlarges the installer and lender's take while leaving your credit unchanged.

The size of the dealer fee isn't fixed, either, which is part of what makes it so slippery to pin down. It varies by lender, by loan product, and by the rate being offered — the lower and longer the promotional rate, the larger the fee tends to be, because the lender has to recover the cost of that cheap money from somewhere. Two installers offering what looks like the same loan can be quietly charging very different fees, and a single installer may offer several loan products with different fee-and-rate combinations sitting side by side. That's exactly why one quoted monthly payment tells you almost nothing on its own: it's the output of a fee you can't see and a rate you can, blended into a single friendly number. Only by pulling out the cash price can you tell which loan is genuinely cheaper, because the option with the lower advertised rate is not reliably the one with the lower total cost.

## Interest is the second layer, not the first

Homeowners tend to fixate on the interest rate and skip past the dealer fee entirely, which is exactly backwards. A 4 percent rate on a $32,000 dealer-financed balance can easily cost more over the life of the loan than a 7 percent rate on a $24,000 balance from your own credit union, because the principal is so much larger to begin with. The rate is real and it matters, but it operates on top of whatever the dealer fee already did to the base. Judging a financing offer by its rate alone is like judging a car by its cup holders — not nothing, but not the number that decides the deal.

Two features inside the loan agreement deserve a slow, careful read, because they trip up more borrowers than the rate ever does. The first is whether the quoted monthly payment quietly assumes you'll apply your tax credit as a lump-sum principal reduction in the first year or two. Many solar loans advertise a low payment that only holds if you "re-amortize" by handing that credit back to the lender on schedule; if you don't, the payment jumps. And if you can't use the full credit — because your tax liability that year is limited — that assumed paydown never happens, the loan re-amortizes upward, and your real monthly cost climbs above the number you were shown. The payment you were sold and the payment you get can be two different animals, separated entirely by an assumption buried in the fine print.

This tax-credit assumption catches people precisely because it feels like good news at the time it's presented. The salesperson shows a low payment, mentions it drops even further once you apply your credit, and the whole thing sounds like the system paying for itself before your eyes. What goes unsaid is that the low payment was never the real payment — it was the payment conditional on your making a large lump-sum principal reduction on schedule, out of your own pocket if the credit doesn't cover the full amount. A homeowner who can't use the entire credit, or who spends it on something else that year, watches the payment re-amortize to a higher number than they budgeted, sometimes for the whole remaining term of the loan. The credit is real and absolutely worth claiming, but it belongs in your own planning, not baked as a silent assumption into a payment you were told was fixed.

It helps to ground the rate-versus-principal point in rough numbers, kept clearly as illustration rather than as current rates. Interest is charged on the balance, so the same percentage simply costs more when the balance is larger. Financing an inflated $32,000 instead of an honest $24,000 means paying interest on an extra $8,000 that only ever existed to fund the dealer fee — and paying it for the entire life of the loan. Over a long term, the interest on that phantom $8,000 alone can run into the thousands, stacked on top of the fee itself. So the dealer fee bites you twice: once as the fee, and again as the interest you pay on the fee, year after year. A slightly higher rate on the true price will very often beat a lower rate on the inflated one, and the only way to know for sure is to compute the all-in total for each path rather than lining up the rates and picking the smaller one.

The second feature is the prepayment terms, because paying the loan down faster is one of the strongest levers you have for cutting total interest, and some loans discourage it. A loan that lets you throw extra at the principal without penalty is worth more than its headline rate suggests; one that boxes you in is worth less. Beyond the loan's own terms, how you pay reshapes not just what solar costs but when it starts paying you back. A cash purchase begins saving from the first sunny day, while a loan defers the crossover point until the payments end or shrink below your utility savings. That interaction — the way financing pushes the break-even date around — is the companion question to this one, and it's worked through in detail in [how paying cash or financing changes when solar pays back](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/).

## Reading a financed quote without getting lost

None of this makes dealer-financed solar a mistake, and it's worth saying plainly before the caveats pile up. For plenty of households the branded loan is a perfectly reasonable trade. If you can't pay cash and don't have access to a home equity line or a competitive personal loan, the dealer loan may be the only path to installing now rather than several years from now — and several years of missed electricity savings carries its own real cost. Speed and simplicity have value. One financing package handled entirely by the installer is genuinely less work than arranging your own loan, coordinating a draw, and managing the paperwork. The goal isn't to avoid financing on principle; it's to price it honestly so that choosing the convenient option is a decision rather than an accident.

A financed quote can be evaluated in a short, repeatable sequence that keeps the fee from hiding. Start by extracting the cash price in writing — if the quote is financed-only, the cash price is the financed total minus the dealer fee, so make them show the difference. Then compute cost per watt using that cash price, dividing the true cash number by the system's watts and comparing it against normal ranges; judging per-watt cost off the inflated financed figure will make a fair system look overpriced and, worse, can make a genuinely bad deal look normal. Next, compare the branded loan against your own financing by getting an actual rate from a credit union or on a HELOC — if financing the real cash price yourself beats the dealer loan's all-in cost, the branded loan's convenience isn't worth the fee. And finally, check the payment assumptions: confirm whether the quoted payment depends on a tax-credit paydown, and ask specifically what the payment becomes if you don't make it.

A couple of habits make that sequence far easier to run under sales pressure, which is exactly when it matters most. Get everything in writing before you decide anything, because a number spoken across a table has a way of shifting by the time it reaches the contract, whereas a written cash price and a written set of loan terms can be compared calmly at home the next morning. And be openly willing to walk away or delay, because the most common tactic in solar sales is manufactured urgency — the sense that today's price or today's incentive vanishes tomorrow — and it exists largely to keep you from doing the very arithmetic described here. A genuinely good deal survives a night's sleep and a quick call to your credit union; a deal that only works if you sign tonight is telling you something about itself.

The deeper reason zero-down financing works so well on buyers is that it reframes a large purchase as a small monthly habit, and a small monthly number is easy to agree to without ever multiplying it out. Solar is a five-figure decision dressed up as a utility-bill swap, and the dealer fee lives comfortably inside that reframing, invisible for as long as you never total the payments. Doing the multiplication — the payment times the number of months, minus what you'd have paid the utility anyway — is the single act that drags the fee back into view. It takes five minutes, and it's the most valuable five minutes in the entire process.

The uncomfortable through-line of the whole thing is that "zero down" is never free. The money is in the fee, the fee is in the financed price, and the interest sits on top of both. A homeowner who asks for the cash price, benchmarks per watt against it, and shops financing as a separate decision will almost always come out ahead of the one who signed for a low monthly payment they never fully added up.
