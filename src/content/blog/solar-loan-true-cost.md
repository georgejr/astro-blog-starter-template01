Two quotes arrive for the same 8 kW system. One says $24,000 cash. The other says "$0 down, low monthly payment" and never prints a total. When you finally add up the payments on the second, the system costs closer to $31,000 before interest even enters the conversation. Nothing was stolen and no rule was broken — you simply met a dealer fee, the single most misunderstood number in residential solar financing.

The gap between those two figures is the real subject here. A solar loan does not just spread a fixed price over time; the financed price itself is usually higher than the cash price, and the interest sits on top of that inflated base. Understanding both layers is what separates a fair financing deal from an expensive one.

## The dealer fee: the markup you rarely see itemized

When a lender offers a low-interest or zero-down solar loan, someone has to pay for that cheap money. That cost is bundled into a dealer fee — a percentage the installer pays the lender to originate the loan, which is then folded into the price you finance. Dealer fees commonly run somewhere between 15% and 30% of the system price, and they are almost never shown as a separate line item.

The mechanics are quietly clever. On a $24,000 cash system with a 25% dealer fee, the installer needs the financed amount to net them the same $24,000 after the fee. So the financed "price" is set around $32,000, the lender keeps roughly $8,000, and the homeowner sees an attractive interest rate on a number that was inflated to create it. A lower advertised rate often just means a bigger hidden fee.

This is why the most useful question you can ask any financed quote is blunt: "What is the cash price for this exact system?" The difference between that number and the financed total is the dealer fee, laid bare. If a company refuses to state a cash price, that refusal is itself information. The same logic drives much of [why solar quotes for the same house differ by thousands](/blog/why-solar-quotes-vary/).

## Cash, own-loan, and dealer-financed side by side

The clearest way to see the layers is to price the same system three ways. Assume a system that costs $24,000 as a true cash price, a 25% dealer fee on the branded loan, and simple illustrative interest terms. These are labeled assumptions to show structure, not current rates.

| Path | Amount financed | Rough total paid before credit | What drives the number |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cash | $0 | ~$24,000 | No fee, no interest |
| Own loan (HELOC / credit union) | $24,000 | $24,000 + your interest | Interest only, on the real price |
| Dealer-financed | ~$32,000 | $32,000 + interest on $32,000 | Dealer fee, then interest on top |

The row that surprises people is the middle one. Bringing your own financing — a home equity line, a credit union personal loan — lets you finance the true $24,000 rather than the inflated $32,000. You still pay interest, but on the honest number and often at a rate you can compare openly. The federal 30% Residential Clean Energy Credit, subject to current IRS rules, applies to what you actually paid for the system, so an inflated financed price does not inflate your credit; it just enlarges the installer and lender's take.

## Interest is the second layer, not the first

Homeowners tend to fixate on the interest rate and miss the dealer fee entirely, which is exactly backwards. A 4% rate on a $32,000 dealer-financed balance can cost more over the life of the loan than a 7% rate on a $24,000 balance from your own lender, because the principal is so much larger. The rate is real, but it operates on top of whatever the dealer fee did to the base.

Two features deserve a close read in any solar loan agreement. The first is whether the monthly payment assumes you will apply the tax credit as a lump-sum principal reduction in the first year or two; many solar loans quote a low payment that quietly balloons if you do not "re-amortize" by handing back the credit. If you cannot use the full credit — because your tax liability is limited — that assumed payment never materializes and your real cost climbs. The second is prepayment terms, since paying the loan down faster is one of the strongest levers you have.

How you pay also changes when the system breaks even, not just what it costs. A cash purchase starts saving immediately; a loan defers the crossover until payments end or shrink. That interaction is worked through in [how paying cash or financing changes when solar pays back](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/), which is the companion question to this one.

## When the branded loan is still worth it

None of this makes dealer-financed solar a mistake. For some households the branded loan is a reasonable trade. If you cannot pay cash and do not have access to a home equity line or a competitive personal loan, the dealer loan may be the only way to install now rather than in several years — and several years of missed savings has its own cost. Speed and simplicity have value, and a single financing package handled by the installer is genuinely less work than arranging your own.

The point is not to avoid financing but to price it honestly. If you know the cash price, know the dealer fee, and have compared the branded loan against at least one outside option, then choosing the dealer loan with open eyes is a defensible decision. What you want to avoid is paying the fee without ever knowing it existed, which is the default outcome for a buyer who only ever sees a monthly payment.

## Reading a financed quote without getting lost

A financed solar quote can be evaluated in a short, repeatable sequence:

1. **Extract the cash price.** Ask for it in writing. If the quote is financed-only, the cash price is the financed total minus the dealer fee — make them show it.
2. **Compute cost per watt on the cash price.** Divide the true cash number by system watts and compare it against normal ranges. Judging per-watt cost off the inflated financed figure will make a fair system look overpriced and hide a genuinely bad one.
3. **Compare against your own financing.** Get a rate from a credit union or HELOC. If financing the real cash price yourself beats the dealer loan's all-in cost, the branded loan's convenience is not worth the fee.
4. **Check the payment assumptions.** Confirm whether the quoted payment assumes a tax-credit paydown, and what happens if you do not make it.

Benchmarking is where a neutral estimate helps. The broader picture of where a quote's dollars go — hardware, labor, soft costs, and margin — is mapped in [where your solar installation money actually goes](/blog/solar-installation-cost-breakdown/).

The uncomfortable summary is that "zero down" is never free. The money is in the fee, the fee is in the financed price, and the interest sits on top of both. A homeowner who asks for the cash price, benchmarks per watt against it, and shops financing separately will almost always beat the person who signed for a low monthly payment they never fully added up.
