Cutting your electricity usage and shifting it are two different moves that people lump together as "saving energy." Cutting lowers the number of kilowatt-hours you consume — a more efficient fridge, LED bulbs, a cooler thermostat setting. Load shifting doesn't touch that number at all. You run the same dishwasher, wash the same laundry, charge the same car; you simply do it at a different hour. The kilowatt-hours are identical. What changes is what each one costs.

That's the whole idea, and whether it's worth doing comes down to a single question: does your electricity cost the same at every hour? If it does, shifting saves you nothing, and you should spend your effort cutting usage instead. If it doesn't — because you're on a time-of-use rate, or because you have solar that no longer earns full retail for exports — then moving a load from an expensive hour to a cheap one is free money, earned with a delay-start button.

## When moving a kilowatt-hour is worth money

Load shifting pays only where the *price* of power varies across the day. Three situations create that variation, and they're worth naming because they decide whether the whole exercise is useful for you.

The clearest is a **time-of-use rate**, where the utility charges more during peak hours (typically late afternoon and evening) and less overnight or midday. The gap between peak and off-peak prices is exactly the reward for shifting — run a heavy load off-peak and you pay the cheap rate for the same kilowatt-hours. How steep that reward is, and how the peak windows are defined, is the heart of [time-of-use rates and solar](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/); the wider the spread, the more shifting is worth.

The second is **solar without full net metering.** If your utility credits exported power at less than the retail rate, every kilowatt-hour you consume while the panels are producing is worth full retail, while every one you export is worth less. Shifting flexible loads into the sunny middle of the day converts low-value exports into high-value self-consumption — the same kilowatt-hour, worth far more because you used it instead of selling it.

The third is a **battery**, which lets you shift power even if your habits can't. It stores cheap or self-generated energy and releases it during expensive hours automatically, doing the shifting on your behalf. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) helps size storage against the loads you'd want it to cover.

One case where shifting does *not* help is worth flagging, because it's a common confusion: under a **tiered rate**, where the price climbs as your monthly total rises, timing is irrelevant. Tiers care how *much* you use in the billing period, not *when*. On a purely tiered structure with a flat time-of-day price, the answer is to cut usage, not shift it.

## Which loads actually move

Not every appliance is a candidate. The ones worth shifting share two traits: they use a meaningful amount of power, and you don't care exactly when they run. Judge each load against those two criteria and the list sorts itself.

- **EV charging** is the ideal shiftable load — large, and almost never time-sensitive as long as the car is ready by morning. A charge timer set to off-peak or midday-solar hours is the single highest-value shift in most homes.
- **Laundry and dishwashing** move easily. A delay-start button or simply choosing when to press "go" reschedules them with zero downside beyond a little planning.
- **Water heating** is shiftable if the tank is well insulated — heat the water off-peak and it holds until you need it. A timer or a heat-pump water heater's scheduling does the work.
- **Pool pumps** run for hours on a schedule you control, making them one of the easiest large loads to move into cheap or sunny hours.
- **Pre-cooling or pre-heating** shifts your HVAC indirectly: run the system hard just before a peak window, then coast through the expensive hours on the thermal mass you banked.

What doesn't move is anything tied to the moment you need it — lighting, cooking, the television, the fridge that runs whenever it runs. Trying to shift those is friction with no reward. The point is to identify the handful of big, flexible loads and leave the rest alone. If you're not sure which of your appliances are the big ones, a quick read of [what uses the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) points you at the loads where shifting is worth the trouble.

## Building a routine you'll actually keep

The failure mode of load shifting isn't the concept — it's remembering to do it every day. The fix is automation wherever possible and one or two habits everywhere else. Set the EV charger and the water heater on timers so they shift themselves; those are the biggest loads and the ones you'd most often forget. Then attach the manual loads to cues you already have: start the dishwasher when you go to bed, run laundry on the weekend afternoon when the sun is high, set the pool pump schedule once and leave it.

The goal is to make the cheap hour the default, so shifting costs you no ongoing attention. A routine that depends on checking the clock every evening won't survive a busy week; a routine built into timers and tied to bedtime survives indefinitely.

## Shifting and cutting are partners, not rivals

The strongest position is to do both, in order. Cutting usage shrinks the bill no matter what rate you're on, and it shrinks the system you'd need if you go solar — which is why [cutting usage first](/blog/reduce-electricity-usage-before-solar/) is the standard advice before sizing an array. Load shifting then works on whatever usage remains, squeezing out the difference between peak and off-peak pricing on the loads you couldn't eliminate.

Think of it as two passes over the same bill. The first pass removes kilowatt-hours you didn't need. The second pass moves the ones you did need to the cheapest hour available. Cutting lowers the quantity; shifting lowers the price of what's left. Neither replaces the other, and the households that save the most simply use both.
