Cutting your electricity usage and shifting it are two different moves that people lump together as "saving energy," and the confusion costs money. Cutting lowers the number of kilowatt-hours you consume — a more efficient fridge, LED bulbs, a thermostat nudged a couple of degrees. Load shifting doesn't touch that number at all. You run the same dishwasher, wash the same laundry, and charge the same car; you simply do it at a different hour. The kilowatt-hours are identical. What changes is what each one costs you, and on the right rate plan that change alone can shave real money off a bill without asking you to give up a single thing.

Whether it is worth doing comes down to one question: does your electricity cost the same at every hour of the day? If it does, shifting saves you nothing, and your effort belongs on cutting usage instead. If it doesn't — because you are on a time-of-use rate, or because you have solar that no longer earns full retail for exported power — then moving a load from an expensive hour to a cheap one is close to free money, earned with a delay-start button and a little planning. The entire skill of load shifting is knowing which of those two worlds you live in, and then acting on it.

## When moving a kilowatt-hour is worth money

Load shifting pays only where the price of power varies across the day, and three situations create that variation. The clearest is a time-of-use rate, where the utility charges more during peak hours — typically the late afternoon and evening, when everyone gets home and demand spikes — and less overnight or in the middle of the day. The gap between the peak and off-peak prices is precisely the reward for shifting: run a heavy load off-peak and you pay the cheap rate for the exact same kilowatt-hours you would otherwise have bought at the expensive one. How steep that reward is, and how the peak windows are defined, is the heart of [time-of-use rates and solar](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/). The wider the spread between peak and off-peak, the more each shifted load is worth, and on some plans the peak rate is two or three times the off-peak rate — a difference large enough to reshape your whole daily routine around.

The second situation is solar without full net metering. If your utility credits exported power at less than the retail rate you pay to buy it back, then every kilowatt-hour you consume while the panels are producing is worth full retail to you, while every kilowatt-hour you export is worth only the lower credit. Shifting flexible loads into the sunny middle of the day converts low-value exports into high-value self-consumption. It is the same physical kilowatt-hour either way, but it is worth far more when you use it yourself than when you sell it back at a discount. Under this kind of export arrangement, the middle of the day becomes your cheapest power of all, and the goal flips from "run things at night" to "run things while the sun is high."

The third situation is a battery, which lets you shift power even when your habits can't. It stores cheap grid power or your own midday solar surplus and releases it during the expensive evening hours automatically, doing the shifting on your behalf while you do nothing. That is the case where storage stops being just backup insurance and starts paying for itself on the daily rate spread. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) helps size storage against the specific loads you would want it to cover, so you can see whether the arithmetic works before you buy.

One case where shifting does not help is worth flagging, because it is a common and costly confusion. Under a tiered rate, where the price climbs as your monthly total rises, timing is irrelevant. Tiers care about how much you use across the billing period, not when you use it. On a purely tiered structure with a flat time-of-day price, moving your dishwasher to midnight accomplishes nothing — the answer there is to cut usage and stay in a lower tier, not to shift it. Before you rearrange your life around off-peak hours, confirm that your rate actually varies by time of day. If it doesn't, you are solving the wrong problem.

## Which loads actually move

Not every appliance is a candidate for shifting, and trying to shift the wrong ones is pure friction with no payoff. The loads worth moving share two traits: they draw a meaningful amount of power, and you don't much care exactly when they run. Judge each load against those two criteria and the list of candidates sorts itself out quickly.

EV charging sits at the top of almost every home's list. It is a large load — often the single biggest a household adds — and it is almost never time-sensitive, as long as the car is ready by morning. A charge timer set to off-peak or midday-solar hours is the highest-value shift most homes have available, and once it is set, it repeats itself every night without a thought. Laundry and dishwashing move nearly as easily: a delay-start button, or simply a habit of choosing when to press go, reschedules them with no downside beyond a little forethought. Water heating is shiftable whenever the tank is well insulated, because heat banked off-peak holds until you need it — a timer or a heat-pump water heater's built-in scheduling does the work quietly. Pool pumps run for hours on a schedule you set once, which makes them one of the easiest large loads to move into cheap or sunny hours and leave there. And your HVAC can be shifted indirectly through pre-cooling or pre-heating: run the system hard just before a peak window opens, then coast through the expensive hours on the thermal mass you banked, letting the house drift within a comfortable range instead of running the compressor at peak prices.

What does not move is anything tied to the exact moment you need it — the lights you switch on at dusk, the stove at dinnertime, the television in the evening, the refrigerator that cycles whenever it cycles. Trying to shift those loads is effort with no reward, and chasing them is how people burn out on the whole idea. The point is to identify the handful of big, flexible loads and leave everything else alone. If you are not sure which of your appliances are the heavy hitters worth targeting, a quick read of [what uses the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) points you straight at the loads where shifting actually earns its keep, so you spend your attention where it pays.

## Building a routine you'll actually keep

The failure mode of load shifting is never the concept — it is remembering to do it every single day, on the busy days as well as the calm ones. The fix is automation wherever possible and one or two anchored habits everywhere else. Set the EV charger and the water heater on timers so they shift themselves; those are the two biggest loads and, not coincidentally, the two you would most often forget in the moment. Once they are scheduled, they do the right thing every day whether you think about it or not, which is exactly what you want from your largest loads.

For the manual loads, the trick is to attach each one to a cue you already have rather than to the clock. Start the dishwasher when you head to bed. Run laundry on the weekend afternoon when the sun is high and, if you have solar, your own production is peaking. Set the pool pump schedule once and never touch it again. The goal is to make the cheap hour the default choice, so shifting costs you no ongoing attention and survives the weeks when you have none to spare. A routine that depends on checking the rate schedule every evening will quietly collapse the first hectic week; a routine built into timers and tied to existing habits like bedtime keeps running indefinitely, which is the only kind of routine that actually saves money over a year.

## Shifting and cutting are partners, not rivals

The strongest position is not to choose between shifting and cutting but to do both, in order. Cutting usage shrinks the bill no matter what rate you are on, and it shrinks the solar system you would need if you go that route — which is exactly why [cutting usage first](/blog/reduce-electricity-usage-before-solar/) is the standard advice before anyone sizes an array. Every kilowatt-hour you eliminate is one you never have to generate, buy, or schedule around. Load shifting then goes to work on whatever usage remains, squeezing out the difference between peak and off-peak pricing on the loads you could not or would not eliminate.

Think of it as two passes over the same bill. The first pass removes the kilowatt-hours you did not really need — the phantom loads, the inefficient appliances, the overheated or overcooled rooms. The second pass takes the kilowatt-hours you genuinely do need and moves them to the cheapest hour available. Cutting lowers the quantity; shifting lowers the price of what is left. Neither one replaces the other, and the households that save the most are simply the ones that use both, in that sequence: eliminate first, then reschedule what remains. Done together, they attack the bill from both sides at once, and they do it without asking you to live with less than you actually want.
