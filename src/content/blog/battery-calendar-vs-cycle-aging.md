A home battery ages on two clocks at the same time, and most owners only think about one of them. The first clock ticks every time you charge and discharge the pack — that's cycle aging, the wear you'd expect from using a thing. The second clock ticks whether you use the battery or not, driven purely by chemistry and temperature — that's calendar aging, and it's the one that surprises people. A battery sitting quietly on a garage wall, cycled gently a few times a week, will still be measurably weaker in ten years than it was on day one. Not because you wore it out, but because time and heat wear it out on their own.

Understanding both clocks matters because they lead to different conclusions about how to buy and use a battery. If you only think about cycle aging, you'll assume that using the battery less makes it last forever — and you'll be wrong, sometimes expensively. If you understand calendar aging too, you'll realize that a battery has a shelf life whether you cycle it hard or baby it, which changes how you think about oversizing, warranties, and where you physically put the thing.

## The two clocks and why they run at different speeds

Cycle aging is the intuitive one. Every full charge-discharge cycle drives lithium ions in and out of the electrode materials, and each round trip causes a tiny amount of irreversible damage — a little lithium gets locked up in unwanted compounds, the electrode structure fatigues microscopically, and the pack's usable capacity ticks down a hair. Do this thousands of times and the losses add up. This is why battery warranties are written partly in cycle counts or in total energy throughput: the manufacturer is betting on how many round trips the chemistry can take before capacity falls below a guaranteed threshold, often around 70% of the original rating. If you cycle a battery once a day, you rack up roughly 365 cycles a year; twice a day for aggressive time-of-use arbitrage, and you're at 730. The depth of each cycle matters too — shallow cycles are gentler than deep ones — which is a large part of why [depth of discharge](/blog/battery-depth-of-discharge/) shows up in every serious battery spec sheet. A pack that only ever swings between 30% and 70% will outlast an identical one drained to empty and filled to full every day.

Calendar aging is the counterintuitive one, and it's a genuinely separate mechanism rather than just slow cycling. Even a battery that never moves a single kilowatt-hour is undergoing chemical reactions at its electrodes. A passivation layer called the solid-electrolyte interphase keeps growing on the anode over time, consuming a little active lithium and slightly raising the pack's internal resistance. This happens as a function of time, temperature, and the state of charge the battery sits at while it waits. It's why an unused laptop or phone battery is noticeably weaker after five years in a drawer than it was new — nobody cycled it, but the calendar didn't care. In a home battery, calendar aging sets a floor on lifespan that no amount of gentle use can push past. You cannot cycle a battery so little that it lasts forever, because doing nothing is itself a slow form of aging.

The two clocks run at very different speeds depending on the battery's job. A pack used purely for occasional backup — sitting near full charge for months, discharging only during rare outages — accumulates almost no cycle wear but full calendar wear. For that battery, calendar aging is essentially the *entire* aging story, and its lifespan is governed by chemistry and temperature rather than by use. A pack doing daily solar self-consumption or time-of-use arbitrage racks up cycle wear quickly, so cycle aging dominates and calendar aging becomes the secondary term. Most real home batteries live somewhere between these extremes, aging on both clocks, with the mix depending on how hard you work them. This is why two identical batteries installed the same week can have quite different capacity a decade later: not because one was defective, but because one cycled daily for arbitrage while the other mostly stood by for backup. Chemistry changes the balance too. The lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry that dominates new home batteries tends to have gentler cycle aging and better calendar stability than the older nickel-based chemistries used in some earlier products and most EVs, which is a big reason LFP became the residential default despite its slightly lower energy density. But even LFP is not immune — it still ages on both clocks, just more slowly, and the calendar floor is still there.

The reason this two-clock reality catches people off guard is that it contradicts how we think about most machines. A car engine, a furnace, a washing machine — these wear out mainly from use, and using them less genuinely extends their life. Batteries are different because they're not really machines; they're chemistry in a box, and chemistry proceeds whether or not you're drawing on it. An unused battery isn't in stasis, waiting patiently for you to need it. It's slowly, continuously changing at the molecular level, and by the time you do need it in year eight or nine, it has already given up some of the capacity you paid for. This is why the marketing instinct to buy a big backup battery and "save it" for emergencies is subtly self-defeating: the battery you're saving is aging on the calendar clock the entire time it sits reserved, so a pack you barely touch for a decade may deliver noticeably less than its rated backup capacity when the outage finally comes. Knowing that reframes the buying decision — you're not purchasing a fixed reserve you can bank indefinitely, you're purchasing a slowly depreciating one, and the value you get from it is highest in its early years.

## Temperature is the accelerator on both clocks

If there's one variable that deserves more attention than cycle count, it's heat. Temperature accelerates nearly every chemical reaction inside a battery, which means it speeds up *both* clocks — calendar aging especially. A battery kept consistently warm ages faster sitting still than an identical battery kept cool ages while cycling daily. This is why the single most consequential decision an owner makes after purchase often has nothing to do with how they operate the battery and everything to do with where they mount it. A pack baking against a south-facing garage wall in a hot climate, routinely sitting at 100°F+ ambient in summer, is on a materially shorter calendar-aging trajectory than the same pack in a climate-controlled basement holding a steady 65 to 70°F. Cold has its own penalties — most batteries throttle charge and discharge rates in freezing temperatures to avoid damage, which reduces available power in winter — but sustained heat is the more corrosive enemy for long-term capacity.

State of charge interacts with temperature to make calendar aging worse or better. A battery that sits at 100% charge for long stretches, especially while warm, ages faster on the calendar clock than one that rests at a moderate charge level. This is the mechanism behind the "storage charge" recommendations you see for devices meant to sit unused, and it's why some home battery systems, if you tell them the pack is mostly for backup, will deliberately hold it at a partial reserve rather than topping it to full and leaving it there. If your battery spends most of its life waiting for an outage, keeping it near full at all times maximizes available backup energy but also maximizes calendar aging — a genuine trade-off between having every kilowatt-hour ready and preserving capacity over the years. There's no universally right answer; it depends on how much you value guaranteed backup depth versus long-term capacity retention.

The practical implications fall out cleanly once you hold both clocks in view. When you size a battery, plan for the capacity you'll want *years from now*, not just on installation day, because both clocks will have shaved something off the rating — a common rule is to expect a pack to hold roughly 70% of its original usable capacity by the end of its warranted life, and to size so that even the aged capacity still covers your real needs. A [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) is the easy way to test whether a given capacity still covers your essential loads once you've discounted it for years of expected fade. When you place the battery, treat temperature as a first-order decision: an indoor, climate-moderated location can add years to the calendar clock compared with a hot exterior wall, and that placement choice is often free or nearly so at install time but impossible to undo cheaply later. When you read a warranty, notice which clock it's actually protecting against — many home battery warranties specify *both* a term in years and a throughput or cycle limit, and the guarantee expires at whichever you hit first. A backup-focused owner who never comes close to the cycle limit is still bounded by the calendar term, so the years figure is the one that matters to them; a heavy-cycling arbitrage user may hit the throughput cap first. Reading [a battery warranty's fine print](/blog/solar-battery-warranty-terms/) with the two-clock model in mind tells you which limit you'll actually reach, and therefore what protection you're really buying.

None of this means a home battery is a bad buy — it means a battery is a consumable asset with a finite life on two axes, and knowing that lets you plan honestly. The pack you install today will be weaker in ten years no matter how gently you treat it, and it will be weaker still if you cycle it hard or cook it in the heat. Size for the aged number, mount it somewhere cool, and match how you operate it to what you actually need it for, and you'll get the full life the chemistry is capable of rather than discovering the two clocks the hard way.
