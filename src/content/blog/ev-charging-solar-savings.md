Every mile you drive costs something to fuel, and the interesting exercise is to price that mile three ways: filled from a gas pump, filled from the grid, and filled from panels on your own roof. The three numbers are far enough apart that the comparison, once you run it, reframes what an EV paired with solar is actually worth. The gap between the second and third numbers — grid power versus self-produced power — is where the specific savings of charging an electric car with solar panels live.

## Three ways to price a mile

Start with a gas car getting 30 miles per gallon. Assume gasoline at $3.50 a gallon — plug in your own regional price — and each mile costs about 11.7 cents to fuel. That's the baseline every EV comparison runs against.

Now the EV. Take the same 0.30 kWh per mile at the wall used for sizing an array, and price the electricity two ways. On grid power at an assumed $0.17 per kWh, a mile costs about 5.1 cents — already less than half the gasoline figure. On home solar, the relevant price isn't a utility rate at all; it's the *lifetime cost of a kilowatt-hour your own system produces*, which is the total system cost spread across all the energy it makes over its life. That figure commonly works out somewhere around $0.08 to $0.13 per kWh depending on install price and sun, so call it $0.11 for illustration. At that rate, a mile costs about 3.3 cents.

| Fuel source | Assumed price | Cost per mile |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gasoline (30 mpg) | $3.50 / gallon | ~11.7¢ |
| Grid electricity | $0.17 / kWh | ~5.1¢ |
| Home solar | ~$0.11 / kWh | ~3.3¢ |

The headline: driving on your own solar tends to land around 3–4 cents a mile, roughly a third of the gasoline cost and meaningfully under grid charging. Over 12,000 miles a year, the swing from gasoline to solar is on the order of a thousand dollars annually, using these placeholder prices.

## Why a self-produced kilowatt-hour is so cheap

The reason solar charging beats even cheap grid power is that you've already paid for the panels. Once a system is installed, the sunshine is free and the marginal cost of the next kilowatt-hour is essentially zero — the only real cost is the upfront investment amortized across decades of production. Spread a system's price over the roughly 25 years and hundreds of thousands of kilowatt-hours it will generate, and each one comes out to those single-digit cents. You're not buying electricity anymore; you're collecting on a purchase you already made.

That's the same logic behind [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) on the rest of your home's load. The EV just adds a large, predictable, year-round appliance to the pile of things that free power now covers — which is exactly what makes cars and solar such a natural pairing.

## The timing asterisk that decides how much you keep

There's a catch, and it's the same one that shadows all solar savings: the sun shines midday, but most people charge at night. Whether that mismatch costs you depends on your utility's rules.

Under full retail net metering, timing is a non-issue. Your midday surplus banks credits at the retail rate, and the car draws them back overnight at the same rate — the 3.3-cents-a-mile figure holds. Where exports pay less than retail, the picture changes: the surplus you exported by day earned less than the grid power you buy back to charge at night, so overnight charging quietly reverts toward the grid cost per mile rather than the solar one. The fix is to charge during daylight when you can — many EVs and chargers can be scheduled for a midday top-up — or to store the surplus in a battery for the evening plug-in.

Time-of-use rates add a sharper wrinkle: charging during a peak evening window can cost several times the off-peak rate, so *when* you plug in swings the math hard. [Time-of-use rates and solar](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/) covers how to read those windows and schedule around them, which for an EV owner is often worth more than any other single adjustment.

## The fine print behind the pennies

A cost-per-mile figure this clean invites a few honest asterisks, none of which overturn the conclusion but all of which sharpen it.

Charging isn't perfectly efficient, and neither is a parked car. Some energy is lost as heat while charging, and an EV sips a little power just sitting still, keeping its systems awake. Both nudge the real kilowatt-hours per mile above the battery-rated figure, which is why the math above priced energy at the wall rather than at the battery. Cold weather widens the gap further: winter battery heating and cabin warmth can push consumption up by a third or more for months, so a northern driver's per-mile cost climbs seasonally even on free solar — the mile still costs pennies, just a few more of them.

The comparison also isolates *fuel* cost and deliberately sets capital aside. The gasoline figure ignores the pump because you don't buy the pump; the solar figure already folds the array's cost into that $0.11 lifetime rate, but it doesn't separately charge you for the home charger, which is a one-time install expense on the EV side of the ledger. Fold that in when you compare total ownership rather than fuel alone.

One more lever hides in plain sight: the savings scale directly with mileage. A 6,000-mile-a-year driver banks half the annual fuel saving of a 15,000-mile commuter, simply because there are fewer miles to move onto cheap power. High-mileage households are where the EV-plus-solar pairing pays back fastest, and low-mileage ones where it matters least. None of these asterisks changes the shape of the result — home solar stays the cheapest of the three ways to move a mile, usually by a wide margin. They just keep the number honest: a well-founded estimate on labeled assumptions, not a promise your dashboard will match to the cent.

## What it does to your overall payback

Adding an EV to a solar household usually improves the system's return, and the reason is structural. Solar's payback is a race between what you spent and what you avoid spending; an EV enlarges the second figure by moving a big chunk of transportation cost off the gas pump and onto free rooftop power. More of your generation gets used at that high-value rate instead of exported for a modest credit, which is precisely the self-consumption that makes a system pay faster.
