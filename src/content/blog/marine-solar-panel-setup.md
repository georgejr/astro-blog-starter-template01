A boat at anchor is one of the purest off-grid problems there is. There's no shore power, no generator you want to run all day, and a house battery bank that has to keep the lights, the fridge, the electronics, and the water pump alive on nothing but what you can capture from the sky. Solar handles this job beautifully when it's sized to the actual loads and wired to survive the marine environment, and it fails frustratingly when it's bolted on as an afterthought. The difference between those two outcomes comes down to a few decisions made before anything gets mounted: how much energy your boat really uses in a day, how much panel it takes to replace that in real cruising conditions, and how the whole system is protected from salt, motion, and shade that a house rooftop never has to deal with.

The right way in is to treat the boat exactly like a small off-grid house, because electrically that's what it is. You build the system from the load list outward — you figure out what draws power and for how long, you size a battery bank to carry that through the night and the cloudy days, and only then do you size the array to refill the bank each day. That load-first logic is the same discipline behind [sizing an off-grid solar system from your actual load list](/blog/off-grid-solar-system-sizing/), and skipping it is the single most common reason a marine solar setup underperforms. Panels sized to a guess almost always come up short, because the real draws on a boat — especially refrigeration — are larger and steadier than people expect.

## Start with the amp-hours your boat actually burns

Marine electrical systems are usually measured in amp-hours at 12 volts rather than kilowatt-hours, and getting comfortable with that unit is the first step, because every marine battery, charge controller, and load is spec'd that way. The exercise is to list every DC load on the boat, estimate how many amps it draws and how many hours a day it runs, and multiply to get amp-hours per day. The refrigerator or freezer is almost always the dominant load — a marine fridge can cycle through 30 to 60 amp-hours a day on its own depending on size, insulation, and how warm the water and air are — followed by cabin and navigation lighting, the water pressure pump, instruments and chartplotter, phone and laptop charging, and the slow parasitic draw of anything left in standby. Add an autopilot, radar, or watermaker and the daily total climbs fast. For a lot of cruising sailboats living at anchor, the honest number lands somewhere around 100 amp-hours a day, and for a bigger boat with more refrigeration and electronics it can be well beyond that.

That daily figure is the foundation for everything else, so it's worth being generous rather than optimistic. On a boat, the cost of undersizing isn't an annoyance — it's a dead bank at 2 a.m. and a fridge full of spoiling food, or a diesel engine you have to run just to charge, which is the exact chore solar is supposed to eliminate. Measure real draws with a battery monitor if you have one rather than trusting nameplate ratings, because a fridge's duty cycle in the tropics looks nothing like its rating on a bench. Once you have a defensible amp-hours-per-day number, you can size the battery bank to hold enough to run overnight and buffer a gray day or two without deep-cycling the bank flat every night, and you can size the array to replace that daily draw during the hours the sun is actually useful.

Sizing the bank is its own decision and it interacts with the panels. A house bank is typically sized so that a normal day's draw is a modest fraction of its capacity, which keeps the batteries off their deepest, most damaging discharges and gives you reserve for cloudy stretches. If your boat burns 100 amp-hours a day and you want to avoid routinely pulling the bank below half, you're looking at a bank measured in the hundreds of amp-hours, and the chemistry choice shapes that: lithium iron phosphate banks tolerate deeper discharge and far more cycles than the flooded or AGM lead-acid banks that dominated boats for decades, which is why so many cruisers have switched despite the higher upfront cost. You can pressure-test how a given daily draw maps onto a battery capacity and how many days of autonomy it buys you with the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/), translating your amp-hour load into the storage you actually need before you commit to an array size.

The system voltage question comes up here too, and it's more consequential on a boat than people expect. Traditional marine electrical systems run at 12 volts, which is simple and compatible with everything, but as loads and array sizes grow, the currents at 12 volts get large enough that wiring gets heavy and voltage drop becomes a real loss. Larger boats increasingly run house systems at 24 volts, or use higher-voltage arrays feeding a charge controller that steps down to a 12-volt bank, precisely to keep those currents manageable. The trade-offs between 12, 24, and higher-voltage architectures — wire size, component availability, and how well the system scales — play out the same way on a boat as anywhere off-grid, and they're worth understanding before you commit, as laid out in [choosing a system voltage that scales](/blog/12v-vs-24v-vs-48v-solar-systems/). For a small boat with modest loads, 12 volts is usually the pragmatic answer; for a heavily loaded cruiser, higher voltage saves copper and grief.

## Sizing the array for real conditions, not lab numbers

Once you know your daily amp-hours and your bank, the array size falls out of a simple question: how much panel does it take to put that many amp-hours back into the battery during a real cruising day? The trap is assuming panels produce their rated wattage all day. They don't. A panel's nameplate is measured under ideal lab conditions, and on a boat the real-world haircut is even steeper than on a rooftop, because panels lie flat or at whatever angle the deck offers rather than tilted toward the sun, they run hot in tropical heat which cuts output, they get shaded by the boom, sails, rigging, and dodger at various times of day, and the boat swings on its anchor so the array's orientation relative to the sun is constantly changing. A realistic rule of thumb is to expect a given panel to return only a portion of what its rating and the day's sun hours would suggest, and to size the array with that derating baked in rather than discovering the shortfall on a cloudy week in an anchorage.

In practice this means a boat that draws 100 amp-hours a day usually wants more panel than a naive calculation implies. Where a rooftop designer might assume four or five productive sun-hours and near-optimal tilt, a boat designer should assume less usable production per rated watt and plan accordingly, which is how modest-sounding daily loads end up calling for a few hundred watts of panel. Many cruising sailboats end up in the 200-to-400-watt range for a comfortable anchor-out lifestyle, with heavily loaded boats going higher, and the ones that are happy tend to be the ones that oversized rather than cut it close. The [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) is a useful way to translate a target daily energy replacement into a panel wattage, and the discipline of building from the load rather than from available deck space is the same one that makes [a first RV solar setup](/blog/rv-solar-starter-sizing/) succeed or fail — boats and RVs share almost identical sizing logic, just with salt water instead of asphalt.

Where you physically mount the panels shapes both the size you need and how well it performs. Rigid panels on a stern arch or a bimini frame are the gold standard for a cruising boat, because they sit up high and relatively unshaded, they're clear of foot traffic, and they can be angled somewhat toward the sun. Semi-flexible panels bonded to a cabin top or bimini fabric are lighter and lower-profile but tend to run hotter and produce less, and they're more prone to shading from the boom and crew moving around the deck. Whatever the mount, shade is the enemy that behaves worst on a boat: the rig throws moving shadows across panels all day, and a single shaded panel wired in series with others can drag down the whole string far out of proportion to the shaded area. This is why marine arrays are often wired to tolerate partial shading — parallel connections or per-panel controllers so that one shaded panel doesn't collapse the output of the rest — and why placement that keeps panels clear of the boom's shadow through the day is worth fighting for. A charge controller with maximum power point tracking is standard for anything but the smallest setups, because it squeezes meaningfully more out of the panels across the variable voltage and temperature a boat sees than a simpler controller can.

Protecting the system from the marine environment is the part that separates a setup that lasts from one that corrodes into failure in a couple of seasons. Salt air attacks every electrical connection, so marine-grade tinned copper wire, sealed and corrosion-resistant connectors, and generous strain relief are not optional — bare copper in a bilge or an exposed junction on deck will green over and fail. Everything has to be mounted to survive constant motion, pounding, and the occasional wave over the deck, which means fasteners that won't work loose, panels rated and secured for the loads a moving boat imposes, and wiring runs protected from chafe where they pass through bulkheads or along the hull. Fusing and a proper disconnect between the array and the bank are safety essentials, not niceties, because a fault in a marine DC system near fuel and enclosed spaces is a serious hazard. Done right, a marine solar array is close to maintenance-free — an occasional rinse of salt off the glass and a check of the connections — and it turns a boat at anchor into a place you can live comfortably for days without touching the engine, which is the entire point of putting solar on a boat in the first place.
