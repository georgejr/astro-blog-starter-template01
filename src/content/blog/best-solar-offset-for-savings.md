Almost every solar shopper starts with the same instinct: build a system big enough to cover 100% of the electricity you use, wipe out the bill entirely, and call it a win. It's a tidy, satisfying target, and under the right utility rules it's exactly right. But "100% offset" is a sizing goal, not a savings goal, and those two things only line up when your utility pays you full retail value for the power you send back to the grid. The moment your utility pays *less* than retail for exports — which is increasingly the norm — the math tilts, and the system that saves you the most dollars is often a little smaller than the one that offsets every last kilowatt-hour. Understanding why the best offset percentage isn't automatically 100% is the difference between right-sizing for savings and overbuilding out of habit.

The confusion starts with what "offset" even means, because people use the word two different ways. One meaning is *energy offset*: your panels produce, over a year, as many kilowatt-hours as your home consumes, so on an annual net basis you generated 100% of your usage. The other meaning is *bill offset*: your electric bill actually goes to zero. Those are the same thing only under full-retail net metering, where every exported kilowatt-hour is credited at the same price you'd pay to buy one, so a kilowatt-hour banked at noon perfectly cancels a kilowatt-hour drawn at night. Break that one-to-one crediting and the two meanings diverge — you can produce 100% of your annual energy and still owe money, because the cheap credits you earned exporting midday don't fully pay for the expensive power you buy after dark. The whole question of the best offset percentage lives in that gap, and our piece on [what a 100% offset actually means for your bill](/blog/100-percent-solar-offset/) unpacks the distinction in detail; this article is about what to do with it.

## Why the last panels save less than the first

Think about the panels on your roof as a stack ranked by how their output gets used, because they're not all equally valuable. The energy your system produces during the day splits into two fates: some is consumed on the spot by your house — the fridge, the AC, whatever's running — and the rest is surplus that flows out to the grid. Self-consumed energy is worth the full retail rate to you, because every kilowatt-hour you use as you make it is a kilowatt-hour you didn't buy. Exported energy is worth only whatever your utility pays for exports, which under modern rules is frequently well below retail. So the value of a kilowatt-hour of production depends entirely on whether it gets used at home or shipped to the grid, and that ratio shifts as you add panels.

The first panels you install are the most valuable, because their midday output is largely soaked up by your home's daytime baseline load — it's self-consumed at full retail value. As you keep adding panels, you eventually produce more at midday than the house can use, and the *additional* production from those later panels increasingly spills onto the grid as export. Those last panels, the ones that push you from 85% offset to 100%, produce energy that mostly leaves the house and earns only the export rate. If that export rate is, say, half of retail, the last panels are saving you half as much per kilowatt-hour as the first ones did. They still save *something* — exports aren't worthless — but their marginal value is lower, and at some point the savings from the next panel no longer justify its cost. That declining marginal value is the core reason 100% offset can overshoot the savings-maximizing point.

Where exactly the sweet spot lands depends on your export compensation, and this is where you have to know your utility's rules rather than reach for a national rule of thumb. Under **full-retail net metering**, there's no penalty for exporting — a banked kilowatt-hour is worth a bought one — so the last panels retain their full value and sizing to 100% offset (sometimes even a touch above, to cover degradation over time) is genuinely optimal. Under **net billing or a below-retail export rate**, the last panels' output is discounted, and the dollar-maximizing offset typically falls somewhere short of 100% — often in the neighborhood of 80% to 90% for a home with a modest daytime load, though the exact figure swings with how much power your house naturally uses during sunlight hours. A home that runs heavy daytime loads — someone working from home, a pool pump cycling at noon, an EV that charges in the driveway during the day — self-consumes more of its production and can justify a larger system before the export discount bites. A home that's empty and quiet all day exports more of everything and hits the diminishing-returns wall sooner. The single most important input, then, is your export rate, and understanding how utilities set and structure that rate is worth the read in our overview of [what your utility pays for extra power](/blog/solar-export-rates/) before you settle on a system size.

None of this means "smaller is always better." It means the savings-maximizing size is set by economics, not by the neat symmetry of covering 100% of usage. Overshoot that point and you're paying full price for panels whose output you'll sell back at a discount, which stretches your payback rather than improving it. Undershoot it badly and you're leaving cheap, self-consumed savings on the table. The goal is to size to the point where the marginal panel's real value — mostly export at your export rate, once the house is covered — still beats its cost. For most homes under below-retail export rules, that lands a bit under full offset, which is exactly why the reflexive "just cover 100%" advice can quietly cost you money.

## Sizing to the money, not the meter

Turning this principle into an actual system size is a matter of running your own numbers, because the sweet spot is genuinely personal — it depends on your usage shape, your export rate, and the cost of the marginal panels. The productive way to approach it is to model a few offset levels and compare their returns rather than their sizes. Take your annual usage and price out a system at, say, 100% offset, then at 90%, then at 80%, and look at what each does to your total lifetime savings and payback period, not just your first-year bill. Under full-retail net metering, you'll usually find the returns keep improving right up to 100% (and the answer is simple: size to your usage). Under net billing, you'll often find the return peaks somewhere short of full offset and then flattens or declines as the export-discounted panels drag the average value down. The [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) is built for exactly this kind of comparison — vary the system size, hold your rate and export assumptions steady, and watch where the payback and lifetime savings actually optimize rather than assuming the biggest system wins.

## How a battery moves the sweet spot upward

The single biggest thing that changes the offset answer isn't a panel decision at all — it's whether you add storage, because a battery reshapes the whole calculation from the ground up. A battery lets you store your midday surplus and use it at night instead of exporting it cheaply and rebuying it at retail, which effectively converts would-be exports into self-consumption. That raises the value of the "last panels" — the ones that were spilling to the grid — because now their output gets stored and used at full retail value rather than sold at a discount. So a home with a battery under below-retail export rules can justify a larger system, closer to or at 100% offset, than the same home without one, because the export penalty that capped the sweet spot no longer applies to the stored energy.

The reason this works is that the sweet spot was never really about panel count — it was about the value of the marginal kilowatt-hour, and the battery raises that value for any energy that would otherwise have left the house cheaply. Once the surplus has somewhere to go besides a low-paying export meter, the diminishing-returns curve that pulled the optimum down toward 80% or 90% flattens back out, and additional panels keep earning close to full retail value up to the point where you're generating more than even a battery-buffered home can absorb across a day. Whether the battery itself pays for that privilege is a separate question — storage is a significant cost, and it has to earn its keep on the export-versus-retail spread across enough daily throughput to justify the price. But the sizing interaction is unambiguous: storage moves the optimal offset upward by turning exports back into self-use. If you're weighing a battery, size the array with and without it, because the right offset genuinely differs between the two cases, and locking in a smaller "net-billing-optimized" array today can leave you undersized the day a battery goes in.

The failure mode to avoid on the other side is oversizing well past the sweet spot in pursuit of a bigger number, a "future-proof" system, or the emotional appeal of a zeroed-out bill. Building substantially more than your savings-maximizing size means each extra panel produces energy you'll mostly export at a discount, so you pay the full installed cost of those panels and recover only the reduced export value — a combination that lengthens payback and lowers your return on the whole system. The dynamics of that overbuild penalty are laid out in our look at [when extra panels slow your payback](/blog/oversized-solar-system-payback/), and they're the mirror image of the diminishing-returns story: past a certain size, more panels are actively worse for your savings under below-retail export rules. The honest exception is anticipated future load — if you're genuinely about to add an EV or a heat pump, sizing for that coming usage is prudent, because those loads will consume the extra production on-site rather than exporting it. But sizing beyond even your foreseeable usage, just to say the system covers 110% of today's bill, is spending money to sell power cheaply.

To actually land on a size, the practical sequence is straightforward. Establish your annual kWh from a full year of bills, confirm how your utility credits exports, and then translate a target offset into a panel count with the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool — but treat that panel count as a starting point to be optimized against savings, not a fixed answer. If you're on full-retail net metering, aim for roughly 100% of usage and stop worrying about it; the symmetry works in your favor. If you're on net billing or a low export rate and you don't have a battery, model a couple of sizes a bit under full offset and let the return, not the round number, pick the winner — for many such homes that lands in the 80% to 90% range. And if you're adding storage, push the size back up, because the battery restores the value of the production that would otherwise have leaked to the grid. The best offset percentage isn't a universal figure; it's the one where the last panel you add still earns its keep, and finding it is the whole reason to run your own numbers instead of chasing a tidy 100%.
