Your solar panels produce two things at once. The first is obvious: electricity, which powers your home and shrinks your bill. The second is invisible and, in a handful of states, sellable: a certificate attesting that the electricity was made from sunlight rather than gas or coal. That certificate is a Solar Renewable Energy Certificate — an SREC — and where a market exists for it, it can turn your roof into a small, separate income stream on top of the power savings.

The catch, and the reason SRECs confuse people, is that they're a policy invention, not a physical thing. Their value comes entirely from a state law creating demand for them, which means an SREC is worth a lot in one state, nothing in the next, and an unpredictable amount from one year to the next in the same state. Understanding where that value comes from is the difference between treating SRECs as a bonus and mistaking them for a guarantee.

## What an SREC is, separate from your electricity

An SREC represents the environmental attribute of your solar generation — the "greenness" — split off from the electrons themselves. When your system generates power, you keep the power (as bill savings or export credit) and, separately, you earn a certificate proving that generation was solar. The two are unbundled: you can sell the certificate to someone who needs it while still using or exporting the actual electricity yourself.

Who needs a certificate you can't plug anything into? Utilities and electricity suppliers, because a state law makes them. That's the whole engine, and it's worth seeing plainly before the numbers.

## How you earn them: one per megawatt-hour

The earning rule is refreshingly simple. You earn one SREC for every megawatt-hour — 1,000 kilowatt-hours — of electricity your system generates. Generation, not export: it counts every kWh your panels make, including the power you consume yourself, because the certificate is about production, not what you did with it.

A rough sense of scale: a typical residential system might generate somewhere between 6 and 12 megawatt-hours a year depending on its size and location, so it earns roughly 6 to 12 SRECs annually. To track and credit this, systems in SREC states register with a certificate-tracking program and usually report generation through a production meter or monitoring. If you're not sure how many megawatt-hours your roof would actually make, that's the input everything else hangs on — the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) estimates annual generation for your system size and region, which is also your SREC count.

## Why the price swings so much

Here's where SRECs stop being simple. Their price is set by a supply-and-demand market, and both sides are driven by policy rather than anything stable.

Demand comes from a state's Renewable Portfolio Standard — a law requiring electricity suppliers to source a rising percentage of their power from solar each year. To prove compliance, suppliers must buy and retire SRECs. If they fall short, they pay a penalty called the Solar Alternative Compliance Payment, which effectively sets a ceiling on what an SREC is worth: no supplier pays more for a certificate than the fine for not having one.

Supply comes from every solar owner in the state generating certificates. And here's the instability: as more people install solar, more SRECs flood the market, and if supply outruns the rising demand the RPS creates, prices fall — sometimes sharply. States with aggressive solar adoption have watched SREC prices drop substantially as the market oversupplied. This is why quoting a current SREC price is a fool's errand; the honest statement is that prices are volatile, set by the gap between a legislated demand curve and however much solar gets built. Treat any specific dollar figure you're shown as a snapshot that may not survive the year.

## Selling them: brokers, aggregators, and contracts

You don't sell SRECs to a utility directly. A few paths connect a homeowner's handful of certificates to the buyers who need them.

- **Spot market through an aggregator.** A broker pools certificates from many homeowners and sells them at the going market price, taking a cut. You get whatever the market pays each period — higher upside, no certainty.
- **A fixed-price contract.** You lock in a set price per SREC for a term of years, trading potential upside for predictability. In a falling market this protects you; in a rising one you leave money on the table.
- **Upfront sale.** Some programs let you sell a block of future SRECs for a single lump payment, converting the uncertain stream into cash now at a discount.

Which suits you is a risk question, not a math one — whether you'd rather have a stable smaller number or a variable larger one. Whatever you choose, SREC income is one more layer in the broader project of [stacking solar incentives](/blog/stacking-solar-incentives/), and it stacks on top of the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit rather than replacing it.

## Where SRECs exist — and where they don't

The single most important fact about SRECs is that they're geographically rare. Only a limited set of states have RPS solar carve-outs strong enough to create a real SREC market, and the details differ sharply among them. New Jersey built one of the most active markets in the country, though it has [restructured how its solar certificates work](/blog/solar-panels-in-new-jersey/) over time. Neighboring Pennsylvania has a market too, but a [softer, lower-priced one](/blog/solar-panels-in-pennsylvania/) that illustrates how much the specific program design matters. A few other states run their own versions under different names.

In most of the country there is no SREC market at all, and your panels' certificates simply have no buyer. That's not a defect in your system — it's the absence of a state law creating demand. If you live in an SREC state, the income is a genuine and sometimes substantial addition to solar's return. If you don't, no amount of generation creates a market that isn't there.

## FAQ

**How much can SRECs actually add to my return?**
It depends entirely on your state's market price and your system's generation, and prices move constantly. In a strong market SRECs can meaningfully shorten payback; in a weak one they add little. Model them as a variable bonus, not a fixed line, and if you want to see how sensitive your return is to them, run your numbers through a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) with and without SREC income.

**Are SRECs the same as net metering?**
No. Net metering credits the electricity you export to the grid against your bill. SRECs pay you for the environmental attribute of generation, separately, and you can earn both where both exist.

**Do I still own the power if I sell the SREC?**
Yes. Selling the certificate transfers only the "green attribute" claim. You keep the actual electricity to use or export as usual.

**Are SREC earnings taxable?**
SREC income may be treated as taxable income, and rules can vary — this is a question for a tax professional familiar with your state, not something to assume either way.

**Will the market still exist in ten years?**
Uncertain. SREC markets depend on state RPS laws, which legislatures can and do revise. Don't build your entire solar case on SREC income continuing unchanged for the life of the system; treat a long income stream as plausible but not promised.
