Your solar panels produce two things at once, and most owners only ever notice one of them. The first is obvious: electricity, which runs your home and shrinks your bill. The second is invisible and, in a handful of states, genuinely sellable — a certificate attesting that the electricity was made from sunlight rather than from gas or coal. That certificate is a Solar Renewable Energy Certificate, an SREC, and where a market for it exists, it can turn your roof into a small, separate income stream sitting on top of the power savings you already expected. Two homes with identical panels can have very different total returns depending on nothing but which side of a state line they sit on, because one has an SREC market and the other doesn't.

The catch, and the reason SRECs confuse almost everyone who first hears about them, is that they aren't a physical thing. An SREC is a policy invention, and its value comes entirely from a state law that manufactures demand for it. That single fact drives everything strange about SRECs: one is worth real money in a state that requires them, worth exactly nothing in the next state over that doesn't, and worth an unpredictable, swinging amount from one year to the next even within the same state. Understanding where that value comes from — and, just as importantly, where it comes from *nothing* — is the difference between treating SREC income as a welcome bonus and mistaking it for a guarantee you can bank the whole project on.

## What an SREC is, and how you earn one

An SREC represents the environmental attribute of your solar generation — the "greenness" of it — split cleanly away from the electrons themselves. When your system generates power, two separate things happen. You keep the power, using it in your home or exporting it for a bill credit, exactly as you would without any of this. And separately, you earn a certificate that proves the generation was solar. The two are unbundled, which is the whole conceptual trick: you can sell the certificate to someone who needs proof of solar generation while still using or exporting the actual electricity yourself. Selling the SREC transfers only the green-attribute claim; it does not touch the power. You keep every kilowatt-hour to use or export as usual, and the buyer gets a piece of paper — increasingly, a database entry — that they can't plug anything into.

An analogy helps the unbundling click, because it's the part that trips people up. Think of your solar generation as producing two separate goods from one act. The first good is the electricity itself, which is like any commodity — you use it or you sell it, and once it's spent it's gone. The second good is a claim, a documented assertion that clean energy was produced, and that claim can be traded entirely apart from the power it describes. It's closer to a receipt than to a product: it proves something happened. A company that wants to say it sourced solar energy, or is legally required to, needs receipts, and your SREC is one. You've already used or exported the actual electricity; what you're selling is the documented proof that it was solar, which the buyer needs for their own compliance and you have no other use for. Nothing physical changes hands, and nothing about your own power supply is affected by the sale.

So who on earth would pay for a certificate you can't use to run a toaster? Utilities and electricity suppliers, and only because a state law forces them to. That compulsion is the entire engine behind SRECs, and it's worth seeing plainly before any dollar figures enter the picture, because it explains why the whole market is as fragile and as geographically patchy as it is. Remove the law and the certificate instantly becomes worthless, since nobody has a voluntary reason to buy the abstract greenness of your roof's output. The demand is legislated into existence, which means it can also be legislated back out. That's not a hypothetical worry, either — states have opened, capped, closed, and redesigned these programs repeatedly, so the same law that creates your income stream is a law that can be amended by a future legislature with different priorities.

The earning rule itself is refreshingly simple, and it's the one number in this whole topic that stays put. You earn one SREC for every megawatt-hour — that's 1,000 kilowatt-hours — of electricity your system generates. Crucially, it's generation, not export: the certificate counts every kilowatt-hour your panels make, including the large share you consume yourself inside the house, because the certificate is about the fact of solar production, not about what you did with the power afterward. A panel that runs your air conditioner all afternoon earns you the same credit toward an SREC as a panel that exports to the grid, since both generated the same clean energy. For a rough sense of scale, a typical residential system might generate somewhere between 6 and 12 megawatt-hours a year depending on its size and location, so it earns very roughly 6 to 12 SRECs annually — a handful of certificates, not a fortune's worth, which is part of why SREC income is best thought of as a bonus layer rather than the core of your return. To track all this and credit you properly, systems in SREC states register with a certificate-tracking program and generally report their generation through a production meter or the system's monitoring. If you don't yet know how many megawatt-hours your particular roof would actually produce, that's the input everything else depends on, because your SREC count is simply your annual megawatt-hours — the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) estimates annual generation for your system size and region, and that estimate doubles as your rough annual SREC tally.

## Why the price swings, and how you sell them

Here is where SRECs stop being simple and start being genuinely unpredictable. The price of an SREC is set by a supply-and-demand market, and both sides of that market are driven by policy rather than by anything stable or physical, which is why the price moves the way it does. Demand comes from a state's Renewable Portfolio Standard — a law requiring electricity suppliers to source a rising percentage of their power from solar each year. To prove they've complied, suppliers have to buy and retire SRECs, one for each megawatt-hour of solar they're required to account for. If they fall short of the requirement, they pay a penalty called the Solar Alternative Compliance Payment, and that penalty quietly sets a ceiling on what any SREC can be worth: no rational supplier pays more for a certificate than the fine for going without one. So the compliance payment caps the upside, and the rising annual requirement is what keeps demand growing in the first place.

Supply, meanwhile, comes from every solar owner in the state generating certificates, and that's the source of the instability. As more people install solar, more SRECs flood into the market, and if that supply outruns the demand the RPS is creating, prices fall — sometimes sharply and quickly. States that experienced aggressive solar adoption have watched their SREC prices drop substantially as the market oversupplied itself, turning what looked like a rich income stream at signing into a thin one a few years later. This is precisely why quoting a current SREC price is a fool's errand and why you should be suspicious of anyone who does it confidently. The honest statement is that SREC prices are volatile, determined by the shifting gap between a legislated demand curve and however much solar the state's homeowners happen to build. Treat any specific dollar figure you're shown as a snapshot of one moment that may not survive the year, let alone the 25-year life of your panels.

There's a practical texture to earning this income that the theory skips over, and it's worth picturing before you count on it. Your system generates certificates continuously as it produces power, but they're issued, tracked, and sold in batches through the registry rather than trickling in daily, so the money tends to arrive as periodic payments — a check or deposit every so often as your accumulated certificates clear the market — rather than as a smooth monthly credit like a bill offset. That timing matters for how you think about the income: it's lumpier and less predictable than the steady bill savings solar delivers, which reinforces treating it as a variable bonus rather than a reliable monthly line you budget around. Your system also has to be certified and registered in the state's program to earn certificates at all, a step your installer usually handles at commissioning but one worth confirming was actually done, since an uncertified system in an SREC state simply generates power without generating the certificates you were counting on.

Given that volatility, how you sell your certificates becomes a genuine risk decision rather than a technicality, because you don't sell SRECs to a utility directly — a few different paths connect your handful of certificates to the buyers who need them, and each one handles the price risk differently. The most common is the spot market through an aggregator, where a broker pools certificates from many homeowners and sells them at whatever the market pays each period, taking a cut for the service; you get the full upside when prices are high and the full pain when they're low, with no certainty either way. The alternative is a fixed-price contract, where you lock in a set price per SREC for a term of years, trading away potential upside in exchange for predictability — in a falling market that contract protects you handsomely, while in a rising one you're left watching money you could have had go to someone else. A third option, offered by some programs, is an upfront sale that lets you sell a block of your future SRECs for a single lump payment now, converting the whole uncertain stream into cash today at a discount. Which of these suits you is a question about your appetite for risk, not a math problem with a right answer: you're choosing between a stable smaller number and a variable, potentially larger one. Whatever you settle on, SREC income is one more layer in the broader project of [stacking solar incentives](/blog/stacking-solar-incentives/), and it genuinely stacks — it sits on top of the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit rather than replacing it or competing with it, so an SREC-state homeowner can claim the federal credit and sell certificates both.

## Where SRECs exist, where they don't, and the fine print

The single most important fact about SRECs, the one that determines whether any of the above matters to you at all, is that they're geographically rare. Only a limited set of states have RPS solar carve-outs strong enough to create a real, liquid SREC market, and even among those the details differ sharply. New Jersey built one of the most active markets in the country, though it has [restructured how its solar certificates work](/blog/solar-panels-in-new-jersey/) more than once over the years, which is itself a lesson in how much these programs can change under you. Neighboring Pennsylvania has a market too, but a [softer, lower-priced one](/blog/solar-panels-in-pennsylvania/), a useful illustration of how much the specific program design — the size of the carve-out, the level of the compliance payment — determines whether a certificate is worth real money or spare change. A few other states run their own versions under different names and rules. Across most of the country, though, there is simply no SREC market at all, and your panels' certificates have no buyer whatsoever. That's not a defect in your system or a sign you did something wrong; it's just the absence of a state law creating demand. If you live in an SREC state, the income is a genuine and sometimes substantial addition to solar's return. If you don't, no amount of generation conjures a market that the legislature never created.

Figuring out which situation you're in isn't hard, but it does take a moment of care, because this is exactly the spot where a salesperson's incentives and yours diverge. Checking whether your state has an active SREC market — and, just as important, what certificates in that market have actually been trading for lately rather than what they traded for at the program's peak — is worth doing before any number enters your mental math. A rep quoting you years of rich SREC income is quoting a market price that may have already collapsed under oversupply, or a program that's since been capped or restructured, and folding that optimistic figure into your payback estimate is how a genuine bonus quietly becomes the load-bearing assumption your whole decision rests on. The safe posture is to confirm the market exists, look at recent prices rather than headline ones, and then deliberately under-weight the income in your planning so that a soft market is a mild disappointment rather than a broken projection. If the certificates pay well, that's upside you'll happily take; if they don't, you never bet on them in the first place.

A handful of practical questions tend to follow once someone realizes they might be in an SREC state, and the answers matter for how you plan. SRECs are not the same thing as net metering, and confusing them is common: net metering credits the actual electricity you export to the grid against your bill, while SRECs pay you separately for the environmental attribute of your generation, and where both exist you earn both at once on the same production. SREC income may well be treated as taxable income, and the rules can vary, so that's a question for a tax professional familiar with your state rather than something to assume in either direction. And the biggest planning question of all — will the market still exist in ten years? — has an honestly uncertain answer, because SREC markets depend on state RPS laws that legislatures can and do revise, tighten, or unwind. The prudent move is to treat SREC income the way its own price behaves: as plausible and welcome but not promised. Don't build your entire solar case on certificate income continuing unchanged for the full life of the system; size and justify the project on the power savings and the federal credit, which are far more stable, and let the SRECs be the upside layer they actually are. If you want to see how sensitive your return really is to that layer, run your numbers through a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) once with SREC income included and once without — the gap between the two is a clear-eyed measure of how much of your case is riding on a market that could shift under you, and knowing that number is what keeps a genuine bonus from quietly becoming a bet.
