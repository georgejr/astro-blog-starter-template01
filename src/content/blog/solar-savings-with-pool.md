A swimming pool changes the solar conversation in a way most household additions don't, because it does two things at once: it makes your electric bill bigger, and it makes that bill unusually well-matched to what solar panels are good at. The pump that keeps the water clean runs for hours in the middle of the day, which is exactly when the panels on your roof are producing hardest. That overlap is the reason a pool owner often gets more real value out of a solar system than a neighbor with the same panels and no pool — not because the panels behave differently, but because more of what they make gets used the moment it's generated. Before any of that pays off, though, you have to know how much electricity the pool is actually costing you, and the honest answer is: probably more than you think, and it depends heavily on equipment you may never have looked at closely.

## What a pool actually adds to your electric bill

The pump is the headline. In most pools it's the single largest energy consumer of the whole setup, and its appetite comes down to two things — how much power the motor draws and how many hours a day it runs. An older single-speed pump is the expensive case: it has one setting, full blast, and it's typically run several hours a day to turn the water over enough to keep it clear. A motor like that pulling on the order of 1.5 to 2 kilowatts for six or more hours a day, most of the year, lands squarely in the territory where the pump alone adds 2,000 or more kilowatt-hours to your annual usage — enough, in many homes, to rival the refrigerator, the dryer, and the dishwasher combined. It's the kind of load that quietly explains a summer bill that seems too high for the number of people in the house. Our rundown of [the appliances quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) puts a pool pump in the same weight class as central air conditioning for a lot of households, and for good reason.

The variable-speed pump is the other case, and it changes the picture enough to matter for your solar math. These pumps can run at low speed for long stretches and ramp up only when needed, and because the power a pump draws falls off sharply as you slow it down, running longer at a low speed uses far less energy than running briefly at full tilt. A well-programmed variable-speed pump can cut the pump's share of the bill substantially compared to an old single-speed unit doing the same filtration job. If you're weighing solar specifically to tame a pool bill, the pump upgrade is worth pricing alongside the panels, because reducing the load before you size a system is almost always cheaper than generating extra power to cover a wasteful one. The two decisions belong in the same conversation, not separate ones.

Then there's heating, which is where pool energy costs either stay modest or explode, depending on how you do it. If your pool is heated by a gas heater, that fuel cost sits outside your electric bill entirely and solar won't touch it — the panels can't offset therms you're buying from the gas company. If it's heated by an electric resistance heater, brace yourself, because those are enormous consumers that can dwarf the pump. And if it's heated by an electric heat pump pool heater, you're in the efficient middle: a heat-pump heater moves heat rather than making it from scratch, using a fraction of the electricity of a resistance unit for the same warmth, though still adding a real load during heating season. Sorting out which of these you have is the difference between a pool that adds a manageable couple thousand kilowatt-hours a year and one that adds many times that. The only way to know your own number is to look at your bills across the seasons and watch how usage climbs when the pool is running — an [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) fed with your actual monthly kilowatt-hours will show you the pool's fingerprint far more reliably than any generic estimate, because the swing between your pool-season and off-season usage is essentially the pool's annual cost laid bare.

## Why pool loads and solar fit together so well

Here's the part that makes solar genuinely attractive for pool owners rather than merely tolerable. The value of a solar kilowatt-hour depends enormously on whether you use it as it's produced or export it to the grid. In most places, power you consume on-site offsets electricity you'd otherwise buy at the full retail rate, while power you export earns some lower credit — often well below retail. So the more of your panels' midday output you can soak up inside the house, the better the economics. A pool pump is close to the ideal load for exactly this, because it's a big, flexible, daytime consumer that you fully control. You don't have to run it at 2 a.m. You can — and if you're going solar, you should — schedule it to run during the peak production hours of the day, converting what would have been low-value exported energy into high-value self-consumed energy. The pump effectively becomes a way to "store" your solar production as clean water instead of sending it to the grid at a discount.

That timing lever is worth being deliberate about. A variable-speed pump on a timer set to run its long, low-speed filtration cycle from mid-morning through mid-afternoon lines the pool's biggest load up almost perfectly with the roof's biggest output. On a clear day, the array might be covering the pump entirely and still have surplus for the rest of the house. Do the same thing with an electric heat-pump pool heater — heating during daylight rather than overnight — and another large load shifts onto free midday production. Compare that to a household that exports most of its midday solar because nobody's home and nothing's running: the pool owner is capturing value the empty-house neighbor is giving away. It's the same principle that makes daytime EV charging and daytime laundry pay off, just applied to the biggest daytime load many homes have. The way this overlap drives [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) is easy to underrate until you see it on a pool owner's bill, where the self-consumption share can run well above what a typical home achieves.

This overlap should also shape how you size the system. Because the pool adds a substantial, predictable, daytime load, it justifies more panels than the same house would need without it — and crucially, those extra panels serve high-value self-consumption rather than low-value export, so they earn their keep instead of dumping surplus onto the grid. A home that would have installed a modest array to cover evening and baseline use can add capacity specifically to carry the pool's daytime draw, and that additional capacity tends to pay back better than the base array does, precisely because it's so well-timed. The engineering details of matching panel output to a pump's real draw are covered in our look at [running a pool pump on solar](/blog/solar-panels-for-pool-pump/); from a savings standpoint, the takeaway is that the pool doesn't just add cost you're trying to erase — it adds the kind of load that makes each additional solar kilowatt worth more.

## Turning the pieces into a savings estimate

Building your own number starts with separating the pool's load from everything else, and the cleanest way to do that is the seasonal comparison you already have sitting in your billing history. Look at your usage in the months the pool runs versus the months it doesn't, and the difference is a fair first estimate of the pool's annual electricity cost. Assume, for a worked example, that this comparison reveals the pump and an electric heat-pump heater together add 3,000 kWh a year, and assume a retail rate of $0.16/kWh. That's roughly $480 a year the pool is costing you in electricity — a figure that immediately reframes whether a pump upgrade or a slightly larger solar array is worth it. Label those assumptions honestly and swap in your own rate and your own seasonal swing, because a pool in Phoenix running eleven months a year and a pool in Ohio running four look nothing alike on the meter.

From there, the solar side comes down to how much of that pool load you can convert into self-consumption. If you schedule the pump and any electric heating into daylight hours and size the array to cover them, a large share of the pool's draw can be offset by power you never buy from the grid — which is the highest-value offset there is. The rest of the household's usage layers on top in the usual way. Rather than guess at the blended result, it's worth running the whole picture — base household load plus the timed pool load, against a system sized to cover both — through a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) with a realistic self-consumption assumption. Because so much of the pool's load can be shifted into production hours, a pool owner can justifiably assume a higher self-consumption rate than the generic defaults, and that single input moves the payback more than almost anything else.

What you'll usually find is that a pool tilts the decision toward solar rather than away from it, provided you do two things in the right order. First, cut the load where it's wasteful — a variable-speed pump and a sensible run schedule can shrink the pool's electricity bill before you generate a single watt, and generating less is always cheaper than generating more. Second, size and time the system so the pool runs on sunshine, turning your biggest controllable load into your best self-consumption opportunity. Get those two moves right and the pool stops being the thing that made your bill scary and becomes the reason your panels are working near their full value every clear afternoon. Get them wrong — an old single-speed pump running overnight while the array exports its midday output for pennies — and you've paid for panels whose best hours are being wasted on an empty grid connection. The pool doesn't decide the outcome. How you schedule it, and how honestly you measure it, does.
