The moment a lot of solar owners dread arrives quietly: a roofer, up on the ladder for an unrelated reason, mentions that the shingles have maybe five years left. The array has been on the roof for eight years and is producing beautifully. Now there is a puzzle. The roof underneath the panels needs replacing, and there is no way to re-shingle a roof with two dozen solar panels bolted to it. The panels have to come off, the roof gets redone, and the panels go back up. In the trade this is called a detach and reset, or a remove and reinstall, and it is one of the more predictable curveballs of owning solar on an aging roof.

## How a detach and reset actually works

The job is more involved than lifting panels off and setting them back down, which is why it carries a real cost rather than a nominal one. It is a coordinated sequence between two trades, the solar crew and the roofers, and the order matters. First a qualified solar technician disconnects the system electrically. That means shutting it down at the inverter and the utility disconnect, then de-energizing and unwiring the array so nothing on the roof is live while people are working around it. Only after the electrical side is safely dead do the panels come off, unbolted from their mounting hardware and carried down, and here is where an important decision gets made: what happens to the racking and the roof penetrations.

There are two philosophies. In the cleaner one, the crew removes not just the panels but the entire mounting system, the rails and the roof-attached feet, so the roofers get a completely bare deck to work with and every old penetration can be properly sealed under the new roofing. In the faster, cheaper one, only the panels come off and the racking stays put while roofers work around it, which saves labor but leaves the old attachment points in place and makes it harder to do a clean, watertight job at each foot. Most careful installers and roofers prefer the full removal precisely because the whole reason you are up there is a new, sound, leak-free roof, and working around old hardware undercuts that. The panels themselves, once down, get stored safely on the ground or in a garage, ideally flat and protected, because a panel is glass and a knock to a corner or a dropped module is an expensive mistake.

One risk in the whole operation is invisible, and it's worth understanding before the crew arrives. Solar panels are glass over silicon, and rough handling during removal, storage, or remounting can create microcracks that never show up as a shattered panel but quietly sap output for years afterward. A module that gets flexed, stacked carelessly against a wall, or set down hard on a corner can look perfectly fine and still come back producing less than it did. This is a big part of why the reconnection isn't truly finished when the last bolt is torqued: a careful crew brings the system back online and then checks each string's production against what it should be, catching a panel that returned weaker than it left rather than letting it hide in the array for months. Ask whether a post-reinstall performance check is part of the job, because "it powers on" and "it produces what it did before" are not the same thing. If your monitoring showed a healthy, documented baseline before the work started, that record is exactly the yardstick you use to confirm nothing was quietly degraded along the way.

Then the roofers do their job on a clean deck. When the new roof is on, the process runs in reverse: the solar crew installs fresh mounting hardware and flashing into the new roof, remounts the panels, rewires the array, and brings the system back online. New flashing and new attachment points on a new roof are genuinely better than reusing the old ones, so a well-executed detach and reset leaves you with a more watertight array than you started with, which is a small consolation for the expense. There is usually a permit and sometimes a reinspection involved, because you are re-attaching an electrical generating system to the structure, and skipping that paperwork can cause headaches later at sale or with the utility. Expect the array to be offline for the duration, anywhere from a few days to a couple of weeks depending on how the two crews' schedules mesh and how the weather cooperates, so you will buy that power from the grid in the meantime.

Coordination is the part that goes wrong most often. The solar company and the roofing company are usually separate businesses with separate schedules, and getting them to hand off cleanly, solar off, roof done, solar back on, without the roof sitting exposed or the array sitting on the ground for weeks, takes real planning. Some solar installers offer detach-and-reset as a service and will coordinate directly with your roofer; some roofers have a solar partner they work with. The worst outcomes come from a homeowner trying to broker between two companies that have never spoken, each waiting on the other. If your original installer is still in business and did the array, they are often the natural first call, both because they know the system and because a reputable installer's workmanship warranty can be voided if someone else disturbs their work. That warranty interaction is worth confirming before anyone touches a panel, because a botched reinstall by an unfamiliar crew can leave you with neither a working warranty nor a clean install.

## What it costs, and how to avoid needing it

The honest range for a detach and reset is wide, because it scales with the size and complexity of the array and with how the labor is priced. A common way to see it quoted is per panel, and per-panel figures generally land somewhere in the range of roughly $75 to $200 or more for the combined remove-and-reinstall labor, depending on the market, the roof's complexity, and whether the racking is fully removed. Run that across a typical residential array and the total commonly falls somewhere between about $1,500 and $6,000, with larger arrays, steep or complex roofs, and battery or complex electrical setups pushing toward and past the high end. Treat those as planning figures rather than a quote; the only number that matters is the one a local crew gives you after looking at your specific array and roof. The [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) can help you frame the scale of your system, which is the main driver of the labor, but a detach-and-reset price really does need to come from someone standing in your driveway.

A few things swing that number. Array size is the obvious one, since it is often priced per panel. Roof complexity matters, because a simple single-plane roof is quicker to work than a cut-up roof with multiple faces, dormers, and awkward access. Whether a battery, backup gateway, or complicated wiring is involved adds electrical labor. And ground logistics play in: a crew that has to store, protect, and re-stage two dozen panels is doing more than one working a small array. There is also the cost of the array being offline, the power you buy from the grid during the outage, which is minor over a week or two but worth noting. What you are paying for, fundamentally, is skilled electrical and mounting labor twice over, plus the roofing work itself, which is a separate bill entirely.

There is one scenario where the cost equation can shift in your favor: a roof being replaced because of storm or hail damage rather than plain old age. When a re-roof is driven by an insurance claim, the detach and reset of the solar can sometimes fall within that claim, since the panels have to come off to repair covered damage and putting them back is part of restoring the property to its prior condition. Coverage varies enormously by policy and by insurer, and nothing here is a guarantee — but if a covered peril is what is forcing the roof work, it is worth asking your carrier directly whether the solar removal and reinstallation labor is included, rather than assuming the whole bill lands on you. Document the array's condition before anyone touches it, have the detach-and-reset quoted as its own clear line item, and submit it alongside the roofing claim. Even a partial contribution from insurance meaningfully changes the math on a job that would otherwise be several thousand dollars straight out of pocket.

All of which points at the real lesson, and it is one best learned before you buy panels rather than after. The cheapest detach and reset is the one you never have to do, and the way you avoid it is by getting the roof and the solar timing right from the start. If your roof is already aging when you go solar, replacing it first, so the new array sits on a roof with 20-plus years of life ahead of it, is almost always cheaper than paying to remove and reinstall panels partway through their life. That tradeoff, and how to judge whether your roof is worth doing first, is exactly the question worked through in [whether to replace your roof before going solar](/blog/replace-roof-before-solar/). A modern solar array can easily outlive a roof that was already halfway through its life at install, so the mismatch is common and entirely avoidable with a little foresight.

It is also worth putting to rest a related worry that this whole scenario tends to stir up, which is whether the panels themselves damaged the roof and caused the need for replacement. Generally they do not; a properly flashed array actually shelters the shingles beneath it from sun and weather, and the roof under panels often ages more slowly than the exposed roof around it. The full picture of that, including where leaks genuinely do come from, is laid out in [whether solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/). The roof under your array is usually failing because it was already old, not because of the panels. One more piece to keep in view if you do end up combining a re-roof with solar work: the labor to detach and reset is generally a maintenance cost and does not qualify for the federal solar credit, but the rules around re-roofing and the tax credit have real nuance, especially on a new install, and they are worth understanding before you assume anything is or is not eligible, which we cover in [what the tax credit does and does not cover when re-roofing with solar](/blog/roof-replacement-solar-tax-credit/). Handled with a little planning, a detach and reset is an expense and an inconvenience but not a crisis. The homeowners who get stung are the ones who ignore an aging roof at install time and then discover, years later, that the roof and the panels have to come apart before either can be made right.
