An 11 kW quote usually shows up for one of two reasons: either your household genuinely burns through a lot of electricity, or an installer sized up to cover an EV and a heat pump you mentioned in passing. Both can be right, but they lead to very different conversations. Eleven kilowatts is a large residential array, comfortably above the national-average install, and at that size the panel count, the roof space, and the annual production all get big enough that it's worth checking the system is matched to your actual consumption rather than rounded up to a satisfying number. The kilowatt rating is a capacity figure, the array's output at its best instant under lab conditions. What you live with is the energy it delivers across a year, and how that stacks up against the kilowatt-hours your home pulls off the meter.

Two questions do most of the work here: how many panels an 11 kW array actually takes, and how much power it makes once real-world losses are counted. Get those straight and the "is this the right size for me?" question mostly answers itself.

## How many panels, and how much roof

The panel count is just division, but the answer moves around because panel wattage isn't fixed. An 11 kW array means 11,000 watts of direct-current nameplate capacity, and you reach that by multiplying the wattage of each panel by the number of panels. So the count depends entirely on which panels go on your roof. With today's common residential modules landing roughly in the 380- to 440-watt range, the arithmetic runs like this: at 400 watts a panel, 11,000 divided by 400 is about 28 panels; at 440 watts, you're down to 25; at a more modest 370 watts, you climb to around 30. The honest planning range for an 11 kW system is therefore about 25 to 30 panels, and where you land inside that band tells you something useful. Higher-wattage panels get you to 11 kW with fewer modules and less roof, which matters if your usable roof area is tight, while lower-wattage panels mean more units and more square footage but sometimes a lower price per panel. This is exactly why comparing two quotes on panel count alone is misleading, and why understanding what a panel's wattage rating actually represents is worth a few minutes; we lay that out in [solar panel wattage explained](/blog/solar-panel-wattage-explained/).

Roof space follows directly from the count. A standard residential panel occupies roughly 18 square feet, so 25 to 30 panels need somewhere in the neighborhood of 450 to 540 square feet of usable mounting area, and "usable" is the operative word. That's not the same as your total roof area. You lose space to setbacks required by fire code around the edges and ridge, to vents, chimneys, skylights, and dormers, and to any roof planes that face the wrong way or sit in shade for part of the day. A large, simple south-facing roof swallows an 11 kW array without strain. A chopped-up roof with multiple small planes, several facing north, might not physically fit 28 panels in good positions no matter how much you want it to, which sometimes forces a smaller system or a split across less-ideal orientations that costs you production. If you're not sure your roof can host an array this size, sketching it out with the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you a realistic panel count and footprint before an installer ever climbs up.

There's a hardware consideration that comes with going this big, too. At 11 kW, you're often near or past the point where a single standard string inverter is sized comfortably, so systems this size may use a larger inverter, multiple inverters, or a microinverter setup with electronics on each panel. That's not a problem, just a design detail that affects both cost and how the system handles shade, and it's one more reason an 11 kW quote deserves a closer read than a small starter system. The takeaway on sizing is simple: 25 to 30 panels, roughly 450 to 540 square feet of good roof, and a design that scales up cleanly if your roof cooperates.

## What it produces, and the household it actually fits

Production is where an 11 kW array either justifies itself or reveals that it's too much house for the roof or too much roof for the house. The rating, 11 kW of DC nameplate, is measured under standardized lab conditions your roof never sustains, so the real question is annual kilowatt-hours. The estimate is a short piece of arithmetic: take the 11 kW, multiply by the peak sun hours your location averages per day, multiply by 365, then subtract roughly 20 percent for the losses every system carries, inverter conversion, wiring, heat, soiling, and panel mismatch. The peak-sun-hour figure is the big regional variable, so the honest answer is a range rather than a single number.

Work a couple of labeled scenarios. In a sunny location averaging about 5.5 peak sun hours a day, 11 kW times 5.5 times 365, less 20 percent, lands around 17,700 kWh a year. In a middling location around 4.5 sun hours, the same math gives roughly 14,500 kWh. In a cloudier northern spot near 4 sun hours, you're closer to 12,900 kWh. Treat those as planning estimates, not promises, since your exact output depends on orientation, tilt, shading, and local weather. The spread itself is the point: the identical 11 kW array can produce well over 4,000 kWh more per year in Arizona than in the Pacific Northwest, which is why national rules of thumb about "how many panels for a home" break down and you have to run your own location. The cleaner way to think about it is production per kilowatt of installed capacity, dividing your expected annual kilowatt-hours by the system's kilowatt rating, which gives you a yardstick to compare your site against any other regardless of system size.

Now line that output against consumption to find the household that fits. An 11 kW system making somewhere between roughly 13,000 and 18,000 kWh a year is aimed at homes that use a lot of electricity, meaningfully above the typical American household. That profile shows up in a few recognizable situations. Large homes with central air conditioning running hard through a hot-climate summer land here. All-electric homes that heat with electric or heat-pump systems and cook with induction rack up high annual totals, especially in cold climates where winter heating dominates. Add an electric vehicle, which can tack thousands of kilowatt-hours onto a year of driving, or a swimming pool with a pump running daily, and consumption climbs into the range where 11 kW starts to make sense rather than look excessive. If your annual usage is closer to average, an 11 kW array will overproduce, and in a world where many utilities no longer credit exports at full retail value, overproducing means sending surplus power to the grid for less than it's worth to you. Bigger is not automatically better; it's better only if you have the load to absorb it.

That's the honest test for anyone staring at an 11 kW proposal. Pull twelve months of electric bills, find your annual kilowatt-hour total, and compare it to the production estimate for your location. If you use 15,000 kWh a year in a moderately sunny area, 11 kW is a sensible match aimed at covering most of it. If you use 9,000 kWh, the same system is oversized, and you'd likely be better served by a smaller, cheaper array that covers your usage without dumping excess power onto the grid at a discount.

## What an 11 kW system costs, and what the credit does to it

Size drives price, and at 11 kW you are pricing a large array, so it helps to anchor expectations with an honest range rather than a single figure. Installed residential solar has generally landed somewhere between about $2.50 and $4.00 per watt before incentives, with the spread driven by your region, your installer, the equipment, and how complex your roof is. Treat that band as a sanity check, not a quote: a number well below it deserves scrutiny for what is being left out, and a number well above it is a prompt to ask what is driving the premium or to gather more bids. Run 11,000 watts against that range and you are looking at a gross price roughly between $27,500 and $44,000, a wide span that mostly reflects those same variables rather than anything mysterious. Where your quote falls inside it tells you more about your local market and roof than about whether you are being treated fairly, which is why comparing several quotes matters far more at this size than chasing a single per-watt target.

The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit reshapes that gross number substantially. It lets you claim 30 percent of your qualifying solar costs against your federal income tax, which on an 11 kW system is a large sum, on the order of $8,000 to $13,000 depending on the gross price. That turns a $35,000 array into an effective cost closer to $24,500 once the credit is realized. Two honest caveats: it is a credit against taxes you owe, not a rebate check, so you need enough federal tax liability to use it, though unused credit can generally carry forward, and you should confirm the current rules against IRS guidance rather than take any installer's summary as gospel. Factored in from the start, the credit is the single biggest lever pulling a big system's price back toward reasonable, and it applies to the whole qualifying package including labor.

Bigger systems also tend to earn a slightly better price per watt than small ones, because many of the fixed costs of a solar job, the permitting, the crew mobilization, the design and inspection overhead, do not scale up much between an 8 kW and an 11 kW install. Those fixed costs get spread across more watts, so the per-watt figure often drifts down as the system grows. That is part of why stepping up to 11 kW usually costs less than proportionally more, and it is a genuine argument for sizing up if, and only if, you have the electricity usage to justify the extra production. The trap is letting that per-watt efficiency tempt you into an oversized system whose surplus power you cannot use, especially where exports are credited below retail. The right size is still the one that matches your consumption; the pricing efficiency of going large is a reason to feel good about a big system you genuinely need, not a reason to build one you do not.

An 11 kW array is a strong, capable system for the right high-usage home. The mistake is treating the size as a status symbol rather than a match to the number on your own electric bill.
