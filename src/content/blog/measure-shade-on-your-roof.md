Before an installer ever sets foot on your property, you can learn a great deal about whether your roof is any good for solar just by paying attention to where the shadows fall. Shade is the single biggest thing an amateur can misjudge, in both directions. People talk themselves out of solar because a tree shades the roof at breakfast, not realizing that morning shade barely dents annual production. Others assume their sunny-looking roof is perfect, then discover after installing that a neighbor's oak swallows the array every winter afternoon. A rough do-it-yourself shade assessment won't replace the fisheye-camera survey a professional runs, but it will tell you whether you're a strong candidate, a marginal one, or a case where trees need to come down first, and it costs you nothing but a little attention across a day. The trick is knowing which shade matters, which doesn't, and how to see the difference before you commit.

The whole exercise rests on one idea that isn't obvious until someone points it out: not all shade is created equal, because not all hours of the day carry the same energy, and the sun's path across your roof changes dramatically with the seasons.

## The sun-path logic that tells you which shade matters

The sun does not travel the same arc every day. In summer it rises high, passing nearly overhead at midday, and its shadows are short. In winter it stays low in the southern sky even at noon, and everything casts long shadows, trees, chimneys, neighboring rooftops, that stretch far across your roof. This seasonal swing is why a shade assessment done only in July is worthless: the July sun clears obstacles that the December sun runs straight into. If you're going to measure shade once, measure it near the winter solstice, because that's the worst case. A roof that stays clear in late December will be clear all year; a roof that's buried in shadow then has a problem you need to know about. Understanding this low-winter-sun behavior is the foundation of every shade judgment you'll make, and it's the same physics a professional shading analysis is built on, which we describe in [how a solar shading analysis works](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/).

The second half of the logic is that the hours around midday matter far more than the edges of the day. The sun delivers most of its usable energy in the middle of the day, roughly between nine in the morning and three in the afternoon, when it's highest and its rays pass through the least atmosphere. Solar people call this the solar window. Shade that falls on your roof during those hours is expensive; it hits the panels when they'd otherwise be producing the bulk of their power. Shade at seven in the morning or six in the evening, by contrast, costs you very little, because the sun is low and weak then and the panels were making almost nothing anyway. This is the insight that flips people's intuition. A tree that shades your roof at dawn is nearly harmless. The same tree shading the roof at one in the afternoon in December is a real problem. When you go out to observe your roof, you're not asking "is there ever shade?" You're asking "is there shade between nine and three, especially in winter?" That's a much narrower and more useful question.

Which direction the potential array faces changes the emphasis slightly. On a south-facing roof, midday shade is the killer and you want that southern sky clear. On an east- or west-facing roof, the relevant window shifts toward morning or afternoon respectively, so a west array cares more about what blocks the afternoon sun. Either way, the same principle holds: identify the hours that carry the energy for your roof's orientation, and watch those hours specifically. If your roof faces multiple directions or the layout is complicated by dormers and vents, the interaction between orientation, obstacles, and shade gets more tangled, and a good installer plans the panel layout to keep modules out of the shadows those features throw.

The usual culprits are predictable. Trees are the biggest and the most dynamic, because unlike a chimney they grow. A tree that clears your roof today may shade it in five years, and evergreens shade year-round while deciduous trees at least drop their leaves in winter, partially reopening the sky in the season the low sun needs it most. Neighboring structures, a two-story house to the south, a tall garage, matter especially in winter when their shadows lengthen. And your own roof's features, chimneys, plumbing vents, satellite dishes, and dormers, cast small but real shadows that shift across the day. Trees deserve their own careful thought because you can sometimes trim or remove them to rescue an otherwise good roof, a decision we weigh in [trimming, removing, or designing around trees](/blog/trees-and-solar-panels/).

## Doing the actual measurement, from a notebook to your phone

The lowest-tech method still works and teaches you the most. Pick a mostly clear day, ideally in late fall or winter, and observe your roof at intervals: mid-morning around nine, midday, and mid-afternoon around three. You can watch from the ground, from an upstairs window, or safely from the roof if you have proper access, and simply note where shadows fall across the area you'd want panels on. Take a photo each time from the same spot so you can compare. What you're building is a mental time-lapse of the shade creeping across your roof through the day. If the panel area stays sunlit through those three checks in winter, you're in good shape. If shadows march across it during the nine-to-three window, you've found your constraint, and you can see exactly which obstacle is responsible, which tells you whether trimming a tree would fix it. This method's weakness is that it captures only the day you happened to observe; to reason about the rest of the year you have to combine it with the seasonal sun-path logic, remembering that summer will be sunnier and that the winter day you're watching is close to the worst case.

Phone apps close that gap and are the single best free tool an amateur has. A category of augmented-reality sun-path apps lets you hold your phone up while standing on or near the roof and see the sun's arc for any date of the year overlaid on your camera view, tracing exactly where the sun will travel across your sky in June and in December. Point it at the southern horizon and you can watch, on screen, whether that winter arc passes behind the neighbor's oak or clears it, without waiting for December to arrive. It effectively lets you time-travel the sun to the worst-case day and see your obstacles against it. These apps aren't as precise as a professional instrument, but for a homeowner deciding whether to pursue solar at all, they turn a vague worry into a concrete answer in ten minutes. Pair one with your naked-eye observation and you'll have a genuinely useful read on your roof.

Online satellite tools add a third angle, literally. Some mapping and solar-estimate services model shadows on your roof from aerial imagery and known building and tree heights, giving you a rough shade score without your leaving the couch. They're convenient and worth a look for a first impression, though their accuracy depends on how well their data captures your specific trees and the fine geometry of your roof, so treat them as a starting point rather than the last word. Where all these methods converge, you can start turning shade into panel decisions: a lightly shaded roof might still host a strong array if you place panels on the clearest planes, and tools like the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) and the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) help you sketch how many panels fit once you've excluded the shaded zones.

## Reading your findings across the whole year

A single winter afternoon of observation gives you the worst case, but interpreting it well means projecting forward and backward across the seasons. If your panel area stayed clear through the nine-to-three window on a late-December day, you can be confident it is clear the rest of the year, because summer only lifts the sun higher and shortens every shadow. That is the reassuring direction. The trickier read is a roof that is clear now but that you know will change. Deciduous trees are the classic case: they drop their leaves by winter, so a bare-branched tree that lets December sun filter through will be a dense green wall come July, shading the roof exactly when production peaks. If your worst-case winter observation looks fine but you have leafy trees to the south or west, mentally rerun the assessment for summer with those trees in full canopy, because that is the shade your winter check will not show you.

Tree growth over time is the slow variable people forget entirely. A sapling that clears your roof today can shade a corner of the array in five years and a third of it in fifteen, quietly eroding production across the exact decades you are counting on the system to pay itself off. When you spot a tree that is marginal now, it is worth thinking about its mature height, not just its current one, and deciding whether you are prepared to keep it trimmed. Sometimes the honest conclusion of a shade assessment is not about the panels at all; it is that a particular tree needs to come down or be topped before solar makes sense, and it is far better to reach that conclusion before you install than after.

Where your findings land in a gray zone, some midday shade but not a wall of it, the right move is to let a professional quantify it rather than guess. Partial, intermittent shade is exactly the case where a fisheye survey earns its keep, because the production loss depends on which panels are affected, at what hours, in what season, and how the system's electronics respond. Your eyeball can tell you there is some afternoon shade on the west plane; the survey turns that into a specific number of kilowatt-hours lost per year, which is the figure that actually drives the decision. Use your own assessment to decide whether to keep going, and let the professional's model set the final expectation.

Two honest caveats keep a DIY assessment from misleading you. First, partial shade often hurts more than it looks like it should. On many systems, shading even one panel can drag down others wired with it, so a shadow that clips a corner of the array isn't necessarily a small loss, and the exact impact depends on the system's electronics, microinverters and optimizers handle partial shade better than a plain string setup. If your roof has unavoidable partial shade, that changes both the hardware choice and the payback math, so it's worth flagging to any installer as something to model rather than gloss over. Second, your amateur read is a screening tool, not a final design. When a professional comes out, they'll put a fisheye camera or a sun-path instrument on your roof and produce an hour-by-hour, month-by-month shade model that quantifies the loss precisely, and their number should override your eyeball estimate. The point of measuring shade yourself isn't to replace that survey. It's to walk into the process already knowing whether your roof is a strong candidate, whether a tree is the one thing standing between you and a good array, or whether the shade is bad enough that you should think hard before spending money, all of which feeds the larger question of [whether your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) in the first place. Ten minutes with your phone and a little attention across one winter day gives you an informed starting position instead of a hopeful guess.
