Every solar panel is a slightly tilted sheet of glass left outdoors for decades, and whatever your local air and weather carry will eventually try to settle on it. What settles, though, is not the same everywhere. A panel in the humid Pacific Northwest collects something biological and stubborn; a panel in the desert Southwest collects something mineral and fine; a panel near farmland in spring collects a yellow film for a few weeks and then stops. The word "dirty" hides real differences, and those differences decide whether rain handles the problem for you or whether you eventually reach for a hose.

Start with the reassuring part. Across most climates, ordinary soiling typically costs a panel somewhere in the **1–5%** range of annual output — a nuisance, rarely a crisis. The tilt of the glass and the next rain do most of the cleaning. The exceptions, where buildup becomes real money, cluster into a few specific types tied to where you live.

## Why grime is a local phenomenon

Three things decide what accumulates: what your air carries, how humid it stays, and how often it rains hard enough to rinse. A steep roof in a rainy climate is close to self-cleaning for loose dust, yet that same moisture invites living growth that rain will never remove. A bone-dry climate has no biological problem at all but no cleaning rain either, so fine mineral dust simply piles up until someone intervenes. The type of buildup and the climate that produces it are two sides of one coin.

## The main kinds of buildup

### Moss and algae in wet climates

In cool, damp, shaded conditions — think the maritime Northwest or heavily treed lots anywhere humid — the enemy is biological. Algae forms a thin green film; moss takes hold along the bottom edge and under the frame where moisture lingers and debris collects. Unlike dust, these are alive and anchored. Rain feeds them rather than removing them, so they accumulate season over season. Left alone, a fringe of moss along the lower edge can shade cells and trap more debris, compounding the loss. This is the buildup most likely to justify deliberate cleaning, because time makes it worse rather than better.

### Lichen: the stubborn one

Lichen is a step beyond moss in tenacity. It bonds to the glass and frame and does not wipe away with a soft brush the way loose soiling does. It is slow to form, favoring older installations in humid regions, but once established it is genuinely difficult to remove without patient, careful work — and aggressive scrubbing risks scratching the glass or anti-reflective coating. Prevention, mostly by keeping panels from sitting in permanent damp shade, beats removal here more than with any other type.

### Desert dust and mineral soiling

Arid and semi-arid climates flip the problem. There is no biological growth, but there is also little cleaning rain, so airborne mineral dust settles and stays. In agricultural or construction-adjacent areas it mixes with soil and gets heavier. When light rain does fall, it can make things briefly worse by leaving muddy streaks and drying spots. Dust is loose and easy to remove, but in a true desert it comes back steadily, which is why dry, dusty regions see the most predictable payoff from occasional cleaning.

### Pollen: loud but temporary

Spring pollen produces the most alarming-looking film — an even yellow coating over the whole array — and the least durable. It is fine, it is seasonal, and the first few good rains after the bloom generally rinse most of it away. The output hit is usually small and short-lived. Pollen is worth knowing about mainly so you do not panic and climb a ladder for something the weather will solve in a week or two.

### Bird droppings and point soiling

Every climate shares one problem: droppings and stray leaf litter that land as concentrated spots rather than an even film. These matter more than their size suggests, because a single opaque blob can shade a cell and drag down a whole string through the way panels are wired. Point soiling is the one kind of grime where a small amount can cause a disproportionate loss, and where spot-cleaning pays off quickly.

## How the climate map translates to a cleaning plan

| Climate type | Dominant buildup | Does rain handle it? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cool and wet | Moss, algae, lichen | No — moisture feeds it |
| Hot and dry | Mineral dust | No — too little rain |
| Temperate, seasonal | Pollen, general dust | Mostly yes |
| Any, with trees or birds | Droppings, leaf litter, point soiling | Only partly |

The pattern is consistent: rain cleans loose, even soiling well and living or concentrated soiling poorly. If your local buildup is dust or pollen, patience and the next storm usually suffice. If it is moss, lichen, or persistent droppings, buildup grows until you act.
