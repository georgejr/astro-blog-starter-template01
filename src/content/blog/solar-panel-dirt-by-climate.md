Every solar panel is a slightly tilted sheet of glass left outdoors for decades, and whatever your local air and weather carry will eventually try to settle on it. What settles, though, is not the same everywhere, and the difference matters more than the single word "dirty" suggests. A panel in the humid Pacific Northwest collects something biological and stubborn that rain actively feeds. A panel in the desert Southwest collects something mineral and fine that rain almost never arrives to rinse. A panel near farmland in spring collects a yellow film for a few weeks and then sheds it on its own. Each of those is soiling, but they behave so differently that lumping them together tells you nothing useful about whether you need to do anything at all.

Start with the reassuring part, because it frames everything that follows. Across most climates, ordinary soiling typically costs a panel somewhere in the **1–5%** range of annual output — a genuine nuisance, but rarely a crisis, and rarely worth the risk of climbing onto a roof to fix. The tilt of the glass and the next good rain do most of the cleaning for free, all year, without anyone lifting a finger. The exceptions, where buildup crosses from nuisance into real lost money, cluster into a few specific types that are tightly tied to where you live. Knowing which type your climate produces is what separates a sensible maintenance plan from either needless ladder-climbing or quiet, ongoing losses you never diagnosed.

## Why grime is a local phenomenon

Three things decide what accumulates on your panels and how long it stays: what your air carries, how humid the air stays, and how often it rains hard enough to actually rinse the glass. Those three variables interact in ways that produce very different outcomes from the same underlying process. A steep roof in a rainy climate is close to self-cleaning for loose dust, because frequent hard rain sheets down the tilted glass and carries the loose material off before it can build into anything. But that very same moisture, so helpful against dust, is an open invitation to living growth that rain will never remove and in fact encourages. Wet and rainy solves one problem while creating another.

A bone-dry climate flips the situation entirely. There is no biological problem at all, because nothing living can get established without moisture, but there is also no cleaning rain to speak of, so fine mineral dust simply settles and piles up week after week until someone intervenes with a hose. Neither climate is straightforwardly better or worse for soiling; they each have their own dominant nuisance, produced by the same weather that also determines whether that nuisance clears itself. The type of buildup and the climate that produces it are two sides of one coin, which is why the honest answer to "should I clean my panels" always starts with "where do you live" rather than "how do they look."

The self-cleaning dynamic is worth understanding on its own, because it does more work than most owners realize. Panels are mounted at a tilt for production reasons, but that tilt is also what makes rain effective — water runs off rather than pooling, taking loose material with it. The steeper the tilt and the more often it rains, the more the roof handles for you. A shallow-pitched array in a dry climate gets the worst of both worlds: not enough angle to shed material aggressively, and not enough rain to trigger the shedding in the first place. That combination, more than any particular type of dirt, is what turns soiling from a self-resolving footnote into a recurring line item you actually have to manage.

## The kinds of buildup, and where each one lives

In cool, damp, shaded conditions — the maritime Northwest, or heavily treed lots anywhere humid — the enemy is biological, and it is the type most likely to justify deliberate action. Algae forms first, a thin green film across the glass that dims output subtly and evenly. Moss follows, taking hold along the bottom edge of the panel and up under the frame, exactly where moisture lingers longest and windblown debris collects to feed it. Unlike dust, these are alive and anchored, drawing on the same rain that would rinse loose soiling away, so they accumulate season over season instead of clearing between storms. Left alone, a fringe of moss along the lower edge does more than shade a strip of cells; it traps additional debris and holds still more moisture, compounding the loss and accelerating its own growth. This is the buildup where waiting makes things strictly worse rather than better, which is what pushes it into the category worth cleaning on purpose.

Lichen is moss's more tenacious cousin, and it deserves separate mention because it does not behave like ordinary soiling at all. It bonds to the glass and frame at a level that a soft brush and a rinse simply will not shift the way they shift loose material. It is slow to form, favoring older installations in persistently humid regions, but once it is genuinely established it is difficult to remove without patient, careful work — and aggressive scrubbing to speed that work along risks scratching the glass or damaging the anti-reflective coating, trading a small output loss for permanent damage. With lichen more than any other type, prevention beats removal: keeping panels out of permanent damp shade, trimming back the branches that keep a corner of the array from ever drying, and addressing early growth before it cements itself.

Arid and semi-arid climates present the opposite problem. There is no biological growth to worry about, but there is also little cleaning rain, so airborne mineral dust settles onto the glass and stays put. In agricultural or construction-adjacent areas the dust mixes with soil and grows heavier and more opaque. And when light rain does finally fall in a dry region, it can briefly make things worse rather than better, leaving muddy streaks and drying into spots that concentrate the soiling instead of removing it. The saving grace is that desert dust is loose and easy to remove when you do get to it — no bonding, no biology, just material sitting on glass. But in a true desert it comes back steadily and predictably, which is exactly why dry, dusty regions see the most reliable payoff from occasional deliberate cleaning. There, cleaning is not a reaction to a problem; it is routine maintenance against a nuisance that never stops arriving.

Pollen produces the most alarming-looking film of all and the least durable. In spring it lays down an even yellow coating over the entire array, dramatic enough that homeowners often panic at the sight. But it is fine, it is strictly seasonal, and the first few good rains after the bloom generally rinse most of it away without any help. The output hit is usually small and short-lived, a matter of a couple of weeks rather than a chronic condition. Pollen is worth knowing about mainly so that you do not haul a ladder up to fight something the weather will resolve on its own in a week or two — the effort and the risk almost never justify the tiny, temporary gain.

One kind of buildup ignores climate entirely, and it is the one that punches above its weight. Every region shares the problem of bird droppings and stray leaf litter that land as concentrated spots rather than an even film. These matter far more than their small size suggests, because of how panels are wired: a single opaque blob can shade one cell, and that shaded cell can drag down the output of an entire string, so a spot the size of a coin costs you far more than its area implies. Point soiling is the one case where a tiny amount of grime causes a disproportionate loss, and correspondingly the one case where spot-cleaning pays off quickly and obviously. If you are going to clean anything, the bird dropping over a junction of cells is the highest-value target on the whole array.

## Turning the climate map into a cleaning plan

Laying the types against the climates that produce them makes the pattern legible at a glance.

| Climate type | Dominant buildup | Does rain handle it? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cool and wet | Moss, algae, lichen | No — moisture feeds it |
| Hot and dry | Mineral dust | No — too little rain |
| Temperate, seasonal | Pollen, general dust | Mostly yes |
| Any, with trees or birds | Droppings, leaf litter, point soiling | Only partly |

The underlying rule is consistent across every row: rain cleans loose, even soiling well, and it cleans living or concentrated soiling poorly or not at all. If your local buildup is dust or pollen, patience and the next storm usually suffice, and the honest maintenance plan is mostly to do nothing while keeping an eye out. If your buildup is moss, lichen, or persistent droppings, the material accumulates until you act, and a plan means deciding when and how to intervene before the loss grows. That distinction — self-resolving versus accumulating — is the heart of [whether panels really need cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/), and it reframes the whole question away from appearance and toward behavior over time.

Two regional notes are worth filing away alongside the main types. In snowy climates, the dynamic that matters most is not summer dust at all but how cleanly snow slides off the glass, because an array buried under snow produces nothing while a clear one sheds and recovers on its own. The trade-offs of removing snow versus letting it slide, and the real risks of trying to clear it, are their own topic covered in [snow on solar panels](/blog/snow-on-solar-panels/). And across every climate, when cleaning genuinely is warranted, doing it without damaging the glass or coating is a careful process in its own right — the wrong brush, the wrong water, or the wrong timing can cost more than the dirt did — which is why the method matters as much as the decision, and is worked through in [cleaning solar panels safely](/blog/how-to-clean-solar-panels/).

Underneath all of it sits a simple economic test that keeps the whole subject in proportion. The real question is never whether the panels look dirty; it is whether the lost output is worth the effort, the cost, and the genuine safety risk of cleaning at all. A few percent of annual production sounds like something until you translate it into dollars through your own usage and rate, and for many homes that translation reveals a figure too small to justify a ladder. Running your own numbers through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) — how much you produce, what it is worth, and what a few percent of it actually amounts to over a year — is often the most honest way to decide whether that climb is worth making, or whether the smarter move is to let the weather keep doing the job it has been doing for free all along.
