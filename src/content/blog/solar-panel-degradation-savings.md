There's a worry that shows up whenever someone runs solar savings numbers for the first time: if the panels lose output every year, doesn't that quietly eat the whole return? The image behind the worry is of a system limping along at half strength a decade in, savings evaporating as the panels fade. It's a reasonable thing to wonder and a wrong picture to hold. Degradation is real, it does reduce your savings, and the reduction is small enough that once you see the arithmetic, it stops being scary and becomes just another line in the model.

The reason the fear overshoots is that "loses power every year" *sounds* like a cliff and *behaves* like a gentle slope. The gap between those two is worth closing with actual numbers, because the honest figure — modest, predictable, single-digit over the system's life — is more useful than either the panic or the marketing that waves the issue away entirely.

## The cliff that isn't there

Modern crystalline-silicon panels degrade slowly. A typical assumption for planning is around 0.5% a year after the first year, with many quality panels rated at or below that. There's usually a slightly larger drop in the very first year of operation — often a percent or two as the panel settles — and then the long, shallow decline sets in.

Put that rate in perspective. At 0.5% a year, a panel doesn't lose half its output in a decade; it loses roughly 5%. The mental model of a system running at a fraction of its rated power midway through its life simply doesn't match how the hardware ages. The physics and typical rates are covered in more depth in [what your system loses each year](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/); the point for savings is that the annual loss is small enough to be almost invisible from one year to the next and only becomes noticeable when you stack up two decades of it.

## What the numbers actually look like

Here's the slope made concrete. Assume a system that produces 10,000 kWh in its first full year and degrades a steady 0.5% annually — both figures chosen as round, illustrative assumptions rather than a promise about any specific system:

| Year | Output vs. year 1 | Illustrative annual production |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 100% | 10,000 kWh |
| 5 | ~98% | ~9,800 kWh |
| 10 | ~96% | ~9,560 kWh |
| 15 | ~93% | ~9,320 kWh |
| 20 | ~91% | ~9,090 kWh |
| 25 | ~89% | ~8,870 kWh |

By year 25 the system is still producing close to 89% of what it made in year one. That's the "year 25 production" figure people brace for, and it's far from a collapse — it's a panel that's given up roughly a tenth of its output over a quarter century. Manufacturer performance warranties are written around exactly this curve, typically guaranteeing something in the mid-80s percent of original output at the 25-year mark, which is why the warranty floor and the real-world slope tend to track each other closely.

## Adding up the lifetime cost of the fade

The savings question isn't about any single year — it's about the total energy the system delivers across its life compared to a hypothetical panel that never faded. Sum the annual production above over 25 years and compare it to 25 years at a flat 10,000 kWh, and the degrading system delivers roughly 94% of the never-fading total. In other words, degradation costs you somewhere around 6% of your lifetime generation versus an imaginary panel frozen at day-one output.

Six percent over 25 years is real money, and it belongs in an honest savings estimate — but it's a haircut, not a haymaker. If your undegraded lifetime savings estimate were, say, a nice round number, degradation trims it by that single-digit fraction. That's the whole effect. The broader accounting of [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) already assumes production that isn't perfectly flat; degradation is simply the mechanism behind the gentle downward drift in the later years of those projections.

## Why it barely moves payback but does move total return

There's a subtlety worth separating: degradation affects your *lifetime* savings more than it affects your *payback period*, and for a simple reason. Payback happens in the early years, when the panels are still near full output. By the time the fade has accumulated to a meaningful percentage — years 15, 20, 25 — you've usually recovered your investment already. So the slow decline mostly nibbles at the *later* savings, the pure-profit years after break-even, rather than delaying break-even itself. This is why [degradation barely moves the payback date](/blog/panel-degradation-solar-payback/) even as it shaves the lifetime total.

That framing matters for how you should treat degradation in a decision. It's a reason to model lifetime returns with a realistic downward slope rather than a flat line — not a reason to hesitate. A good [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) bakes an annual degradation rate into its projection, so the lifetime number it reports already reflects the fade; you don't need to discount it further in your head.

### The first-year drop and the warranty floor

Two practical notes keep the picture honest. First, budget for that slightly larger first-year settling loss, so your "year one" production expectation is the post-settling figure rather than the nameplate rating on a lab test sheet. Second, if a system ever degrades *faster* than its performance warranty allows — dropping below the guaranteed curve — that's a warranty claim, not something you silently absorb. The warranty exists precisely to cap your downside on abnormally fast fade.

The arithmetic lands somewhere reassuring and unglamorous. Your panels will make a little less each year, they'll still be doing real work in their third decade, and the total cost of all that fading is a small, knowable fraction of your lifetime savings — one you can model with a single degradation rate rather than fear as an open-ended drain.
