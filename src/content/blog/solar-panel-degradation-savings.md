There's a particular worry that surfaces whenever someone runs solar savings numbers for the first time: if the panels lose a little output every single year, doesn't that quietly eat the whole return from underneath? The image behind the worry is vivid — a system limping along at half strength a decade in, the promised savings evaporating as the glass slowly gives up. It's a reasonable thing to imagine and a wrong picture to carry around. Degradation is real, it does reduce your savings, and the reduction turns out to be small enough that once you've seen the actual arithmetic, it stops being frightening and becomes just another modest line in the model.

The reason the fear overshoots so badly is that "loses power every year" *sounds* like a cliff and *behaves* like a gentle slope. Those are wildly different things, and the gap between them is worth closing with real numbers, because the honest figure — modest, predictable, single-digit across the system's entire life — is far more useful to a buyer than either the panic on one side or the marketing that waves the whole issue away on the other. Neither the dread nor the dismissal helps you decide anything. The math does.

## The cliff that isn't there

Modern crystalline-silicon panels degrade slowly, and the key word is slowly. A typical planning assumption is a loss of around 0.5% a year after the first year, with many quality panels rated at or below that figure. There's usually a slightly larger drop across the very first year of operation — often a percent or two as the panel settles into its working life — and then the long, shallow decline sets in and holds remarkably steady for decades. That 0.5% annual rate is the number worth anchoring to, because almost everything else about degradation follows from it, and it's small enough that people consistently underestimate how gentle the real curve is.

Put that rate in perspective and the imagined cliff simply disappears. At 0.5% a year, a panel doesn't lose half its output over a decade; it loses roughly 5%. A system that made a given amount of energy in year one is still making about 95% of that in year eleven. The mental model of a solar array running at a fraction of its rated power midway through its life doesn't match how the hardware actually ages — not even close. Panels are unusually well-suited to aging slowly because there's so little to wear out: no moving parts, no fluids, no combustion, just a sealed sheet of silicon under glass converting light. What degradation there is comes from slow, well-understood physical processes in the materials, and manufacturers have spent decades tightening those down. The physics and the typical rates are covered in more depth in [what your system loses each year](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/); for the savings question, the point that matters is that the annual loss is small enough to be nearly invisible from one year to the next. You would never notice it happening. It only becomes a number worth accounting for when you stack up two full decades of it and look at the total.

Here's that slope made concrete. Assume a system that produces 10,000 kWh in its first full year and then degrades a steady 0.5% annually — both numbers chosen as round, illustrative assumptions to reason from rather than a promise about any particular system on any particular roof:

| Year | Output vs. year 1 | Illustrative annual production |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 100% | 10,000 kWh |
| 5 | ~98% | ~9,800 kWh |
| 10 | ~96% | ~9,560 kWh |
| 15 | ~93% | ~9,320 kWh |
| 20 | ~91% | ~9,090 kWh |
| 25 | ~89% | ~8,870 kWh |

By year 25, the system is still producing close to 89% of what it made in year one. That figure — call it "year 25 production," the number people brace themselves for — is a long way from a collapse. It's a panel that has given up roughly a tenth of its output over a full quarter century of daily work outdoors. It's worth noticing, too, that manufacturer performance warranties are written around exactly this curve. They typically guarantee something in the mid-80s percent of original output at the 25-year mark, which is why the warranty floor and the real-world slope tend to track each other so closely: the warranty is essentially the manufacturer betting on the same gentle decline the physics predicts, with a small margin for safety.

What's actually happening inside the panel as it fades is worth a sentence, because it demystifies the number. The decline comes from slow, ordinary physical processes: years of ultraviolet exposure gently degrading the encapsulant and the cells, the daily expansion and contraction of thermal cycling working at the materials, and a small early loss as the cells stabilize under their first serious exposure to light. These aren't defects or signs of a problem — they're the expected weathering of a device that lives outdoors and gets rained on, heated, and frozen for decades. Manufacturers have spent years tightening these mechanisms down, and the trend has been toward slower degradation, not faster; several newer cell designs are rated below the old half-a-percent rule of thumb. Long-term field studies of installed systems have broadly supported degradation in that low single-digit-fraction-per-year range for quality modules, which is why the 0.5% planning figure has held up as a reasonable default rather than an optimistic one. The takeaway is that the slope isn't a manufacturing flaw you might dodge with a luckier panel; it's the physics of leaving silicon out in the weather, and it's been measured, bounded, and warrantied precisely because it's so predictable. A panel that fades at this rate isn't wearing out early — it's doing exactly what a well-made panel is supposed to do on its way to a long working life.

## Adding up the lifetime cost of the fade

The savings question isn't really about any single year's output — it's about the total energy the system delivers across its whole life, compared against a hypothetical panel that somehow never faded at all. That comparison is where degradation's true cost shows up, and it's smaller than the year-by-year figures might lead you to guess. Sum the annual production from the table above across all 25 years, and then compare that sum to 25 years running at a flat, never-declining 10,000 kWh. The degrading system delivers roughly 94% of the never-fading total. Put the other way around, degradation costs you somewhere in the neighborhood of 6% of your lifetime generation, measured against an imaginary panel frozen forever at its day-one output.

It's worth seeing why that total lands near 94% rather than something scarier, because the intuition that "losing power every year" should compound into a big number is exactly what trips people up. The loss each year is small, and crucially it's applied to a base that itself only shrinks slowly, so the reductions never snowball. In the early years, when the system produces the most, the fade has barely begun, so those big-production years are collected almost in full. By the time the cumulative loss grows into double digits, the annual production it's shaving is already the smaller output of an older system. The math works out to a lifetime total only a handful of percent below the never-fading ideal, because the deepest cuts land on the least-productive years. A useful way to pressure-test this is to ask what happens if you're wrong about the rate. Double the assumption — model a full 1% a year instead of 0.5% — and the lifetime haircut roughly doubles too, to somewhere around 11 or 12% rather than 6%. That's a meaningfully worse panel, and it's still not a catastrophe; it's the difference between keeping about 94% and about 88% of the ideal lifetime energy. The answer is robust to the assumption in a way that should be reassuring: even a pessimistic degradation rate doesn't turn the fade into the return-killer the initial worry imagines. What you actually want to avoid is the genuinely bad panel — the bottom-of-the-market module with a poorly specified or poorly honored performance warranty, which is where abnormally fast fade actually shows up. That's a reason to buy a reputable panel with a credible performance guarantee, not a reason to fear degradation as a category. The gap between a good panel's 6% and a bad one's much larger loss is a quality decision you make at purchase, and it's a far bigger lever than the difference between any two reasonable rate assumptions.

None of this argues for ignoring degradation — it argues for sizing it correctly. A few percent off the lifetime total belongs in the estimate, sitting alongside the other honest deductions like inverter replacement and occasional maintenance, but it is not the dominant term in the calculation. When people rank the things that determine whether solar pays, degradation sits well below the electricity rate, the system price, and the incentives — a genuine factor, and correctly a small one.

Six percent over 25 years is real money, and it absolutely belongs in an honest savings estimate — but it's a haircut, not a haymaker. If your undegraded lifetime savings estimate came to some nice round figure, degradation trims it by that single-digit fraction and no more. That is the entire effect, start to finish. It doesn't compound into something menacing, it doesn't accelerate, and it doesn't have a hidden second act. The broader accounting of [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) over their lifetime already assumes production that isn't perfectly flat; degradation is simply the mechanism behind the gentle downward drift you see in the later years of those projections. When a good savings estimate slopes slightly downward over time instead of holding a flat line, this 0.5%-a-year fade is why. It's not an error in the projection and it's not a nasty surprise waiting in year twenty — it's a known, bounded, roughly-6% adjustment baked into the honest version of the number from the start.

This is also the right place to distinguish two things that get muddled: outright failure versus gradual fade. Degradation is the slow, expected, warranty-covered decline every panel undergoes. It is not a panel breaking. A panel that fails outright is a separate and uncommon event handled by the product warranty, not the performance warranty. When people fear "the panels wearing out," they're usually blending these two ideas into one dread, when in reality the slow fade is the one that's near-certain and cheap, and the sudden failure is the one that's rare and covered. Keeping them separate is half the battle in seeing degradation clearly.

## Why it barely moves payback but does move total return

There's a subtlety here worth pulling apart carefully, because it changes how you should treat degradation in an actual decision: the fade affects your *lifetime* savings noticeably more than it affects your *payback period*, and the reason is a matter of timing. Payback happens in the early years of the system's life, when the panels are still near full output and losing almost nothing. By the time the decline has accumulated into a meaningful percentage — years 15, 20, 25 — you have, on a typical system, long since recovered your original investment. So the slow fade mostly nibbles at the *later* savings, the pure-profit years that come after break-even, rather than pushing the break-even point itself further out. The years degradation eats are the years you were already ahead. This is precisely why [degradation barely moves the payback date](/blog/panel-degradation-solar-payback/) even as it shaves a few percent off the lifetime total — the two questions have their money in different decades.

Putting that to work in an actual decision means letting the model, not your anxiety, carry the degradation. When you evaluate solar, you want a projection that slopes the production gently downward at a realistic rate and then reports both the payback year and the lifetime total. Those two outputs answer two different questions — how soon am I whole, and how much do I make in the end — and degradation touches the second far more than the first. There's a second reason the early years dominate that has nothing to do with degradation and reinforces the same conclusion: the time value of money. A dollar saved in year two is worth more than a dollar saved in year twenty, because you have it sooner and can do other things with it. Both effects — the panels fading and future dollars discounting — push the weight of solar's value toward the near term, which is a comforting alignment. The savings that matter most are the ones you collect first, when the panels are freshest and the dollars are worth the most, and those are precisely the savings degradation leaves untouched. So the practical posture is neither to ignore the fade nor to dread it: model it with a single sensible rate, let it pull the later years down where it belongs, and judge the system on the resulting numbers. If you're comparing two proposals, you can even read the assumed degradation rate as a small quality signal — a projection built on an honest 0.5%-ish rate and a credible performance warranty is more trustworthy than one that quietly assumes a flat line to make the lifetime figure look bigger than it will be. A projection that pretends panels never fade isn't optimistic; it's just wrong, and the gap it hides is exactly the 6% this whole exercise set out to measure.

That distinction matters for how you should fold degradation into a decision. It's a solid reason to model your lifetime returns with a realistic downward slope rather than an optimistic flat line — but it is not a reason to hesitate about going solar, because the part it touches is the profit you collect after you've already won your money back. A good [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) bakes an annual degradation rate directly into its projection, so the lifetime number it reports to you already reflects the fade. You don't need to mentally discount its output any further; if anything, discounting it twice would make solar look worse than the honest math says it is. Two practical notes round the picture out and keep it honest in both directions. First, budget for that slightly larger first-year settling loss, so that your "year one" production expectation is the post-settling figure rather than the nameplate rating printed on a lab test sheet under ideal conditions — starting from the lab number sets you up to feel disappointed by a panel that's performing exactly as expected. Second, know that if a system ever degrades *faster* than its performance warranty allows, dropping below the guaranteed curve, that's a warranty claim to file rather than a loss to silently absorb. The performance warranty exists precisely to cap your downside on abnormally fast fade, which means the 6% lifetime figure isn't just a typical outcome — it's roughly the floor the warranty protects, with genuinely bad panels being someone else's problem to remedy. Your panels will make a little less each year, they'll still be doing real, useful work well into their third decade, and the total cost of all that slow fading is a small, knowable slice of your lifetime savings — one you can model with a single degradation rate rather than dread as an open-ended drain on the return.
