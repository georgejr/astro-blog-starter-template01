A solar quote is not priced like a bag of apples, where half as much costs half as much. A meaningful share of every installation is fixed: the permit, the interconnection application, the inspection, the crew mobilizing to your house, the design and engineering. Those costs barely move whether you install 3 kilowatts or 10. So when you shrink a system, the fixed portion doesn't shrink with it — it just spreads over fewer watts, and the price per watt climbs.

That's the whole reason a "minimum" system size exists as a concept. There's no rule against installing two panels; it's that the math gets steadily worse as you go smaller, until the per-watt cost is so high the system can't pay for itself in any reasonable time. For most US homes, the practical floor lands somewhere around 3–4 kW — not a hard law, but the zone where fixed costs stop overwhelming everything else.

## Why small systems cost more per watt

Split a solar quote into two buckets: costs that scale with size, and costs that don't. Panels, racking, and wiring scale — double the system, roughly double those. Permitting, inspection, design, sales overhead, and crew mobilization mostly don't. This second bucket is the reason a small system carries a fee for being small.

The following illustrates the effect with a labeled assumption: hold fixed costs at a flat $6,000 and let hardware-and-labor run at $1.50 per watt. Watch what happens to the total per-watt price as the system shrinks.

| System size | Fixed costs | Scaling costs @ $1.50/W | Total | Effective $/W |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 kW | $6,000 | $3,000 | $9,000 | $4.50 |
| 4 kW | $6,000 | $6,000 | $12,000 | $3.00 |
| 6 kW | $6,000 | $9,000 | $15,000 | $2.50 |
| 10 kW | $6,000 | $15,000 | $21,000 | $2.10 |

The figures are assumptions to show the shape, not a quote — but the shape is real. That 2 kW system costs more than twice the per-watt price of the 10 kW one for the same panels on the same roof. This is also why the deep dive on [why half your solar bill isn't hardware](/blog/solar-soft-costs/) matters most at small sizes: soft costs are precisely the part that refuses to shrink.

## Where the floor actually sits

The practical minimum isn't a single number; it's the point where per-watt cost climbs faster than the value the system delivers. Three things push that floor around.

Your electricity rate is the biggest lever. A small system in a high-rate territory can still pay back reasonably because each kilowatt-hour it produces offsets expensive grid power. The same small system where power is cheap struggles, because the offset per unit is worth less. A tiny array can make sense on an expensive bill and flop on a cheap one — identical hardware, opposite verdicts.

Your goal matters too. If you're trying to zero out a large bill, a sub-4 kW system was never going to get there and the question is moot. But if you have a small bill, a modest roof, or you just want to cover a baseline load, a small system can be exactly right — the [costs of solar for a small home](/blog/solar-cost-small-house/) walk through where that lands. And a few installers offer streamlined small-system packages that trim the fixed overhead, which effectively lowers the floor for their customers specifically.

## When a small system is still worth it

A small array clears the bar in a few recognizable situations. A high electricity rate rescues the per-watt penalty, because expensive avoided power makes even pricey solar pay. Modest usage that a small system can substantially offset means you're not leaving much value on the table by going small. A tight budget that rules out a larger purchase can make a small owned system better than no system, provided the payback still pencils. And the intent to expand later — sizing the initial install so panels can be added down the road — can justify starting small today.

Where it stops being worth it is the mirror image: cheap power, a large bill the system can't dent, and no plan to grow it. In that case the fixed-cost penalty buys you very little offset, and the payback stretches past the point of interest. Whether that describes your situation is the same core question as [is solar worth it at all](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/), just asked at the small end of the range.
