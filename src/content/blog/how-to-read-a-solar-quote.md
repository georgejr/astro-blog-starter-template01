A solar proposal is engineered to feel finished. It usually arrives as a glossy PDF: an aerial photo of your roof with neat blue rectangles laid across it, a large "estimated 25-year savings" figure set in a confident font, and a signature line waiting near the bottom. The layout implies the hard analysis is done and the only thing left for you is a yes or a no. That impression is the most expensive thing in the document. A quote isn't a verdict — it's a stack of assumptions, and nearly every one of them can be checked, questioned, or in some cases negotiated. The homeowners who end up unhappy are rarely the ones who asked too many questions. They're the ones who read the headline number and skipped the dozen lines that produced it.

Reading a proposal well is mostly a translation exercise. Behind the design, every quote is answering four plain questions: how big is the system, how much will it actually make, what does it cost once incentives are accounted for, and what am I really promised if something underperforms or breaks. Once you can find those four answers in any PDF, comparing two installers stops being a contest of who has the slicker brochure and becomes a straightforward comparison of substance.

## What the system specs and production estimate are telling you

Near the top of the proposal sits the hardware: a panel model and its wattage, an inverter type, a panel count, and a total system size in kilowatts. That last number is the spine everything else scales from, so it's worth deriving yourself rather than trusting the headline. If the sheet lists twenty 400-watt panels, the system is 8 kW DC, because 20 × 400 equals 8,000 watts. Do that multiplication and you'll occasionally catch a system size that's been rounded up a little generously, or a page where the panel count and the kilowatt figure quietly disagree.

Cross-check the specs against the roof drawing while you're there. If the design shows sixteen panels on the layout but the equipment list says twenty, someone changed the plan and forgot to update a page, and you want that resolved before you sign, not discovered on install day. The inverter line matters more than most buyers realize: it tells you whether the system runs on a single string inverter, on microinverters, or on optimizers, and that choice shapes both the price and how the array copes with shade and future panel-level problems. If those terms are unfamiliar, the difference is worth understanding now, because it's awkward and costly to change after the fact. Panel wattage itself is less decisive than total system size — a handful of high-wattage panels and a larger count of modest ones can land on the same annual energy, so don't be swayed by a big number on a single module.

The production estimate is where the entire savings case is anchored, and it deserves the most scrutiny of anything on the page. Every proposal states an expected annual output, usually in kilowatt-hours per year, and that figure is built from your system size, your latitude and local sun hours, your roof's tilt and orientation, an allowance for shading, and an assumed system-loss factor. A credible proposal names its source — typically a modeling tool that pulls typical-year weather data for your address — rather than presenting the number as if it fell from the sky.

The fastest way to pressure-test it is to divide annual production by system size, which gives you specific yield: kilowatt-hours per installed kilowatt per year. Across most of the United States that lands somewhere between roughly 1,100 and 1,600, with sunny, south-facing, unshaded roofs near the top of the band and shaded or poorly oriented roofs toward the bottom. Treat those figures as a reasoned range rather than a promise for your specific roof. If a quote claims 1,800 kWh per kilowatt for a partly shaded, north-sloping roof in a cloudy region, the estimate is optimistic — and an optimistic production number quietly inflates the savings headline and shortens the payback the salesperson is showing you. When two proposals disagree on output for the same roof, the one assuming a more aggressive yield will always look like the better deal on paper, which is precisely why the yield check comes before the savings comparison rather than after it.

Two more lines in this part of the sheet reward a close look, and both are easy to skim past. The first is the relationship between the DC system size and the inverter's AC rating. Panels are rated in DC watts, but the inverter can only pass so much AC power through to your home, so a system described as 8 kW DC might legitimately pair with a 7.6 kW inverter. That's a normal, deliberate design choice rather than a mistake — a modestly undersized inverter trims only the very top of the production curve on the brightest afternoons, often costs less, and loses almost nothing over a full year. Understanding it as a design decision, though, keeps you from mistaking it for a discrepancy, or worse, from letting a salesperson quietly describe the larger DC number as if it were what actually reaches your meter. The second is monitoring: check whether it's included and at what granularity — panel-by-panel, string-level, or whole-system — because that granularity is exactly what lets you catch a single underperforming panel years from now instead of staring at a vague, unexplained shortfall you can't diagnose or prove. A proposal that names specific, reputable panels and a specific inverter model, and that spells out the monitoring level, is simply telling you more than one that lists "premium high-efficiency modules" and leaves everything else generic. That specificity is itself a signal about how carefully the company behind the proposal actually thinks, and it's a signal you get for free just by reading the equipment section closely instead of racing past it to the price.

## The price section, and the financing math hiding beneath it

Pricing is where quotes diverge most, and where two proposals for the same roof can look thousands of dollars apart while describing nearly identical work. Three distinct figures are worth isolating. The gross price is the full contracted cost before any incentives touch it. The federal credit is typically shown as 30% of eligible costs under the Residential Clean Energy Credit — but read that line carefully, because it's a credit against your federal tax liability, not an instant discount, so it does nothing to lower the check you actually write to the installer. The net price is simply the gross minus the incentives the proposal assumes you'll receive. A quote that leads with the net figure and buries the gross is steering your eye toward the smallest number, and the smallest number is the one that depends most on assumptions about your tax situation.

The honest way to compare two quotes is on gross price, not net, because installers make different assumptions about which incentives apply to you, and those assumptions are theirs, not facts about your finances. To make gross prices comparable across different system sizes, convert each to cost per watt by dividing the gross price by the DC system size in watts. Say one quote is $24,000 gross for an 8 kW system and another is $21,000 for a 7 kW system. Dividing gives $3.00 per watt against $3.00 per watt — identical pricing, despite a $3,000 gap in the sticker. Without that division you might have grabbed the cheaper-looking number and paid the same rate for less system. [Cost per watt](/blog/solar-cost-per-watt/) is the single most useful figure for putting proposals on equal footing, and you can sanity-check your own number against typical ranges with a [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) before you let any headline savings figure into your head.

If you're paying cash, the gross-and-net picture is close to the whole story. If you're financing, the loan terms can matter more than the hardware, and this is where the fine print earns its keep. Watch for a dealer fee folded into loan-based quotes: to advertise a strikingly low interest rate, some lenders charge the installer a fee that gets baked into the system price. A 1.99% loan carrying a 25% dealer fee can cost you more over its life than a 7% loan with no fee at all, because you're paying interest on a padded principal. The tell is a gap between the cash price and the financed price for the exact same system on the same proposal — if the financed version costs meaningfully more before any interest, that difference is the fee in disguise. It's also worth checking whether the advertised monthly payment assumes you'll apply the federal tax credit back to the loan principal within a set window; if you don't, or can't use the full credit, the payment often steps up to a higher tier after that window closes. None of this makes financing a bad idea. It just means the "as low as $X per month" line is a conditional promise with several assumptions stacked behind it, not a fixed cost you can count on.

A few smaller price mechanics hide in this same section and can move the real cost more than they first appear to. Watch for adders — separate line items for things like a steep or complex roof, a two-story install, an unusually long conduit run, or a required main-panel upgrade — because a headline cost per watt can look perfectly competitive right up until three or four adders stack on top of it. Ask directly whether the quoted price is firm or subject to a change order if the site survey turns up something unexpected, and get the conditions for any such change in writing, so that a later adjustment is one you agreed to in advance rather than one that simply materializes on an invoice. If the proposal is a lease or a power-purchase arrangement rather than an outright purchase, look hard for an annual escalator — a clause that raises what you pay by a few percent every year — because that single line can quietly erode or erase the savings the headline promised over the life of the agreement, and it deserves the same scrutiny you'd give a loan term. Confirm, too, how sales tax and any permit or interconnection fees are handled: bundled into the number you see, or added on later. A price that excludes them isn't the price you'll actually pay. None of these mechanics is automatically a reason to walk away, but each is a place where the confident number on the front page can drift from the number on your final invoice, and reading them now — while you still have leverage and haven't signed — is the difference between one fixed understanding and a series of small surprises delivered to you one at a time after the contract is already in force.

## Warranties, exclusions, and reading a second quote against the first

A proposal is also a contract sketch, and the guarantees vary far more between companies than buyers expect. Several warranties are usually in play at once, and they're not interchangeable. The panel product warranty covers manufacturing defects in the modules. The panel performance warranty guarantees the panels still produce a stated percentage of their original rating after 25 years or so. The inverter warranty stands separately and often runs a different length depending on the inverter type. And the workmanship warranty — the installer's own guarantee on the labor, the mounting, and the roof penetrations — is the one most tightly bound to the specific company in front of you. A thirty-year hardware warranty means little if the outfit responsible for the labor side isn't around in five years to honor a roof leak, which is why the durability of the installer matters as much as the paperwork.

Look, too, for a production guarantee. Some installers promise the system will generate a minimum amount of energy over a defined period and compensate you if it falls short; many make no such commitment at all. Its presence or absence tells you how much confidence the company is willing to put in its own production estimate — the same estimate that anchors your savings case. Then scan deliberately for what the price does not include. Main panel upgrades, roof reinforcement, tree trimming, and permit or interconnection fees sometimes appear as "not included" or as separate line items you might reasonably have assumed were bundled. A lower headline price with three meaningful exclusions can end up more expensive than a higher one with none, and these buried exclusions are a large part of [why quotes for the same house vary](/blog/why-solar-quotes-vary/) by thousands of dollars.

Once you can read a single proposal this way, the entire point of getting a second one is to expose the assumptions baked into the first. Put the two side by side on the numbers that are genuinely comparable: gross cost per watt, specific yield, the panel and inverter tier, the length of the workmanship warranty, and the list of exclusions. Deliberately ignore the "estimated 25-year savings" figures when you compare, because each installer built that number on its own private assumptions about rate escalation, degradation, and production, and no two are calculated the same way. Comparing those headline figures is comparing two different spreadsheets, not two systems.

A structured walk through that comparison lives in the guide to [comparing solar quotes](/blog/compare-solar-quotes/), which lines up the categories worth weighing against each other. The goal was never to find the lowest sticker price. It's to understand exactly what each price buys — the hardware, the labor, the guarantees, and everything left off the page — so that when you finally sign, nothing in the PDF has the power to surprise you later.

Two things belong in that comparison even though they never appear as a line item anywhere on the proposal. The first is the installer's track record: how long they've operated in your area, whether they use their own trained crews or subcontract the roof work out, and what recent local customers say about the parts that went wrong — a slow leak, a monitoring glitch, a permit that stalled for months — rather than only the parts that went right. A company that still answers the phone a year after installation is worth a premium no spec sheet can capture, because every warranty you're comparing is only as good as the business standing behind it, and a thirty-year guarantee from a company that folds in five is worth nothing at all. The second is the fit between what each proposal promises and what your roof and usage genuinely need. A quote can be cheaper per watt and still be the wrong system — oversized for your consumption, sited on a plane that underproduces, or built around an inverter architecture that struggles with the specific shade your roof happens to have. Hold each proposal against the four questions you started with: how big is it, how much will it really make, what does it truly cost after only the incentives you can actually use, and what are you promised if something fails. The proposal that answers all four clearly, with specific equipment and honest assumptions, is usually the one to trust even when it isn't the lowest number on the page. Price matters, but it belongs at the end of the process as the final filter, not at the beginning as the first. The buyers who are still satisfied years later are almost never the ones who chased the cheapest sticker. They're the ones who understood exactly what they were signing and found no surprises waiting inside it.
