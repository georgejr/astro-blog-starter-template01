Community solar sells you a share of someone else's array. You don't buy panels, you don't touch your roof, and you don't pay for an installation. Instead you subscribe to a portion of a large solar project built somewhere in your utility region, and the electricity that share produces shows up as credits on your monthly bill. In exchange, you pay the subscription operator for those credits — but at a discount to their face value. That discount is the entire savings story, and as an orientation figure it typically lands somewhere in a 5–15% range on the portion of your bill the subscription covers.

That is a very different proposition from rooftop solar, and the difference is the first thing to be clear about before deciding whether it's worth signing up for. Owning an array on your own roof can cut a bill by far more than 15%, and eventually it eliminates the cost of that power entirely once the system is paid off. Community solar never does either. What it offers instead is a modest, ongoing discount with no upfront cost, no equipment on your property, and no long-term commitment tied to your specific roof. That combination is unattractive if you're comparing it head-to-head against owning — and genuinely valuable if owning isn't available to you, which is precisely the audience community solar exists to serve.

## How the credits actually flow

The mechanics are worth getting straight, because "community solar" describes a billing arrangement rather than a physical connection, and the mental picture most people start with is wrong. Your home does not draw power from the shared array. It draws power from the grid exactly as it always has, through the same wires and the same meter. Somewhere else in your utility territory — a field, a warehouse roof, a former brownfield — a large solar installation generates electricity and feeds it into that same grid. Your subscription assigns you a defined slice of that installation's output, and each month the utility credits your bill for the value of the electricity your slice produced. The electrons never travel to your house in any meaningful sense; the accounting travels to your bill.

You then pay the community solar provider for those credits, but for less than their face value, and that gap is your saving. Walk through a simplified month: if your assigned share generated $100 worth of bill credits and you paid the provider $90 for them, you came out $10 ahead that month. Multiply that spread across a year of production and you have your annual saving. This is why the headline number is expressed as a percentage discount rather than a fixed dollar figure — the credits scale with how much your share actually produces, which varies with the seasons and the weather, and your saving is a slice of that variable amount. A summer month with strong production generates more credits than a gray winter month, so the dollar saving moves around even though the percentage discount stays roughly constant.

Because the value tracks credits rather than a flat payment, the arithmetic behind community solar is a lighter version of the same question that governs rooftop solar. It's still fundamentally about how much a solar array offsets on a power bill — the same logic worked through in [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) — except that a third party owns the panels, takes on all the cost and risk of building and maintaining them, and keeps most of the upside for doing so. Your share of that upside is the discount, and it's smaller than an owner's share precisely because you took none of the owner's cost or risk. That's not a flaw in the model; it's the trade the model is built on.

## Who it fits, and where it falls short

The appeal of community solar is sharpest for the households that can't do rooftop solar at all, and for them it's often the only route to any solar savings whatsoever. Renters have no standing to install panels on a roof they don't own. Condo and apartment owners rarely control the roof above them. People in the middle of a lease, or planning to move within a few years, have little reason to invest in a rooftop system they won't own long enough to pay back. For all of these, a rooftop install isn't a worse option — it's simply not an option, and community solar is what fills that gap with a subscription that follows the account rather than the property.

The second natural fit is homeowners who could theoretically install panels but whose roofs don't cooperate. A roof that's heavily shaded, too small to fit a worthwhile array, oriented the wrong way, or structurally unsuited to carry panels can make a rooftop system a genuinely poor investment — the exact set of conditions assessed in [whether your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/). When the honest answer there is no, community solar sidesteps the roof entirely, since the array's location and orientation are the provider's problem, not yours. And beyond the roof-constrained, there's a third group: owners who could install but simply don't want the upfront cost, the maintenance responsibility, or the multi-decade commitment. For them a subscription is a low-friction way to shave a bill without launching a home-improvement project.

Where community solar falls short is a matter of design, not defect, and it's worth being blunt about so the decision is made with clear eyes. The ceiling is low: the discount is the whole benefit, so a 10% cut stays a 10% cut, it will never approach the bill reduction an owned rooftop system delivers, and it never stops being a monthly payment. There's no asset at the end of it — you're renting a discount, not building equity, and the day you cancel, the savings stop with nothing left behind. You also get no backup power, because you own no hardware; a grid outage takes your community-solar-supplied home down exactly like any other. Program terms, cancellation rules, and savings guarantees vary widely between providers and between states, so two subscriptions advertising similar discounts can carry very different fine print. And availability is genuinely uneven: many regions have no community solar program at all, because these projects depend on specific state policy structures to exist in the first place. A great community solar deal in one state has no counterpart across the border in another.

## Reading a subscription honestly

Judge a community solar offer on two things: the discount rate and the terms wrapped around it. A larger discount is obviously better, but read carefully how it's calculated, because the structure matters as much as the headline. Some offers are a clean fixed percentage off the credit value — simple and predictable. Others are structured so the "savings" depend on production that swings month to month, which makes the advertised number more of an average than a guarantee. Then read the exit. A subscription that's difficult or expensive to leave is worth less than its headline discount implies, because your ability to walk away if a better option appears is part of the value. Cancellation windows, notice periods, and any lock-in belong in your evaluation right next to the discount percentage.

Two logistics are worth checking before you sign, because they shape how painless the arrangement actually is day to day. The first is how you're billed. Some programs consolidate everything onto your existing utility bill, so the credits and your payment for them net out in one place, while others send a separate invoice from the subscription operator — which means two bills to track and two payments to make instead of one. The second is what happens if you move. A subscription tied to your utility account may not travel with you across service territories, so a relocation can force a cancellation you never planned for, which is one more reason the exit terms deserve as much attention as the discount itself. Neither of these is a dealbreaker, but both are exactly the kind of friction a clean headline savings number quietly leaves out.

For a homeowner who genuinely could go either way, the comparison to draw is community solar's steady, modest discount against rooftop solar's larger but front-loaded return. This is where people sometimes talk themselves out of ownership for the wrong reason — they assume that without generous net metering, a rooftop system barely beats a subscription. Often it still wins comfortably over its full lifespan, even under less favorable export rules, because ownership eventually zeroes out the cost of that power while a subscription bills you forever. The scenarios in [whether solar still saves money without net metering](/blog/solar-savings-without-net-metering/) show how that holds up more robustly than the intuition suggests. The way to settle it for your own situation is to put a real number on the rooftop side: run your own rooftop payback through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/), and weigh the subscription against it. If a roof install pays back in a range you'd accept, its lifetime savings will usually dwarf what a subscription returns over the same years. If the payback is unacceptable, or your roof rules a rooftop system out entirely, then the community option's modest discount may well be the best solar deal actually available to you — and a modest, real saving with zero cost and zero commitment is a perfectly good thing to take.

## FAQ

**How much does community solar actually save?**
Typically a modest percentage off the covered portion of your bill — often somewhere in a 5% to 15% range, depending on the program and how the discount is structured. It won't match the savings of an owned rooftop system, but it requires no upfront cost, no equipment, and no long-term commitment to a specific roof.

**Do I need a good roof or any equipment?**
No, and that's the entire point. Your home draws power from the grid as usual, and the solar array is located elsewhere in your utility region. No panels are installed on your property, so your roof's condition, size, shading, and orientation are all irrelevant to whether you can subscribe.

**Is community solar available everywhere?**
No. These programs depend on state-level policy to exist, so many areas have none at all. Availability, terms, and discount levels vary widely from state to state and provider to provider, which makes reading the specific offer in front of you far more useful than any national average.
