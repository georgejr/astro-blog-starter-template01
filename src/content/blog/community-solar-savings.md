Community solar sells you a share of someone else's array. You don't buy panels, you don't touch your roof, and you don't pay for an installation. Instead you subscribe to a portion of a large solar project built somewhere in your utility region, and the electricity that share produces shows up as credits on your monthly bill. In exchange, you pay the subscription operator for those credits — at a discount. That discount is the entire savings story, and as an orientation figure it typically lands somewhere in a 5–15% range on the portion of your bill the subscription covers.

That's a very different proposition from rooftop solar, and the difference matters. Owning an array on your own roof can cut a bill by far more than 15% and eventually eliminate the cost of that power entirely once the system is paid off. Community solar never does that. What it offers instead is a modest, ongoing discount with no upfront cost, no equipment, and no commitment to your specific roof — which is exactly why it exists for the households rooftop solar leaves out.

## How the credits actually flow

The mechanics are worth getting straight, because "community solar" describes a billing arrangement, not a physical connection. Your home still draws all its power from the grid exactly as before. Somewhere else in your utility territory, a shared solar farm generates electricity and feeds it to that same grid. Your subscription assigns you a slice of that farm's output, and the utility credits your bill for the value of the electricity your slice produced that month.

You then pay the community solar provider for those credits, but at less than their face value — that gap is your saving. If your share generated $100 of bill credits and you paid the provider $90 for them, you netted $10 that month. Multiply across a year and you have the annual saving, which is why the headline number is a percentage discount rather than a fixed dollar figure: it scales with how much your share produces and what you pay for it.

Because the value tracks credits rather than a fixed payment, the arithmetic resembles a lighter version of the [how much solar panels actually save](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) question — just with a third party owning the panels and taking most of the upside.

## Who community solar is built for

The appeal is sharpest for the households that can't do rooftop solar at all. Renters, condo owners, and anyone without control of their roof are shut out of a rooftop install by definition, and community solar is often the only way for them to get any solar savings on a power bill.

The other natural fit is homeowners whose roofs don't work. A roof that's too shaded, too small, wrongly oriented, or structurally unsuited can make a rooftop system a poor investment — the exact conditions covered in [whether your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/). When the answer there is no, community solar sidesteps the roof entirely. And for owners who could install but don't want the upfront cost, the maintenance responsibility, or the long commitment, a subscription is a low-friction way to shave a bill without a project.

### Where it falls short

Community solar's ceiling is low by design. The discount is the whole benefit, so a 10% cut is a 10% cut — it won't approach the bill reduction of an owned rooftop system, and it never stops being a payment. There's no asset at the end; you're renting a discount, not building equity. You also don't get backup power, since you own no hardware, and program terms, cancellation rules, and savings guarantees vary widely between providers and states. Availability is uneven too: many regions have no program at all, because these projects depend on specific state policy structures to exist.

## Reading a subscription honestly

Judge a community solar offer on two numbers: the discount rate and the terms around it. A larger discount is better, but read how it's calculated — some are fixed percentages off credit value, others are structured so the "savings" depend on production that varies month to month. Check the cancellation policy and any lock-in, because a subscription that's hard to leave is worth less than its headline discount suggests.

For a homeowner who could go either way, the comparison to draw is community solar's steady small discount against rooftop solar's larger but front-loaded return. Even without net metering, an owned system can still beat a subscription over its lifespan in many cases — the scenarios in [whether solar still saves money without net metering](/blog/solar-savings-without-net-metering/) show how that holds up. Running your own rooftop payback through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) gives you the number to weigh the subscription against: if a roof install pays back in a range you'd accept, its lifetime savings will usually dwarf a subscription's; if it doesn't, or your roof rules it out, the community option's modest discount may be the best solar deal actually available to you.

## FAQ

**How much does community solar actually save?**
Typically a modest percentage off the covered portion of your bill — often somewhere in a 5% to 15% range, depending on the program and how the discount is structured. It won't match the savings of an owned rooftop system, but it requires no upfront cost.

**Do I need a good roof or any equipment?**
No. That's the point. Your home draws power from the grid as usual, and the solar array is located elsewhere. No panels are installed on your property, so roof condition and orientation are irrelevant.

**Is community solar available everywhere?**
No. These programs depend on state-level policy to exist, so many areas have none. Availability, terms, and discount levels vary widely, which makes reading the specific offer more important than any national average.
