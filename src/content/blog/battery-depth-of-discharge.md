Two home batteries can both advertise "13.5 kWh" on the box and still hand you different amounts of energy to actually use, and the reason hides in a spec most shoppers skim past: depth of discharge. It is the difference between how much energy a battery *contains* and how much the manufacturer will let you *take out* before you recharge it. Get that distinction wrong and you will oversize or undersize your storage without realizing you did, either overpaying for capacity you cannot reach or coming up short on the backup you thought you had bought. It is a simple idea once you see it clearly, so it is worth pinning down properly rather than leaving it as a bit of fine print you nodded past on the spec sheet.

## What the number measures, and why it exists at all

Depth of discharge, usually written DoD, is the percentage of a battery's total capacity that you draw down in use. A battery taken from full to 20 percent remaining has been discharged to 80 percent depth of discharge. Run it all the way down to empty and you have hit 100 percent DoD. It is the mirror image of state of charge, which describes the same moment from the other end: 80 percent depth of discharge and 20 percent state of charge are the same instant, just measured from opposite directions. Once that framing clicks, the spec stops being abstract. A rated DoD is simply the maker telling you how far down into the tank they are willing to let you reach on a normal cycle, expressed as a share of the whole tank.

The reason the number exists in the first place traces back to how batteries wear out. Draining a battery completely, over and over, tends to age it faster than shallower cycling does. Historically, deep and repeated full discharges shortened battery life noticeably, so manufacturers learned to specify a maximum recommended depth of discharge and hold the rest back as a protective buffer. That reserved slice never shows up in your daily usable energy. It exists purely to keep the cells healthy over years of cycling and to keep the warranty intact, which is why the maker, not you, sets the limit. When you read a DoD figure on a spec sheet, you are reading how much of the battery its designers consider safe to cycle regularly without accelerating wear beyond what the warranty assumes. A conservative DoD is a design choice trading a little usable energy today for a longer service life; an aggressive one does the reverse. Neither is inherently wrong, but they are different bets, and the spec sheet is where the bet is disclosed. Understanding that the buffer is a deliberate health reserve, rather than wasted capacity or a marketing trick, is the first step to reading these numbers without either overvaluing the headline figure or resenting the reserved portion.

It also helps to know that a rated DoD is a recommendation backed by the warranty, not a hard wall the battery slams into. A management system may physically allow a slightly deeper draw in an emergency, and manufacturers sometimes distinguish between the depth you should cycle to every day and a lower floor the hardware will tolerate occasionally. The everyday number is the one that matters for planning, because it is the depth the warranty assumes you will respect over thousands of cycles. Treating the occasional deeper reach as your normal operating point is how a battery ages faster than its rating promises, which is precisely the outcome the buffer exists to prevent. Read the rated DoD as the line the designers drew for healthy daily use, and let the rare deeper discharge stay rare.

## Nominal capacity versus what you can actually use

This is where depth of discharge stops being trivia and turns into a dollars-and-decisions issue, because batteries carry two capacity numbers that are emphatically not the same. The **nominal** or **total** capacity is the full energy the cells hold, and it is the big number that leads the marketing. The **usable** capacity is that nominal figure multiplied by the rated depth of discharge, and it is the energy you can actually pull on a normal cycle, which is to say the number you will live on when the power is out or the sun is down. A 10 kWh battery rated for 90 percent depth of discharge gives you about 9 kWh of usable energy. The same 10 kWh nominal battery rated at 80 percent gives you 8 kWh. That difference, a full kilowatt-hour, is real capacity you either can or cannot count on, and it is completely invisible if you only compare the headline nominal numbers on two competing products. Two batteries with identical nominal ratings can leave you meaningfully further apart in what they deliver, and the gap is exactly the DoD.

This is closely tied to the broader distinction between the energy rating and the power rating on a battery spec, which trips up a lot of shoppers who assume a single big number tells the whole story. We untangle that pair in [kWh versus kW for home batteries](/blog/battery-kwh-vs-kw/), and it is worth reading alongside this, because depth of discharge is specifically the multiplier that converts the energy rating you are quoted into the energy you will actually use. The honest way to compare products, then, is on usable capacity, never on nominal, because a battery with a smaller total capacity but a higher rated DoD can genuinely deliver more real energy than a physically larger unit that reserves a bigger protective buffer for itself. The bigger box is not automatically the more capable one once you account for how much of each the maker will let you touch.

Chemistry is the reason different batteries reserve different buffers, and it explains why usable capacity has crept so close to nominal in modern systems. The two common home-battery chemistries tolerate deep discharge differently, which is one of several reasons the choice between them matters, a comparison we lay out in full in [LFP versus NMC home batteries](/blog/lfp-vs-nmc-home-batteries/). Lithium iron phosphate, LFP, which dominates current home storage, tolerates deep cycling well and is frequently rated for depth of discharge in the range of 90 to 100 percent. That tolerance is why many modern LFP systems advertise usable capacity nearly equal to their nominal capacity: the reserved buffer is small because the chemistry does not need a large one to hit its cycle-life targets. Older lead-acid batteries sat at the opposite extreme, and the contrast is stark. Pushing a lead-acid bank much past 50 percent DoD dramatically shortened its life, so an off-grid lead-acid system had to be sized at roughly double its usable target just to keep the cells in a safe operating range. Half the bank was buffer. The move to lithium chemistry is a big part of why home storage got so much more compact for the same usable energy, and depth of discharge is precisely the lever that changed. It also connects to how long a battery lasts overall, because manufacturers rate cycle life *at* a specified DoD, and the two figures travel together in the warranty, a relationship we go deeper on in [what actually wears a solar battery out](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/). The rated DoD, in other words, is not only about today's usable energy; it reflects a design balance between how much of the battery you get to use and how many years the cells will hold up while you use it.

## Turning DoD into a sizing number

For practical sizing, the rule is short and it saves people from a common mistake: build your plan around usable capacity, not the number printed on the box. Decide how many kilowatt-hours you actually need to carry overnight or ride through an outage, then work backward to a nominal capacity using the product's rated depth of discharge. Skipping that step is exactly how people end up a kilowatt-hour or two short of the backup they were sure they had purchased, because they matched their overnight load to a nominal figure that includes energy the battery will never release. Sizing to usable capacity closes that gap before it becomes a dark-house surprise.

There is one more wrinkle that reduces your everyday energy below even the rated DoD, and it catches owners who have not thought it through. The depth you are *allowed* and the depth you actually *use* each day are not always the same. Many home systems let you set a backup reserve, a slice of capacity deliberately held back so there is something in the tank if the grid fails unexpectedly. When you enable that reserve, your everyday cycling stops short of the rated maximum even though the hardware could physically go deeper. That is a settings choice layered on top of the manufacturer's DoD limit, and it further trims the energy available for routine daily use. If you plan to keep a reserve for outages, and many people do, factor it in the same way you factor the rated DoD: it comes off the top of what you can count on night to night, stacking with the manufacturer's buffer rather than replacing it. A battery rated for 90 percent DoD with a 20 percent reserve set aside is only cycling about 70 percent of its nominal capacity on an ordinary day.
