A cracked panel or a sudden dip in production forces a question that sounds simple and isn't: fix it, swap it, or leave it? The answer starts with a fact about how panels are actually built, because that fact quietly removes one of the three options for the panel itself. A solar module is a sealed laminate — tempered glass, a layer of silicon cells, a plastic encapsulant that bonds everything, a protective backsheet, an aluminum frame, and a junction box on the rear — all fused into a single weatherproof unit at the factory under heat and pressure. It is not an appliance you can open, diagnose, and repair component by component. There is no cover to unscrew and no cell to solder back in place; the laminate is meant never to be opened again once it leaves the line. That one design reality shapes the entire decision, because for the module itself, "repair" is essentially off the menu. The real choice for a failed panel is narrower than it first appears: replace it, or leave it in place and monitor it. Understanding that from the outset keeps you from chasing a fix that does not exist and focuses the decision on the questions that actually have answers.

## What's actually broken, and how your wiring changes the stakes

Before deciding anything, the first job is to separate a failed module from a failed thing attached to the module, because the second category is often genuinely repairable even though the first is not. Failures that live inside the sealed laminate are the ones you cannot fix in place. Cracked or shattered glass, almost always from impact — hail, a falling branch, a dropped tool during roof work — is the most visible of these. Microcracks and cell damage are more insidious, sometimes invisible to the eye, and they create hotspots that drag down a panel's output and can worsen over time. Delamination, where the bonded layers begin to separate and let moisture creep in, and a discolored or degrading backsheet, are slower failures of the laminate itself. So is internal degradation running faster than the normal, gentle fade that every panel is expected to show across its life. Because all of these live inside the fused laminate, each one makes the module a replace-or-leave unit rather than a repair candidate. [Common solar panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) catalogs how each of these presents so you can recognize which one you may be looking at, and [how tough solar panels really are](/blog/solar-panel-hail-storm-damage/) covers the impact damage behind most of the sudden, dramatic failures — useful context, because a panel that looks catastrophically broken and one that is quietly underperforming call for very different urgency.

The more encouraging category is everything around the module, and a surprising share of "the panel is dead" calls turn out to belong here. The connectors that link panels together — the standardized MC4 plugs — can corrode or be improperly crimped during installation, throttling or killing a connection while the panel behind them is perfectly fine. Wiring can be damaged by abrasion, rodents, or weather. A junction box or one of its internal bypass diodes can fail. A mount can loosen. And very often the real culprit is not the panel at all but the inverter, or a single microinverter or optimizer attached to the panel, which fails far more frequently than the module it serves. All of these are field-serviceable by a technician, which is exactly why diagnosis has to come before any decision to replace. Swapping a perfectly good panel will not fix a corroded connector, and replacing an inverter will not mend a shattered module — so spending money before you know which layer actually failed is how homeowners end up paying twice. The discipline is to insist on a real diagnosis first: is the fault inside the laminate, or in something bolted to it? That single question routes you toward either the narrow replace-or-leave decision for a genuine module failure or the ordinary, cheaper repair of a component around it, and getting the routing right is most of the value of the whole exercise.

The instinct when a single panel fails in a twenty-panel array is that you have lost one-twentieth of your output, or about five percent, and you can afford to take your time. Sometimes that instinct is exactly right. Sometimes it is badly wrong, and the reason comes down to how your panels are wired together — which means the same physical failure can be a minor annoyance on one system and a significant daily loss on another. On a traditional string inverter, panels are wired in series, connected end to end like segments of a single garden hose. In a series circuit, the current that can flow is limited by the weakest link in the chain, so a panel throttled by a hotspot, a partial failure, or heavy shade does not just lose its own share — it can pull down the production of the entire string it belongs to. One compromised panel drags on all its series neighbors, turning a one-panel problem into a whole-string loss that is far larger than that panel's nominal fraction of the array. That is a strong argument for diagnosing and addressing a string-system failure promptly rather than letting it sit, because every sunny day you delay, you may be paying for lost production across many panels, not one.

On a system built with microinverters or power optimizers, the picture is far more forgiving, and this is one of the main reasons those architectures exist. Each panel operates independently, with its own small inverter or optimizer managing it, so a single failed panel costs you roughly that panel's share of production and no more — its neighbors are not chained to it and carry on at full output. The identical failure that would drag a whole string down on a string inverter is quarantined to one module on a module-level system. Same broken panel, very different urgency and very different cost while you decide what to do, which is why knowing which architecture you have is the first thing to establish once you know a panel has genuinely failed. If you land on replacement, one further practical obstacle shapes the job on string systems in particular: matching. Solar panel models are revised and retired constantly, and a module bought several years ago may simply be discontinued, so dropping a brand-new, higher-wattage panel into an old series string can create a mismatch — because, again, a series string runs at the pace of its members, and a mismatched panel either drags the string down or is itself throttled to fit. The replacement often needs to be electrically similar to its neighbors rather than the best panel available on the shelf today. This is far easier on module-level systems, where a mismatched replacement mostly affects only itself, and harder on string systems, where an installer may have to source a compatible used or new-old-stock panel, or reconfigure the string entirely. It is a solvable problem in every case, but it is the reason "just replace the one panel" occasionally turns into a more involved job than the homeowner expected, and it is worth anticipating before you assume a swap will be quick.

## Making the call: warranty, safety, and cost

Once you know what failed and how your system is wired, the decision usually resolves along a few clear lines. The table below sketches the common cases; treat the cost notes as general guidance rather than quotes for your situation.

| Situation | Usual best move |
| --- | --- |
| Under product warranty, clear defect | Claim it — replacement panel likely covered |
| Impact damage, out of warranty, string inverter | Replace promptly (it may be dragging the string) |
| Impact damage, out of warranty, microinverters | Replace, but less urgent — loss is isolated |
| Problem is a connector, wire, or inverter | Repair the component; keep the panel |
| Minor cosmetic damage, output normal | Often fine to leave and monitor |

The warranty line at the top deserves the most emphasis, because it changes the economics of the whole decision, and it contains a trap that catches people off guard. A panel's product warranty may cover a defective module for many years, which sounds like it makes a replacement free — but a warranty on the part rarely covers the labor to swap it, and roof labor is a real and sometimes substantial cost. You can be handed a free replacement panel and still face a meaningful bill for the technician's time to get on the roof, remove the old module, install the new one, and reconnect it, possibly reconfiguring a string in the process. That split — who pays for the component versus who pays for the work — is exactly what [workmanship vs equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) untangles, and it is worth reading your actual coverage before you assume a warranty claim comes at no cost. Knowing which warranty covers what, and for how long, is often the single biggest factor in whether replacement is cheap or expensive.

For damage that came from an event rather than a defect, there is a third payer worth checking before you reach for your own wallet: your homeowner's insurance. Sudden impact damage from hail, a windborne branch, or a falling limb is frequently covered under a standard policy, and a claim can pick up both the panel and the labor in a way a manufacturer's warranty never will, since warranties exclude accidental damage while insurance is built for exactly it. Document the damage with photos and dates before anything is touched, and weigh the likely repair cost against your deductible so a small claim does not cost you more in the long run than it returns. Sourcing the work is the other practical hurdle. The cleanest path is to call the company that installed the system, since they know the equipment, the string layout, and the warranty status — but installers do go out of business, and if yours has, you will need a solar service company willing to work on someone else's array, which is a real and growing niche. Matching an out-of-production panel sometimes means turning to the used and salvage market, where a compatible module of the right vintage can be found even when the model is long discontinued. It is a solvable problem in nearly every case; it is just worth knowing before a failure that "find someone to swap one panel" occasionally takes a few phone calls rather than one.

Two forces tend to pull the "leave it and monitor" option out of reach even when the production loss alone might not justify acting. The first is safety, and it is non-negotiable. Shattered glass, exposed conductors, visible scorching, or any sign of overheating is not a cosmetic judgment call — those conditions need to be addressed regardless of how much output the panel is or isn't costing you, because the risk is no longer about kilowatt-hours. The second is the series effect covered above: on a string system, a struggling panel taxes its neighbors, so leaving it in place can quietly cost more production than the one panel represents, which shifts the math toward acting sooner than the panel's own share would suggest. Outside of those two situations — a genuine safety concern, or a string-dragging failure — a panel with only light cosmetic damage and normal, healthy output is frequently best left alone and simply watched, since the cost and roof risk of touching it may exceed any benefit. When you are weighing the out-of-pocket side of a replacement, the panel, the labor, and the occasional string reconfiguration together, the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) helps you set that expense against the value of the production you would actually recover, which is ultimately the number the whole decision turns on. A cheap, isolated fault on a microinverter system that is barely denting your output is a genuinely different decision from a string-dragging failure you will keep paying for on every sunny day you delay — and framing it that way, in recovered dollars against spent dollars, is what turns an anxious "should I fix it?" into a decision you can actually stand behind.
