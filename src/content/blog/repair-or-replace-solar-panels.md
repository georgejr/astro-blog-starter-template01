A cracked panel or a sudden dip in production forces a question that sounds simple and isn't: fix it, swap it, or leave it? The answer starts with a fact about how panels are built. A solar module is a sealed laminate — tempered glass, silicon cells, a plastic encapsulant, a backsheet, an aluminum frame, and a junction box — fused into one weatherproof unit at the factory. You can't open it up and fix a cell the way you'd fix an appliance. That single design reality shapes the whole decision: for the module itself, "repair" usually isn't on the menu, so the real choice is replace or leave alone.

## What's actually broken, and whether it's even the panel

Before deciding anything, separate a failed *module* from a failed *thing attached to* the module, because the second is often genuinely repairable.

### Failures inside the sealed module

These are the ones you generally can't fix in place:

- **Cracked or shattered glass**, usually from impact — hail, a falling branch, a dropped tool.
- **Microcracks and cell damage**, sometimes invisible, that create hotspots and drag down output.
- **Delamination**, where the layers separate and let moisture in, or a discolored, degrading backsheet.
- **Internal degradation** faster than the normal slow fade panels are expected to show.

Because these live inside the laminate, the module is a replace-or-leave unit. [Common solar panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) catalogs how each of these presents, and [how tough solar panels really are](/blog/solar-panel-hail-storm-damage/) covers the impact damage that causes most sudden failures.

### Failures around the module that a technician can fix

Plenty of "the panel is dead" calls turn out to be something adjacent and repairable: a corroded or improperly crimped MC4 connector, damaged wiring, a faulty junction box or bypass diode, a loose mount, or — very often — the inverter or a single microinverter/optimizer rather than the panel at all. These are field-serviceable. Diagnosis has to come first, because replacing a perfectly good panel won't fix a bad connector, and swapping an inverter won't fix a shattered module.

## Why one bad panel matters more than one-twentieth

The instinct is that a single failed panel in a twenty-panel array costs you 1/20th of your output, or about 5%. Sometimes that's true. Sometimes it's much worse, and the reason is how your panels are wired.

On a traditional string inverter, panels are wired in series, like a garden hose with several segments. In series, current is limited by the weakest link — so one panel throttled by a hotspot or partial failure can pull down the entire string it belongs to, not just its own share. That turns a one-panel problem into a whole-string production loss, which is a strong argument for fixing it promptly.

On a system with microinverters or power optimizers, each panel operates independently. A single failed panel costs you roughly that panel's share and no more, because its neighbors aren't chained to it. Same failure, very different urgency — and knowing which architecture you have tells you how much a single dead panel is actually costing you while you decide.

## The replacement complication: matching

If you land on replacement, one practical obstacle shapes the job: matching. Solar panel models are updated frequently, and a module bought several years ago may be discontinued. Dropping a brand-new, higher-wattage panel into an old series string can create a mismatch, because — again — the string runs at the pace of its members. The replacement often needs to be electrically similar to its neighbors, not simply the best panel on the shelf today.

This is easier on module-level systems (microinverters or optimizers), where a mismatched panel mostly affects only itself, and harder on string systems, where the installer may need to source a compatible used or new-old-stock panel, or reconfigure the string. It's a solvable problem, but it's why "just replace the one panel" is occasionally more involved than it sounds.

## Putting it together

The decision usually resolves along a few clear lines. The table sketches the common cases; treat the cost notes as general guidance, not quotes.

| Situation | Usual best move |
| --- | --- |
| Under product warranty, clear defect | Claim it — replacement panel likely covered |
| Impact damage, out of warranty, string inverter | Replace promptly (it may be dragging the string) |
| Impact damage, out of warranty, microinverters | Replace, but less urgent — loss is isolated |
| Problem is a connector, wire, or inverter | Repair the component; keep the panel |
| Minor cosmetic damage, output normal | Often fine to leave and monitor |

The warranty line deserves emphasis, because it changes the math entirely. A panel's product warranty may cover a defective module for many years, but a warranty on the *part* rarely covers the *labor* to swap it — and roof labor is a real cost. The split between who pays for the component and who pays for the work is exactly what [workmanship vs equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) untangles; read your coverage before assuming a claim is free.

Two forces pull the "leave it" option out of reach. First, safety: shattered glass, exposed conductors, or signs of overheating aren't a cosmetic call — get them addressed. Second, the series effect: on a string system, a struggling panel taxes its neighbors, so leaving it can cost more production than the one panel represents. Outside those cases, a panel with light cosmetic damage and normal output is often best monitored rather than touched.

When you're weighing the out-of-pocket side of a replacement — panel, labor, and the occasional string reconfiguration — the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) helps you put the repair against the value of the production you'd recover, which is ultimately the number the decision turns on. A cheap, isolated fault on a microinverter system that's barely denting your output is a very different decision than a string-dragging failure you'll pay for every sunny day you delay.
