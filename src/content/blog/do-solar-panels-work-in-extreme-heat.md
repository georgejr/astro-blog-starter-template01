There's an intuition, and it's a reasonable one, that a blazing hot day is a solar panel's dream — all that sun beating down must mean maximum power. The intuition is half right and half backward. Panels do need sunlight, and sunny places do produce more solar energy over a year than cloudy ones. But heat and light are two different things, and a panel actually cares about them in opposite directions. More light means more power. More heat means less power. On a scorching afternoon you get plenty of the first and too much of the second, and the result is a panel that's producing well in absolute terms but noticeably below what it could do if the same bright sun fell on it at a cooler temperature. Understanding why comes down to a bit of physics that every panel's spec sheet quietly admits: photovoltaic cells lose efficiency as they get hot.

This isn't a defect or a marketing dodge — it's a fundamental property of how semiconductors turn light into electricity, and it's built into how panels are rated and how installers design around it. The effect is large enough that it shapes real-world production in hot climates and small enough that it never makes solar a bad idea somewhere sunny. Both of those things are true at once, which is why the honest answer to whether panels work in extreme heat is "yes, and also they'd work a bit better if it were cooler." Getting the mechanism straight helps you read your own summer production sensibly, understand why a desert array might slightly underperform a naive estimate, and avoid the two opposite myths — that heat helps, and that heat ruins solar.

## Why a hot cell makes less power than a warm one

A solar cell generates electricity when photons of light knock electrons loose in a semiconductor, creating a voltage that drives current through your system. The current a cell produces depends mostly on how much light hits it — bright sun, more current — and that part behaves the way intuition expects. Voltage is where heat sneaks in. As the cell's temperature rises, the voltage it can produce falls, and because power is voltage times current, that falling voltage drags the panel's power output down even though the light hasn't changed. The current actually creeps up very slightly with temperature, but the voltage drop dominates by a wide margin, so the net effect of heat is always a loss. A panel at 65°C on a hot roof is producing meaningfully less power than the identical panel would at a mild 25°C under the same sunlight.

Manufacturers quantify this precisely with a number called the temperature coefficient of power, printed on every panel's datasheet. It expresses how much the panel's output changes for each degree Celsius above the standard test temperature of 25°C, and for typical modern silicon panels it lands around -0.3% to -0.4% per degree Celsius. Take a coefficient of -0.3%/°C and consider a panel whose cells reach 60°C on a hot, sunny afternoon — a completely normal cell temperature for a rooftop panel in summer, since the dark glass and cells run much hotter than the surrounding air. That's 35 degrees above the 25°C rating, and at -0.3% per degree, the panel is producing about 10% less power than its nameplate rating implies at that moment, purely because of temperature. On the worst afternoons in the hottest climates, where cells can push past 65°C, the temperature-driven loss can climb into the 15% range. That's why a panel's rated wattage is a lab figure measured at a cool cell temperature you rarely see on a real roof under full sun.

The key distinction that trips people up is between air temperature and cell temperature. The panel doesn't care what your thermometer reads in the shade; it cares how hot the cells themselves get, and cells sitting in direct sun run far hotter than the air around them — often 20 to 30 degrees hotter. This is also why airflow behind the panels matters: a panel mounted with a gap above the roof, letting air circulate behind it, runs cooler and therefore produces slightly more than an identical panel sealed flat against a hot surface with no ventilation. Installers know this, which is why rooftop racking leaves an air gap, and it's part of why building-integrated panels that sit flush against a roof can run a touch warmer and lose a bit more to heat than standard rack-mounted arrays. The temperature coefficient is a fixed property of the panel, but how hot your cells actually get is partly a design choice, and good mounting quietly recovers some of the loss.

None of this means hot-climate solar is a poor investment — far from it. It means the raw sunshine advantage of a hot, sunny place is offset a little by the heat penalty, so the net production is strong but not as extreme as the sun hours alone would suggest. This tension is exactly what makes a place like Arizona such an instructive case: it has some of the best sunlight in the country and some of the worst heat penalties, and the two forces partly cancel, leaving a still-excellent but slightly moderated result. The way that balance plays out for a real desert homeowner, and why summer output can plateau rather than peak on the very hottest days, is worked through in [when extreme heat works against your Arizona panels](/blog/solar-panels-in-arizona/). Abundant sun wins the year; brutal heat just shaves the top off the sunniest afternoons.

## What this means for real production and system design

Put the pieces together and a counterintuitive pattern emerges: a panel's single best production conditions aren't the hottest days of summer but the cool, brilliantly clear days of spring and fall, when the sun is high and strong but the air is cold enough to keep the cells efficient. Owners who watch their monitoring apps closely often notice their highest instantaneous power readings on a crisp, sunny day in April or October rather than in the dead of July, and now you know why — the light is nearly as strong but the cells are running cool, so the voltage stays high and the panel delivers closer to its full rated power. Summer still wins on total energy because the days are longer and the sun is up for more hours, but on a per-moment basis, cold and bright beats hot and bright every time. This is the same physics that makes cold climates quietly better for panel efficiency than people assume, a point developed in [how cold climates help solar](/blog/solar-panels-in-cold-climates/): a freezing, sunny winter day is a panel's ideal operating environment, snow permitting.

For system design and estimation, the practical upshot is that you should never expect a panel to deliver its nameplate wattage under real summer sun, and good production estimates already account for this. When an installer or an online tool projects your annual output, the derating for temperature is baked into the model alongside losses for wiring, inverter conversion, dust, and shading — it's one of the standard haircuts between the rating on the box and the energy you actually harvest. If you're estimating your own production, the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) applies these real-world losses so the number you get reflects hot-roof conditions rather than the optimistic lab figure. A homeowner who sizes a system expecting rated output on hot days will be disappointed; one who plans around realistic, temperature-adjusted production will find their system meets expectations. The heat penalty isn't a surprise to the industry — it's a routine input.

It helps to see the size of the effect over a whole year rather than a single afternoon, because that's where people either over- or underreact. Assume a hot-climate array whose cells routinely run 30 to 35 degrees above the 25°C rating during the sunniest midday hours; at a coefficient near -0.3%/°C, the worst hours of the hottest months shed roughly 10% of rated output. But those brutal midday peaks are only a slice of the year — mornings, evenings, winter, and the cool clear days of spring and fall all run far closer to the rating, and some run above it. Averaged across all the hours a system actually produces, the annual energy lost to temperature is much smaller than that headline 10% peak figure suggests, typically a modest single-digit percentage of yearly output. That's why hot, sunny states remain excellent for solar despite the penalty: the loss is real, concentrated in the hottest hours, and swamped by the sheer quantity of sunshine the rest of the time.

It's worth separating the temperature efficiency question from a couple of things it gets confused with. First, extreme heat over years can contribute to the very slow, gradual degradation that all panels experience, but that long-term aging is a separate and small effect from the day-to-day voltage drop we've been discussing — a hot climate doesn't dramatically shorten a panel's life, and panels are built and warrantied to operate reliably in desert conditions for decades. Second, heat losses are entirely different from the reduced output on cloudy days, which is about less light reaching the panel rather than hot cells wasting it. Those are opposite problems: clouds cut the current by blocking light, heat cuts the voltage by warming the cells, and a panel can suffer either or both. The way panels behave under diffuse, cloudy light is its own story, told in [what panels really do without direct sun](/blog/do-solar-panels-work-on-cloudy-days/), and keeping the two mechanisms distinct helps you diagnose your own system: a summer afternoon dip is likely heat, a gray-day dip is likely light.

The clean summary of the whole question is that heat and sun pull in opposite directions, and sun wins the war while heat wins a few battles. Panels absolutely work in extreme heat — they produce enormous amounts of energy in the sunniest, hottest parts of the country, and the economics there are among the best anywhere. They simply produce a little less on the hottest afternoons than the same panels would under cooler skies, because rising cell temperature costs voltage. That's the fact behind the myth: more sun genuinely does mean more power, but more heat, specifically, does not. Once you hold those two apart, the behavior of your array across the seasons stops being mysterious and starts being exactly what the physics predicts.
