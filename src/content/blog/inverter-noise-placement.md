Homeowners planning a solar install sometimes picture the inverter as a loud machine bolted to the house — something that will drone through the wall of the room behind it. The reality is milder than the worry. A modern string inverter running under load produces a faint electrical hum, usually somewhere around 45 decibels up close, roughly the level of a quiet library or a refrigerator across the room. The question worth attention is not whether it will be deafening. It is where to put a box that runs warm, hums a little, and works best somewhere shaded and ventilated.

## Why an inverter hums at all

The sound has a physical cause, which is why it never fully disappears. An inverter switches direct current from your panels into alternating current thousands of times a second, and its transformer and magnetic components vibrate slightly at those frequencies. That vibration is the hum. Larger cooling fans, where fitted, add a second, airier sound that rises when the unit works hardest on a hot, sunny afternoon.

A few practical points follow from that:

- The hum is loudest at midday under full sun, when the inverter is converting the most power, and falls silent at night when there is nothing to convert.
- A steady, low hum is normal and healthy. A new *buzzing*, *rattling*, or *clicking* that was not there before is worth reporting — mechanical noises can flag a loosening mount or a fan problem rather than the ordinary electrical hum.
- Microinverters change the picture entirely. Because they sit on the roof under each panel rather than in one wall-mounted box, there is effectively no indoor noise to place — one of the quieter-living reasons some homeowners lean toward [microinverters over a string inverter](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/).

## The three things placement has to satisfy

Where an inverter goes is a compromise between four demands, and noise is only one of them. The other three usually matter more.

**Heat is the real enemy.** Inverters are electronics, and electronics age faster when they run hot. An inverter baking on a south- or west-facing exterior wall in direct afternoon sun will run hotter, may throttle its output on the worst days to protect itself, and tends to wear sooner. Shade and airflow extend its life, which is why placement is partly a longevity decision, not just a comfort one — and the inverter is already [the component most likely to fail first](/blog/solar-inverter-failure/), so anything that keeps it cool pays off.

**Access matters.** The inverter needs clearance around it for ventilation and for a technician to reach it. Its display or app connection is also how you check that the system is working, so somewhere you occasionally pass is better than a sealed crawlspace.

**Distance and code have a say.** Long wire runs between panels, inverter, and service panel cost money and lose a little energy, so installers prefer to keep the inverter reasonably close to the main panel. Local electrical code and the manufacturer's manual also set clearance and mounting rules that quietly rule some spots out.

## Garage, exterior wall, or utility room?

Each common location trades those demands differently.

The **garage** is the default for good reason. It is shaded, close to the service panel in most homes, easy to access, and its ambient noise floor is high enough that a 45-decibel hum vanishes into it. If your garage is attached and you have a choice of walls, keep the inverter off any wall shared with a bedroom or a home office, and you will rarely notice it.

An **exterior wall** works and is sometimes unavoidable, especially on homes without a garage. The rule is to favor a north- or east-facing wall, or any spot that stays shaded through the afternoon, and to avoid mounting it in full western sun. Weather-rated inverters handle the outdoors fine; heat is the concern, not rain.

A **utility or mechanical room** is a clean option when the room already houses the panel and equipment and is not adjacent to a quiet living space. The caution here is ventilation: a small, closed closet can trap heat, so the room needs enough air volume or airflow to shed it. A laundry or mechanical room usually qualifies; a sealed hallway closet often does not.

The place to think twice about is anywhere sharing a wall with a bedroom, nursery, or office. The hum is quiet, but quiet-at-noon is exactly when a shift worker is trying to sleep or a call is underway, and a thin shared wall can transmit low-frequency sound better than its decibel rating suggests.

## FAQ

**Is it normal for a solar inverter to make noise?**
Yes. A steady, low hum during daylight is the ordinary sound of an inverter converting power, usually around 45 decibels up close. It quiets at night. A new buzz, rattle, or click, by contrast, is worth reporting to your installer.

**How loud is a solar inverter, really?**
For a typical residential string inverter, about the level of a quiet room or a fridge heard from across a kitchen. Larger inverters with cooling fans get a bit louder at midday under full sun, then settle as the day cools.

**Can I put the inverter inside my house?**
Yes, in a garage, utility room, or mechanical room with adequate ventilation. Avoid small sealed closets, which trap heat, and walls shared with bedrooms or offices, where even a faint hum can carry.

**Does inverter placement affect performance?**
Indirectly but genuinely. A shaded, well-ventilated spot keeps the inverter cooler, which helps it avoid heat-related throttling on the hottest days and tends to extend its working life.
