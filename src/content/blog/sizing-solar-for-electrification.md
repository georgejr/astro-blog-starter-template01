A gas furnace, a gas water heater, a gas range, and two cars running on gasoline never appeared on your electric bill. Move all four onto electricity and they do — all at once, and often larger than the load you started with. That is the trap waiting for anyone who sizes a solar array against last year's power bill while planning to electrify the house over the next few years. You end up with a system built for the home you're leaving behind, not the home you're building, and you discover the shortfall the first winter the heat pump runs hard.

The fix is not complicated, but it does require doing the arithmetic in the right order: figure out what the fully electrified home will actually consume, then size panels against that number rather than against the fossil-fuel bill you're about to shrink. The two numbers can differ by a factor of two, and the gap is where most under-sized electrification arrays go wrong.

## What electrification does to your kilowatt-hours

Start with a plain baseline. A US home that already runs its lights, refrigerator, laundry, electronics, and central air on electricity — but heats, cooks, and makes hot water with gas, and fuels its cars at a pump — typically buys somewhere in the 8,000 to 11,000 kWh a year range from the grid. Call it 9,500 kWh for a worked example, and label that as an assumption you should replace with your own twelve months of bills. That number is the deceptive one, because it's the number a salesperson will size against if you let them, and it describes a house you're deliberately dismantling.

Now add the loads back one fuel at a time. A cold-climate heat pump carrying most of a home's heating and cooling is usually the biggest single addition. Depending on climate, envelope, and how much backup electric-resistance heat kicks in on the coldest days, a heat pump commonly adds somewhere between 3,000 and 7,000 kWh a year to a house that previously burned gas for heat. The spread is enormous because it's mostly a climate and insulation question, which is why a generic estimate is close to useless here and a load model tuned to your actual heating degree days matters. The [heat pump calculator](/heat-pump-calculator/) is built to put a defensible number on that specific load rather than guessing, and it's worth running before you touch the solar math, because the heat pump is the load most likely to blow up your annual total.

An electric vehicle is the next big block, and it's the most predictable of the group because it scales cleanly with miles. Figure roughly 0.30 to 0.35 kWh per mile for a typical EV, so 12,000 miles a year lands near 3,600 to 4,200 kWh at the wall, a little more once you account for charging losses. Two EVs in the driveway double that, and a household that drives more than average can push a single car past 5,000 kWh. Unlike the heat pump, the EV load is easy to estimate and hard to argue with: annual miles times efficiency, plus a small charging overhead, and you have it.

Hot water and cooking round out the set and are smaller but not trivial. Swapping a gas water heater for a heat-pump water heater — the efficient kind, not old-fashioned electric resistance — typically adds something in the neighborhood of 1,200 to 2,000 kWh a year for a family, with resistance models running two to three times that. Induction cooking replacing a gas range adds a modest 400 to 800 kWh for most households; it feels dramatic at the stovetop but it's the smallest line on this list. Add a heated garage, a workshop, or a pool and the numbers climb further, but for a standard house the big four are heat, driving, hot water, and cooking.

Stack them onto the 9,500 kWh baseline and the fully electrified total for our example home lands somewhere around 17,000 to 19,000 kWh a year — call it 18,000 kWh as a planning figure. That is nearly double the electric bill the house shows today, and every kilowatt-hour of it is a load solar can offset. The reason this matters so much for sizing is that solar is one of the few purchases where building for the future is cheaper than retrofitting for it: adding panels to an existing array later means a second permit, a second interconnection application, sometimes a second inverter, and a per-watt price that no longer enjoys the economies of a single larger job. Sizing once, for the electrified house, almost always beats sizing twice.

There's a real tension hiding in that advice, though, and it's worth naming rather than glossing over. Electrification often happens over years, not in a single weekend — the heat pump this year, the EV in two years, the water heater when the old one finally dies — and sizing today for loads you won't add until 2029 means paying now for panels that will export cheaply until the loads catch up. Whether that's wise depends heavily on your export compensation. Under full retail net metering, over-producing in the early years is nearly free because the credits bank against later consumption; under a weak export tariff, those early exports are sold at a discount and the case for building ahead of the load weakens considerably. The honest answer is to size for the electrification you're genuinely committed to and can name a date for, treat the speculative stuff as a maybe, and leave physical roof space for a later expansion rather than filling every square foot on day one.

## Turning the bigger load into a panel count

Once you have an honest annual kWh target, converting it to a system size is the same arithmetic as any other solar sizing job — it's just being run against a larger number. Production per kilowatt of panels depends on where you live: a rough national planning range is 1,200 to 1,600 kWh per year for every kW of installed capacity, with the low end reflecting cloudier northern states and the high end the sunny Southwest. Take the middle, 1,400 kWh per kW, and our 18,000 kWh electrified home needs about 12.9 kW of solar to offset its full annual consumption. At 400-watt panels, that's roughly 32 panels; at higher-wattage modules, a few fewer. Compare that to the same house sized against its pre-electrification 9,500 kWh bill, which would have called for under 7 kW and about 17 panels — barely half the array. The gap between those two systems is the entire point of doing this exercise before you sign.

That said, offsetting 100% of a much larger annual load is not automatically the right target, and this is where electrification sizing diverges from the simple "cover your bill" rule. Two things complicate it. First, the biggest new loads are seasonal and don't line up with solar's seasonal output. A cold-climate heat pump does most of its work in December and January, precisely when a rooftop array produces the least — short days, low sun, and possibly snow cover. Solar production peaks in summer, when the heat pump's heating load is zero and only its cooling load remains. So a system sized to cover the heat pump's annual kWh will bank a large summer surplus and draw heavily from the grid in winter, and whether that annual netting works in your favor depends entirely on your utility's rules. Under true net metering, the summer surplus rolls forward to cover the winter deficit and the annual match holds. Under net billing or a monthly-reset export scheme, the summer overproduction is sold cheap and the winter shortfall is bought at full retail, and the array under-delivers on savings even though the annual kilowatt-hours balance on paper.

Second, roof space and budget frequently cap the system below the full-offset number, and that's not a failure — it's a normal outcome that deserves a deliberate choice about what to prioritize. If your roof tops out at 10 kW and the full-offset target is 12.9 kW, you're going to offset roughly 78% of the electrified load and buy the rest from the grid, which is a perfectly reasonable place to land. The question becomes which loads you most want the solar to cover, and the answer usually favors the ones that run during daylight. This is where the interaction between solar and electrification stops being about annual totals and starts being about timing, which is the part most sizing tools handle poorly.

And because the heat pump is both the largest and the least predictable of the new loads, it's worth sizing that one load carefully on its own first. The broader mechanics of matching an array to a growing household load are covered in [what size solar system do you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/), and the heat-pump-specific version of the problem — where winter output and heating demand collide — is worked through in detail in [resize your solar array before adding a heat pump](/blog/solar-sizing-for-heat-pump/).

## Timing the loads so the panels actually cover them

The most useful thing an electrifying homeowner can do, after getting the annual number right, is think about when each new load runs relative to when the sun is up — because in most of the country, the rules increasingly reward power you consume on-site over power you export. Two of the big four electrification loads are wonderfully flexible in their timing, and two are not.

The EV is the flexible superstar. A car that sits in the driveway during the day, or that you can schedule to charge on a timer, can soak up midday solar production that would otherwise export at a discount. A homeowner who charges at noon on a sunny Saturday is effectively buying that solar energy at their retail avoided rate rather than selling it at the export rate, and the difference between those two numbers is the whole game under net billing. Even for commuters whose cars are gone during the day on weekdays, weekend daytime charging and scheduled charging windows recover a meaningful share of the benefit. The heat-pump water heater is nearly as flexible — it has a tank that stores energy as hot water, so running it midday on a timer or with a solar-diversion control turns it into a de facto thermal battery, heating water with surplus panels instead of pulling from the grid in the evening. These two loads reward daytime scheduling and make a smaller array punch above its weight.

Heating and cooling are the stubborn ones. You can't tell the heat pump to wait for the sun when the house is cold at 6 a.m. in January, and air conditioning, while it does run during peak solar hours, also runs deep into hot evenings after production has fallen off. These loads mostly run when they need to run, and the array either covers them in real time or it doesn't. That reality is a large part of why so many electrifying households end up looking at a battery — not primarily for backup, but to shift a slice of the day's solar production into the evening heating and cooling window, capturing value that would otherwise be lost to a weak export rate. Whether that battery pencils out is a separate calculation that depends on the spread between your retail and export rates, and it's easy to talk yourself into more storage than the math supports, so run it honestly before committing.

The practical sizing posture that comes out of all this is straightforward to state and worth repeating. Build the annual load model for the electrified house, not the fossil-fuel house. Size the array against that larger number, discounting toward self-consumption if your export rate is weak. Leave physical roof room for the loads you're committed to but haven't installed yet. Schedule the flexible loads — the car, the water heater — into daylight so a right-sized array covers more of them directly. And treat the heat pump's winter shortfall as a known feature of the design rather than a surprise, planning for grid purchases in the darkest months whether that means banked summer credits or a battery or simply a winter electric bill you've already budgeted for. Do that, and the fully electrified home runs on an array that was built for it. Skip it, and you own a system sized for a house that no longer exists.
