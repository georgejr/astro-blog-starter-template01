The solar lease versus buy decision is really a question about who ends up owning the incentives. A lease promises no upfront cost and someone else's problem if anything breaks, which sounds like the easy answer. Ownership costs money on day one and hands you the maintenance, which sounds like the hard one. Yet across a system's twenty-five-year life, ownership almost always costs less, and the reason comes down to where the tax credit, the equity, and the escalating payments land.

## The two ownership models, briefly

Every residential solar arrangement is a variation on one of two structures, and naming them clearly is half the battle.

### Ownership, with cash or a loan

You buy the equipment. Pay cash and you are done; finance it and you make loan payments until the balance clears, after which the system's output is free for the rest of its life. Either way, the panels are yours, they add to your home's value, and every incentive attached to the system flows to you. The trade-off is that the responsibility, and the upfront outlay or debt, is yours too. How the cash-versus-loan choice reshapes the timing is covered in [cash or loan for solar payback](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/).

### Third-party ownership, via lease or PPA

A company installs and owns the system on your roof. Under a lease you pay a fixed monthly amount to use it; under a power purchase agreement you pay per kilowatt-hour it produces. You get lower bills with little or nothing down, but you never own the hardware, and the incentives belong to the company that does.

## Where the cost difference comes from

Three transfers of value explain almost the entire gap between leasing and buying. Each one favors the owner.

The first is the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, worth 30% of the system cost. Only the system's owner can claim it. Buy the system and it is yours; lease it and the leasing company keeps that 30%, though they may pass a diluted version back to you as a slightly lower rate. The credit is the single largest reason ownership wins, and [how the solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/) covers who qualifies and how it is claimed.

The second is home equity. An owned system generally adds to resale value, so part of your cost comes back if you sell. A leased system does the opposite: it can complicate a sale, because a buyer has to be willing to assume the lease or the seller has to buy it out.

The third is the escalator, covered in its own section below, which quietly raises lease payments over time while an owner's cost stays flat or ends entirely once a loan is paid off.

Here is how the pieces line up over a system's life. The figures are illustrative assumptions, not quotes; run your own numbers before deciding.

| Factor | Buying (cash or loan) | Leasing / PPA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Upfront cost | Full price, or financed | Little to nothing down |
| 30% federal credit | Goes to you | Goes to the provider |
| Monthly obligation | Ends when loan clears | Continues for the full term |
| Payment over time | Flat, then zero | Often rises via escalator |
| Home value effect | Generally adds value | Can complicate a sale |
| Maintenance | Your responsibility | Provider's responsibility |
| Lifetime cost | Lower | Higher |

## The escalator clause that erodes lease savings

The detail that turns a decent-looking lease into a mediocre one is the escalator: a clause that raises your monthly payment by a fixed percentage every year, often in the low single digits. It is easy to overlook, because the first-year payment is what the salesperson quotes and it usually beats your current electric bill.

Play it forward. A payment rising a few percent annually compounds, and after fifteen or twenty years it can climb well above where it started. Whether that outpaces your utility's own rate increases is the gamble: if grid rates rise faster, the lease stays a bargain; if they plateau while your escalator keeps climbing, you can end up paying more for leased solar than you would have paid the utility. An owner faces no such clause. A cash owner's cost is fixed at the purchase; a loan owner's payment is fixed by the loan and then disappears.

## When a lease still makes sense

Ownership winning on lifetime cost does not make leasing wrong for everyone. The case for a lease is strongest in a specific situation: you have little or no federal tax liability, which means you could not fully use the 30% credit anyway, and you want lower bills without the upfront cost or the maintenance responsibility. If the credit is worthless to you personally, the biggest advantage of ownership shrinks, and a lease with a modest or zero escalator can be a reasonable trade for the convenience.

Financing muddies this comparison in one important way. A zero-down solar loan can carry dealer fees baked into the balance, which narrows the gap between a loan and a lease if you are not careful. The way to see through it is to compare total lifetime cost, not the monthly payment a salesperson leads with; [what a solar loan really adds](/blog/solar-loan-true-cost/) unpacks those embedded fees.

The clean way to decide: price the purchase honestly first, credit included. Start from an installed cost with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), then run the ownership case through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) with the 30% credit applied. Only after you know what ownership actually costs and returns can you judge whether a lease's convenience is worth the value you would be handing away. For most buyers who can use the credit, that value is too large to give up.
