The first panel on your roof and the last panel on your roof cost about the same to install, but they do not earn the same money. That asymmetry is the entire reason an oversized system can pay back more slowly than a right-sized one — even though bigger systems usually get a lower price per watt. A quote that's larger than your usage isn't automatically a better deal, and the mechanism behind why is worth understanding before you say yes to more capacity.

The key is what happens to each kilowatt-hour a panel produces. There are only two destinations: you use it on-site, or you export it to the grid. Those two destinations are usually worth very different amounts.

## Why a panel's value falls as the array grows

A kilowatt-hour you consume the instant it's produced is worth the full retail rate, because it's a kilowatt-hour you didn't have to buy. A kilowatt-hour you export is worth only whatever your utility credits for exports — which under classic net metering equals the retail rate, but under net billing or a weak export tariff can be a fraction of it. [Solar export rates](/blog/solar-export-rates/) covers how widely that credit varies from one utility to the next.

Now watch what happens as you add panels. A small array produces less than you use at almost every moment, so nearly all of its output is consumed on-site at full retail value. As the array grows, it starts producing more than your home is using during sunny midday hours, and that surplus spills onto the grid. The bigger the system, the larger the share of its total output that exports rather than offsets. Each additional panel therefore earns a blend that tilts more and more toward the low export rate.

That's the diminishing return, stated physically: you're not adding worse panels, you're adding panels whose output increasingly lands in the low-value bucket. Under full net metering the effect is mild, because both buckets are worth the same. Under net billing it can be severe.

## The arithmetic, with a worked example

Assume a home that uses 10,000 kWh a year, a retail rate of $0.18/kWh, and an export credit of $0.06/kWh — a net-billing setup where exports are worth a third of retail. Assume each incremental block of panels adds 2,000 kWh of annual production, and that as the system grows, a rising share of that block gets exported.

| Production block | Share used on-site | Blended value of that block |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First 2,000 kWh | ~95% | ~$0.174/kWh |
| Up to 6,000 kWh | ~85% | ~$0.162/kWh |
| Up to 10,000 kWh | ~65% | ~$0.138/kWh |
| 10,000–12,000 kWh (past usage) | ~20% | ~$0.084/kWh |

The shares are illustrative assumptions, not measured values, but the pattern is the real one. The first block of production is worth nearly full retail. The block that pushes you past your own consumption is worth less than half as much, because most of it exports at $0.06. You paid roughly the same per watt for those last panels, but they earn less than half the return — so their simple payback is more than twice as long as the first panels'. Averaged in, they drag the whole system's payback out.

This is the counterpart to a fact that trips people up: the last panels on a project are often the *cheapest* to install, because the fixed costs — the truck roll, the permit, the design — are already paid. Cheap to add is not the same as quick to pay back. If those cheap panels export their output at a discount, low cost and low earning can still leave you with a longer payback than a smaller, tighter design. [Does a bigger solar system pay back faster](/blog/solar-payback-by-system-size/) walks through where the cost advantage and the earnings disadvantage cross.

## When oversizing is still the right call

None of this makes bigger systems wrong. It makes *unconsidered* bigness wrong. Several situations justify capacity beyond today's usage:

- **A known future load.** If an EV, heat pump, or addition is genuinely coming, the surplus stops being surplus once that load arrives — and buying the panels now, while the crew is already on your roof, avoids paying the fixed project costs twice.
- **Full net metering.** Where exports are credited at retail, the low-value bucket doesn't exist, so the diminishing-return effect nearly vanishes and sizing to 100% (or a utility's cap) is reasonable.
- **A battery or shiftable loads.** Storage, or the ability to run a pool pump and water heater at midday, converts would-be exports back into full-value self-consumption, restoring the return on those extra panels.

The failure mode is buying capacity past your usage under a weak export rate with no future load and no way to self-consume the surplus. That's the oversizing penalty in its purest form: you paid for panels whose output you're essentially donating to the grid at wholesale.
