"What's the payback on solar?" has no single answer, and any source that hands you one number is quietly hiding the assumptions underneath it. Payback swings hard on three inputs — how much sun your roof gets, what you pay per kilowatt-hour, and what a system costs installed — and different combinations of those three produce wildly different timelines from the same technology. The way to make that concrete isn't to quote a national average that describes no actual house. It's to run the same arithmetic three separate times, for three genuinely different homes, with every assumption written down in plain sight so you can substitute your own numbers and watch the answer move.

Everything below uses labeled, illustrative assumptions rather than live prices, and it's worth being upfront about that. Installed cost is expressed per watt within the broad range of roughly $2.50 to $4.00 that residential systems have generally occupied before incentives; the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is applied at 30% of system cost, consistent with IRS guidance. These are deliberately round example inputs chosen to illustrate the mechanics, not quotes for your address, and you should treat them as a template to fill in rather than a promise. The mechanics behind each variable — why sun, rate, and cost each pull the timeline the way they do — are unpacked in [solar payback, explained: every factor that moves the number](/blog/solar-payback-factors/), which is the companion piece to the worked math here.

## The arithmetic every payback estimate runs

Every example uses the same simple skeleton, and once you can see the five steps, you can run them for any house including your own:

1. **Gross cost** = system size in watts × installed price per watt.
2. **Net cost** = gross cost − the 30% federal credit.
3. **Annual production** = system size in kW × peak sun hours × 365 × a 0.8 real-world derate that accounts for heat, wiring losses, and inverter inefficiency.
4. **Annual savings** = production × the value of each kilowatt-hour to this particular home.
5. **Payback** = net cost ÷ annual savings.

Four of those five steps are mechanical — plug in numbers, turn the crank. Step four is where homes genuinely diverge, and it's the one most estimates get lazy about. A kilowatt-hour is worth the full retail rate when it offsets your own usage, because that's a kilowatt-hour you didn't have to buy. The same kilowatt-hour is worth only the export rate when it's sold back to the grid, and in a growing number of markets that export rate sits well below retail. So a home that consumes most of what it produces values its output near retail, while a home that exports a big share values its output at a blend of retail and the lower export rate. That distinction is the hinge the whole exercise turns on, and it's why two systems producing identical amounts of electricity can have paybacks years apart. Keep an eye on it as the three homes unfold, because it explains the punchline. If the underlying idea of a break-even point is new to you, [solar payback period explained](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/) covers the fundamentals before you dig into the scenarios.

## Three homes, three timelines

### Home A: sunny, expensive power, fast break-even

Picture a household in a high-rate, high-sun market — strong irradiance paired with steep utility prices and, importantly, full retail net metering that values every kilowatt-hour the system makes at the retail rate.

Assumptions: a 6 kW system at an assumed $2.80 per watt installed; 5.5 peak sun hours on average; a retail rate of an assumed $0.24 per kWh, with net metering crediting all production at that rate.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $16,800 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$5,040 |
| Net cost | $11,760 |
| Annual production | ≈ 9,600 kWh |
| Annual savings | ≈ $2,300 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 5 years** |

High rates do almost all the heavy lifting in this scenario. When every kilowatt-hour you offset is worth $0.24, even a modest system pays itself back quickly, and the roughly twenty remaining years of the panels' productive life are largely gravy on top of a system that has already earned its keep. Notice that the fast payback here has less to do with the system being cheap and more to do with each unit of avoided grid power being expensive — that's the pattern to watch.

### Home B: middle-of-the-road, and the number most people land near

Now a more typical household — decent but unspectacular sun, mid-range rates, and standard net metering that offsets usage at the retail rate.

Assumptions: a 7 kW system at an assumed $3.00 per watt installed; 5.0 peak sun hours; a retail rate of an assumed $0.16 per kWh, with production offsetting usage at that rate.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $21,000 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$6,300 |
| Net cost | $14,700 |
| Annual production | ≈ 10,200 kWh |
| Annual savings | ≈ $1,635 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 9 years** |

This is the mid-case, and something close to nine years is roughly where a lot of real, fairly priced systems land in ordinary markets. Nothing about it is exceptional — average sun, average rates, a fair price — which is exactly what makes it a useful reference point rather than a cherry-picked best case. Shift any single input and the date moves, but a payback somewhere near the end of the first decade is a reasonable baseline expectation for a typical home buying a reasonably priced system, and it's a healthier anchor than either the five-year fantasy a salesperson might float or the twenty-year horror story a skeptic might quote.

### Home C: cheap power, weaker sun, poor exports — the slow case

Finally, a household in a low-rate region with modest sun and, critically, an export rate well below retail, so surplus production earns far less than power consumed on-site.

Assumptions: an 8 kW system at an assumed $3.20 per watt installed; 4.5 peak sun hours; a retail rate of an assumed $0.12 per kWh, but with only about 70% of production used on-site at that rate while the remaining 30% is exported at an assumed $0.04 per kWh.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $25,600 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$7,680 |
| Net cost | $17,920 |
| Annual production | ≈ 10,500 kWh |
| Blended value per kWh | ≈ $0.096 |
| Annual savings | ≈ $1,010 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 18 years** |

Two forces drag this timeline out at once. Cheap grid power means each offset kilowatt-hour is worth little to begin with, and the below-retail export rate means the surplus this larger system produces is very nearly given away. Look closely at what that produces: Home C generates more electricity than Home B — 10,500 kWh against 10,200 — yet saves less money, ending up with the longest payback of the three. That's the whole lesson of these examples compressed into two rows of a table. Production is not payback. In a market like this one, a smaller array sized tightly to on-site usage, deliberately avoiding those low-value exports, would very often pay back faster than the bigger system modeled here, because it would stop building capacity right at the point where each additional kilowatt-hour stops being worth much.

Set the three side by side and the point becomes impossible to miss:

| | Home A | Home B | Home C |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Net cost | $11,760 | $14,700 | $17,920 |
| Annual production | ≈ 9,600 kWh | ≈ 10,200 kWh | ≈ 10,500 kWh |
| Value per kWh | $0.24 | $0.16 | ≈ $0.096 |
| Annual savings | ≈ $2,300 | ≈ $1,635 | ≈ $1,010 |
| Payback | ≈ 5 years | ≈ 9 years | ≈ 18 years |

The production rows are nearly identical across all three homes; the payback rows are more than three times apart. That gap is the entire argument. What separates these households isn't how much electricity their panels make — it's what each kilowatt-hour is worth once it's made, which is set by their rates and their export rules, not by the sun on their roofs. A shopper who fixates on system size or annual output is watching the wrong number.

## What these examples deliberately leave out

Honest worked examples should flag their own simplifications rather than pretend the arithmetic is the whole story, and three omissions matter enough to name. The first is rate inflation. Electricity prices have generally tended to rise over time, and rising rates quietly shorten every payback above, because your savings grow year over year while your fixed system cost stays put — a kilowatt-hour you avoid buying is worth more each year the utility raises its price. The examples hold rates flat, so treat every timeline as conservative on this axis; the real break-even often arrives a bit sooner than a flat-rate model suggests. The second omission is panel degradation. Output fades a fraction of a percent per year as the panels age, nudging paybacks slightly longer in the opposite direction. In practice this roughly, partially offsets rate inflation rather than dominating it — the two effects push against each other, which is one reason a flat-rate, no-degradation model like the one above lands in a reasonable middle rather than being wildly off.

The third omission is financing, and it's the biggest. Every example above assumes you pay cash. Pay with a loan instead and the interest changes the math — sometimes a little, sometimes a lot, depending on the rate and term — and the timing of when savings exceed payments becomes its own question separate from the cash break-even. [Cash or loan: how you pay changes when solar pays back](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/) works through that difference in full, and it's essential reading if you're not writing a check for the whole system, because a financed system can hit a "positive cash flow from day one" milestone long before it reaches the cash-payback point these tables describe.
