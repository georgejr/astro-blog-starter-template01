"What's the payback on solar?" has no single answer, and any source that gives you one number is hiding the assumptions underneath it. Payback swings wildly on three inputs — how much sun your roof gets, what you pay per kilowatt-hour, and what a system costs installed — and different combinations produce wildly different timelines. The way to make that concrete isn't a national average. It's to run the same arithmetic three times, for three genuinely different homes, with every assumption written down so you can swap in your own.

Everything below uses labeled, illustrative assumptions, not live prices. Installed cost is expressed per watt within the broad $2.50–$4.00 range residential systems have generally occupied before incentives; the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit is applied at 30% of system cost, per IRS guidance. These are round example inputs, not quotes for your address. The mechanics behind each variable are unpacked in [solar payback, explained: every factor that moves the number](/blog/solar-payback-factors/).

## The formula, stripped down

Every example uses the same simple skeleton:

1. **Gross cost** = system size (watts) × installed price per watt.
2. **Net cost** = gross cost − 30% federal credit.
3. **Annual production** = system size (kW) × peak sun hours × 365 × a 0.8 real-world derate for heat, wiring, and inverter losses.
4. **Annual savings** = production × the value of each kilowatt-hour to this home.
5. **Payback** = net cost ÷ annual savings.

Step 4 is where homes diverge most, because a kilowatt-hour is worth the full retail rate when it offsets your own usage and only the export rate when it's sold back. Keep an eye on that distinction as the examples unfold. If the underlying idea of a break-even point is new, [solar payback period explained](/blog/solar-payback-period-explained/) covers the fundamentals first.

## Home A: sunny, expensive power, fast break-even

A household in a high-rate, high-sun market — think strong irradiance paired with steep utility prices and full retail net metering.

Assumptions:

- System: 6 kW at an assumed $2.80/W installed.
- Sun: 5.5 peak sun hours average.
- Rate: an assumed $0.24/kWh, with net metering valuing all production at that rate.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $16,800 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$5,040 |
| Net cost | $11,760 |
| Annual production | ≈ 9,600 kWh |
| Annual savings | ≈ $2,300 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 5 years** |

High rates do most of the heavy lifting here. When every kilowatt-hour you offset is worth $0.24, a modest system pays itself back fast, and the roughly 20 remaining years of a panel's life are largely gravy.

## Home B: middle-of-the-road, and the number most people land near

A more typical household — decent but not spectacular sun, mid-range rates, standard net metering.

Assumptions:

- System: 7 kW at an assumed $3.00/W installed.
- Sun: 5.0 peak sun hours average.
- Rate: an assumed $0.16/kWh, with production offsetting usage at that rate.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $21,000 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$6,300 |
| Net cost | $14,700 |
| Annual production | ≈ 10,200 kWh |
| Annual savings | ≈ $1,635 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 9 years** |

This is the mid-case, and roughly 9 years is close to where a lot of real, well-priced systems land in ordinary markets. Nothing here is exceptional — average sun, average rates, a fair price — which is exactly why it's a useful reference point. Shift any one input and the date moves, but a payback somewhere near the end of the first decade is a reasonable expectation for a typical home buying a fairly priced system.

## Home C: cheap power, weaker sun, poor exports — the slow case

A household in a low-rate region with modest sun and, critically, an export rate well below retail — so surplus production earns far less than power used on-site.

Assumptions:

- System: 8 kW at an assumed $3.20/W installed.
- Sun: 4.5 peak sun hours average.
- Rate: an assumed $0.12/kWh retail, but only about 70% of production is used on-site at that rate; the remaining 30% is exported at an assumed $0.04/kWh.

| Step | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Gross cost | $25,600 |
| Federal credit (30%) | −$7,680 |
| Net cost | $17,920 |
| Annual production | ≈ 10,500 kWh |
| Blended value per kWh | ≈ $0.096 |
| Annual savings | ≈ $1,010 |
| **Payback** | **≈ 18 years** |

Two forces drag this timeline out. Cheap grid power means each offset kilowatt-hour is worth little, and the below-retail export rate means the surplus this larger system produces is nearly given away. Notice that Home C generates *more* electricity than Home B yet saves *less* money — a direct illustration of why production alone never determines payback. In a market like this, a smaller array sized tightly to on-site usage, avoiding low-value exports, would often pay back faster than the bigger system modeled here.

## The three side by side

| | Home A | Home B | Home C |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Net cost | $11,760 | $14,700 | $17,920 |
| Annual production | ≈ 9,600 kWh | ≈ 10,200 kWh | ≈ 10,500 kWh |
| Value per kWh | $0.24 | $0.16 | ≈ $0.096 |
| Annual savings | ≈ $2,300 | ≈ $1,635 | ≈ $1,010 |
| Payback | ≈ 5 years | ≈ 9 years | ≈ 18 years |

The production rows are nearly identical; the payback rows are three times apart. That's the whole point. What separates these homes isn't how much electricity the panels make — it's what each kilowatt-hour is worth once it's made.

## What these examples deliberately leave out

Honest worked examples should flag their own simplifications. Three matter:

- **Rate inflation.** Electricity prices tend to rise over time, which quietly shortens every payback above, because your savings grow while your cost stays fixed. The examples hold rates flat, so treat the timelines as conservative on this axis.
- **Panel degradation.** Output fades a fraction of a percent per year, nudging paybacks slightly longer. In practice this roughly partially offsets rate inflation rather than dominating it.
- **Financing.** Every example assumes cash. Pay with a loan and the interest changes the math — sometimes a little, sometimes a lot — as [cash or loan: how you pay changes when solar pays back](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/) works through.

Swap your own numbers into the five-step skeleton and you'll get a timeline that reflects your roof instead of an average. The exercise is less about landing on a precise year than about seeing which of the three inputs is steering your own result — because that's the one worth negotiating hardest.
