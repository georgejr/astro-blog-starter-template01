Pumping water off the grid is one of the few solar applications where you can often skip the expensive battery bank entirely, and the reason is elegant: you don't need to store the electricity, because you can store the water instead. A tank full of water sitting uphill or on a stand is a battery of a different kind — it holds the day's pumping until you need it, at a tiny fraction of the cost of holding the equivalent energy in lithium cells. That single insight reshapes how you design a solar well system for a remote cabin, a homestead, or livestock water on a property with no utility service. Instead of building an array plus a battery bank plus an inverter to run an ordinary pump around the clock, you can build a modest array that drives a pump directly whenever the sun is up, filling a tank you draw from at your leisure. It's cheaper, simpler, and more robust — fewer components to fail in a place where the nearest electrician is a long way off.

## Why water pumping is the friendliest off-grid load there is

Most off-grid loads are demanding because they need power on your schedule, not the sun's. Lights want to work after dark. A refrigerator cycles day and night. That mismatch between when you use power and when the panels make it is exactly why off-grid electrical systems need batteries — the storage bridges the gap between production and demand. Water pumping is different because the thing you actually want, water in a tank, can be produced whenever there's sun and consumed whenever you're thirsty. Decouple the pumping from the using with a storage tank and the battery becomes optional. The pump runs during the day, lifting water into a tank; you and your animals draw from that tank at any hour. The tank does the time-shifting that a battery would otherwise have to do, and a poly tank costs a rounding error compared to a lithium bank of equivalent "capacity" measured in days of water.

The design that makes this cleanest is the DC submersible pump paired with a solar charge controller or pump controller, running array-direct — meaning the panels feed the pump through a controller with no battery in the loop at all. When the sun comes up, the controller starts driving the pump; as the light strengthens, the pump speeds up and moves more water; as clouds pass or evening falls, it slows and eventually stops. There's no inverter converting DC to AC and back, no battery to maintain and eventually replace, and far fewer points of failure. A good pump controller adds the sophistication that makes this practical: it manages the pump's startup, protects against running dry when the well draws down, and often includes maximum power point tracking to squeeze more water out of the available sunlight. The difference that MPPT makes versus a simpler controller — and when the cheaper option is genuinely fine — is the same trade-off examined in [PWM vs. MPPT charge controllers](/blog/pwm-vs-mppt-charge-controllers/), and for solar pumping the tracking usually earns its keep because it lets the pump start earlier in the morning and keep moving water under weaker light.

This whole approach sits inside the broader logic of off-grid design, where the governing question is always how to match a power source with no utility backstop to the loads you actually need. The general framework for that — building a system around a real load list rather than a guess — is laid out in [sizing an off-grid solar system from your actual load list](/blog/off-grid-solar-system-sizing/), and water pumping is the load that most rewards thinking carefully about *when* you need the output rather than just how much. It also helps to be clear-eyed about what off-grid really means as a design philosophy versus grid-tied solar, because the two are genuinely different machines with different priorities; the contrast is drawn out in [grid-tied vs. off-grid solar](/blog/grid-tied-vs-off-grid-solar/). A grid-tied system optimizes for exporting surplus and drawing backup from the utility. An off-grid pump system optimizes for reliability and self-sufficiency with the fewest parts that can break — and eliminating the battery, when a water tank can do its job, is a big step toward both.

## Sizing the array and the pump to the water you actually need

Sizing a solar pump system runs backward from the water, not forward from the panels. Start with how much water you need per day — for a household, livestock, irrigation, or some combination — because that number, together with how far the pump has to lift the water, sets everything else. The lift is the critical and often-underestimated variable. A pump doesn't just move water; it moves it against gravity and friction, and the total height it has to overcome is called the total dynamic head, measured in feet. That includes the vertical distance from the water level in the well up to the tank, plus the extra "virtual" height that friction in the pipe adds, plus any pressure you need at the outlet. A pump lifting water 50 feet does far less work per gallon than the same pump lifting it 250 feet, which means a deep well demands either a more powerful pump, a bigger array, or both to deliver the same daily gallons. Two properties needing identical water can require very different systems purely because one well is shallow and the other is deep.

The energy arithmetic follows from those two numbers — gallons per day and total dynamic head — and it's worth walking through with labeled assumptions. Suppose a homestead needs 300 gallons a day, and the total dynamic head works out to 150 feet. The physical work to lift that water is fixed by physics, and a DC submersible pump matched to that head might move it at a rate that requires, say, a few hundred watts of pump input while it's running. If the pump needs to move 300 gallons and it does perhaps 1.5 gallons a minute at that head, it must run roughly 200 minutes — a bit over three hours — to fill the day's tank. It only gets those productive hours when the sun is strong, so you need an array sized to drive the pump through enough of the day's peak sun to accumulate that run time. In a location with, say, 5 peak sun hours, an array of a few hundred watts is plenty to run a few-hundred-watt pump for three-odd hours around midday, with margin for cloudy stretches. Those figures are illustrative and yours will differ with your head, flow, and sun — but the method is what matters: gallons times head sets the work, the work sets the pump's power and run time, and the run time sets the array. The [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) helps translate a target wattage into a physical array, and if you're reasoning from daily energy needs, [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) is a useful cross-check on the array size.

The tank is what buys you resilience, and it's the cheapest resilience you'll ever purchase. Sizing the storage tank for several days of water — not just one day's worth — means a run of cloudy weather doesn't leave you dry, because a full tank carries you through the low-production days until the sun returns to refill it. This is the water equivalent of days of autonomy in a battery system, except a poly tank holding several days of water costs a tiny fraction of a battery bank holding several days of electricity. That cost asymmetry is the whole argument for the water-storage approach: oversizing the tank is cheap insurance, whereas oversizing a battery bank is expensive. A common, robust design pairs a modestly-sized array and pump with a generously-sized tank, letting the tank absorb both daily timing and multi-day weather variability. You pump when you can and draw when you need, and the tank smooths out the mismatch between a sunny midday and a cloudy week.

One design fork deserves a clear word, because it changes the whole parts list: the choice between a purpose-built DC solar pump and a conventional AC pump. A DC submersible pump is designed to run directly off the array through a pump controller, which is why it can skip the battery and inverter entirely — it's the leanest, most failure-resistant option, and it's what most new off-grid pumping installs choose. A conventional AC pump, the kind a grid-connected home would have, needs an inverter to make the AC it runs on, and because of its startup surge it usually also wants a battery to supply that inrush cleanly rather than relying on a fluctuating array. So if you already own an AC pump and want to solarize it, you're generally back in battery-and-inverter territory, which is more hardware, more cost, and more to maintain. If you're building fresh, a DC pump matched to your well is almost always the simpler path. The decision often comes down to whether you're retrofitting existing equipment or starting clean, and it's worth settling early because it determines whether a battery is in your future at all.

There are practical wrinkles worth planning around. If your tank sits uphill or on a stand, gravity gives you pressure at the taps for free — no second pump, no battery to run one at night. If you need pressurized water on demand at any hour, a small pressure tank and a separate pressure pump can draw from the storage tank, and that's the one place a modest battery might re-enter the picture to run the pressure pump after dark, though many homesteads simply gravity-feed and accept lower pressure. Dry-well protection matters too: a controller that shuts the pump off when the well draws down faster than it recharges prevents burning out a pump that's pumping air, which is a real risk on low-yield wells. And the pump has to be matched to the well's recovery rate — a pump that empties the well faster than water seeps back in will cycle on protection all day and never fill the tank, no matter how much sun you have. Getting the pump, the well, and the array matched is the heart of a good design, and it's why sizing from real numbers — your gallons, your head, your well's yield, your sun — beats grabbing a kit off a shelf. Done right, a solar well pump is one of the most satisfying off-grid systems there is: no fuel, no grid, no battery to replace, just panels quietly lifting water into a tank every time the sun clears the horizon.
