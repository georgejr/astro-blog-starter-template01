Connecticut does not get much sun by American standards. Its irradiance sits closer to New England's gray middle than to anything in the Southwest, and its winters are short on daylight. On paper that reads like a weak solar state, the kind you'd expect to see near the bottom of any ranking. In practice it's one of the stronger ones for a homeowner's return, and the reason has almost nothing to do with weather. Connecticut households pay some of the highest electricity rates in the continental United States, and the value of a solar system tracks the price of the power it replaces far more closely than it tracks the number of sunny days.

That one fact reframes the entire decision. A kilowatt-hour your panels generate in Connecticut offsets an unusually expensive kilowatt-hour you'd otherwise have to buy, so each unit of production is simply worth more here than the same unit would be in a cheaper-power state that happens to get more sun. The counterintuitive result — expensive power beating abundant sunshine — is the exact mechanism behind [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/), and Connecticut sits firmly on the favorable side of that divide. Where the state gets genuinely complicated is not whether solar makes sense, but how you're compensated for it, because Connecticut asks new solar homeowners to make a tariff choice that most states don't put in front of them.

## The rate is doing the work, not the weather

Every solar panel does the same physical job everywhere on earth: it turns sunlight into electricity you then don't have to buy from the utility. What changes from state to state isn't the panel — it's the price of the electricity you're avoiding. That price is the multiplier on everything. In a service territory where residential power runs well above the national average, as Connecticut's does, solar's savings per kilowatt-hour are proportionally larger, and a system pays for itself faster than the same hardware would in a region where grid power is cheap.

This is why a modest system in Connecticut can out-earn a larger one somewhere sunnier, which sounds backward until you separate the two halves of the equation. Production — how many kilowatt-hours your array makes — is only one half. The other half is what each of those kilowatt-hours is worth against your bill, and in a high-rate state that second half is doing most of the work. A sunnier state might let your panels produce 20% or 30% more energy, but if its electricity costs half as much per kilowatt-hour, the sunnier system still saves you less money. Dollars saved, not kilowatt-hours generated, is what pays back a solar loan, and dollars saved is a function of your rate first and your sunshine second.

The rate advantage compounds in a way that makes the case sturdier over time, not weaker. Connecticut's electricity prices haven't just been high; they've trended upward for years, and rising grid prices quietly shorten a solar payback because the savings grow while the system's cost stays fixed at what you paid on day one. A system sized to your usage this year keeps offsetting power whose price keeps climbing, so the annual savings in year ten can be meaningfully larger than in year one — the effect worked through in [how rising electricity rates shorten solar payback](/blog/electricity-rate-increases-solar-payback/). For a Connecticut homeowner deciding today, that combination of a high starting rate and a history of increases is the most durable argument for going solar in the state, more durable than any incentive, because it rests on the structural cost of grid power rather than on a policy that could change. You're not betting on the sun. You're betting that expensive power stays expensive, which in Connecticut has been a safe bet for a long time.

None of this means sunshine is irrelevant in Connecticut — it just means it's the junior partner in the equation. Production still follows the seasons here more sharply than it does in the Southwest, with strong summer output and thin winter yields, so a system sized to cover a full year's usage leans on its summer surplus to carry the darker months. Under a self-consumption-oriented tariff, that seasonal swing shapes how much of your own generation you actually use in real time versus send to the grid, which feeds back into both the sizing and the storage decision. The steady fact underneath all that seasonality is, again, the rate. Whether a given kilowatt-hour lands in July or January, it offsets expensive Connecticut grid power, and that dollar value doesn't fade with the daylight the way raw production does. It's precisely why the state rewards a well-sized system despite having a lumpier production calendar than a sunnier state would.

## Buy-all versus netting: Connecticut's tariff fork

Connecticut moved away from traditional retail net metering some years ago and now asks new solar homeowners to choose between two tariff structures, and that choice deserves careful attention because it changes both how and how much your panels earn. Most states hand you a single compensation scheme and you take it as given. Connecticut hands you a fork, and the two branches are genuinely different economic engines rather than two names for the same arrangement. Getting this decision right matters as much as getting the system size right.

Under the netting option, your panels first serve your own home in real time, and only the surplus your household doesn't immediately use flows out to the grid for a credit. This resembles the net metering model most people picture when they imagine home solar: self-consumption is the priority, and you're compensated for the excess you export beyond your own needs. Structurally, it rewards households whose daytime production lines up reasonably well with their daytime consumption, because every kilowatt-hour you consume the moment your panels make it avoids the full retail rate directly — the most valuable thing a solar kilowatt-hour can do. The more of your own production you use on the spot, the better this structure treats you.

Under the buy-all option, the arithmetic inverts. All of your system's generation is sold to the utility at a set tariff rate, and separately you buy back all of your household's consumption at the retail rate. You're not offsetting your own usage in real time at all; in effect you're running two independent meters — one for everything you produce and sell, one for everything you consume and buy. Whether buy-all beats netting for a given household turns on the relationship between the buy-all sell rate you're paid for production and the retail rate you're charged for consumption, which is exactly the distinction drawn out in [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/). If the sell rate is attractive relative to retail, buy-all can come out ahead; if it lags retail significantly, self-consuming your own power under the netting structure tends to win. There's no universal answer — it depends on the specific rates attached to each option when you enroll and on how your usage falls across the day.

The tariff choice also ripples into how you should size the system and whether a battery earns its place. Under a self-consumption-oriented netting structure, matching production to your usage genuinely matters, and shifting flexible loads — the dishwasher, the EV charger, the pool pump — into daylight hours squeezes more value from every panel by turning would-be exports into full-retail self-consumption. A battery is worth more here too, because storing your midday surplus to use in the evening converts low-value exported energy into high-value avoided retail purchases. Under a buy-all structure, self-timing your usage against production matters far less, since you're selling everything you generate regardless of when your household happens to draw power, and a battery's self-consumption benefit largely evaporates because there's no self-consumption to optimize. The practical takeaway is that the tariff you choose should drive the system design — the sizing, the load habits, the battery decision — rather than the other way around, and a design that's optimal under one structure can be merely adequate under the other.

## Running your own Connecticut numbers

Because so much of the Connecticut case rests on your specific rate and your specific tariff choice, statewide averages will mislead you more than they help. Two households on the same street can see meaningfully different economics depending on which structure they enrolled under, how their usage falls across the day, and whether they added storage. The state's high rates make solar attractive across the board, but "attractive" spans a wide range, and where your particular situation lands inside that range is a question only your own inputs can answer.

It also pays to be conservative about the incentive layer when you run these numbers, because the rate advantage is what makes the Connecticut case sturdy, not any single program. The 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit currently lowers the up-front cost of a purchased system, claimed on your taxes per IRS guidance, and it materially shortens payback — but it's a benefit to treat as today's rule rather than a permanent fixture, and the state-level programs and tariff terms attached to solar can shift for new customers over time. A Connecticut decision that pencils only with every incentive stacked at its most generous is more fragile than it looks. One that still works on the strength of the rate alone, with the incentives as upside rather than as load-bearing, is the decision that holds up — and given how high and persistent the state's rates have been, that sturdier version is well within reach for most households here.
