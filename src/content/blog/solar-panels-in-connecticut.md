Connecticut does not get much sun by American standards. Its irradiance sits closer to New England's gray middle than to the sunbelt, and its winters are short on daylight. On paper that looks like a weak solar state. In practice it's one of the stronger ones, and the reason has almost nothing to do with weather. Connecticut homeowners pay some of the highest electricity rates in the continental United States, and the value of solar tracks the price of the power it replaces far more than it tracks the amount of sunshine.

That single fact reframes the whole decision. A kilowatt-hour your panels make in Connecticut offsets an unusually expensive kilowatt-hour you'd otherwise buy, so each unit of production is worth more here than the same unit in a cheap-power state that gets more sun. The counterintuitive result — expensive power beating abundant sun — is the mechanism behind [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/), and Connecticut sits firmly on the favorable side of it.

## The rate story that carries the state

Every panel does the same physical job everywhere: it turns sunlight into electricity you don't have to buy. What changes state to state is the price of the electricity you're avoiding. In a service territory where residential power runs well above the national average — as Connecticut's does — solar's savings per kilowatt-hour are simply larger.

This is why a modest system in Connecticut can out-earn a bigger one somewhere sunnier. Production is only half the equation; the other half is what each unit of that production is worth against your bill. High rates also compound over time, and Connecticut's have trended upward for years. Rising grid prices quietly shorten a solar payback because the savings grow while the system cost stays fixed — the effect worked through in [how rising electricity rates shorten solar payback](/blog/electricity-rate-increases-solar-payback/). For a homeowner deciding today, an expensive and climbing rate is the most durable argument for going solar in the state.

## Buy-all versus netting: Connecticut's tariff fork

Connecticut moved away from traditional retail net metering and now asks new solar homeowners to choose between two tariff structures. The choice is unusual enough that it deserves careful attention, because it changes how — and how much — your panels earn. In broad terms, the program hands you a fork between keeping your power and selling all of it.

### The netting option

Under the netting structure, your panels first serve your own home, and only the surplus flows to the grid for a credit. This resembles the net metering model most people picture: self-consumption is the priority, and you're compensated for what you export beyond your own use. It tends to favor households whose daytime production lines up reasonably with their consumption, because every self-consumed kilowatt-hour avoids the full retail rate directly.

### The buy-all option

Under buy-all, the arithmetic inverts. All of your system's generation is sold to the utility at a set tariff rate, and separately you buy all of your household consumption back at the retail rate. You're not offsetting your own use in real time; you're running two meters, in effect — one for what you produce and sell, one for what you consume and buy. Whether this beats netting depends on the relationship between the buy-all sell rate and your retail buy rate, which is exactly the distinction drawn out in [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/).

The practical takeaway is that these are genuinely different economic engines, not two labels for the same thing. One rewards using your own solar; the other pays you a fixed price for producing it and charges you retail for living in the house. The right pick depends on your consumption pattern and the specific rates attached to each option when you enroll.

## What the tariff choice does to sizing and storage

The fork also changes how you should size the system and whether a battery earns its keep. Under a self-consumption-oriented netting structure, matching production to your usage matters, and shifting flexible loads into daylight hours squeezes more value from each panel. Under a buy-all structure, self-timing your usage against production matters less, because you're selling everything regardless — the sizing question becomes more about the sell rate than about daytime alignment.

Storage interacts with this too. A battery that lets you use more of your own solar is worth more under a netting model, where self-consumption is directly rewarded, than under a buy-all model where all generation is sold anyway. None of this has a universal answer; it turns on the exact rates and your own load shape. What's constant is that the tariff you choose should drive the system design, not the other way around.
