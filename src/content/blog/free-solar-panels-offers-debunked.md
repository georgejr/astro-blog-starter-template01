There is no government program handing out free rooftop solar, and no company installs $20,000 of equipment on your house out of generosity. When an ad promises "free solar panels" or "no-cost solar," it is almost always describing a lease or a power purchase agreement — arrangements where a third party owns the hardware and you pay them, monthly, for decades. The word "free" refers only to the upfront cost being zero. Everything after that is a bill, and the size and shape of that bill is the part the marketing works hardest to keep out of frame.

None of which makes these deals automatically bad. For a homeowner who can't use the federal tax credit, or who doesn't have the cash or the appetite to finance a purchase, a well-structured lease can genuinely lower a power bill with no money down. The problem is that the pitch sells a feeling — free — while the value lives entirely in the mechanics, and the mechanics are exactly what a headline like "$0 down solar" is designed to skip past. Understanding what you're signing tells you who actually benefits, who owns the system on your roof, and where the cost you were promised didn't exist quietly reappears.

## What "free" actually means once you read the contract

Two structures dominate the "free solar" pitch, and the ad copy for both is nearly identical even though they work differently. A lease rents you the equipment: you pay a fixed monthly amount to have the panels on your roof and use their output, roughly regardless of exactly how much they produce in a given month. A power purchase agreement, or PPA, sells you the electricity instead of the hardware — you pay a set price per kilowatt-hour for whatever the panels generate, so your payment tracks production. One is a rental of the machine; the other is a metered purchase of what the machine makes.

What both share is the part that matters most, and it's the part the ads never state plainly: a third party owns the system. They install it, they maintain it, and — because ownership is what confers the benefit — they claim the tax credit and any incentives, not you. The 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit goes to whoever owns the equipment, claimed on their taxes per IRS guidance. In a lease or PPA, that owner is the company. The monthly savings you're offered are what's left over after the provider has already captured the incentive value for itself. You're being quoted the residual, framed as a gift.

That single fact is why owning and leasing produce such different lifetime numbers. When you buy, the tax credit lands in your pocket, and once the system is paid off your cost for that power drops to essentially nothing for the rest of its life. When you lease, the credit lands in the provider's pocket, and you keep paying every month for as long as the contract runs. The gap between those two outcomes over 20 or 25 years is large enough that it should anchor the whole decision, and it's the entire subject of [leasing versus buying solar panels](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/). The ad wants you comparing your lease payment to your current electric bill. The comparison that actually decides it is your lease payment against ownership.

## The escalator and the other clauses that bite later

The clause that quietly reshapes a "free solar" offer is the annual escalator — a provision that raises your lease payment or PPA rate every year for the life of the contract. Escalators vary: some contracts are flat at 0%, some run above 3%, and a common assumption sits around 2.9% per year. That figure sounds trivial. Compounded across a 20-to-25-year term, it isn't.

Follow the arithmetic on a PPA that starts at an assumed $0.14 per kilowatt-hour with a 2.9% annual escalator — labeled assumptions, to show the shape rather than quote a live rate. By year 15, that rate has compounded past roughly $0.21 per kilowatt-hour. By the end of a 25-year term, it's above $0.28 — for power coming off panels that have been sitting on your roof the entire time, produced by a system whose cost to the provider never went up. The pitch compares only the year-one payment against today's utility bill and shows a saving. Whether that saving survives to year 15 depends entirely on whether your escalator stays below your utility's own rate increases over the same period, and that is a bet on two moving numbers, not a guarantee. How a PPA's rate and escalator actually compound over the term is worked through in [how solar PPAs price your power](/blog/solar-ppa-costs-explained/), and running the full curve is the difference between knowing the deal and trusting the brochure.

The escalator rarely acts alone. The catch in a "free solar" contract is usually not one dramatic clause but several ordinary ones that only sting years later. Ownership staying with the company for the full term is the root of it — the tax credit and incentives are theirs, not yours, for the life of the agreement. Transfer at sale is the sleeper: when you sell the house, the buyer generally has to qualify to assume the lease or PPA, or you have to buy it out, and a lien or UCC filing tied to the system can complicate the closing at the worst possible moment. Buyout terms are frequently front-loaded, so leaving early costs more than simply adding up the remaining payments would suggest. Production guarantees vary too — some contracts promise a minimum output and credit you when the system underperforms, while others leave that shortfall risk sitting with you. None of these makes a lease fraudulent. Together they make it a long-term financial commitment dressed as a giveaway, which is exactly why a "$0 down" headline deserves the same scrutiny you'd give any 25-year contract. The broader pattern of how these zero-down structures actually pencil out is examined in [the real savings math behind $0-down solar deals](/blog/zero-down-solar-savings-math/).

## Pricing the deal on its own terms

The way to evaluate a "free solar" offer is to delete the word "free" from your reading of it and price the contract as what it is: a decades-long stream of payments in exchange for a stream of electricity or the use of equipment. Add up every payment over the full term, escalator included, and put that total next to two other numbers — what you'd otherwise pay the utility over the same years, and what buying a comparable system outright would cost you including the tax credit you'd keep. Only against those two benchmarks does the offer reveal whether it's a discount or just a differently-shaped bill.

Owning almost always wins on lifetime dollars, for the simple reason that you keep the 30% credit and you stop paying once the system is bought, while a lease keeps billing you the whole term. But "almost always" isn't "always," and there are real situations where a lease or PPA is the better available option: you can't use the tax credit because you don't have the tax liability to absorb it, you don't have the cash or credit profile to finance a purchase on decent terms, or you specifically value having someone else carry the maintenance and performance risk. In those cases a no-money-down arrangement can still trim a bill, and there's nothing wrong with taking it — as long as you took it with the numbers in front of you.

One argument for a lease deserves a fair hearing rather than a reflexive dismissal, because the sales pitch leans on it and it isn't nothing: the provider owns the maintenance and performance risk. If an inverter fails or production sags, that's their problem to fix, not yours, and for a homeowner who has no interest in thinking about the system once it's installed, that hands-off quality carries genuine value. The catch is scale. Rooftop solar is famously low-maintenance — panels have no moving parts, and most systems ask little of their owners for years at a stretch — so the risk you're paying the provider to absorb is modest to begin with. Weigh it honestly against what you're giving up, and it rarely comes close to offsetting the combined cost of surrendering the tax credit and riding an escalator for two decades. It's a real benefit, worth pennies against the dollars it's being used to justify.

Run both scenarios before signing anything. If the "free" offer still comes out ahead for your specific situation after that comparison, it may genuinely fit your circumstances — take it knowingly. If it doesn't, you'll have learned, in dollars, exactly how much the word "free" was quietly costing you over the life of the contract.

## FAQ

**Is there any truly free government solar program?**
No federal program gives homeowners free panels. There are incentives — most prominently the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit — that lower the cost of a system you buy, and some states or utilities run rebates or income-qualified programs. But "free solar" advertising almost always points to a lease or PPA, where the upfront cost is zero and the payments run for decades, not to a giveaway.

**Who gets the tax credit on leased solar?**
The system's owner, which in a lease or PPA is the third-party company, not you. That's a core reason buying tends to beat leasing over the full lifespan for anyone with the tax liability to actually use the credit.

**Can I get out of a solar lease if I sell my house?**
Usually you either transfer the agreement to a buyer who qualifies to assume it, or you buy it out. Both can complicate a home sale, and buyout costs are often front-loaded, so read the transfer and buyout clauses closely before you sign rather than discovering them at closing.
