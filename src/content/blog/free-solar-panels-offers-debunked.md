There is no government program handing out free rooftop solar, and no company installs $20,000 of equipment on your house out of generosity. When an ad promises "free solar panels" or "no-cost solar," it is almost always describing a lease or a power purchase agreement — arrangements where a third party owns the hardware and you pay them, monthly, for decades. The word "free" refers to the upfront cost being zero. Everything after that is a bill.

That's not automatically a bad deal. For a homeowner who can't use the federal tax credit or doesn't want to finance a purchase, a well-structured lease can still cut a power bill. The problem is that the marketing hides the mechanics, and the mechanics are the whole story. What you're signing determines who benefits, who owns the system, and where the cost you were promised didn't exist quietly shows up.

## What "free" actually means in each contract

Two structures dominate the "free solar" pitch, and they work differently even though the ad copy is identical.

A **lease** rents you the equipment. You pay a fixed monthly amount to use the panels on your roof, regardless of exactly how much power they make. A **power purchase agreement**, or PPA, sells you the electricity the panels produce at a set per-kilowatt-hour rate — you pay for output, not for the hardware. In both, a third party owns the system, maintains it, and, crucially, claims the tax benefits and any incentives, because they are the owner, not you.

That last point is the one the ads never mention. The 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit goes to whoever owns the system. In a lease or PPA, that's the company. The savings you're offered are what's left after they've taken the incentive value for themselves. The difference in lifetime economics between owning and not owning is the entire subject of [leasing versus buying solar panels](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/), and it's stark enough that it should drive the decision.

## The escalator is where the deal changes shape

The number that quietly reshapes a "free solar" offer is the annual escalator — a clause that raises your lease payment or PPA rate every year. A common assumption is somewhere around 2.9% per year, though contracts range from a flat 0% to more than 3%. It sounds small. Over a 20-to-25-year term it is not.

Consider a PPA starting at an assumed $0.14/kWh with a 2.9% annual escalator. By year 15 that rate has climbed past $0.21/kWh, and by the end of a 25-year term it's above $0.28 — all for power from panels that have been sitting on your roof the whole time. The pitch compares year-one solar payments against today's utility bill and shows a saving. Whether the saving survives depends entirely on whether your escalator outruns the utility's own rate increases, and that's a bet, not a guarantee. The mechanics of how a PPA's rate and escalator compound are worked through in [how solar PPAs actually price your power](/blog/solar-ppa-costs-explained/).

## Where the catch usually lives

The catch in a "free solar" deal is rarely one dramatic clause. It's several ordinary ones that only bite later:

- **Ownership stays with the company** for the full term, so the tax credit and incentives are theirs, not yours.
- **The escalator** can push your payment above what the utility would have charged, erasing the promised saving in later years.
- **Transfer at sale** is the sleeper problem: when you sell the house, the buyer must qualify to assume the lease or PPA, or you buy it out. A lien or UCC filing tied to the system can complicate the closing.
- **Buyout terms** are often front-loaded, so leaving early costs more than the remaining payments suggest.
- **Production guarantees** vary; some contracts promise a minimum output and credit you if the system underperforms, while others leave that risk with you.

None of these makes a lease fraudulent. They make it a long-term financial commitment dressed as a giveaway, and they're the reason a "$0 down" headline deserves the same scrutiny as any 25-year contract. The broader pattern of how zero-down offers actually pencil out is examined in [the real savings math behind $0-down solar deals](/blog/zero-down-solar-savings-math/).

## How to evaluate the offer honestly

Ignore the word "free" and price the deal on its terms. Add up the payments over the full contract, escalator included, and compare that total against what you'd pay the utility over the same years and against what an outright purchase would cost. Owning almost always wins on lifetime dollars because you keep the tax credit and stop paying once the system is bought; a lease or PPA can still make sense when you can't use the credit, don't have the cash or credit for a purchase, and value having someone else own the maintenance risk.

Run both scenarios before you sign. Estimate what a purchased system of the same size would cost with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), then compare its payback against the lease's total payments using the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If the "free" offer still comes out ahead for your situation after that comparison, it may genuinely fit. If it doesn't, you'll have learned exactly how much the word "free" was costing you.

## FAQ

**Is there any truly free government solar program?**
No federal program gives homeowners free panels. There are incentives — most notably the 30% federal tax credit — that lower the cost of a system you buy, and some states or utilities run rebates or income-qualified programs. But "free solar" advertising almost always points to a lease or PPA, not a giveaway.

**Who gets the tax credit on leased solar?**
The system's owner, which in a lease or PPA is the third-party company. That's a core reason buying tends to beat leasing over the full lifespan for anyone with the tax liability to use the credit.

**Can I get out of a solar lease if I sell my house?**
Usually you either transfer the agreement to a qualifying buyer or buy it out. Both can complicate a sale, so read the transfer and buyout clauses closely before signing rather than at closing.
