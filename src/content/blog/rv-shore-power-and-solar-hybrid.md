Most people shopping for RV solar imagine an either/or: hookups at the campground or panels in the wild. The setup that actually fits how most people travel is neither extreme — it's a hybrid that takes whichever source is available, charges the same battery bank, and never asks you to flip a bunch of switches when you pull into a site with power. Built right, the rig doesn't care where the electrons come from.

## The battery bank is the hub, not the shore cord

The mental model that makes a hybrid setup click: your loads don't run off shore power *or* solar directly. They run off the battery bank, and both shore power and solar are just chargers feeding that bank. Once you see the battery as the center of the system, the wiring stops feeling like two competing setups bolted together.

Shore power reaches the battery through the RV's converter or an inverter-charger, which turns 120-volt AC from the pedestal into DC to top up the batteries. Solar reaches the same battery through a charge controller, which conditions the panels' variable output into a clean charge. Both are pointed at the same target. When you're plugged in, the converter carries the house loads and keeps the batteries full; when you unplug, solar quietly takes over without you touching anything.

If you're still deciding how much solar to put on the roof in the first place, [your first RV solar setup](/blog/rv-solar-starter-sizing/) covers sizing the array and bank to a real load list, which is the step that should come before any of this wiring.

## How the sources take turns

A good hybrid system prioritizes on its own. The general order of preference looks like this:

- **Shore power when it's plugged in.** Grid power is cheap and unlimited at a hookup, so the converter or inverter-charger handles the loads and charges the bank whenever you're connected.
- **Solar the rest of the time.** Off the pedestal, the charge controller runs the show, refilling what the batteries gave up overnight.
- **The alternator as a bonus.** Many rigs add a DC-to-DC charger so the engine tops up the house batteries while you drive, which is handy on cloudy travel days.

The battery bank buffers all of it. Because everything charges the same bank, there's no switching logic to get wrong — the loads simply pull from whatever charge is there, and the sources refill it in the background.

### Where the inverter and transfer switch fit

An inverter turns battery DC back into 120-volt AC for your outlets and larger appliances when you're not plugged in. Rigs that want seamless behavior use an inverter-charger, a single box that both charges from shore power and inverts for the outlets, with an automatic transfer switch inside. That transfer switch is the quiet hero of the hybrid: plug in and it passes shore power straight through to your outlets and switches to charging mode; unplug and it flips to inverter mode off the battery, usually fast enough that you barely notice. The AC-versus-DC coupling choices and inverter sizing get more involved on bigger builds, but for most travel trailers and motorhomes a single inverter-charger keeps it simple.

## Panels, portable or roof, feed the same bank

Whether your solar lives on the roof or leans against the rig on the ground doesn't change the hub-and-charger logic — both routes end at the charge controller and the battery. The trade-offs between the two mounting styles, from convenience to chasing the sun, are laid out in [roof-mounted versus portable panels](/blog/rv-roof-vs-portable-solar-panels/). Many hybrid users run a modest roof array for passive daily charging and keep a portable panel to deploy when they're parked in shade.

One decision that ripples through the whole build is system voltage. A 12-volt house system is simplest and matches most RV appliances, but larger banks and inverters increasingly favor higher voltages for thinner wiring and less loss — the [12V, 24V, or 48V comparison](/blog/12v-vs-24v-vs-48v-solar-systems/) is worth reading before you buy components you can't easily change later.

## Sizing the bank for how you actually camp

The hybrid's whole appeal is flexibility, so size the battery for the times you're *off* hookups, not the times you're on them. Ask how many hours you typically spend between charging opportunities and what runs during them — the fridge, water pump, lights, and any 120-volt draws through the inverter. Feed a realistic daily load into the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) to see how much usable capacity you actually need, and check the array against your roof space with the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/).

The reason this setup is the one most people end up wanting is that it removes the decision. You plug in when power's there, you don't when it isn't, and the rig sorts out the rest. The engineering is just making sure every source lands on the same battery and that the transfer switch handles the handoff without waking you up.
