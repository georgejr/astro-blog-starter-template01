Most people shopping for RV solar frame it as an either/or: hookups at the campground or panels out in the wild. The setup that actually matches how the majority of people travel is neither extreme. It's a hybrid that takes whichever source happens to be available, charges the same battery bank, and never asks you to flip a row of switches when you pull into a site with power. Built right, the rig genuinely doesn't care where the electrons come from — you plug in when there's a pedestal, you don't when there isn't, and the system sorts out the rest in the background. Understanding why that works comes down to one mental shift about what sits at the center of the whole electrical system.

## The battery bank is the hub, and both sources are just chargers

The idea that makes a hybrid setup click is that your loads don't run off shore power or solar directly. They run off the battery bank, and both shore power and solar are simply chargers feeding that bank. Once you picture the battery as the center of the system with everything else pointed at it, the wiring stops looking like two competing setups awkwardly bolted together and starts looking like one system with multiple ways to refill the same tank.

Shore power reaches the battery through the RV's converter or, on more capable rigs, an inverter-charger. Either device takes the 120-volt AC coming off the campground pedestal and turns it into DC to top up the batteries and carry the house loads while you're connected. Solar reaches that same battery through a charge controller, which conditions the panels' variable output — bright noon sun, thin morning light, a passing cloud — into a clean, controlled charge the battery can accept. Both devices aim at the identical target. When you're plugged in, the converter or charger carries the loads and keeps the bank full; when you unplug, solar quietly takes over the refilling job without you touching a thing. The battery in the middle buffers all of it, so there's no moment where the rig has to choose a source and no switching logic for you to get wrong.

A good hybrid system also prioritizes on its own, and the order of preference is common sense once you see it. Shore power wins whenever it's plugged in, because grid power at a hookup is cheap and effectively unlimited — there's no reason to lean on stored energy when the pedestal is doing the work and topping the bank at the same time. Off the pedestal, the charge controller runs the show, refilling overnight's draw as soon as the sun clears the trees. Many rigs add a third source, a DC-to-DC charger that lets the engine's alternator top up the house batteries while you drive, which turns an otherwise wasted travel day — especially a cloudy one — into charging time. All three feed the same bank, and because they do, the loads simply pull from whatever charge is present while the sources refill it behind the scenes.

One question that comes up constantly with hybrid setups is what happens at the moment of transition, and the honest answer is that a well-built system makes it a non-event. When you're boondocking on solar and pull into a site with a pedestal, you plug in the shore cord and the converter or charger immediately takes over the charging and the loads, while solar quietly steps back to whatever topping-up is still needed. When you leave and unplug, solar picks the job back up without a pause you'd notice. Nothing about the battery bank cares which source last touched it — it's the same electrochemistry storing the same energy, refilled by whichever charger has power available. That indifference is the entire design goal, and it's why the hybrid feels less like managing two systems and more like owning one flexible system with a couple of ways to feed it. The failure mode to avoid is a build where the two sources fight, or where you have to manually disconnect one before engaging the other; a properly wired hybrid never puts you in that position.

The inverter and the transfer switch are where the seamlessness actually lives, and they're worth understanding rather than treating as a black box. An inverter turns the battery's DC back into 120-volt AC for your outlets and larger appliances when you're not plugged in. Rigs that want the setup to feel automatic use an inverter-charger — a single box that both charges from shore power and inverts for the outlets — with an automatic transfer switch built inside. That transfer switch is the quiet hero of the whole arrangement. Plug in, and it passes shore power straight through to your outlets while switching the unit into charging mode; unplug, and it flips to inverter mode off the battery, usually fast enough that you barely notice a flicker. On bigger builds the choices around AC-versus-DC coupling and inverter sizing get more involved, but for the typical travel trailer or motorhome, a single well-chosen inverter-charger keeps the entire thing simple and reliable. ## Feeding the same bank, and sizing it for how you actually camp

Whether your solar lives permanently on the roof or leans against the rig on the ground doesn't change the hub-and-charger logic one bit — both routes end at a charge controller and then the battery. The trade-offs between the two mounting styles are real, though, and they shape day-to-day life more than the wiring does. Roof panels charge passively all day with zero effort, but they only see whatever sun falls on wherever you happened to park, which is often partial shade under a nice tree. Portable panels let you chase the sun and keep the rig itself in the shade, at the cost of hauling them out, aiming them, and packing them up each time. A lot of hybrid users land on a sensible middle: a modest roof array for hands-off daily charging, plus a portable panel to deploy when they're parked somewhere shady and want more. The full comparison, from convenience to output to security, is laid out in [roof-mounted versus portable panels](/blog/rv-roof-vs-portable-solar-panels/), and it's worth reading before you commit to one approach, because the two aren't mutually exclusive.

One decision ripples through the entire build and is genuinely hard to change later: system voltage. A 12-volt house system is the simplest and matches most factory RV appliances, wiring, and accessories, which is why it's the default on the majority of rigs. But as banks and inverters grow, higher voltages start to make sense, because moving the same power at a higher voltage means lower current — and lower current means thinner, cheaper wire and less energy lost as heat along the runs. A big inverter pulling heavy current at 12 volts needs cables thick enough to be genuinely awkward to route, a problem that eases considerably at 24 or 48 volts. This is exactly the kind of choice you want to make before buying components you can't easily swap, and the [12V, 24V, or 48V comparison](/blog/12v-vs-24v-vs-48v-solar-systems/) walks through where each voltage stops making sense and the next one takes over. For a small weekend trailer, 12 volts is almost always right; for a full-time rig running air conditioning off batteries, the calculus shifts.

Sizing the battery bank is where the hybrid's whole appeal gets translated into a number, and the guiding principle is to size for the times you're off hookups, not the times you're on them. When you're plugged in, the pedestal covers everything and the bank barely matters; the capacity you're really buying is the buffer that carries you through the hours and days between charging opportunities. So the honest questions are how long you typically go between plugging in or seeing strong sun, and what actually runs during that stretch — the fridge cycling day and night, the water pump, the lights, the furnace fan on cold mornings, and any 120-volt draws routed through the inverter. Add those up into a realistic daily load rather than a wishful one, and feed it into the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) to see how much usable capacity you genuinely need, remembering that usable is less than nameplate once you account for not draining the bank flat. Then check the array that would refill that bank against your available roof space with the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/), so the panels and the battery are matched rather than one starving the other.

The reason this setup is the one most people end up wanting, once they've lived with an RV for a season or two, is that it quietly removes a decision they were tired of making. You plug in when power's there and you don't when it isn't, and the rig handles the handoff without any input from you. The engineering underneath is almost anticlimactic: make sure every source — shore power, solar, and the alternator if you add it — lands on the same battery bank, and make sure the transfer switch manages the shore-power handoff cleanly so it never wakes you up at 2 a.m. Get those two things right, and the flexibility that sold you on the hybrid in the first place is simply there, every trip, without you thinking about it.
