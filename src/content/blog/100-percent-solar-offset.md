Ask most homeowners what size system they want and the answer comes out almost before they've thought about it: enough to cover the whole bill. A 100% offset — a year's production matching a year's usage — feels like the natural finish line, the point where the utility finally stops taking your money. It's a clean, satisfying goal, and for a long time it was straightforwardly the right one. But in a growing number of markets it's now slightly more system than the economics justify, and the reason has nothing to do with how much energy you use or how ambitious you are. It comes down to a single number buried in your utility's tariff: what they pay you for the surplus you can't use yourself. Get that number wrong in your planning and you can end up buying the least valuable panels on the roof and calling it a win.

## What "100% offset" actually measures, and why the surplus rule decides everything

Offset is an annual, netted figure and nothing more: total kilowatt-hours produced across a year divided by total kilowatt-hours consumed across the same year. A 100% offset means those two numbers match over twelve months. What it emphatically does not mean is that your panels power your house in real time. Solar overproduces in the middle of the day and through the summer; your home draws most of its power in the evening and through the winter. The annual match is an accounting result stitched together from months of surplus and months of shortfall, not a moment-to-moment balance — a distinction that trips up a lot of first-time buyers, and one unpacked more fully in [what a 100% offset really means for your bill](/blog/solar-offset-explained/). That timing gap between when you produce and when you consume is the entire reason offset percentage and dollar savings aren't the same thing. To reach 100% on paper, you have to export a great deal of midday and summer surplus and then pull energy back — in value, not in the same electrons — during the dark, low-production months. Whether that's a good trade or a lousy one depends completely on the rules your utility applies to the exchange.

Under full-retail net metering, every kilowatt-hour you export banks at the same price you'd pay to buy one. Summer surplus offsets winter purchases one-for-one, dollar for dollar, and in that world chasing 100% offset makes obvious sense. The last panel you add is nearly as valuable as the first, because whatever it overproduces gets stored at full retail value and comes back to you at full retail value. There's no penalty for the timing mismatch, so there's little reason not to size all the way up. The moment your utility moves to net billing or an export tariff, though, the math bends hard. Now the energy you export earns less — sometimes far less — than retail, while the power you buy back at night and in winter still costs you the full retail price. The two ends of the same system suddenly behave like different investments. Panels whose output you consume on-site the instant it's generated remain worth the full retail rate you avoid paying. Panels that push your production past your real-time needs generate surplus valued at the lower export rate, and that gap can be large. As you scale a system toward 100% offset, you're increasingly adding capacity that produces during hours the house is already covered — so that capacity spends more and more of its time feeding cheap exports rather than displacing expensive retail purchases. The system keeps getting physically bigger and more expensive, while the marginal value of each panel you add keeps shrinking. The difference between those two billing worlds — retail-for-retail versus export-at-a-discount — is the single pivot the whole sizing decision turns on, and it's worth understanding precisely, because it's laid out in [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/). Miss it and you'll size for a world your utility left behind.

## Why the sweet spot often sits below 100% — and when it doesn't

In an export-penalized market, the most valuable kilowatt-hours your system will ever produce are the ones you self-consume, and those come from the first chunk of capacity — enough to cover your daytime baseload and shave the peak off your bill. Every panel beyond that point spends a growing share of its life feeding surplus into the grid at the discounted export rate. Follow that logic and the economic optimum stops sitting at a full 100% offset. Under many modern rate structures it lands somewhere around **80–90%** offset instead. A system built to that level costs less than a full-offset system, and — this is the part people miss — the panels you chose not to buy were precisely the ones that would have earned the least, because their output would have been the surplus dumped at the export rate. You give up a small slice of bill reduction and, in exchange, keep a better return on every dollar you did spend. Push in the other direction, past 100% and into deliberate oversizing, and the effect only intensifies: now you're adding panels whose entire output is cheap export, and they can actively drag on the system's overall return. The point where extra capacity starts working against you is the subject of [the oversizing penalty](/blog/oversized-solar-system-payback/), and it's a real threshold, not a hypothetical one.

None of this applies cleanly where full-retail net metering still holds, and it's worth saying that plainly so the "aim below 100%" advice doesn't get stamped onto situations it doesn't fit. Where surplus banks at retail value, 100% remains a perfectly defensible target, and even slight oversizing costs you very little because the marginal panel keeps its value. There are also good reasons to size for a full offset — or close to it — that have nothing to do with the export rate. Rising or electrifying load is the biggest one. If an EV, a heat pump, or simply a growing household is on the horizon, sizing to today's usage undershoots tomorrow's, and building headroom in now can easily beat paying for a costly array expansion in three years, when adding a few panels to an existing system carries its own fixed costs and permitting all over again. A strong personal preference for energy independence is another legitimate reason, provided it's a choice made with the trade-off understood rather than a default reached for by reflex — some people genuinely value producing as much of their own power as possible and are happy to trade a percentage point of return for it. The common thread running through the exceptions is that full offset earns its keep when the extra panels either hold their value under the local rules or serve a load that's genuinely coming. What tends to overspend is the reflexive, market-blind pursuit of 100% simply because it's a round number and feels like the point of the whole exercise. The right offset was never a universal figure. It's a function of your export rate, and it moves as policy moves.

## Sizing it deliberately

The way to avoid both under-building and over-building is to stop treating "what size system" as one question and split it into two, because they have different answers and different inputs. The first question sets the ceiling: what is your real annual usage, including any electrification you can already see coming down the road? That number tells you how large a system would ever make sense — the array that reaches a full 100% offset of your genuine, forward-looking consumption. The second question decides how close to that ceiling you should actually build: what does your utility pay for exports? If the answer is full retail, build to the ceiling without much hesitation. If the answer is a discounted export rate, the ceiling becomes the top of a range rather than the target, and the smart move is to stop short of it.

How far short depends on the shape of your own consumption, which is worth thinking through concretely rather than reaching for a generic percentage. A household with someone home during the day — running appliances, working remotely, cooling the house through the afternoon while the panels are at their peak — self-consumes a larger share of its production directly, and that pushes the sensible offset higher, because more of what the panels make is displacing expensive retail power rather than being dumped as cheap export. A household that empties out every weekday, everyone at work and school precisely when the sun is strongest, self-consumes far less; its midday surplus flows to the grid at the discounted rate almost by default, which pulls the sensible offset lower. Same roof, same utility, same annual usage — but a different daily rhythm produces a genuinely different right answer. This is also the point where a battery changes the calculation, because storing midday surplus to use after dark converts would-be exports back into self-consumption and restores the value of the upper panels. Where export rates are stingy enough, adding storage can justify sizing closer to a full offset than the export rate alone would ever support — provided the battery's own cost pencils out on its own terms, which is a separate question rather than a free pass to oversize.

In practice, run your annual kilowatt-hours — today's, plus honest headroom for anything you know is coming — through the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) to find the array that hits 100%. Then treat that array as the maximum sensible size, not the goal. If you're on an export-penalized rate, test a system a notch smaller against it and compare what each one returns. More often than not, the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) will show the slightly smaller build earning more per dollar spent, because you've trimmed off exactly the low-value export panels while keeping all the high-value self-consumption capacity. That comparison is the whole exercise: you're not guessing at a percentage, you're pricing the last chunk of the system against what it actually earns. A full offset is a fine outcome when the numbers back it, and in a full-retail net metering market they usually do. It's a poor default when they don't, and the only way to know which world you're sizing for is to find out what your utility pays for the surplus before you decide how much surplus to build.
