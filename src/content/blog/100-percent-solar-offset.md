Ask most homeowners what size system they want and the answer is instinctive: enough to cover the whole bill. A 100% offset — annual production matching annual usage — feels like the natural finish line, the point where the utility stops taking your money. It's a clean goal. It's also, in a growing number of markets, slightly more system than the economics justify. Whether full offset is the right target depends less on your ambition than on what your utility pays for the surplus you can't use yourself.

## What "100% offset" actually measures

Offset is an annual, netted figure: total kilowatt-hours produced over a year divided by total consumed. A 100% offset means those two numbers match across twelve months. It does not mean your panels power your house in real time. Solar overproduces at midday and in summer; your home draws power at night and in winter. The annual match is an accounting result stitched together from months of surplus and months of shortfall — the distinction that trips people up is unpacked in [what a 100% offset really means for your bill](/blog/solar-offset-explained/).

That timing gap is the whole reason offset percentage and savings aren't the same thing. Reaching 100% on paper requires exporting a lot of midday and summer surplus and pulling it back — in value, not in electrons — during the dark months. How good that trade is comes down entirely to your utility's rules.

## The rule that decides everything: what surplus is worth

Under full-retail net metering, every exported kilowatt-hour banks at the same price you'd pay to buy one. Summer surplus offsets winter purchases one-for-one, and chasing 100% offset makes clean sense: the last panel you add is nearly as valuable as the first, because its overproduction is stored at full value.

The moment your utility moves to net billing or an export tariff, the math bends. Now exported energy earns less — sometimes far less — than retail, while the power you buy back still costs full price. Under those rules the two ends of a system behave very differently:

- Panels whose output you consume on-site as it's generated are worth the full retail rate you avoid paying.
- Panels that push you past your real-time needs produce surplus valued at the lower export rate.

As you scale toward 100% offset, you add more and more capacity that produces during hours you're already covered — so its output increasingly earns the export rate rather than the retail rate. The system keeps getting bigger; the *marginal* value of each added panel keeps shrinking. The difference between these two billing worlds is the pivot the whole sizing decision turns on, and it's laid out in [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/).

## Why the sweet spot often sits below 100%

In an export-penalized market, the most valuable kilowatt-hours are the ones you self-consume. Those come from the first chunk of system capacity — enough to cover your daytime baseload and shave the peak of your bill. Beyond that, each additional panel spends more of its time feeding cheap exports.

This is why, under many modern rate structures, the economic optimum lands somewhere around **80–90%** offset rather than a full 100%. A slightly smaller system costs less, and the panels you *didn't* buy were the ones that would have earned the least. You give up a small slice of bill reduction and keep a better return on every dollar spent. Push past 100% into deliberate oversizing and the effect intensifies — the point where extra panels actively drag on returns is the subject of [the oversizing penalty](/blog/oversized-solar-system-payback/).

None of this applies cleanly where full-retail net metering still holds. There, 100% remains a defensible target and even slight oversizing costs little. The right offset isn't a universal number — it's a function of your export rate, and it moves as policy moves.

## When aiming for full offset still makes sense

Plenty of situations justify sizing for a complete offset or close to it, and they're worth naming so the "less than 100%" advice doesn't get overapplied:

- **Full-retail net metering.** Surplus banks at retail value, so the marginal panel stays valuable.
- **Rising or electrifying load.** If an EV, a heat pump, or a growing household is on the horizon, sizing to today's usage undershoots tomorrow's. Building in headroom now can beat a costly expansion later.
- **A strong personal preference for energy independence** over squeezing the last percentage point of return — a legitimate reason as long as it's a choice made with the trade-off understood, not by accident.

The common thread is that full offset is right when the extra panels either retain their value or serve a load that's coming. It's the reflexive, market-blind pursuit of 100% that tends to overspend.

## Sizing it deliberately

The way to avoid both under- and over-building is to separate two questions. First, what's your real annual usage, including any electrification you can already see coming? Second, what does your utility pay for exports? The first sets the ceiling of a sensible system; the second decides how close to that ceiling you should build.

Run your annual kilowatt-hours through the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) to find the array that reaches 100%, then treat that as the top of the range rather than the target. If you're on an export-penalized rate, test a system a notch smaller and compare the returns — the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) will usually show the slightly smaller build earning more per dollar. A full offset is a fine outcome when the numbers back it. It's a poor default when they don't, and the only way to tell which you're in is to price the surplus.
