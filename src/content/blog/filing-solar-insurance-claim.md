A hailstorm rolls through, a tree limb comes down, or a windstorm peels a panel loose, and suddenly you're staring at an expensive-looking problem on your roof with no idea who to call first. The instinct is often to phone the solar installer, but that may be the wrong first move, because storm and impact damage to a solar array is usually a homeowners insurance matter, not a warranty one — and how you handle the opening hours and days of that situation shapes how smoothly the claim goes and how much you end up paying out of pocket. Filing a claim on a damaged solar system isn't fundamentally different from filing one for a damaged roof or a fallen fence, but panels add a couple of wrinkles: they're specialized equipment that not every adjuster understands, and the line between insurable damage and a warranty or maintenance issue can be blurry. Knowing the process ahead of time — before the storm — is what lets you move quickly and confidently when something actually hits.

## Before you file: is this even an insurance claim?

The first question to answer isn't "how do I file" but "should I file, and with whom," because filing the wrong kind of claim wastes time and can even count against you. Homeowners insurance generally covers sudden, accidental damage from covered perils — hail, wind, lightning, fire, falling objects like tree limbs, and similar events. If a storm cracked your panels or ripped part of the array off the roof, that's squarely the kind of thing a homeowners policy is meant to handle, and most standard policies treat roof-mounted solar as part of the dwelling, so the array is covered the same way the roof under it is. What insurance does not cover is the stuff that warranties and maintenance are for: a panel that failed from a manufacturing defect, gradual wear, degradation over time, or a problem caused by faulty installation. Those belong to the equipment manufacturer's warranty or the installer's workmanship warranty, not to your insurer, and trying to route them through a claim will get denied.

So the triage is this. If the damage came from a discrete external event — you can point to the storm, the impact, the lightning strike — it's likely an insurance claim. If the panel simply stopped working with no external cause, or is producing less over time, or shows a defect like internal cracking without an impact, that's a warranty conversation first. The two can overlap in confusing ways; hail, for instance, can cause damage that isn't visible from the ground and only shows up as reduced production, which can look like a defect until an inspection reveals impact marks. When you're genuinely unsure whether a drop in output is storm damage or a component failing on its own, it's worth understanding the difference between fixing and replacing a panel and what drives that call, which is covered in [repair or replace when a solar panel fails](/blog/repair-or-replace-solar-panels/). Getting the category right up front — insurance versus warranty — saves you from filing a claim that shouldn't be filed and from missing one that should.

It's also worth confirming, before anything is damaged if possible, that your policy actually covers the array the way you assume it does. Most standard homeowners policies include roof-mounted solar as part of the dwelling coverage, but the details matter: some policies have specific language about solar, ground-mounted systems are sometimes treated differently than roof-mounted ones, and leased or third-party-owned systems may be the leasing company's responsibility rather than yours to insure. The broader question of how homeowners insurance treats solar — what's typically covered, what to check, and where the gaps tend to be — is laid out in [does homeowners insurance cover solar panels](/blog/homeowners-insurance-solar-panels/), and reading it before you ever need to file means you're not learning your coverage terms in the middle of a crisis. The moment right after a storm is a bad time to discover an exclusion you could have caught earlier, so a five-minute policy check on a calm day is cheap insurance against a nasty surprise.

## Documenting the damage and working the claim

When damage does happen and you've decided it's an insurance matter, the process rewards moving deliberately in a specific order, and the first priority is safety and prevention rather than paperwork. If a panel is dangling, wiring is exposed, or the roof itself is compromised, keep people away from the area and be aware that a damaged solar array can still carry live electricity — the panels produce voltage whenever light hits them, so don't touch damaged components or standing water near them, and if there's any electrical hazard, get a professional involved before anyone goes near it. If you can safely prevent further damage — putting a tarp over an open roof penetration, for example — do so, because insurers expect you to take reasonable steps to mitigate additional loss, and failing to protect the property after the initial event can reduce what they'll pay. But don't make permanent repairs yet, because the adjuster needs to see the damage first.

Documentation is the heart of a solar claim, and thorough documentation is what separates a smooth payout from a drawn-out fight. Photograph and video everything, from multiple angles and distances — the damaged panels close up, the array in context, the surrounding roof, any debris or the tree limb that caused it, and the interior if there's water intrusion. Capture the date. If you have a solar monitoring app, screenshot the production data showing the drop-off, because a documented before-and-after in generation is powerful evidence that the system was working and then wasn't, and it timestamps the loss to the event. Dig out your original installation paperwork, the equipment specifications, and any records of the system's value, because the insurer will want to know what was installed and what it's worth. The more complete your record, the less room there is for the adjuster to lowball or dispute the extent of the damage. Storms and hail are among the most common causes of solar damage claims, and understanding just how tough panels are — and what kinds of impacts actually harm them versus bounce off — helps you describe the damage credibly, which is the subject of [how tough are solar panels, really](/blog/solar-panel-hail-storm-damage/).

With documentation in hand, contact your insurer to open the claim, and be ready to describe what happened, when, and the nature of the damage. The insurer will assign an adjuster who will inspect the property, and this is a step where solar creates a specific risk worth managing: not every adjuster is knowledgeable about solar equipment, and a general adjuster may undervalue the array or miss damage that a specialist would catch. It's entirely reasonable, and often wise, to have your solar installer or a qualified solar technician present for the inspection or to provide their own damage assessment and repair estimate, so the adjuster is working from an expert evaluation rather than a guess. That professional estimate — detailing which components are damaged, what it costs to repair or replace them, and the labor to do so — becomes the basis for the claim, and having it in hand strengthens your position. To sanity-check whether an estimate for replacing damaged panels or a section of the array is in a reasonable range, grounding yourself with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) gives you a sense of what the equipment and installation should run, so you can tell whether a settlement offer is fair or thin. Keep a written record of every interaction with the insurer — dates, names, what was said — because a paper trail protects you if the claim gets complicated.

## Deductibles, repair-versus-replace, and the aftermath

Before you get too far into a claim, run the numbers on your deductible, because for smaller damage the deductible can eat most or all of the payout and change whether filing even makes sense. Homeowners policies carry a deductible you pay before coverage kicks in, and here's a detail that catches solar owners off guard: many policies, especially in storm-prone regions, apply a separate and often higher deductible for wind and hail damage, frequently calculated as a percentage of your home's insured value rather than a flat dollar amount — commonly in the 1-to-2% range, which on a well-insured home can be several thousand dollars. If a hailstorm cracked one panel worth a few hundred dollars to replace, a percentage-based wind/hail deductible can exceed the entire cost of the repair, meaning you'd pay out of pocket anyway and gain nothing from filing except a claim on your record. So check which deductible applies and do the arithmetic before you file; claims that fall below or near the deductible are usually better handled without involving the insurer at all.

Assuming the damage clears the deductible comfortably, the next decision is repair versus replacement, and with solar this is more nuanced than it looks. A single cracked panel in an older array can be surprisingly awkward to replace, because the exact model may no longer be manufactured, and mismatched panels can create electrical and warranty complications — sometimes the practical fix is different from a straightforward like-for-like swap. Your installer's assessment matters here, and so does the insurer's settlement basis: policies pay either replacement cost (what it takes to replace with new equipment) or actual cash value (replacement cost minus depreciation for the age of the system), and which one you have makes a large difference for an older array. Knowing whether your policy is a replacement-cost or actual-cash-value policy tells you roughly what to expect from the settlement, and it's one more thing worth confirming in your policy language before a loss. The considerations that drive the repair-or-replace call on a damaged panel — availability, matching, warranty, and cost — are worth understanding in [repair or replace when a solar panel fails](/blog/repair-or-replace-solar-panels/) so you can weigh in on the decision rather than deferring entirely to the adjuster.

Once the claim is settled and the work is done, a few closing steps protect you going forward. Keep the full claim file — photos, estimates, correspondence, and the final settlement — with your home records, because it documents the system's history for a future sale or a future claim. Have the installer verify the repaired system is producing correctly and is safely reconnected, and watch your monitoring data in the weeks after to confirm output returned to where it should be, since a repair that looks fine but underperforms suggests something was missed. And take the episode as a prompt to review your coverage: if the claim revealed that your array was underinsured, that a coverage gap left you exposed, or that a leased system's damage wasn't your policy's responsibility, now is the time to fix that with your agent rather than after the next storm. The forum-fueled anxiety that solar makes homes uninsurable or claims impossible is mostly overblown — panels are ordinary insured property, and claims for genuine storm damage are routine — but the reality behind the worry, and where the real coverage nuances live, is separated from the panic in [solar and home insurance](/blog/solar-panels-and-home-insurance/). A homeowner who knows their policy, documents thoroughly, brings expert estimates to the table, and does the deductible math before filing turns a storm-damaged array from a stressful ordeal into a manageable claim.
