An electric water heater is one of the quiet giants of a home's electricity bill — a load that runs in the background, day and night, heating and reheating a tank of water most people never think about until it fails. That steadiness is exactly what makes it an interesting candidate for solar to offset. Unlike an air conditioner that only runs in summer or an EV that only charges when you're home, a water heater draws power year-round on a predictable rhythm, and predictable, substantial loads are precisely what a well-sized solar array is good at covering. The question worth answering isn't whether solar can offset a water heater — it can — but how much of that load it realistically covers and what the resulting savings are actually worth once you put a number on them.

The answer swings hugely on one detail: whether you have a conventional resistance-element tank or a modern heat-pump water heater. Those two appliances do the same job with wildly different efficiency, and that gap changes both how much electricity there is to offset and how many panels it takes. Getting the savings math right means starting with how much your specific water heater draws, then figuring out how well solar production lines up with when it draws, and only then translating that into dollars. Skip the first step and you'll either overestimate the savings or oversize the array chasing a load that's smaller than you assumed.

## How much electricity a water heater actually draws

A conventional electric water heater works by brute force: it passes current through resistance heating elements submerged in the tank, converting electricity directly into heat. It's simple, cheap to buy, and reliable, but it's also thermodynamically unremarkable — one unit of electricity in, roughly one unit of heat out. For a typical household, that adds up to a lot of kilowatt-hours over a year. A resistance tank serving a family commonly lands somewhere in the neighborhood of 3,500 to 5,000 kilowatt-hours annually, depending on household size, hot-water habits, incoming water temperature, and the tank's insulation. Call it roughly 4,000 kilowatt-hours a year as a working figure, and you're looking at one of the two or three largest single loads in an all-electric home — often rivaling or exceeding the refrigerator, the dryer, and much of the lighting combined. It's a big, steady target, which is what makes it worth a serious look for solar offset. Where water heating actually falls in the pecking order of household loads is worth seeing in context, and [the appliances quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) puts the tank next to everything else competing for your kilowatt-hours.

A heat-pump water heater changes the arithmetic completely, and it's the single most important variable in this whole discussion. Instead of generating heat directly, it works like a refrigerator running in reverse — it moves heat out of the surrounding air and into the water, spending a small amount of electricity to relocate a larger amount of heat. That efficiency advantage is dramatic: a heat-pump water heater typically delivers roughly two to four times as much heat per unit of electricity as a resistance element, which means it does the same job on a fraction of the power. The same household that burned around 4,000 kilowatt-hours a year on a resistance tank might use only a quarter to a third of that on a heat-pump model — very roughly 1,000 to 1,500 kilowatt-hours annually. That's a much smaller load to offset, which cuts both ways for the solar conversation: fewer panels are needed to cover it, but the dollar savings from offsetting it are correspondingly smaller because there was less electricity being spent in the first place. The heat pump has already captured most of the savings through sheer efficiency before solar even enters the picture.

Knowing which appliance you have, and roughly what it draws, is the foundation everything else rests on, so it's worth pinning down rather than guessing. If you don't know your water heater's annual consumption, you can estimate it from the tank's element wattage and your household's hot-water use, or better, isolate it from your bills by comparing months. The mechanics of how a tank's electricity use builds up — and why household size and habits move it so much — are covered in [what a water heater really costs to run](/blog/water-heater-electricity-usage/), which is the right place to get a grounded number for your own home before you try to size solar around it. The reason this precision matters is that the water heater's share of your total bill determines how much offsetting it can possibly save you. If you're spending, say, 4,000 kilowatt-hours a year on a resistance tank at a typical residential rate, that's a meaningful annual sum you could target with solar. If you've already switched to a heat pump and you're spending a quarter of that, the water-heater-specific savings from solar are real but modest, and the bigger financial move was the heat pump itself. Either way, the honest first step is to know the number, not to assume it.

## Matching that load to solar production and timing

Once you know how much your water heater draws, the next question is how well solar can actually cover it — and the answer depends less on total kilowatt-hours than on timing, because a water heater and a solar array don't naturally run on the same schedule. A solar array produces on the sun's clock, peaking around midday and generating nothing at night. A water heater, left to its own thermostat, tends to fire whenever the tank temperature drops — often heavily in the morning when everyone showers and again in the evening after dinner dishes and baths, with the tank quietly reheating overnight to make up for standby losses. Those peak-demand moments fall largely outside the array's productive hours, which means a naive pairing wastes some of the opportunity: the panels are making surplus power at noon while the water heater is drawing hardest at 7 a.m. and 8 p.m.

This timing mismatch is where a little cleverness turns a decent offset into a very good one. The most straightforward lever is to shift the water heater's heavy reheating into the middle of the day, when the panels are flush. A water heater on a timer or a smart controller can be told to do its main heating during peak solar hours — running the elements or the heat pump at midday to bring the tank up to temperature, so the well-insulated tank then coasts on stored hot water through the evening demand. In effect, the tank becomes a crude thermal battery: you heat water with your own solar production while the sun is up, and you draw that hot water down after dark without needing the grid. This matters because of how solar billing works. A kilowatt-hour your water heater pulls straight from the panels at noon offsets electricity at the full retail rate you'd otherwise pay. That same solar kilowatt-hour, exported to the grid because the water heater wasn't running to use it, often earns only a lower export credit. Timing the heating to coincide with production upgrades the value of that energy from the export rate to the retail rate, and it costs nothing but a timer setting.

Heat-pump water heaters deserve a special note here because they pair with solar almost ideally. Their draw is low and can be spread over a longer heating period, which fits comfortably within a solar array's midday production window — a heat pump sipping power for a couple of hours around noon is easy for even a modest array to cover entirely from production. There's a small climate caveat worth naming: a heat-pump water heater pulls heat from the air around it, so it slightly cools and dehumidifies the space it sits in. In a warm garage or a humid basement in summer, that's a bonus. In a conditioned space in winter, it's mildly working against your heating. None of that changes the core solar story, but it's the kind of real-world detail that separates an honest account from a sales pitch. The broader point is that water heating is one of the most solar-friendly loads a home has, precisely because it's large, steady, and — with a timer — shiftable into the sunny hours. That combination is unusual; most big household loads are either seasonal or tied to a schedule you can't move. The water heater you can move, and moving it is what converts solar production you'd otherwise export cheaply into retail-rate savings on a load you were always going to have.

## Putting a dollar figure on the offset

Turning all of this into an actual savings number requires a worked example with its assumptions on the table, because a vague "solar saves on your water heater" is useless for a real decision. Assume a resistance electric water heater drawing 4,000 kilowatt-hours a year, a retail electricity rate of $0.16 per kilowatt-hour, and a solar array with enough capacity and the right timing to offset the great majority of that load — say 90% of it — through a mix of direct daytime heating and net metering credits carrying the rest. That 90% offset covers 3,600 kilowatt-hours a year, and at $0.16 that's about $576 in annual savings attributable to the water heater alone. Over the multi-decade life of the panels, with electricity rates historically trending upward, the cumulative figure runs well into five figures for that single appliance. Change the rate and the offset percentage and the number moves, but the shape holds: a resistance tank is a large enough load that offsetting it with solar is genuinely worth real money each year.

Run the same exercise for a heat-pump water heater and the number shrinks proportionally, exactly as you'd expect. Assume the heat pump draws 1,200 kilowatt-hours a year instead of 4,000, the same $0.16 rate, and the same 90% solar offset. That's 1,080 kilowatt-hours covered, or about $173 a year in water-heater-specific savings. The offset is still real and still worth capturing, but it's a smaller prize because the heat pump already did the heavy lifting on efficiency before solar arrived. This is the honest tension in the whole topic: the appliance that gives solar the most to offset is the inefficient one, and the efficient appliance leaves solar less to work with. The best overall outcome for a household is usually the combination — an efficient heat-pump water heater to slash the raw consumption, plus solar to offset most of what remains — but you shouldn't expect the solar-on-a-heat-pump savings to rival the solar-on-a-resistance-tank savings, because there simply isn't as much electricity there to save.

The practical way to size and value this for your own home is to isolate the water heater's contribution rather than lose it inside your whole bill. Sizing solar around a single appliance in isolation is the wrong frame — you size an array for your whole household load, and the water heater is one substantial slice of it — but understanding that slice tells you how much of the array's job is water heating and what that portion is worth. The water heater is rarely the reason to go solar on its own, but it's often one of the biggest, steadiest, and most easily solar-covered loads in the house — which makes it a satisfying line item to watch drop once the panels are running.
