The refrigerator is the appliance people ask about most, and it is easy to see why. It runs around the clock, it never gets switched off, and that constant hum feels like it should demand a serious chunk of solar. It does not. A modern fridge is one of the most efficient always-on loads in the entire house, and the honest answer to how many solar panels it takes to run one usually lands at a single panel, occasionally two. Getting to that answer cleanly, rather than guessing, is worth doing carefully, because the exact same steps size solar for any appliance you care to point them at. Once you can do the fridge, you can do the well pump, the chest freezer, or the whole house.

## Start with the fridge's daily appetite, measured in kilowatt-hours

Everything begins with kilowatt-hours per day, not watts, and this is the step where most back-of-the-envelope estimates go wrong. A refrigerator's compressor does not run continuously; it cycles on and off to hold temperature, kicking in when the interior warms and shutting down once it hits the setpoint. That means the nameplate wattage stamped on the unit tells you almost nothing about daily consumption, because the compressor is only actually drawing that wattage for a fraction of each hour. A fridge with a 150-watt compressor that runs a third of the time is really averaging something like 50 watts across the day, and that averaged draw, multiplied out over 24 hours, is what matters. What you are after is the total energy the fridge pulls over a full day, and that figure varies far more by age and type than most people expect.

The numbers below are representative daily-consumption assumptions, not measurements of any specific unit. Your own fridge's draw depends on its age, its size, the temperature of the room it sits in, and how often the door gets opened. A garage fridge baking in summer heat works harder than the same model in a climate-controlled kitchen, and a unit with an automatic defrost cycle or an ice maker carries a small extra load those features add several times a day.

| Refrigerator type | Assumed daily use | Assumed annual use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Modern efficient full-size | 1.0–1.5 kWh/day | ~365–550 kWh/yr |
| Older full-size (10+ years) | 2.0–3.0 kWh/day | ~730–1,100 kWh/yr |
| Compact / mini fridge | 0.4–0.8 kWh/day | ~150–290 kWh/yr |
| Large or second garage fridge | 2.0–4.0 kWh/day | ~730–1,460 kWh/yr |

The spread across that table is the entire point. A modern efficient refrigerator and a fifteen-year-old garage unit can differ by a factor of two or three, which means the single most effective thing you can do for the solar math is know which one you actually own. If your fridge predates the last decade of efficiency improvements, replacing it can cut the load in half before you touch the solar side of the equation at all, and that swap often pays for itself faster than adding panels to feed the old unit's appetite. The cheapest kilowatt-hour is the one you never have to generate. The most reliable way to pin down your own number is not to trust the yellow energy label, which reports a test-lab estimate, but to put a plug-in energy meter between the fridge and the outlet and read the accumulated kilowatt-hours after a few days of normal use, ideally spanning a warm day and a cool one so you capture the range. That single measurement replaces all the guessing and gives you a figure you can size against with confidence. If you are not sure how the fridge stacks up against everything else pulling power in your home, [the biggest electricity users in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/) puts it in context, and you may find the refrigerator is a smaller villain than the water heater or the air conditioner, which changes where you should focus if lowering the whole bill is the goal.

One more source of confusion is worth heading off: households often have more than one cold appliance, and each is its own load. A kitchen fridge, a garage beer fridge, and a chest freezer in the basement are three separate draws that add up, and the garage or basement units frequently consume more than the kitchen one despite holding less, because they are older hand-me-downs sitting in unconditioned space where summer heat forces the compressor to run far longer. A chest freezer, for its part, tends to be quite efficient per cubic foot because cold air spills down rather than out when you open the lid, so it may draw less than you would guess. If you are sizing solar to cover cold storage, add up every unit you actually run rather than picturing only the main refrigerator, because the second and third appliances are exactly the ones that quietly double the number you were about to plan around.

## Turning kilowatt-hours into panels

Once you have a daily kWh figure for the fridge, the panel count comes down to how much a single panel produces where you live. A modern residential panel of roughly 400 watts does not deliver 400 watts all day; it delivers something closer to its wattage multiplied by your local peak sun hours and then trimmed by a real-world efficiency factor that accounts for inverter losses, wiring, heat, and dust. That derate factor is why nameplate wattage always overstates what actually reaches your loads, and ignoring it is the second most common way these estimates go wrong after confusing watts with kilowatt-hours.

Here is the worked version. Assume a 400-watt panel, 4.5 average daily peak sun hours, and a 0.80 factor for combined system losses. That panel yields about 400 × 4.5 × 0.80 = 1,440 watt-hours, or roughly 1.4 kWh, on an average day across the year. A modern fridge drawing 1.2 kWh per day is comfortably covered by that single panel with a little headroom to spare. An older unit at 2.5 kWh per day needs closer to two panels to keep pace. That is the whole reason the answer to running a refrigerator on solar sits at 1–2 panels for the overwhelming majority of households: one for an efficient modern fridge in decent sun, two for an older or oversized one, or for anyone in a cloudier region where each panel returns fewer daily kilowatt-hours. Sun hours are the input that swings this most from place to place. The sunny Southwest might see a panel deliver closer to 1.8 kWh on an average day, comfortably covering even a thirsty fridge on its own, while the cloudy Pacific Northwest or the upper Midwest in winter might see the same panel manage well under a kilowatt-hour, which is exactly when the second panel earns its place. The per-panel side of this deserves its own careful look, and [what one solar panel produces in a day](/blog/solar-panel-output-per-day/) works through how sun hours and losses move that 1.4 kWh figure up or down for real locations rather than a national average.

The number that scares people, then, is smaller than the intuition, but there is a catch in how you should think about it. On a grid-connected home you would never actually buy one or two panels in isolation and wire them to the refrigerator, because "running the fridge on solar" there does not mean powering the compressor from a specific panel at each instant. It means offsetting the fridge's annual energy use with panel production across the year. Your array feeds the house and the grid during the day, the fridge draws from the grid at 2 a.m. when the panels are dark, and net metering or your utility's export credit reconciles the two flows on your bill. That framing quietly removes the storage problem: a grid-tied home needs no battery to keep the fridge cold overnight, because the grid itself is doing the job the battery would do, and one or two panels' worth of annual production simply cancels the fridge off your statement. In practice those panels are never a standalone purchase; they fold into whole-home system sizing, where the fridge is just one line among many loads the array is built to cover. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) lets you plug in your own sun hours and appliance load instead of leaning on the averages here, which is the right way to turn the fridge's real measured draw into a panel count for your specific roof and climate.

This is also why framing the question as "panels to run the fridge" can quietly mislead a grid-tied homeowner. You do not shop for a one- or two-panel system and bolt it to the refrigerator; you size a whole-home array to your total annual usage, and the fridge is simply one line inside that total, worth roughly the 1–2 panels the math assigns it. Its value in the calculation is that it teaches you the method: measure a load in kilowatt-hours per day, divide by what a panel yields in your climate, and you have the panel count for anything, from a well pump to an electric vehicle charger. The fridge is the friendly first example precisely because its answer is small and stable, running the same modest load every day of the year regardless of season, which makes it the easiest place to build intuition before you point the same arithmetic at the loads that actually dominate your bill.

## Off-grid changes the whole calculation

Take the grid away and the refrigerator transforms from an easy load into one of the more demanding ones, precisely because of the trait that made it seem intimidating in the first place: it runs all night, when the panels produce nothing. On a grid-tied home that overnight draw is invisible, absorbed by the utility connection. Off-grid, every watt-hour the compressor pulls between sunset and sunrise has to come out of a battery that the sun refilled during the day. So off-grid fridge sizing is really two problems stacked together, and the panel count is only half of it. You are sizing a battery big enough to carry the fridge through the dark hours and any cloudy stretches, and then sizing enough panel to refill that battery every day through the losses of charging and discharging.

Two rules of thumb do most of the work. First, size the battery to carry the fridge's full daily draw plus a healthy margin for gray weather, because the compressor keeps cycling whether or not the sun is out, and a battery that only covers a sunny day's overnight use will leave you with a warm fridge after two cloudy days in a row. A fridge pulling 1.5 kWh per day, planned for two days of autonomy, implies roughly 3 kWh of energy that has to be available, and because most batteries should not be drained to empty, the nameplate capacity you buy has to be larger still, often meaningfully so depending on the chemistry's usable depth of discharge. Second, size the panels to recharge that battery within the available sun hours, which almost always means more panel than the grid-tied answer, because you are no longer just offsetting a meter but physically refilling storage. Round-trip losses in the battery mean you have to generate a fair bit more than the fridge consumes just to break even on energy, and you have to do it in the limited window when the sun is strong, not spread across the whole day. There is also a hardware wrinkle grid-tied setups never hit: the compressor's startup surge. An induction compressor motor can momentarily draw several times its running wattage as it starts, and the off-grid inverter has to be rated to deliver that surge or the fridge will trip it, which is why a modestly sized inverter that looks adequate on average power can still fail to start the appliance. Some off-grid builders sidestep this entirely with a DC compressor fridge that runs straight off the battery bank without an inverter at all, trading a higher appliance cost for lower losses and no surge headache.

There is a related scenario that catches grid-tied owners off guard, and it is worth naming because it is the moment the fridge question suddenly matters to them too. A standard grid-tied array without a battery does not keep the refrigerator running during a blackout; it shuts down the instant the grid drops, for safety reasons, so the panels on your roof provide nothing while the neighborhood is dark. Keeping the fridge cold through an outage therefore requires a battery, and sizing that battery is the same overnight-autonomy problem the off-grid case describes, just triggered by a storm rather than by living off the grid full time. Households that specifically want the fridge to survive outages sometimes choose a small dedicated setup for exactly that load, or a 12-volt DC compressor fridge that sips directly from a modest battery, which is a common trick in RVs and cabins because it dodges both the inverter and its startup surge entirely while running on a fraction of the energy an AC unit demands.

The practical upshot is that an off-grid fridge a single panel would "offset" on a grid-tied home can need two or three panels plus a dedicated battery to run reliably through a run of bad weather. That is not a contradiction of the earlier 1–2 panel figure; it is a different question. Grid-tied asks how much annual energy the fridge uses, an averages problem the grid smooths out for you. Off-grid asks how to guarantee power at 3 a.m. on the third overcast day, which is a reliability problem, and reliability always costs extra hardware because you are engineering for the worst realistic stretch rather than the typical one. This is exactly why off-grid loads deserve their own full accounting rather than a rule of thumb, and [sizing an off-grid system from your load list](/blog/off-grid-solar-system-sizing/) walks through the process appliance by appliance, adding up the daily watt-hours and building the battery and array to match. The refrigerator is usually the anchor load in that list, the thing that has to keep running no matter what, and sizing the whole system around keeping it cold through the worst weather you realistically expect is what separates an off-grid setup that works from one that leaves you rescuing groceries into a cooler.
