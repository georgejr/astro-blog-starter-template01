Somewhere in the paperwork for a new roof is a line that says the warranty is void if the roofing is altered or penetrated by another party. A solar array bolts to the roof through dozens of lag screws driven into the rafters, which certainly counts as penetrating and altering. So the fear is reasonable: does mounting solar quietly tear up the roof warranty you paid for?

The honest answer is that it depends on which warranty you mean, who does the work, and whether the paperwork gets handled. "Roof warranty" is really two separate promises stacked on the same roof, and solar touches each of them differently. Sorting them out is the difference between a genuine risk and a rumor that scares people off a sound installation.

## Two warranties, two different exposures

The material warranty comes from the shingle or membrane manufacturer and covers defects in the product itself — shingles that fail prematurely, granules that shed too early, a membrane that delaminates. Drilling mounting holes generally does not void this coverage, because the manufacturer is guaranteeing the material, not the installation around it. What it will not do is cover a leak caused by someone else's penetration. If water gets in at a poorly flashed solar foot, that's not a shingle defect, and the material warranty was never going to pay for it.

The workmanship warranty is the one that actually gets exposed. That's the roofer's own promise that the roof was installed watertight, and it typically carries a clause voiding coverage if anyone else penetrates or modifies the roof. When a solar crew drills through a roof the original roofer warrantied, that roofer can reasonably disclaim responsibility for any leak near the array — they can't stand behind waterproofing that a third party cut into. This is where a lot of homeowner grief originates, and it's less about solar being dangerous than about which contractor owns the leak afterward. The broader question of whether panels harm the roof at all is covered in [will solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/); the warranty issue is narrower and more about paperwork than physics.

## How a penetration is supposed to be waterproofed

A correctly mounted array doesn't rely on caulk to keep water out, and understanding the proper method tells you what to inspect. On a standard asphalt-shingle roof, each mounting point is a lag bolt driven into a rafter, and the waterproofing comes from flashing, not sealant.

The sequence a competent crew follows looks like this:

- Locate the rafter and drill a pilot hole so the lag bites structural wood, not just decking.
- Seal the hole and set the mount, usually with a compatible sealant under the base plate.
- Slide a metal flashing up under the course of shingles above the penetration, so the shingle overlaps the top edge of the flashing.
- Let gravity do the work: water running down the shingles flows over the top of the flashing and back onto the surface, never reaching the bolt.

Done right, the flashing is the waterproofing and the sealant is a backup, not the main defense. Done wrong — a glob of caulk over an exposed hole with no flashing tucked under the upslope shingle — it will hold for a season or two and then leak once the sealant cracks in the sun. Newer mounting systems use flashing-integrated feet or, on some products, butyl-sealed bases designed to eliminate the tuck-under step, but the principle is the same: the water path has to be managed mechanically, not glued shut.

## Protecting your coverage before the crew arrives

The mistake that voids coverage isn't drilling holes; it's drilling them without handling the warranty chain. A few steps before signing keep you protected.

Ask the solar company for their workmanship warranty in writing, and read what it covers and for how long. A serious installer warranties their roof penetrations — commonly for a decade or more — and that warranty is what replaces the roofer's disclaimed coverage over the array footprint. The distinction between that installer promise and the equipment maker's promise is worth understanding on its own, and [workmanship versus equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) breaks down who is on the hook for what.

Then handle the roofer side:

- Call the original roofer (if the roof is newer) and ask how they treat solar. Some have a preferred installer or an approved-penetration process that preserves their workmanship coverage.
- Confirm whether your installer offers a longer roof-penetration warranty on the array area, since their coverage effectively takes over where the roofer's steps back.
- Get every warranty term in writing and keep the mounting-hardware documentation; manufacturer material warranties usually survive regardless, but you want the paper trail if a leak ever appears.

If the roof is already near the end of its life, the cleaner move is to deal with it before the panels go up, so you're not disturbing a fresh warranty at all — the trade-offs of that timing are laid out in [replacing your roof before going solar](/blog/replace-roof-before-solar/). ## FAQ

### Does drilling holes automatically void my roof warranty?

Not the manufacturer's material warranty in most cases — that covers product defects, not third-party penetrations. It's the roofer's workmanship warranty that typically carries a clause disclaiming responsibility once another party penetrates the roof. The fix is to have the solar installer warranty their own penetrations.

### Who fixes a leak that appears near the panels?

Usually the solar installer, under their workmanship warranty, since the leak originates at a penetration they made. That's exactly why getting their roof-penetration coverage in writing matters — it's the coverage that fills the gap the original roofer steps out of.

### Are flashed mounts really leak-proof?

No roof penetration is guaranteed forever, but properly flashed mounts that route water over the top of the flashing and back onto the shingles have a long, reliable track record. Leaks are far more often a sign of skipped flashing or caulk-only shortcuts than of the mounting method itself.
