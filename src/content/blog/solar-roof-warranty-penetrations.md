Somewhere in the paperwork for a new roof is a line that says the warranty is void if the roofing is altered or penetrated by another party. A solar array bolts to the roof through dozens of lag screws driven into the rafters, which very obviously counts as penetrating and altering. So the fear that stops a lot of people cold is entirely reasonable: does mounting solar quietly tear up the roof warranty you just paid a small fortune for?

The honest answer is that it depends on which warranty you mean, who does the work, and whether the paperwork gets handled before the crew shows up. "Roof warranty" sounds like one thing, but it's really two separate promises stacked on the same roof, and solar touches each of them differently. People who get burned almost always got burned in the gap between the two — not because drilling holes is inherently dangerous, but because nobody sorted out which contractor owns a leak after the fact. Untangling those two promises is the difference between a genuine risk you can manage and a rumor that scares homeowners off a perfectly sound installation.

## Two warranties, and only one is really exposed

The material warranty comes from the shingle or membrane manufacturer, and it covers defects in the product itself: shingles that fail prematurely, granules that shed too early, a membrane that delaminates before its time. Drilling mounting holes generally does not void this coverage, because the manufacturer is guaranteeing the material as manufactured, not the installation work happening around it years later. What that warranty was never going to do is pay for a leak caused by someone else's penetration. If water finds its way in at a poorly flashed solar foot, that's not a shingle defect — the shingle is doing exactly what it was built to do — so the material warranty sits it out. This is the warranty people worry about most and, in most cases, the one least affected by going solar.

The workmanship warranty is the one that actually gets exposed, and it's worth understanding why. That's the roofer's own promise that they installed the roof watertight, and it typically carries a clause voiding coverage if any other party penetrates or modifies the roof afterward. When a solar crew drills through a roof the original roofer warrantied, that roofer can reasonably disclaim responsibility for any leak near the array. They can hardly stand behind the waterproofing of a roof that a third party cut dozens of new holes into, using hardware and methods the roofer never saw. This is where most homeowner grief actually originates. It's rarely about solar being a hazard to the roof; it's about who is on the hook if water shows up two winters later, three feet from a mounting foot. If the original roofer has stepped back and the solar installer never stepped forward, you're the one holding the leak.

It helps to recognize that the disclaiming clause is standard boilerplate, not a sign anyone expects trouble. Roofers write it because they can't reasonably be asked to guarantee a roof that other trades will later cut into, the same way an automaker won't warranty an engine that a third-party shop has opened up. Solar installers, for their part, deal with this constantly and the good ones have a clean answer ready: they warranty their own penetrations and hand you the paperwork without being asked. So the clause looks alarming in isolation, but in a well-run installation it simply hands responsibility for the array footprint from one contractor to another, cleanly and on purpose. The failures happen in the gap — when a low-bid installer never offers a penetration warranty, or a homeowner never asks for one, and the roofer's disclaimer lands on nobody. That gap is entirely avoidable, and avoiding it costs nothing but a few pointed questions before signing. The homeowners who get burned are almost never the ones who understood the two-warranty structure going in; they're the ones who assumed "roof warranty" was a single thing and never noticed which half of it solar actually touched.

The two-warranty split looks slightly different on roof types other than asphalt shingle, but the logic holds. A membrane or metal roof still carries a manufacturer's material warranty on the product and, usually, a separate installer or manufacturer workmanship warranty on the watertight installation, and it's again the workmanship side that a third-party penetration threatens rather than the material side. Some commercial-style membrane manufacturers are particularly strict, requiring that only their certified applicators touch the membrane or the warranty is forfeited outright — which makes coordinating the solar penetrations with an approved roofer especially important on those roofs, and a genuine trap for a homeowner who lets a general solar crew cut into a specialized membrane. The takeaway is the same on every roof: find out, before the crew arrives, which of your two warranties the mounting work touches and who will stand behind the roof afterward, because the answer varies by roof type and by manufacturer far more than most homeowners expect.

That framing answers the question people ask first — does drilling holes automatically void my warranty? For the manufacturer's material coverage, usually not, because it protects against product defects, not third-party penetrations. For the roofer's workmanship coverage, effectively yes over the area the array touches, because the disclaiming clause was written precisely for this situation. But "void" is the wrong word to end on, because the exposure is filled by a different promise: the solar installer's own workmanship warranty on the penetrations they make. The whole game is making sure that replacement coverage exists and is written down before anyone climbs onto your roof. It's also worth keeping the warranty question separate from the physical one. Whether panels damage a roof at all — sagging, accelerated wear, trapped moisture — is a distinct issue with its own evidence, laid out in [will solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/); the warranty problem is narrower, and it's about paperwork and liability far more than about physics.

## How a penetration is actually kept watertight

A correctly mounted array doesn't rely on a bead of caulk to keep water out, and understanding the proper method tells you exactly what to inspect and what to be suspicious of. On a standard asphalt-shingle roof, each mounting point is a lag bolt driven into a rafter, and the waterproofing comes from flashing — a piece of metal that redirects water — not from sealant. That distinction is the single most useful thing a homeowner can know about solar penetrations, because it separates a durable install from one that will betray you in a few years.

The sequence a competent crew follows is straightforward once you see the logic behind it. They locate the rafter and drill a pilot hole so the lag bites into structural wood rather than just the thin roof decking, which is what actually holds the array down in a windstorm. They seal the hole and set the mount, usually with a compatible sealant under the base plate as a first line of defense. Then comes the part that matters most: they slide a metal flashing up under the course of shingles above the penetration, so the shingle overlaps the top edge of the flashing. Now gravity does the real work. Water running down the shingles flows over the top of the flashing and back onto the roof surface, and it never reaches the bolt hole underneath. Done this way, the flashing is the waterproofing and the sealant is merely a backup, a belt to go with the suspenders.

Done wrong, the same penetration is a slow-motion leak. A glob of caulk smeared over an exposed hole, with no flashing tucked under the upslope shingle, will hold for a season or two and then fail once the sealant cracks and shrinks under years of UV and thermal cycling. The tell is that the waterproofing depends on a material that is guaranteed to degrade, rather than on a water path managed by the geometry of overlapping metal and shingle. That's why "are flashed mounts really leak-proof" has a nuanced answer: no roof penetration is guaranteed forever, but a properly flashed mount that routes water over the top of the flashing and back onto the shingles has a long, boring, reliable track record. When leaks do appear near an array, they're far more often a symptom of skipped flashing or a caulk-only shortcut than of the mounting method itself. Newer mounting systems make the shortcut harder to take — flashing-integrated feet, or products with butyl-sealed bases engineered to eliminate the tuck-under step entirely — but the underlying principle never changes. The water path has to be managed mechanically, not glued shut and hoped over.

None of this requires you to climb onto the roof yourself, but a little of it is inspectable from the ground with binoculars or from a ladder at the eave, and knowing what good looks like is worth the few minutes. Flashing tucked properly under the upslope shingles is nearly invisible from below, which is the point — you shouldn't see raw metal collars sitting on top of the shingles with a ring of caulk around each one. If you can spot exposed holes, surface-mounted feet with visible sealant doing all the work, or flashing sitting over the shingles rather than under them, that's a crew that skipped the step that matters. The roof type changes the specifics without changing the logic: tile roofs use special tile-replacement flashings or hooks that route water beneath the tiles, metal roofs often clamp to the standing seams with no penetration at all, and flat membrane roofs get penetrations heat-welded or flashed into the membrane by someone who knows that system. In every case the question to ask your installer is the same and it's a fair one to put to them directly: how, specifically, is each mounting point on my roof type kept watertight, and is it flashing or is it sealant doing the work? An installer who answers confidently and specifically is telling you something reassuring; one who waves at "we caulk everything really well" is telling you something too.

## Protecting your coverage before the crew arrives

The mistake that actually strands a homeowner isn't drilling holes; it's letting the holes get drilled without securing the warranty chain first. A handful of steps before you sign keep you covered, and they cost nothing but a few phone calls and some insistence on getting things in writing.

Start with the installer, because their promise is what fills the gap the roofer leaves. Ask the solar company for their workmanship warranty in writing, and read what it actually covers and for how long. A serious installer warranties their own roof penetrations — commonly for ten years or more — and that warranty is precisely what replaces the roofer's disclaimed coverage over the footprint of the array. It's worth understanding how that installer promise differs from the equipment maker's promise on the panels and inverter, because they cover completely different failures; [workmanship versus equipment warranties](/blog/solar-workmanship-vs-equipment-warranty/) breaks down who is on the hook for what, and the roof-penetration coverage lives firmly on the workmanship side. This also answers the practical question of who fixes a leak that appears near the panels: usually the solar installer, under exactly that workmanship warranty, because the leak originates at a penetration they made. Which is the whole reason getting their roof-penetration coverage in writing, with a clear duration, matters so much.

Then handle the roofer side of the chain. If the roof is newer and still under an active workmanship warranty, call the original roofer and ask how they treat solar. Some have a preferred installer or an approved-penetration process that preserves their coverage, and a few will even do the flashing work themselves and hand it off. Confirm whether your solar installer offers a longer roof-penetration warranty specifically on the array area, since their coverage effectively takes over where the roofer's steps back. And get every warranty term in writing while keeping the mounting-hardware documentation, because manufacturer material warranties usually survive regardless, but you want a clean paper trail if a leak ever appears and two contractors start pointing at each other. The homeowner who kept the documents and the written coverage is the one who wins that argument.

The documents worth filing together are few and easy to name: the roofer's original workmanship warranty and any statement of how they treat solar, the solar installer's workmanship warranty with its specific duration and its explicit coverage of roof penetrations, the mounting-hardware and flashing product information, and the panel and inverter equipment warranties. Keeping them in one folder — physical or digital — turns a future leak from a stressful scramble into a two-minute reference check. It's also worth photographing the completed mounts before the panels go on if you happen to be home on installation day, because a clear picture of properly tucked flashing is exactly the evidence that settles a dispute years later when memories have faded and the crew has moved on. This is unglamorous paperwork, and it's tempting to skip it in the excitement of finally going solar, but it's the difference between a covered repair and an argument you can't win.

One caveat about installer warranties is worth naming: a warranty is only as good as the company standing behind it. A ten-year penetration warranty from an installer who may not be in business in five years is thinner than it looks on paper, which is one more reason the choice of installer matters as much as the warranty language itself. Ask how long the company has actually operated, whether the workmanship coverage is backed by insurance or a third-party guarantor rather than just the company's own promise, and what happens to your coverage if they're acquired or quietly close their doors. None of this is cause for paralysis — plenty of solid installers stand behind their work for decades — but a roof-penetration promise is a long-term commitment, and you want it made by someone likely to still be around to honor it when a leak finally tests it.

Timing is the last lever, and it can sidestep the whole problem. If the roof is already near the end of its service life, the cleaner move is to deal with it before the panels go up, so you're not disturbing a fresh warranty at all and you're not bolting a twenty-five-year array onto shingles with five years left.
