The moment a solar contract lands in front of you, the sales relationship is over and the legal one begins. Everything that was said in the living room — the production promises, the "don't worry, that's covered," the payment that would supposedly vanish once the tax credit arrived — either appears in the document or effectively did not happen. A contract is the only version of the deal that a court, or the company's own service department three years from now, will honor.

That makes reading it slowly the highest-leverage hour in the whole project. The clauses that cause the most disputes are rarely hidden in fine print; they are in plain sight and simply skimmed past by a buyer who has already mentally moved in. A few sections reliably deserve a second, careful pass.

## Pinning down the price

Start where the money is defined, because ambiguity here is the most expensive kind. The contract should state a single, unambiguous total price for a specifically described system — panel model and count, inverter type, battery if included — not a range and not a monthly payment standing in for a price. If the deal is financed, the document should let you see the actual system price separately from the financing, so you can tell what the equipment costs versus what the loan adds.

## How the credit and payment schedule are framed

Watch for how the tax credit is characterized. A contract or its associated loan should never guarantee that you will receive a specific credit amount; eligibility for the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit depends on your own tax situation under current IRS rules, and that is a matter for your tax professional, not the installer's promise. Be especially wary of a financed structure whose monthly payment quietly assumes you will hand the credit back to the lender as a principal payment — if you cannot use the full credit, that assumed payment never arrives and your real cost climbs. The mechanics of separating true price from financing are covered in [reading a solar quote line by line](/blog/how-to-read-a-solar-quote/).

Also confirm what triggers each payment. A reasonable contract ties payments to milestones — deposit, installation, activation — rather than demanding the full amount before a panel is on the roof. Large upfront payments concentrate your risk if the company stumbles.

## Production guarantees and what they actually promise

Many contracts include a production guarantee: a promise that the system will generate at least some number of kilowatt-hours per year. That sounds reassuring, and it can be, but the value is entirely in the details of how it is measured and what happens if the system falls short.

Read for three things. What is the guaranteed number, and is it set conservatively enough that the system will clear it easily, or optimistically enough that a normal year might miss? How is actual production measured and reported — automatically, or only if you notice and file a claim? And what is the remedy if production falls short — a cash payment for the shortfall, or a vague promise to "address" it? A guarantee with a low bar and a weak remedy is closer to marketing than protection. The right questions to ask about this before signing are gathered in [questions to ask solar installers](/blog/questions-to-ask-solar-installers/).

If the system also carries an escalator — common in leases and power-purchase agreements, where the rate you pay rises a set percentage each year — model where that payment lands in year ten and fifteen, not just year one. An escalator that starts below your utility rate can climb past it over a long contract.

## Warranties and the gaps between them

Solar warranties are not one warranty; they are usually three, and disputes cluster in the seams between them. The panel manufacturer warrants the modules. The inverter manufacturer warrants the inverter. The installer warrants their workmanship — the labor, the roof penetrations, the wiring. A problem is only fully covered if all three hold, and if the company that has to coordinate them still exists.

Read the workmanship warranty closely, because it is the one most within the installer's control and most variable between companies. How many years does it run? Does it cover the labor to diagnose and fix a covered problem, or only the replacement part while you pay for the roof visit? Critically, does it cover roof leaks caused by the mounting penetrations, and for how long? A roof leak traced to a solar attachment years later is a classic dispute, and the contract is where responsibility for it is either assigned or left dangerously vague.

The durability of these warranties depends on the durability of the company behind them, which is why vetting the installer is inseparable from reading the contract. The full vetting process — track record, licensing, financial stability — is laid out in [choosing a solar installer](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/). A twenty-five-year workmanship warranty from a company unlikely to see year six is worth less than a ten-year warranty from a firm with deep local roots.

## Cancellation, timelines, and what happens if things go wrong

The last cluster of clauses governs the unhappy paths, and they matter precisely because you hope never to use them.

- **Cancellation rights.** Many jurisdictions grant a short right-to-cancel window after signing; the contract should state it plainly. Look also at what it costs to cancel later, after the window closes but before installation — some contracts impose steep fees.
- **Timeline and delays.** A good contract gives a realistic schedule and says what happens if permitting or interconnection drags. Understanding the normal sequence helps you judge whether a promised timeline is credible at all.
- **Change orders.** What happens if the site survey finds the roof needs work or the electrical panel must be upgraded? The contract should specify how added costs are handled and whether you can walk away if they exceed a threshold, rather than leaving you committed to an open-ended bill.
- **Dispute resolution.** Look for mandatory arbitration clauses and where any dispute must be filed. These do not necessarily disqualify a contract, but you should know they are there before you sign, not after.

## Turning a careful read into leverage

None of this requires a lawyer for most straightforward residential installs, though a legal review is reasonable for a large or unusual contract. What it requires is refusing to treat the signing as a formality. Read the price definition, the production guarantee, the three warranties and their seams, and the exit clauses, and ask for written clarification on anything vague — a reputable installer will provide it without friction.

Before you get to the contract at all, a defensible sense of what the system should cost keeps the price section honest.
