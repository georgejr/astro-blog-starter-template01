A pitched-roof solar array is bolted through the roof into the rafters. A flat-roof array often is not bolted to anything. Instead it sits on the roof, tilted up on frames, held in place by concrete blocks — its own weight and clever aerodynamics doing the job that lag bolts do elsewhere. The first time you see a ballasted system, the obvious question is the right one: what stops the whole thing from sliding off or blowing away in a storm?

The answer is a designed balance of weight, friction, and wind engineering, and it is a mature, common approach on commercial and residential flat roofs alike. Understanding how it works — and what it asks of your roof structure — is the difference between a clean, penetration-free install and an overloaded roof.

## Why flat roofs get a different mounting system

On a sloped roof, gravity pulls panels down against the mounts and the pitch sheds water, so bolting through the surface into rafters is straightforward. A flat or low-slope roof changes both facts. There is no natural tilt to catch the sun, and every hole you drill sits in a surface where water can pool, turning each penetration into a potential leak path.

Ballasted racking solves both problems at once. The panels are mounted on tilt frames — usually angled somewhere in the range of a shallow 5 to 15 degrees — so they face the sun and shed rain and snow rather than lying dead flat. And the frames are weighted down with ballast blocks instead of being bolted through, so the waterproof membrane stays intact. Fewer penetrations means fewer places for the roof to leak, which is a large part of the appeal on a membrane roof. The broader question of whether mounting hardware threatens a roof at all is addressed in [whether solar panels wreck your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/); ballasted systems sidestep much of it by not drilling.

The tilt angle is a deliberate compromise. Steeper tilt catches more sun and sheds snow better but raises the array's profile to the wind, which demands more ballast and casts longer shadows that force rows farther apart. Shallow tilt cuts wind load and packs rows tighter but gives up some production and lets dirt and snow linger. On a flat roof the designer is trading production against wind and weight, not chasing the ideal angle that a pitched roof might allow — a different version of the trade-off explored in [how roof pitch affects output](/blog/roof-pitch-solar-panels/).

## The weight question your roof has to answer

Ballast is dead load, and dead load is the whole design constraint. A ballasted array adds weight across the roof, and that weight has to be something the structure can carry on top of everything else it already bears — the roof's own materials, plus snow, plus maintenance traffic.

As a planning assumption, ballasted residential arrays commonly add something in the range of 3 to 6 pounds per square foot spread across the mounted area, though the figure varies with wind zone, tilt, parapet height, and the specific racking product. Windier sites and higher tilts need more ballast to stay put, pushing toward the upper end or beyond; sheltered sites with low tilt need less. That load is not trivial, and it is exactly why a flat-roof solar project leans so heavily on a structural check before anything is ordered.

A structural engineer, not the sales rep, should confirm the roof can carry the added dead load with an appropriate margin, accounting for local snow and wind. Older roofs, large spans, or lightweight structures may need reinforcement, which adds cost and can change whether ballasted mounting is even the right call versus a penetrating or hybrid system. This is the flat-roof-specific piece of the broader suitability question walked through in [whether your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) — the same logic, with weight-bearing capacity moved to the front of the line.

## How ballasted arrays survive wind

The counterintuitive part is that these unbolted arrays are engineered for high wind, and the engineering is more than "pile on enough concrete." Modern ballasted racking uses deflectors and shaped frames that manage the airflow over and under the tilted panels, reducing the uplift and drag forces the wind can generate. Less aerodynamic force means less ballast is required to resist it, which keeps the roof load down.

Designers also treat the roof unevenly on purpose. Wind forces concentrate at roof edges and corners, so those zones get extra ballast or, in some designs, a few strategic mechanical attachments, while the sheltered interior of the array carries less. Parapet walls that block wind can reduce required ballast; an exposed roof with no parapet increases it. The result is an array tuned to the specific roof and wind zone rather than a uniform slab of weight — and it is validated against building-code wind requirements for the location, which is a large part of what the permitting and engineering review checks.

## Fitting an array onto a flat roof

Layout on a flat roof follows a different logic than on a pitched one. Because the rows are tilted and free-standing, each row casts a shadow that would fall on the row behind it if they were packed too close. Designers space the rows to keep them from shading each other through the productive hours, which means a flat roof fits fewer panels per square foot of roof than a pitched roof of the same footprint. Usable area is real area minus inter-row spacing, minus setbacks from the roof edge for fire access and wind, minus any rooftop equipment like HVAC units and vents.

That spacing penalty is worth modeling before you assume a big flat roof means a big system. A useful sequence is to estimate the true usable area after setbacks and row spacing, then translate that into a realistic panel count and system size. The [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) helps turn available roof area into a plausible array, and the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) works the other direction, from your electricity usage to the system size you would want to fit up there.

Flat-roof solar is neither exotic nor compromised — it is a well-developed approach that trades a few panels' worth of spacing and a serious structural conversation for a penetration-free install and easy roof access. The two things that make or break it are the same two every time: an engineer confirming the roof can carry the ballast, and a layout that respects row spacing so the array actually produces what the sales estimate promised.
