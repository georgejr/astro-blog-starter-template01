A homeowner with a busy roofline — gables poking out at angles, a hip rolling down into a valley, a dormer or two breaking up the main slope — often assumes solar is simply off the table. The picture in their head is a single clean rectangle of panels marching across an unbroken plane, and their roof plainly isn't one. So they talk themselves out of the whole idea before they ever call an installer, which is a shame, because the assumption is usually wrong.

Complex roofs get solar all the time. What changes on a cut-up roof isn't whether an array is possible but how it is laid out. Instead of one big block, a designer tiles panels across several smaller planes, works around the obstructions, and stitches the pieces together electrically so they behave as one system. You do give something up in the trade — some usable area, a little per-panel output on the surfaces that don't face south, occasionally a slightly higher cost per watt — but the array still gets built and still pays for itself. The misconception survives mostly because every marketing photo shows the easy roof, the one that never existed on the house you actually own.

## Your roof isn't one rectangle — it's a set of planes

The single most useful mental shift is to stop picturing solar as one indivisible slab of glass. Modern residential solar is a collection of individual panels, each a rectangle of roughly three and a half by five and a half feet, and each one can go almost anywhere that a few of its neighbors fit. A designer doesn't look at your roof and ask whether a giant rectangle will land on it. They look at it as a set of separate flat surfaces — the trade term is roof planes — and they fill each surface independently with as many panels as its shape, size, and orientation justify.

Seen that way, a complicated roof is not a roof that rejects solar. It is simply a roof with more planes, each of them smaller. A plain gable roof offers two big planes, front and back. A home with a couple of dormers, a hip end, and an attached garage might offer six or eight little ones, each facing a slightly different direction and each with its own quirks. The design question stops being "how many panels fit on this rectangle" and becomes "which of these planes are worth using, and how many panels does each one hold." That is a more involved question, but it is an answerable one, and answering it is ordinary work for a competent designer.

How a designer sizes up all those planes is less mysterious than it sounds. Most start from aerial or satellite imagery, and increasingly from a drone flight or a 3D scan, which lets them measure each plane's dimensions, compass orientation, and pitch without climbing onto the roof for the first pass. Each plane gets a heading — due south, southwest, east, and so on — because the heading, together with the pitch, largely determines how much energy a panel on that surface will make across a year. The planes then sort roughly into tiers. South-facing surfaces are the prize and get filled first. East and west planes come next; they produce less than south but still plenty, and they have the useful habit of shifting their output toward morning and evening, which can actually match a household's real usage better than a south-only array does. North-facing planes sit at the bottom of the list and usually get skipped outright in the US, because a panel aimed away from the sun's daily arc gives up so much output that the mounting hardware and labor rarely justify it. Steeply shaded planes get cut for the same economic reason — shade is a different problem from orientation, but it lands on the spreadsheet the same way. The upshot is that two houses with identical total roof area can support very different arrays depending on how that area is distributed among headings. A home with one big south face beats a home with the same square footage scattered across a north dormer, an east sliver, and a shaded valley — not because the second roof is built worse, but because less of it points somewhere worth using. That is the real reason a designer talks in planes rather than square feet. It also explains why two installers can quote the same house and propose different panel counts: they may have judged the marginal planes differently, one willing to use a small southeast face the other skipped as not worth the hardware. Neither is necessarily wrong. On a complex roof, the array is partly a set of judgment calls about which surfaces clear the bar, which is exactly why the plane-by-plane reasoning is worth understanding before you sit down to compare bids.

Not every plane makes the cut, and a good designer will tell you which ones they are skipping. Small south- and west-facing planes usually earn their place; a decent-sized surface tilted toward the afternoon sun produces enough to be worth the mounting hardware and labor. Tiny north-facing slivers usually don't, because a north plane in the US points away from the sun's path and produces a fraction of what the same panels would make on a better surface. Deeply shaded planes get cut for the same reason. So the array that results isn't a compromise so much as a curated selection: the strong surfaces used, the weak ones left bare, the whole thing sized to what the good planes can hold. Whether your roof is a sensible candidate in the first place comes down to the same fundamentals that decide it on any house — orientation, pitch, shading, and the structural condition of the deck underneath — the whole checklist laid out in [the roof suitability guide](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/), just applied plane by plane instead of all at once. A busy roof doesn't fail that test differently. It just takes the test several times over.

## The geometry that actually costs you panels

Here is the part that catches people off guard: the output penalty on a complex roof rarely comes from the slopes themselves. It comes from everything you are forced to leave empty around the edges and the obstructions, and on a chopped-up roof there are a lot more edges and obstructions to work around.

Start with fire codes, because they are the quiet reason a roof holds fewer panels than its square footage suggests. Most US jurisdictions require clear pathways and setbacks on residential arrays — strips of empty roof near ridges and eaves, and often a clear path running from eave to ridge so that firefighters can move across the roof and cut ventilation holes if the house ever burns. On a simple roof those setbacks nibble a modest fraction off the perimeter and you barely notice. On a roof divided into small planes, every single plane has its own perimeter that must stay clear, and the setbacks eat proportionally more of each surface. A 200-square-foot plane loses a far larger share of itself to a three-foot border than a 600-square-foot plane does, for the same reason a small field loses more of its area to a fence line than a big one. Multiply that across six or eight little planes and the empty margin adds up fast. This is the single biggest reason the number of panels a complex roof can hold falls well short of what a back-of-envelope area calculation predicts.

Fire setbacks are only the start of what has to stay clear. A typical roof is dotted with obstructions that each carve out their own no-go zone: plumbing vent stacks, bathroom and kitchen exhaust vents, the furnace flue, a chimney, the occasional satellite dish, and often one or two skylights. Every one of them needs a margin around it, both so panels don't shade against it and so the crew can flash and seal the surrounding roof properly. On a big, clean plane those interruptions barely register. On a small plane already shrunken by its setbacks, a single vent stack near the middle can be the difference between fitting six panels and fitting four. Designers also work to keep an array visually coherent — rows aligned, edges squared to the ridge and the rake — partly because a ragged layout looks bad from the street and partly because clean rows install faster and stay watertight more reliably. That instinct occasionally leaves a stray usable-looking spot empty, because filling it would strand one lonely panel at an odd angle, out of line with everything else on the plane. All of which is why the honest figure for what a complex roof can hold has to come from a real layout rather than a rule of thumb about watts per square foot. The rules of thumb were calibrated on simple roofs, and a cut-up roof breaks them in the direction of fewer panels almost every time. So when you hear that a roof is "big enough" for a certain system based on its footprint alone, treat that as an optimistic ceiling rather than a working estimate — the setbacks, the obstructions, and the geometry all quietly subtract from it before the first panel is ever set. The gap between total roof and usable roof is the figure that surprises people most, and it's almost always wider on a cut-up roof than a homeowner expects walking into the process. Going in expecting that gap makes the eventual layout feel like sensible engineering rather than a disappointment.

Then there are the obstructions the roofline creates for itself. Dormers do double damage: they subtract their own footprint from the plane they interrupt, and they cast shade across nearby panels when the sun is low. A careful designer keeps panels a comfortable distance from a dormer's shaded side rather than accept a row that limps every morning or every evening. Hips and valleys add a different problem, which is that they create diagonal edges while panels stubbornly remain rectangles. Filling a triangular plane down to a hip line means the last row steps in short, stranding a wedge of usable-looking roof that no rectangle can actually occupy. Valleys collect the awkward leftover geometry where two planes meet, and they channel water and debris, so panels stay clear of them by design. The cumulative effect is that a complex roof's genuinely usable area is meaningfully smaller than its total area — sometimes dramatically so — which is exactly why square footage is such an unreliable guide to system size. That gap between total roof and usable roof is worth understanding before you shop, and it is the same reason [roof size translates so imperfectly into system size](/blog/how-roof-size-affects-solar/) even on simpler homes. On a cut-up roof, the disconnect just gets wider.

## Keeping mixed orientations honest, and what it costs

The other defining challenge of a multi-plane roof is that its planes face different directions, so they peak at different times of day and produce different amounts of energy. On an older string-inverter design, this was a genuine electrical problem. Wiring panels of different orientations into one series string meant the weakest-performing panel could drag down the rest, because a string of panels in series behaves a little like a chain moving at the speed of its slowest link. A shaded or east-facing panel could quietly tax its brighter south-facing neighbors, and the whole string underperformed.

Module-level power electronics largely dissolved that problem, which is why they are close to standard on complex roofs now. Two approaches do the work. Microinverters put a small inverter on each panel, so every panel converts its own output independently and a west plane simply reports its numbers alongside a south plane without either one interfering with the other. Power optimizers achieve much the same independence with a different hardware split: they condition each panel's output on the roof before sending it down to a single central string inverter. Either way, panels on different planes stop competing, and that electrical freedom is precisely what makes a chopped-up roof workable in the first place. The full trade-offs between the two — cost, failure points, monitoring — are worth weighing on their own terms, and they are laid out in [string inverters versus microinverters](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/). The practical upshot for a complex roof is that a designer can confidently combine a good south plane and a good west plane, knowing each will contribute its full, independent share rather than one holding back the other. That same logic is what makes spreading panels across east- and west-facing planes a reasonable design when that is the geometry the roof offers, instead of a reason to walk away. Module-level electronics carry a second, quieter benefit on a busy roof: they report each panel's output individually, so when one plane underperforms — a dormer's shadow creeping across it in winter, a leaf-clogged valley trapping debris on the lowest row — the monitoring app shows you exactly which panels are affected rather than one mysteriously low system total. On a complex roof, where trouble tends to be local to a single plane rather than shared across the whole array, that per-panel visibility is genuinely worth having when something eventually goes wrong.

Two real costs deserve to be priced in honestly. First, planes that don't face due south produce less per panel — a west or east plane might deliver somewhere in the range of 15 to 20 percent less annual energy than an equivalent south-facing plane, depending on pitch and location. Treat that as a labeled planning assumption rather than a fixed figure for your house. It is a reduction, not a disqualification; those panels still generate real, useful kilowatt-hours. Second, a design that needs many small groupings, extra flashing points, and more labor to thread around obstructions can carry a modestly higher cost per watt than a clean single-plane install, because the crew spends more time per panel. Neither of these sinks the project. They just mean the array on a busy roof is often sized to what the good planes can hold rather than to the whole electric bill, which makes working backward from your usage the more useful exercise. Run your annual consumption through the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool to learn the panel count your usage actually calls for, then use the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) to translate a given plane's real dimensions — setbacks already subtracted — into how many panels it can realistically hold. If the strong surfaces can't fit the full count, a designer prioritizes the best orientations first and you accept a slightly smaller system, which is a perfectly ordinary outcome. The one thing worth insisting on for a complicated roof is a production estimate built plane by plane rather than as a single round number, because your planes face different ways, sit at different pitches, and can even shade one another — a taller gable throwing winter shadow across a lower plane, a dormer clipping the morning sun off its neighbor. Modeling software accounts for all of it and shows the expected output of each cluster of panels. If a proposal for a busy roof hands you one lump production figure with no plane-by-plane breakdown, ask for the detail; on a simple roof that shortcut is harmless, but on a cut-up one it hides exactly the variation you are paying a designer to understand. A good installer answers a complex roof with a layout and a breakdown, not a rejection — and a company that waves your roof off as impossible has told you something about the company, not the roof.
