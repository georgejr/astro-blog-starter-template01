A reasonable person with $25,000 sitting in cash and a decent roof faces a genuine fork: hand it to a solar installer, or drop it into a broad-market index fund and keep paying the electric company. Framed that way, solar has to justify itself as an investment, not just as a green gesture, and the comparison people reach for is the stock market. It's the right instinct and the wrong direct comparison, because the two returns aren't the same kind of number. One is a volatile, taxable, market-driven return you might get. The other is a steadier, effectively tax-free, inflation-linked return you avoid paying out. Line them up naively and you'll either talk yourself out of a good decision or into a bad one.

The goal here isn't to declare a winner. It's to translate the two into the same units so you can actually compare them, and to be honest about where each one wins.

## Why a solar return and a market return aren't the same animal

When you buy an index fund, you're hoping for appreciation and dividends. Over long stretches, the broad US stock market has historically delivered something in the neighborhood of a high-single-digit to roughly ten percent average annual nominal return, or closer to seven percent after inflation. Those are long-run historical averages, not a promise, and anyone who lived through a bad decade knows the average hides brutal years. That return is also taxable: dividends get taxed as you receive them, and gains get taxed when you sell, so your after-tax take is smaller than the headline. And it's volatile. The sequence matters, especially if you might need the money at a bad moment.

Solar doesn't appreciate. It does the opposite of a growth asset: it slowly degrades, losing a fraction of a percent of output per year. What it produces instead is a stream of avoided electricity bills, and that stream has three properties that make it genuinely different from a stock return. First, it's effectively tax-free. Money you don't spend on electricity isn't income, so there's no tax on it, which means a solar "return" of a given percentage is worth more than the same headline percentage from a taxable investment. To match, say, a 7 percent tax-free bill reduction, a taxable investment in a moderate bracket has to earn meaningfully more than 7 percent before tax. That gap is the single most underappreciated point in this whole comparison, and it quietly favors solar.

Second, the solar return is inflation-linked in the one way that matters to you. Its size tracks your electricity rate, and electricity rates have a long history of drifting upward. When the utility raises rates, your avoided-cost savings rise right along with them, without you doing anything. In a decade of rising power prices, a solar system's effective return climbs; in that sense it behaves like a hedge against exactly the cost it offsets. We dig into that dynamic on its own in [solar as a hedge against rising electricity rates](/blog/solar-hedge-rising-electricity-rates/), and it's a big part of why the naive comparison undersells solar.

Third, the return is low-volatility and largely predictable. A well-designed system on a decent roof produces a fairly consistent number of kilowatt-hours year after year, weather noise aside. There's no sequence-of-returns risk, no bad decade that wipes out your break-even. The main variables, degradation and rate changes, move slowly and mostly in solar's favor. Against that, the stock market offers higher potential upside and far higher variance. Both can be right choices; they're just answering different appetites.

There's one more asymmetry worth stating plainly: liquidity. An index fund is money you can sell on any trading day. A solar array is money welded to your roof. You recover it only through the bills you avoid and, imperfectly, through added home value if you sell. If there's any real chance you'll need that $25,000 back in a hurry, the stock beats solar on that axis alone, regardless of returns. Solar is a commitment to a house as much as an investment in a technology, and that's a feature for some households and a dealbreaker for others.

## Putting both in the same units so you can actually decide

To compare fairly, convert the solar decision into an investment metric and then adjust for taxes. The crudest version is payback: the number of years until cumulative bill savings equal the net cost after the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, which lets you claim 30 percent of qualifying costs against your federal tax (verify the current details against IRS guidance). A system that pays back in, say, ten years and then keeps producing for another fifteen-plus is delivering free power for the back half of its life. But payback alone is a blunt tool, because it ignores the value of the years after break-even and treats a dollar saved in year one the same as a dollar saved in year twenty. For a real comparison against a stock, you want the return expressed as an annual rate. The cleaner approaches are the ones investors already use: internal rate of return and net present value, which discount future savings back to today's dollars and spit out a percentage or a lump-sum value you can hold up next to a fund's expected return. We walk through that method in [the NPV and IRR view of a solar investment](/blog/solar-npv-irr/), and it's the honest way to make the two numbers comparable.

Here's a labeled worked example to show the shape of it, using round assumptions you should replace with your own. Assume a $25,000 system, a 30 percent federal credit that drops the net cost to $17,500, first-year bill savings of about $1,800, electricity rates rising a couple of percent a year, and panels degrading about half a percent a year. In year one you're saving roughly ten percent of your net cost. As rates climb and the loan-free years stack up, cumulative savings over a 25-year window can total well more than double the net cost, which pencils out to an internal rate of return commonly landing in the high-single-digits to low-teens for a decent site. Because that return is effectively tax-free, its tax-equivalent figure is higher still. That's competitive with the stock market's long-run historical average, with far less volatility, and it's why so many finance-minded homeowners conclude solar is a reasonable place for money they weren't going to gamble anyway. Run your own version of that arithmetic with the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) and check the break-even year with the [solar payback calculator](/solar-payback-calculator/) before you trust any single number, because the inputs, especially your electricity rate and sun exposure, swing the result more than anything else.

The comparison also isn't strictly either-or, and pretending it is muddies the decision. Solar and an index fund draw on the same dollars only if you have exactly enough cash for one. Many households finance the system and keep the cash invested, which changes the question entirely into whether the loan rate is below the effective return, a different calculation with its own traps. And the two assets do different jobs in a portfolio. The index fund is liquid, taxable, volatile, and uncorrelated with your utility bill. Solar is illiquid, tax-advantaged, steady, and directly correlated with a cost you'd otherwise pay every month for as long as you own the house. Treating solar as a bond-like, inflation-protected slice of your finances, rather than a growth bet, is closer to the truth and leads to better decisions than pitting it head-to-head against equities.

## The scenarios where each one clearly wins

Abstractions only get you so far, so it helps to name the households where the answer is nearly settled before you touch a calculator. The stock market wins cleanly in a few situations. If your electricity is cheap, under a dime a kilowatt-hour in a low-rate state, the pile of money solar can save shrinks so much that even a modest, liquid market return usually beats it, and the illiquidity of a roof-bound asset stops being worth it. If your roof is heavily shaded, wrongly oriented, or too small to host a worthwhile array, solar's return collapses while an index fund does not care about your trees. And if there is a real chance you will move within a handful of years, before the system has broken even, the market's any-day liquidity is decisive, because you cannot easily pull equity back out of panels bolted to a house you are selling.

Solar wins just as cleanly at the other end. A homeowner with ordinary-to-high electricity rates, a solid unshaded roof facing a good direction, and firm plans to stay in the house for a decade or more is looking at a tax-free, inflation-linked return that competes with equities at a fraction of the volatility. The longer that owner stays and the higher their rates climb, the more decisively solar pulls ahead, because every rate increase quietly fattens the return with no action on their part. For that profile, parking the same cash in a conservative, taxable investment often underperforms the bill they would have kept paying.

The messiest and most common case sits in the middle: decent rates, a reasonable roof, an uncertain time horizon. Here the honest move is to run the actual numbers rather than lean on intuition, because small differences in rate, sun exposure, and how long you stay tip the balance either way. This is also where financing reshapes everything. If you would finance the system rather than pay cash, you are no longer choosing between solar and the market at all; you are keeping the cash invested and asking whether the loan rate sits below solar's effective return. When the loan is cheap and the return is solid, you can, in effect, own both the panels and the portfolio, a genuinely different and often better position than the either-or framing implies.

None of this makes solar a guaranteed win or a guaranteed loss. It makes it a decision with knowable inputs. Pin down your real electricity rate, get an honest read on your roof, be truthful about how long you will stay, and the profiles above will tell you which side of the line you are on before you ever discount a single future cash flow.

Where does that leave the person with $25,000 and a good roof? If you value liquidity above all, or your roof is shaded, your rates are cheap, or you might move soon, the stock market's flexibility probably wins, and the deeper question of whether solar clears the bar at all is worth working through in [is solar actually worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/). If your roof is solid, your rates are ordinary-to-high, you plan to stay put, and you'd otherwise have parked the money somewhere conservative, solar's tax-free, inflation-linked, low-volatility return is not the poor cousin of an index fund. It's a different instrument that, on the right house, quietly beats the after-tax return of the safe alternatives while doing a job no fund can do, which is to stop a recurring bill from ever hitting your account. The mistake isn't choosing one over the other. It's comparing them as though they were the same kind of money.
