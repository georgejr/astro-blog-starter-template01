Ask an installer to size your system and you'll usually get a number that's suspiciously easy to nudge upward. Bigger systems mean bigger contracts, so the incentive tilts toward "more." But too big and too small are both real mistakes with real costs — just different ones. The right size isn't the largest array your roof allows, and it isn't the cheapest one that fits your budget. It's the one matched to how much electricity you actually use and the rules your utility plays by.

## Two failure modes, not one

Most sizing advice frames the risk as going too small. In reality there are two distinct ways to get it wrong, and they push in opposite directions.

Undersize, and you leave money on the table every month — but the money you leave is the *most valuable* money, because it's the top of your bill. Oversize, and you overpay up front for production that either gets exported at a poor rate or, in some places, isn't credited at all. Understanding both is the only way to land in the middle, and the full framework for arriving at a target lives in [what size solar system do you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/).

## The cost of going too small

An undersized system feels safe because it's cheaper, but it quietly underperforms in ways that compound.

**It leaves the priciest kilowatt-hours on the bill.** Many utilities charge tiered rates, where your last kilowatt-hours each month cost more than your first. Solar offsets consumption starting from the top of that stack, so a system that covers only part of your usage is offsetting your *cheapest* power while leaving the expensive top tier on the bill. The result: a system that covers 70% of your kilowatt-hours might cut your bill by less than 70%, because the remaining usage sits in the low-cost tiers. That's the counterintuitive penalty of undersizing.

**It ignores where your load is heading.** Solar sized to today's usage can be underbuilt for tomorrow's. An EV in the driveway or a heat pump replacing a gas furnace can add thousands of kilowatt-hours a year. A system sized to your current bill leaves that new load fully exposed to grid rates.

**Expansion later is rarely cheap or clean.** Adding panels after the fact means new permitting, possibly a new inverter or interconnection review, and a second mobilization of a crew — losing the economies of doing it all at once.

## The cost of going too big

Oversizing is the mistake the sales process nudges you toward, and it has its own penalties.

**Surplus is worth less than what you paid to make it.** In markets where exports earn less than the retail rate — increasingly the norm — the extra production from an oversized array is sold cheap to the utility instead of offsetting expensive retail power. You paid full price for panels producing power you're giving away at a discount. This is the mechanism behind [the oversizing penalty on payback](/blog/oversized-solar-system-payback/): past the point that covers your own usage, each added panel returns less.

**Some utilities cap what they'll credit.** Interconnection rules and net metering programs sometimes limit system size to roughly your historical usage, or won't credit production beyond it. Build past that ceiling and the excess generation may earn nothing at all.

**The payback stretches.** More panels mean more up-front cost. If that extra cost buys low-value exports, the whole system's break-even point moves further out, even though your monthly bill hits zero sooner.

## Where "just right" actually sits

The Goldilocks size is usually an array matched to your annual consumption, with a deliberate adjustment for what's coming.

- **Start from a full year of usage**, not a single bill, so seasonal swings average out.
- **Add for known near-term load growth** — an EV or heat pump you'll realistically buy within a couple of years — rather than speculative someday additions.
- **Respect your utility's rules.** If exports pay poorly or system size is capped, aim to cover your own usage rather than to overproduce. If you have generous full retail net metering, the penalty for a bit of extra size is smaller.

Aiming for a 100% offset feels like the natural target, but it's not automatically the optimum — the case for and against that goal is worth reading in [should you aim for 100% solar offset](/blog/100-percent-solar-offset/). A quick way to pressure-test a proposed size against your roof and usage is the [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/); if a quote comes in noticeably larger than your usage justifies, that gap is your cue to ask what's driving it.

The reason this matters is that sizing is where a lot of the return is won or lost, and it's the one variable a salesperson has the most incentive to push in the wrong direction. Too small quietly bleeds value from the top of your bill; too big buys cheap exports at full price. Land in between, adjusted for your rates and your future load, and the rest of the economics has a fighting chance.
