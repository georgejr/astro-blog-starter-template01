Ask an installer to size your system and you'll usually get a number that turns out to be suspiciously easy to nudge upward. Bigger systems mean bigger contracts, so the commercial incentive tilts steadily toward "more," and a sales conversation rarely pushes back toward a smaller array. The problem is that too big and too small are both genuine mistakes with genuine costs — just different costs, pointing in opposite directions. The right size isn't the largest array your roof can physically hold, and it isn't the cheapest one that squeezes into your budget either. It's the array matched to how much electricity you actually use, adjusted for the specific rules your utility plays by. Getting there means understanding both ways it can go wrong, because they're not the same failure and you can't guard against one by leaning toward the other.

## Two failure modes, not one

Most sizing advice frames the risk as going too small, as though the only danger is not building enough. In reality there are two distinct ways to get it wrong, and they push against each other, which is exactly what makes the sweet spot a real target rather than a direction. Undersize the system and you leave money on the table every month — but the money you leave behind is the most valuable money, because it sits at the top of your bill where each kilowatt-hour costs the most. Oversize it and you overpay up front for production that either gets exported at a poor rate or, in some places, earns nothing at all. One error bleeds value from the expensive end of your usage; the other spends real capital to manufacture cheap surplus. Landing between them is the whole game, and the full framework for arriving at a defensible target — starting from usage rather than from roof capacity — lives in [what size solar system do you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/), which is the piece to read before you let any quote anchor you.

What makes this genuinely tricky is that the two mistakes don't feel the same when you're making them. Undersizing feels prudent and safe — you're spending less, the quote is smaller, and the system still does something. Oversizing feels generous and future-proof — you're covering everything, maybe with a little extra for good measure. Both feelings are traps, because the cheaper-feeling undersize quietly forfeits your most expensive power, and the future-proof-feeling oversize quietly buys production worth a fraction of what you paid to make it. Neither mistake announces itself, which is why so many systems end up on one side or the other of right.

## The cost of going too small, and the cost of going too big

An undersized system feels safe precisely because it's cheaper, but it underperforms in ways that compound quietly over the years. The most counterintuitive penalty is that it leaves the priciest kilowatt-hours on the bill. Many utilities charge tiered rates, where your last kilowatt-hours each month cost more than your first, and solar offsets your consumption starting from the top of that stack downward. A system that covers only part of your usage is therefore offsetting your cheapest power while leaving the expensive top tier untouched — so a system that covers 70% of your kilowatt-hours might cut your bill by noticeably less than 70%, because the 30% still on the bill sits in the low-cost tiers you never reached. That's the undersizing penalty most people never see coming, and it means a too-small system disappoints relative to its size in a way the sticker price never warned about.

Undersizing also tends to ignore where your load is heading, which is its second cost. A system sized precisely to today's usage can be badly underbuilt for tomorrow's, and household electricity demand rarely sits still. An electric vehicle in the driveway can add thousands of kilowatt-hours a year on its own; a heat pump replacing a gas furnace shifts heating load onto the meter; a growing family simply uses more. A system sized to your current bill leaves all of that new load fully exposed to grid rates, and the third cost compounds it: expanding an array after the fact is rarely cheap or clean. Adding panels later means new permitting, possibly a new inverter or an interconnection review, and a second mobilization of a crew — losing the economies you'd have captured by doing it all at once. The gap you left to save money up front gets filled later at a premium, if it gets filled at all.

Oversizing is the mistake the sales process actively nudges you toward, and it carries its own distinct penalties. The central one is that surplus is worth less than what you paid to make it. In the growing number of markets where exports earn less than the retail rate, the extra production from an oversized array gets sold cheap to the utility instead of offsetting expensive retail power you'd otherwise buy — you paid full price for panels whose output you're now giving away at a discount. That's the mechanism behind [the oversizing penalty on payback](/blog/oversized-solar-system-payback/): past the point that covers your own usage, each additional panel returns less than the one before it, and the marginal panels can drag the whole system's economics down. Some utilities compound this by capping what they'll credit at all — interconnection rules and net metering programs sometimes limit system size to roughly your historical usage, or refuse to credit production beyond it, so build past that ceiling and the excess generation may earn literally nothing. And in every case, more panels mean more up-front cost, so if that extra cost is buying low-value exports, the system's overall break-even point moves further out even though your monthly bill might hit zero sooner. A bill of zero and a fast payback are not the same achievement, and oversizing can deliver the first while quietly damaging the second.

There's a psychological wrinkle that makes undersizing's future-load problem easy to rationalize away, and it deserves a mention. A homeowner sizing to today's bill can always tell themselves they'll deal with the EV or the heat pump later, when the need is concrete, and that patience feels responsible. But solar rewards decisions made all at once far more than decisions made in sequence, because the fixed costs of a project — the permitting, the crew mobilization, the interconnection paperwork, the design work — get paid whether you install six kilowatts or nine. Spreading those fixed costs across a system built once, at the size you'll actually need in a couple of years, is meaningfully cheaper per watt than paying them twice for two smaller projects. So the "I'll expand later" plan usually costs more in total than committing to the right size now, provided the future load is genuinely coming rather than hypothetical. The discipline is telling the difference between the load you can name and schedule and the one you're merely imagining.

The asymmetry between the two mistakes is worth sitting with. Undersizing forfeits value at the expensive top of your bill but keeps your capital outlay low; oversizing spends more capital to produce surplus worth little. Which one is more costly in your specific case depends heavily on your utility's export rules — under generous full retail net metering, a bit of extra size is nearly harmless because exports credit at retail, while under a below-retail export regime, oversizing is genuinely expensive because every surplus kilowatt-hour is worth a fraction of what it cost. That dependence on the rules is why there's no universal "aim slightly big" or "aim slightly small" advice that holds everywhere.

## Where "just right" actually sits

The Goldilocks size is usually an array matched to your annual consumption, with a deliberate, modest adjustment for what's genuinely coming. Start from a full year of usage rather than a single bill, so that seasonal swings — the summer air-conditioning spike, the winter lull or surge depending on how you heat — average out into a number that reflects your real annual demand rather than one unrepresentative month. Then add for known near-term load growth, meaning an EV or a heat pump you'll realistically buy within a couple of years, not the speculative someday additions that tempt every homeowner into overbuilding for a future that may never arrive. There's a real difference between sizing for the electric car sitting in your driveway next spring and sizing for the pool you've vaguely thought about, and only the first belongs in the calculation.

Above all, respect your utility's rules, because they change what the right answer is. If exports pay poorly or system size is capped, aim to cover your own usage rather than to overproduce, since surplus in that environment is worth little and may be worth nothing. If you have generous full retail net metering, the penalty for a bit of extra size is smaller, and modest overbuilding to cover future load is more forgivable. Sizing is where a large share of the return is quietly won or lost, and it's the single variable a salesperson has the most incentive to push in the wrong direction. Too small bleeds value from the top of your bill month after month; too big buys cheap exports at full retail price. Land in between, adjusted honestly for your rates and your realistic future load, and the rest of the economics — the payback math, the lifetime savings, the resale value — has a genuine fighting chance to work out the way the brochure promised.
