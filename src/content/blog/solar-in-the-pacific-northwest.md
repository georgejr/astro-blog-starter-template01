The Pacific Northwest has a reputation that scares people off solar before they run a single number: gray skies, constant drizzle, moss on everything. Ask a resident of Seattle or Portland whether their region gets enough sun for panels and you will often get a laugh. Yet the West of the Cascades is dotted with functioning residential arrays that pay for themselves, and the reason is a quirk of geography that the rainy-day stereotype completely hides. The region does not fail to get sun; it gets its sun on a wildly uneven schedule, hoarding almost all of it into a handful of long, dry, brilliant summer months and then going nearly dark for the wet half of the year.

That lopsided delivery is the single most important fact about pacific northwest solar, and everything about sizing, financing, and setting expectations flows from it. A system here does not earn steadily month after month the way one in a mild, even climate like coastal California does. It runs a boom-and-bust cycle across the calendar, and the whole economic case rests on whether the boom is big enough to carry the bust.

## The summer surplus has to carry the winter trough

Start with daylight, because latitude does something in the Northwest that milder-sounding descriptions miss. Seattle sits near 47 degrees north, farther north than Toronto or the northern tip of Maine, and Portland is not far behind. That far up, the length of the day swings enormously across the year. Around the summer solstice, the region gets roughly sixteen hours between sunrise and sunset, and because the summers are famously dry, those long days come with clear skies day after day through July and August. A panel in the Northwest in midsummer is genuinely productive, competitive with far sunnier-sounding states, simply because the sun is up for so long and the marine cloud has burned off.

Winter is the mirror image and it is brutal for a solar panel. Around the winter solstice, daylight shrinks to about eight and a half hours, the sun barely climbs above the southern horizon, and the sky spends most of that short window under thick, wet overcast. Between the short days, the low sun angle, and the near-constant cloud, a December in the Northwest can deliver only a small fraction of a June's energy. The swing between the best and worst months in this region is dramatic, often on the order of six to one between a strong summer month and a weak winter one, which is far steeper than the two- or three-to-one swing typical of a sunnier, drier climate. This is not a defect to be engineered away. It is the fundamental shape of the resource, and a Northwest system has to be understood as a machine that overproduces for a few months and underproduces for many.

The mechanism that makes this workable is net metering, the utility billing arrangement that lets your meter run backward when you produce more than you use. During the long summer days, a properly sized Northwest array will frequently make far more than the home consumes, and that surplus gets credited to your account. Through the dark winter, when the panels can barely keep the lights on, the home draws those banked credits back down. The summer boom, in effect, prepays the winter bust. Whether this works cleanly for you depends on the specific net metering terms your utility offers, because the value of a banked summer kilowatt-hour and how long you are allowed to hold it before it expires vary between the region's many utilities. This is one of the places where the details of solar in Washington and Oregon genuinely diverge, since the two states have different regulatory frameworks and each is served by a patchwork of public and private utilities with their own rules.

It is worth being precise about what "banking" does and does not mean, because homeowners often picture a savings account that fills up with dollars over the summer. What actually accumulates is usually a credit measured in kilowatt-hours or in dollar value, and the terms attached to it decide how useful the summer surplus really is. Some utilities let credits roll month to month for a full year and then true up, wiping the slate at an annual settlement date; others cash out any leftover surplus at a low avoided-cost rate rather than the retail rate, which means over-banking beyond your annual need earns you very little. The practical consequence in the Northwest, where the surplus is concentrated into a few months and the drawdown into many, is that the timing of that annual true-up date matters. A settlement date set at the end of summer would be poorly matched to a region that spends winter drawing credits down, so knowing when your utility resets the ledger, and at what rate it values any excess, is part of judging whether a given system size makes sense. This is the kind of detail that never appears in a national rule of thumb but quietly shapes the real return on a Northwest array.

There is a second regional wrinkle that cuts the other way, and an honest look at Northwest solar has to name it: electricity here is cheap, because much of the region runs on abundant hydroelectric power. Solar saves money by displacing the utility rate you would otherwise pay, so a lower starting rate means each kilowatt-hour your panels make is worth less than the same kilowatt-hour would be in a high-rate state like California or Massachusetts. That does not make solar a bad deal in the Northwest, but it does make the payback longer and the margin for error smaller than in expensive-power states. It means the design has to be tight, the quote has to be competitive, and the decision leans more on long-run rate increases and personal preference than on a dramatic overnight saving. ## Sizing and expectations for a boom-and-bust climate

Because the resource is so seasonal, the way you size a Northwest system matters more than it does in an even climate, and the instinct many homeowners bring, "just cover my winter usage," is the wrong starting point. If you size the array to make enough in December to run the house in December, you will have built something enormous that pours out a massive, largely unusable surplus every summer, most of which the utility will credit at a rate lower than what you paid to install the panels making it. Oversizing for the dark months wastes money on capacity that spends July drowning in credits you cannot fully use.

The better approach in this region is to size against your annual total consumption and let net metering handle the seasonal timing. You build a system whose full-year production roughly matches your full-year usage, accept that it will bank credits from spring through early fall and spend them from late fall through winter, and let the calendar average things out. Getting that annual match right is exactly the kind of estimate a [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool is built for, since it works from your yearly kilowatt-hours rather than from any single month, and pairing it with a full [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) that accounts for Northwest sun hours will give you a defensible target rather than a guess. The key mental shift is to stop thinking month by month and start thinking about the year as a single ledger.

Orientation and tilt deserve more thought in the Northwest than in a sunnier place, precisely because the resource is so lopsided. In a climate with abundant year-round sun, a slightly imperfect roof angle costs a little and nobody notices. In the Northwest, where the summer months carry the whole economic load, you want the array positioned to make the most of exactly those long, high-sun-angle summer days. A south-facing roof at a moderate tilt captures the summer strongly, and while a steeper tilt would favor the low winter sun, chasing winter production in a region where winter is nearly a write-off is usually the wrong optimization. East and west faces still work and still produce, but on a Northwest roof the penalty for pointing panels away from the summer sun compounds with the region's already-seasonal resource, so the placement conversation matters. The takeaway is not that a non-ideal roof rules out solar here; plenty of Northwest homes go solar on imperfect roofs. It is that the region gives you less margin for a lazy layout, and the difference between a well-oriented array and a poorly oriented one shows up most in the summer months that were supposed to fund the year.

Setting expectations honestly is the other half of the job, because a Northwest homeowner who watches the monitoring app in January will see numbers that look alarming if nobody prepared them. A well-designed system here can spend the darkest weeks producing a small trickle, and that is not a malfunction; it is the resource doing exactly what the latitude and weather dictate. The right frame is to judge the system on its annual output and its full-year bill impact, not on any single dreary month. Homeowners who come in expecting steady, even production every month are the ones who end up disappointed, while those who understand the boom-and-bust rhythm tend to be satisfied, because the machine is doing what a Northwest machine does. The broader question of whether panels pay off under persistently gray skies is worth working through in the general treatment of [solar savings in cloudy climates](/blog/solar-savings-cloudy-climates/), which lays out why cloud cover shapes the total without erasing it.

The realistic bottom line for the region is that solar works in the Pacific Northwest, but it works differently than the brochures imply and differently than it does almost anywhere else in the country. The summers do a genuinely heavy share of the lifting, the winters are a near-write-off you plan around rather than fight, and the cheap regional power means the financial case is real but modest and depends heavily on getting a competitive install and understanding your specific utility's net metering rules. A homeowner who internalizes the seasonal shape, sizes to the annual total rather than the winter low, and judges the result across a full calendar will find that the gray-skies reputation was always beside the point. What matters is the year, and across a Northwest year, the long bright summers pay more of the bill than the wet winters take away.
