A 1,200 kWh monthly electric bill puts you comfortably above the national average, in the territory of a larger house, a family that runs central air hard through the summer, or a home that has already picked up an electric vehicle or a heat pump. It's a usage level where solar tends to make real financial sense, because there's a substantial bill for the panels to attack. The number people actually want when they see that 1,200 on their statement is a system size — how many kilowatts of panels, how many modules on the roof, and whether it will physically fit. Getting there is mostly arithmetic, but it's arithmetic with two levers that swing the answer by several panels in either direction: how much sun your location delivers, and how much of your usage you're actually trying to cover.

Start with the annual figure, because solar is sized against a year, not a month. Twelve months at 1,200 kWh is 14,400 kWh a year, and that total is what a system has to produce to fully offset your consumption. But 1,200 is almost certainly a monthly *average* pulled from a bill that swings hard with the seasons — a summer cooling month might hit 1,700 kWh while a mild spring month sits at 800. The annual total is what matters for sizing, so if you have a single month's bill in hand rather than a full year, be a little careful: a 1,200 kWh reading taken in July overstates your yearly usage, and one taken in April understates it. The cleanest input is twelve months of actual kWh added up, and if you have that, our walkthrough on how to [turn a full year of bills into the right system size](/blog/size-solar-from-annual-kwh/) is the more precise route than reasoning from a single month.

## From 14,400 kWh a year to a system size

To convert an annual kWh target into a system size in kilowatts, you divide by a production ratio — the number of kilowatt-hours a single kilowatt of panels generates in a year where you live. That ratio is the entire reason two identical 14,400 kWh homes need different-sized systems in different states. A kilowatt of panels in sunny Arizona might produce around 1,700 kWh a year; the same kilowatt in the cloudier Northeast might produce closer to 1,200. Same hardware, very different yield, because the sun resource differs. The production ratio already bakes in the everyday losses that keep a system from hitting its nameplate rating — inverter conversion, wiring, heat, dust, and the fact that panels rarely face perfectly south at a perfect tilt.

Here's how the division plays out across a few representative production ratios. These are round, labeled illustrations, not guarantees for your specific roof:

| Annual usage | Production ratio (kWh per kW/yr) | System size needed |
|---|---|---|
| 14,400 kWh | 1,100 (cloudy North) | ~13.1 kW |
| 14,400 kWh | 1,300 (mixed climate) | ~11.1 kW |
| 14,400 kWh | 1,500 (sunny South) | ~9.6 kW |
| 14,400 kWh | 1,700 (desert Southwest) | ~8.5 kW |

The spread is striking. The same 1,200 kWh home needs anywhere from roughly 8.5 kW in Phoenix to more than 13 kW in a gray northern climate to reach full offset. This is why any single "1,200 kWh means X panels" claim is only half-true — it's true for one production ratio and wrong for the rest. When people quote "about 9 to 10 kW for a 1,200 kWh home," they're implicitly assuming a middling-to-sunny climate, which covers a lot of the country but not the extremes. To pin down your own ratio, feed your annual usage and location into the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool, which applies a region-appropriate production estimate rather than a national average.

Translating kilowatts into an actual panel count is the next step, and it depends on the wattage of the modules your installer uses. Residential panels have crept up over the years, and a common modern module sits somewhere in the 400-watt neighborhood. A 10 kW system built from 400-watt panels is 25 panels; the same 10 kW built from 440-watt panels is fewer than 23. Higher-wattage panels let you reach the same system size with fewer modules, which matters most when roof space is tight. Across the range in the table, a 1,200 kWh home is generally looking at something in the low-to-mid 20s of panels in a sunny climate and pushing past 30 in a cloudy one. If you want to sanity-check the panel count and see how wattage shifts it, the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) lets you vary module size and watch the count move.

One honest caveat on the "full offset" target: sizing to produce 100% of your annual usage does not mean your electric bill hits zero. Solar production and household demand rarely line up hour to hour — you make surplus power at midday and buy grid power after sunset — so what actually happens to your bill depends heavily on how your utility credits the energy you export. Under generous full-retail net metering, a system sized to your annual usage can come close to zeroing out the energy portion of the bill. Under a lower export rate, the same system leaves more of a bill behind, and you might even choose to size a bit smaller to avoid exporting cheaply. That's a savings question rather than a sizing question, but it's worth holding in mind so the panel count you land on reflects what you're really trying to accomplish.

## Will 9 or 10 kW actually fit, and should you build it all at once?

A 1,200 kWh home is where roof space starts to become a genuine constraint rather than an afterthought. A 10 kW system of 400-watt panels is 25 modules, and each modern panel occupies roughly 21 to 22 square feet. That's around 525 to 550 square feet of *usable* array area — not total roof area, but the specific south-, east-, and west-facing planes that are unshaded, unobstructed, and large enough to hold panels in tidy rows. On a simple gable roof with a big south face, 25 panels fit without drama. On a complex roof cut up by dormers, hips, valleys, vents, and a chimney, finding 550 clean square feet can be the hard part, and it's the reason two homes with identical bills sometimes end up with different system sizes: one had the roof for it and one didn't.

When the roof can't hold a full-offset system, you have a few levers. Higher-wattage panels squeeze more kilowatts into the same footprint, which is often the cleanest fix — 30 panels at 440 watts deliver noticeably more than 30 at 400. Premium high-efficiency modules push that further, generating more watts per square foot, though they cost more per panel. Failing that, you accept a smaller system that covers, say, 80% of your usage rather than 100%, which still eliminates the great majority of the bill and often improves the economics anyway if your utility pays little for exports. Partial offset is not a failure; for many 1,200 kWh homes it's the smarter target. The point is that roof geometry, not just usage, sets the ceiling on what you can build, and it's worth measuring your usable planes early rather than discovering the limit after you've fallen in love with a number.

There's also the question of building for today's bill versus tomorrow's. A 1,200 kWh household is often on an upward usage trajectory — an EV in the driveway can add 250 to 400 kWh a month on its own, and swapping a gas furnace for a heat pump can push winter usage up substantially. If any of that is on your horizon, sizing purely to your current 14,400 kWh leaves you undersized the day the new load arrives, and adding panels later is rarely cheap on a per-watt basis because you pay for a second mobilization, possibly a second permit, and sometimes inverter changes. It's frequently worth sizing the array a notch above today's usage if you can see the future load coming and the roof has room. On the other hand, deliberately overbuilding far beyond any realistic usage just to chase a bigger system wastes money on panels whose output you'll export at a low rate. The sweet spot is honest anticipation: size for the usage you can genuinely foresee, not for a hypothetical or for today's snapshot alone.

It helps to bracket a 1,200 kWh home against its neighbors on the usage ladder. A home at 1,000 kWh a month needs a meaningfully smaller array, and the sizing logic for that level is laid out in our guide to [how many panels cover 1,000 kWh a month](/blog/how-many-solar-panels-for-1000-kwh-per-month/); a home at 1,500 kWh a month climbs toward a larger, roof-hungry system covered in [sizing for 1,500 kWh a month](/blog/how-many-solar-panels-for-1500-kwh-per-month/). A 1,200 kWh household sits between the two, which is a useful place to be — big enough that solar clearly pays, not so big that a typical roof can't hold the array in most climates. Reading the level below and the level above yours gives you a feel for how sensitive the panel count is to usage, which in turn tells you how much a few efficiency upgrades might shrink the system you need.

If you take one habit away from all this, let it be sizing against a full year of real kWh rather than a single month, and running your actual location through a tool that applies a local production ratio instead of trusting a one-size-fits-all "9 to 10 kW" figure. The rule of thumb gets you into the right neighborhood; your sun hours, your roof, and your plans for future loads decide the exact address. A 1,200 kWh home almost always ends up somewhere between a mid-single-digit and low-teens kilowatt system, and the difference between those extremes is entirely explained by where you live and how much roof you have to work with — which is why the arithmetic is worth doing for your own house rather than borrowing someone else's answer.
