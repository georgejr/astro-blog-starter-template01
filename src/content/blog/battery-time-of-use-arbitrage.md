A home battery is usually sold as a place to stash your afternoon solar for the evening. But if your utility charges different prices at different hours, the battery can do something a commodities trader would recognize: buy energy when it's cheap, hold it, and use it when the same energy would cost several times as much. You never sell anything back — you simply avoid buying at the painful rate by spending what you stored at the gentle one. That maneuver is battery time-of-use arbitrage, and whether it's worth doing comes down to three numbers: a price spread, a round-trip loss, and a bit of wear on the battery each cycle.

## The idea, and the loss that complicates it

Time-of-use rates split the day into pricing windows. Overnight and midday tend to be cheap; the late-afternoon-into-evening stretch, when everyone gets home and demand spikes, is expensive — sometimes dramatically so, with peak rates running three or four times the off-peak price on aggressive tariffs. A battery lets you decouple *when you buy* power from *when you use* it. Charge the battery from the grid during a cheap window, then discharge it to run your house through the expensive one, and every kilowatt-hour you pull from the battery during peak is a kilowatt-hour you didn't buy at the peak price. The battery becomes a time machine for electricity, moving cheap energy forward a few hours to the moment it's worth the most.

It's worth being precise about what makes this different from the usual solar story, because the two get blurred together. Solar self-consumption stores *your own free generation* — the panels make power you didn't pay a per-kilowatt-hour price for, and the battery holds it for later. Arbitrage stores *cheap purchased grid power* — you did pay for it, just at the low off-peak rate. The two strategies can run on the same battery on the same day: solar fills it when the sun's out, and the grid tops it off overnight if the panels came up short. But the arbitrage case stands entirely on its own, even for a battery with no solar attached at all, as long as the rate spread is wide enough to make the trade profitable. That's an important point for anyone considering a battery without panels — the economics don't depend on generation, only on the gap between cheap and expensive hours. The distinction between these operating strategies, and how a battery decides which one it's running at any given moment, is laid out in [battery operating modes decoded](/blog/solar-battery-operating-modes/).

Here's the catch that turns a clean idea into an arithmetic problem: a battery doesn't give back everything you put into it. Pushing energy in and pulling it back out both cost a little, lost as heat in the power electronics and the chemistry itself. Round-trip efficiency — energy out divided by energy in — typically runs around 90% for a modern home battery, so 10 kWh charged in yields only about 9 kWh back out. That missing tenth means you have to *buy more* cheap kilowatt-hours than you'll actually avoid buying at peak, which shrinks the gross spread into a smaller net one. You're paying the off-peak rate on the full amount you charge, but you only get to displace peak power with the smaller amount you can discharge. The full mechanics of that loss, and why it hides quietly in the spec sheet where buyers rarely look, are in [round-trip efficiency](/blog/battery-round-trip-efficiency/).

The rule that falls out of the loss is simple and worth memorizing: arbitrage only makes sense when the price gap between cheap and expensive hours is wide enough to survive the round-trip haircut and still leave a worthwhile margin. A wide spread easily absorbs the 10% loss and leaves real money on the table; a narrow spread gets mostly eaten by the loss, and what's left may not justify the wear you're putting on the battery to earn it. Everything else about the strategy is downstream of that one comparison — how big is the spread, and does it clear the loss with room to spare.

One honesty check belongs at the front of the whole exercise: confirm that charging a battery from the grid to arbitrage it is actually permitted and worthwhile under your utility's rules and your system's incentives. Some programs and interconnection agreements — particularly where a battery was installed with the help of a solar-linked incentive — restrict or discourage charging storage from the grid, and a few rate structures make grid-charging economically pointless even where it's allowed. Pulling power from the grid to store also means paying for it on top of whatever fixed and delivery charges your tariff attaches, which can quietly shave the spread further than the round-trip loss alone. None of this is a reason to abandon the idea, but it's a reason to read your rate schedule and your interconnection terms before you count on the savings, rather than assuming a battery is free to buy and hoard cheap power at will.

The size and shape of the pricing windows themselves also shift with the season and the utility, which changes how often the play is even available. Many time-of-use tariffs run a wider peak-to-off-peak spread in summer, when air-conditioning demand strains the grid in the late afternoon, and a gentler spread in the milder months. Some layer in a short "super-peak" window with an especially punishing rate, which is exactly the slice a battery is most valuable at avoiding. Because the spread drives everything, a strategy that clearly pays in July on a summer tariff might barely pay in October on the same account, and the honest version of arbitrage adjusts to that rather than running the battery on autopilot year-round.

## Running a full cycle with real numbers

Numbers make the rule concrete, so work an illustrative cycle with every figure treated as an assumption you'd replace with your own tariff. Picture a wide-spread tariff first, the kind where arbitrage clearly earns its keep.

| Window | Assumed rate | Battery action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Off-peak (overnight) | $0.12 / kWh | Charge |
| Peak (4–9 p.m.) | $0.45 / kWh | Discharge |

The gross spread is $0.33 per kWh, which looks enormous before losses. Now apply the 90% round-trip efficiency. To deliver 9 kWh during the peak window, you have to charge 10 kWh overnight at $0.12, spending $1.20. Those 9 kWh you get back displace peak power worth 9 × $0.45, or $4.05. The net gain for the cycle is $4.05 − $1.20 = $2.85, which works out to about $0.32 saved per kilowatt-hour actually delivered. Run that most days of the year on a battery cycling roughly 10 kWh, and it adds up to real money — on the order of several hundred dollars annually under these placeholder rates. The loss took a bite, but a $0.33 spread is more than wide enough to shrug it off, and the strategy is plainly worth running.

Now shrink the spread to test the rule from the other direction, because this is where most homeowners actually live. Suppose off-peak is $0.18 and peak is $0.28 — a gentler tariff with only a $0.10 gross gap.

| Window | Assumed rate | Battery action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Off-peak | $0.18 / kWh | Charge |
| Peak | $0.28 / kWh | Discharge |

Charging 10 kWh now costs $1.80 to deliver 9 kWh worth 9 × $0.28, or $2.52. The net is $0.72, barely $0.08 per delivered kilowatt-hour. That thin margin may not justify the extra daily cycle once you account for the wear it puts on the battery, and it's a fraction of what the wide-spread case earned from the same physical effort. The two examples use the same battery, the same 90% efficiency, and the same daily routine; the only thing that changed was the spread, and it swung the outcome from clearly worthwhile to arguably not worth the bother. That's the whole lesson in two tables: the spread, not the battery, decides whether arbitrage pays.

The battery's usable capacity sets the ceiling on how much any of this can earn, which is the other half of the sizing question. The per-kilowatt-hour margin only matters multiplied by the number of kilowatt-hours you can actually shift each day, and that's limited by the battery's usable capacity and how deeply you're willing to cycle it. A 10 kWh battery discharged from full to near-empty every evening moves far more value than the same battery kept at a gentle depth of discharge to protect its life. So the real annual number is the daily net margin times the kilowatt-hours cycled times the number of days the play is worth running — and each of those three terms is something you control or can estimate. A wide spread on a large battery cycled daily is a genuinely worthwhile line of savings; a thin spread on a small battery cycled cautiously is a rounding error dressed up as a strategy.

Your own tariff is the only one that matters, so the practical move is to find your actual peak and off-peak rates on a recent bill and run this same two-step calculation — charge cost against displaced peak value, after the efficiency haircut. The [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) lets you drop your own rates in and see which side of the line you fall on, and the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) folds the result into the battery's overall return so you can judge whether arbitrage meaningfully changes the payback or just nibbles at the edges. A battery bought mainly for backup that also happens to earn a little arbitrage on the side is a different proposition from one bought specifically to trade a wide daily spread, and the numbers tell you which situation you're actually in.

## When arbitrage gives way to solar, and what it costs the battery

If you have solar, the battery can't be in two places at once, and its most valuable job usually isn't grid arbitrage at all — it's holding your own free solar for the evening peak. Discharging stored solar during peak avoids that expensive power at *zero* input cost, rather than the off-peak rate you'd pay to charge from the grid. On a sunny day, filling the battery from the panels beats filling it from the grid every single time, because free beats cheap by exactly the off-peak rate. So on days when solar can fill the battery, solar wins the battery's capacity outright, and grid arbitrage doesn't get a look in.

Arbitrage earns its place on the days and in the seasons when solar can't fill the battery: overcast stretches, short winter days when the panels barely cover daytime load, or a home whose evening consumption outruns what the panels managed to bank. On those days the choice isn't solar-versus-arbitrage — the solar simply isn't there — it's cheap grid power stored overnight versus expensive grid power bought at peak, which is the arbitrage case in its pure form. Many systems handle this coordination automatically, prioritizing solar whenever it's available and only reaching for cheap grid power to top up when the sun falls short. That layered logic — solar first, off-peak grid second, peak grid avoided — is the coordination covered in [time-of-use rates and solar](/blog/time-of-use-rates-solar/), and it's why a well-configured system quietly does the right thing without you scheduling anything by hand.

Setting this up is mostly a matter of configuring the battery once rather than managing it daily, which is worth knowing before the strategy sounds like a part-time job. Modern home batteries let you define the time-of-use windows and a target strategy, after which the system charges, holds, and discharges on its own according to the rules you set, drawing from solar first and cheap grid power second without you touching it. The homeowner's job is the up-front thinking — reading the tariff, deciding whether the spread justifies grid-charging at all, and setting the windows correctly — not standing at the panel flipping switches at 4 p.m. That's a genuine advantage over imagining arbitrage as active trading, but it cuts both ways: because the system runs quietly in the background, a badly chosen configuration can grind the battery down on a thin spread for months without anyone noticing, which is exactly why the up-front math deserves care.

The honest bottom line is that time-of-use arbitrage is a real but modest lever for most homes, not a reason on its own to buy a battery. Where it earns its keep is as a bonus on top of a battery you already wanted for backup or solar self-consumption: on a wide-spread tariff, letting the system also shift cheap grid power into expensive hours adds a few hundred dollars a year for essentially no extra effort once it's configured. Where the spread is narrow, the sensible move is to let the battery focus on backup and storing your own solar and skip the grid-charging entirely, sparing the cycles. Buying a battery purely to arbitrage a marginal spread rarely pencils out, because the savings are too thin to cover the battery's cost and the cycles you'd spend chasing them. As a rider on a purchase justified by other benefits, though, a well-configured arbitrage strategy on the right tariff is close to free money, and that's the frame that keeps expectations honest.

The footnote that keeps the whole strategy honest is wear. Every arbitrage cycle is a charge and a discharge the battery wouldn't otherwise perform, and batteries age by cycles as much as by years. Cycling hard every single day to chase a thin spread trades warranty life for marginal savings — a genuinely bad deal when the spread is small, because you're spending a finite resource, the battery's cycle life, to earn pennies. When the gap is wide, the calculus flips: the savings comfortably outrun the incremental wear, and running the play daily is clearly correct. The honest version of arbitrage weighs the daily gain against the cost of a cycle every time, and only runs the trade when the spread makes it lopsided in your favor. In the wide-spread example, cycling daily is easy to justify; in the narrow one, you may be quietly buying pennies with battery life you'll wish you'd saved. That's the difference between using a battery as a smart tool and grinding it down for a rounding error — and the spread, once again, is the number that tells you which one you're doing.
