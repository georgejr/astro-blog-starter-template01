A power purchase agreement is usually sold as the easy door into solar: no money down, panels on your roof next month, a lower electric bill starting the day the system switches on. Every one of those claims can be literally true, which is exactly why the pitch lands so well — and exactly why it's worth slowing down before you sign. A PPA isn't a way to buy solar cheaply. It's a way to *not buy solar at all*, while still using the power the panels make. Getting that distinction straight is the whole game, because it decides who owns the equipment on your roof, who pockets the incentives, and how much you actually come out ahead over the twenty-plus years the contract runs.

The single most common misconception is that a PPA is "free solar," or that it saves roughly as much as owning a system would. Neither holds up under any real examination. With a PPA you trade away the upfront cost and the ownership upside in exchange for simplicity and near-zero risk — a genuinely good deal for some households and a distinctly thin one for others. Which kind of household you are depends on details the sales conversation tends to skip, so it's worth walking through how the money actually flows before the low first-year number does your thinking for you.

## "Free solar" isn't what a PPA is

Under a PPA, a third-party company — the developer or the financier behind the deal — pays for, owns, and maintains the entire solar system sitting on your roof. You don't buy the equipment, you don't own it, and you're not responsible for keeping it running. Instead, you sign an agreement to buy the *electricity* those panels produce, at an agreed price per kilowatt-hour, for the length of the contract, which commonly runs 20 to 25 years. The panels are theirs; the power is what you're purchasing.

So your "cost" under a PPA isn't a system price at all — there's no equipment for you to put a price tag on. It's a rate, measured in dollars per kilowatt-hour, applied to however much electricity the system happens to generate. If the panels make 9,000 kWh this year, you pay the PPA company for 9,000 kWh at your contract rate, and you buy whatever additional electricity your home needs from the utility as you always have. The appeal, and it's a real one, is that this contract rate is set below your current utility rate, so from the very first month your combined bill — the PPA payment plus your reduced utility bill — is designed to come out lower than your old all-utility bill. Nothing here is free, though. You've simply swapped one electricity supplier for another that happens to live on your roof, and you're still paying for every kilowatt-hour you consume; some of them now just come from a different vendor at a different price.

It helps to know who's on the other side of the deal and why they want it. The owner of a PPA system is typically a developer or a financier — sometimes the same company that installs it, often a financing partner standing behind the installer. Their business is built on monetizing the things a homeowner frequently can't: the federal tax credit, the depreciation a business owner of solar equipment can claim, and the economies of owning many systems at once. They capture that value at scale, hand a slice of it back to you as a below-market electricity rate, and keep the rest as their return. That's not a trick; it's the entire reason a PPA can offer a lower bill with nothing down. But it clarifies what you are and aren't getting. You're getting cheaper electricity and freedom from maintenance, because the system's upkeep, repairs, and monitoring are the owner's problem rather than yours. You're not getting an asset. At no point do the panels become yours, you build no equity in them, and there's nothing on your side of the ledger that appreciates or that you could sell. Compare that to buying, where the system is a capital asset you own outright, one that can add value to your home and keeps saving you money long after it's paid for itself. The PPA converts that ownership upside into someone else's return in exchange for removing every barrier to entry. For a household that lacks the tax appetite, the cash, or the stomach for maintenance to capture the ownership upside anyway, trading away a benefit you couldn't have used is a reasonable deal. For a household that could have captured it, the PPA is quietly handing a financier value that might have been yours.

Notice, too, what "no money down" does to the comparison in your head. Removing the upfront cost doesn't make solar free; it moves the cost into a stream of payments you'll make for two decades. A thing with no sticker price still has a price — it's just spread out and, with an escalator attached, growing. Keeping that in view is what stops "nothing down" from doing your thinking for you.

That structure makes a PPA a close cousin of a solar lease, and the two get confused constantly because they're built from the same idea: a third party owns the hardware and you pay to benefit from it. The practical difference is how you're billed. A PPA charges you per kilowatt-hour the system produces — pay for what it makes. A lease charges you a fixed monthly rent for the equipment regardless of production — pay a flat fee to have it. Both sit on the opposite side of a bright line from ownership, and how all three arrangements stack up against actually buying is worked through in [leasing versus buying solar](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/). For the purposes of understanding a PPA's cost, the thing to hold onto is that you're buying a stream of power at a rate, not a system at a price — and a rate can move over time in ways a purchase price never does.

## How the price climbs, and who gets the tax credit

The starting PPA rate is always pitched as a discount to your current utility rate, and it usually is one on day one. The clause that decides the long-run value, and the one that deserves the most scrutiny, is the escalator: a provision that raises your PPA rate by a set percentage every single year for the life of the contract. A common escalator runs 2–3% a year, though some contracts are offered flat with no escalator at all. That difference is enormous, and it's easy to miss because both versions produce an identical, attractive first-year bill.

The escalator is the number to interrogate because it compounds, quietly, year after year, in the background of a contract you signed and forgot. A rate that starts comfortably below your utility rate can drift steadily upward, and whether it stays a bargain depends entirely on how your *utility's* rates move over the same two decades — something neither you nor the PPA company can actually predict. If utility rates happen to rise faster than your escalator, the PPA keeps saving you money and looks smarter every year. If your escalator outruns utility inflation, the gap between the two narrows, and late in the contract it can even invert, leaving you paying more for the power off your own roof than you'd have paid the grid for the same electricity. So run the mental model before you sign: take the starting rate, apply the escalator across the full term year by year, and set that rising line against a realistic guess for where utility rates are headed. A PPA with a low or zero escalator is a fundamentally different financial product from one climbing 3% a year, even when the first-year bill on the two looks identical on the page. The whole value of the deal is hiding in a single percentage most buyers glance past.

There's a genuine upside buried in the PPA structure that's worth stating fairly, because the per-kilowatt-hour model isn't all downside. Since you pay only for the electricity the system actually produces, the risk of underproduction largely sits with the owner rather than with you. If the panels make less than projected in a cloudy year, you buy fewer kilowatt-hours from them and simply draw a bit more from the grid; you aren't stuck servicing a loan on a system that underdelivered. Some PPAs go further and attach an explicit production guarantee, compensating you if output falls short of a stated figure. That transfer of performance risk is a real feature, and it's part of what the arrangement offers in exchange for the ownership upside you're giving up. The flip side is that you're equally locked into paying for whatever the system does produce, at your contracted and escalating rate — you can't opt out of the good years to dodge the escalator. So the structure cuts both ways: less downside risk from a weak system, less freedom to walk away from an expensive contract. Weigh both, not just the reassuring half the pitch emphasizes, and remember that a guarantee is only ever as good as the company standing behind it years from now.

Then there's the part the "just as good as owning" framing quietly leaves out. Because the PPA company owns the system, *it* — not you — claims the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, along with any other incentives that flow to the system's owner. You never touch the credit. The company monetizes those incentives and bakes their value into the low rate it offers you, which is a real part of the mechanism that lets the discount exist in the first place; you do benefit from the credit indirectly, through a lower price. But you don't claim it, you don't control it, and you can't apply it against your own taxes. For a homeowner who *could* have used that credit — someone with the tax liability to absorb it and the cash or financing to buy a system outright — this is the core reason ownership usually wins on lifetime dollars. As an owner, you keep the incentive yourself instead of handing it to a financier who returns a fraction of it through your rate. The credit is substantial enough to reshape the entire comparison, which is why it's worth understanding [how the federal solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/), and checking current IRS guidance for the details, before you decide a PPA's convenience is worth giving it up. The flip side is just as real: if you genuinely *can't* use the credit — too little tax liability, or no appetite for any upfront cost — then letting a company that *can* monetize it pass some of that value back to you through a below-market rate is a perfectly legitimate reason a PPA might fit your situation better than buying would.

## PPA against owning, and the clauses that decide the endgame

Laying the models side by side makes the trade explicit, because a PPA's cost only means something in comparison to the alternatives:

| | Cash purchase | Solar loan | PPA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | High | Low / none | None |
| Who owns the system | You | You | Third party |
| Federal tax credit | You claim it | You claim it | Company claims it |
| Maintenance responsibility | You | You | The company |
| Lifetime savings | Highest | High | Lowest of the three |
| Financial risk to you | Highest | Moderate | Lowest |

The pattern down those columns is consistent and worth reading as a whole rather than row by row. A PPA delivers the least lifetime savings and carries the least risk. Ownership delivers the most savings and demands the most responsibility, both financial and practical. A solar loan sits in between — you own the system and claim the credit while spreading the cost over time, a genuine middle path examined in [how paying cash or financing changes the payback](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/). None of these three is universally "best," and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling one of them. A PPA suits someone who wants solar's bill relief without capital to spare, tax appetite to use, or any interest in maintenance obligations. Ownership suits someone optimizing squarely for total dollars saved and willing to put money down or take on a loan to get there. The right answer is a function of your finances, not a feature of the product.

The home-sale friction deserves more than a passing mention, because it's where PPAs most often surprise people. When you sell, the buyer typically has to qualify for and assume the remaining contract, which means a credit check and a willingness to inherit a decades-long power agreement they didn't choose. Plenty of buyers are fine with it; some balk, and a deal can stall while everyone works out whether the PPA transfers, whether the seller buys it out, or whether the price adjusts to account for it. A buyout, if that's the path, is priced by the PPA company and isn't always cheap. None of this makes a PPA a bad choice — it makes it a commitment worth understanding as a commitment, one that follows the house rather than the person who signed it. If there's any real chance you'll move well before the term ends, read the transfer and buyout language with particular care, and picture the conversation you'd have with a future buyer's agent. The savings a PPA delivers are real but modest and front-loaded with reassurance; the obligations it creates are real too, and they tend to surface at exactly the moments — selling, refinancing — when you'd least want a surprise.

Two parts of a PPA contract deserve reading twice, because they bite years down the road when the salesperson is long gone. The first is the end-of-term options, which define what happens when the agreement finally expires. Typically you'll be able to renew the contract, have the company remove the system from your roof, or buy the aging equipment at a depreciated price. Know which of those options you'll actually have, because a system approaching the end of a 25-year term isn't worth anywhere near what a new one is, and "buy it out" can mean very different things depending on how the depreciation was written. The second, and arguably the more important, is the transfer terms at home sale, because the contract is attached to the property rather than to you personally. When you sell the house, the buyer generally must either assume the PPA or you must buy it out to clear it. A prospective buyer who has no interest in inheriting a 20-year power contract tied to a house they're purchasing can genuinely complicate a sale, so you want to understand the transfer and buyout language long before you're the one standing at a closing table holding it. Because a PPA has no system price to shop against, comparing it fairly to owning means comparing streams of payments rather than sticker prices. To make that comparison concrete, price out what buying the same system would cost using the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), subtract the federal tax credit you'd keep as an owner, and weigh that net figure against the full sum of PPA payments across the term with the escalator applied. The two paths rarely come out equal — and seeing the gap laid out in actual dollars, rather than a reassuring first-year bill, is the surest way to know whether a PPA's convenience is worth what it quietly costs you in lifetime savings.
