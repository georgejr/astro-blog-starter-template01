A power purchase agreement is often sold as the easy door into solar: no money down, panels on your roof, a lower electric bill from day one. All of that can be literally true, which is exactly why the pitch works — and why it's worth slowing down. A PPA isn't a way to buy solar cheaply. It's a way to *not* buy solar at all, while still using the power it makes. Understanding that distinction is the whole game, because it determines who owns the system, who claims the incentives, and how much you actually save over twenty-plus years.

The single most common misconception is that a PPA is "free solar" or that it saves about as much as owning a system does. Neither holds up. With a PPA you trade the upfront cost and the ownership upside for simplicity and zero risk — a real deal for some households, a thin one for others. Here's how the money actually flows.

## "Free solar" isn't what a PPA is

Under a PPA, a third-party company — the developer or financier — pays for, owns, and maintains the solar system on your roof. You don't buy the equipment. Instead, you agree to buy the *electricity* those panels produce, at an agreed price per kilowatt-hour, for the length of the contract (commonly 20 to 25 years).

So your "cost" isn't a system price at all. It's a rate — dollars per kilowatt-hour — applied to however much the system generates. If the panels make 9,000 kWh this year, you pay the PPA company for 9,000 kWh at your contract rate, and you buy whatever else you need from the utility as usual. The appeal is that this rate is set below your current utility rate, so from month one your combined bill is meant to drop. Nothing is free; you've simply swapped one electricity supplier (partly) for another that happens to sit on your roof.

This makes a PPA a close cousin of a solar lease. The practical differences between the two, and how both compare to buying, are worked through in [leasing versus buying solar](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/) — a PPA is the "pay per kWh" flavor, a lease is the "pay a fixed monthly rent" flavor of the same third-party-ownership idea.

## How the price is set, and why it climbs

The starting PPA rate is pitched as a discount to your utility rate. The catch that decides the long-run value is the **escalator**: a clause that raises your PPA rate by a set percentage every year for the life of the contract. A common escalator runs 2–3% a year, though some contracts are offered flat.

That escalator is the number to interrogate, because it compounds. A rate that starts below your utility rate can, with a steady annual escalator, drift upward year after year — and whether it stays a bargain depends entirely on how your *utility's* rates move over the same period. If utility rates rise faster than your escalator, the PPA keeps saving you money. If your escalator outruns utility inflation, the gap narrows and can even invert late in the contract, leaving you paying more for the roof's power than you'd pay the grid.

Run the mental model before signing: take the starting rate, apply the escalator across the full term, and compare that rising line against a realistic guess for utility rates. A PPA with a low or zero escalator is a fundamentally different product from one climbing 3% a year, even if the first-year bill looks identical.

## Who gets the tax credit (not you)

Here's the part the "just as good as owning" framing quietly omits. Because the PPA company owns the system, *it* — not you — claims the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit and any other owner incentives. Those incentives are already baked into the low rate they offer you, which is the mechanism that makes the discount possible. But you never touch the credit yourself.

For a homeowner who could use the credit — who has the tax liability to absorb it and the cash or financing to buy — this is the core reason ownership usually wins on lifetime dollars: you keep the incentive instead of handing it to a financier. The credit is substantial enough to reshape the entire comparison, which is why it's worth understanding [how the federal solar tax credit works](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/) before deciding a PPA's convenience is worth giving it up. If you *can't* use the credit — too little tax liability, or no appetite for the upfront cost — then letting a company that can monetize it pass some of that value back to you is a legitimate reason a PPA might fit.

## A PPA against owning: the honest trade

Laying the models side by side clarifies what you're actually choosing between:

| | Cash purchase | Solar loan | PPA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | High | Low / none | None |
| Who owns the system | You | You | Third party |
| Federal tax credit | You claim it | You claim it | Company claims it |
| Maintenance responsibility | You | You | The company |
| Lifetime savings | Highest | High | Lowest of the three |
| Financial risk to you | Highest | Moderate | Lowest |

The pattern is consistent: a PPA delivers the least lifetime savings and the least risk, ownership delivers the most savings and takes on the most responsibility, and a loan sits between — you own the system and claim the credit while spreading the cost, a middle path examined in [how paying cash or financing changes the payback](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/). None of these is universally "best." A PPA suits someone who wants solar's bill relief without capital, tax appetite, or maintenance obligations. Ownership suits someone optimizing for total dollars saved.

## The clauses that decide the endgame

Two parts of a PPA contract deserve reading twice, because they bite years down the road.

The **end-of-term options** define what happens when the agreement expires: typically you can renew, have the company remove the system, or buy the aging equipment at a depreciated price. Know which options you'll have, since a system approaching the end of a 25-year term isn't worth what a new one is.

The **transfer terms at home sale** matter even more, because the contract is attached to the property. When you sell, the buyer generally must assume the PPA or you must buy it out. A prospective buyer who doesn't want a 20-year power contract tied to the house can complicate a sale, so understand the transfer and buyout language before you're the one holding it.

Because a PPA has no system price to shop, comparing it to owning means comparing streams of payments, not sticker prices. To make that comparison concrete, price out what buying the same system would cost with the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/), subtract the tax credit you'd keep as an owner, and weigh that against the sum of PPA payments over the term. The two paths rarely come out equal — and seeing the gap in dollars is the surest way to know whether the PPA's convenience is worth what it costs you in savings.
