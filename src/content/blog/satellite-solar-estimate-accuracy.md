Type your address into a solar mapping tool and, in seconds, it hands back a roof outline, a panel count, and an annual production figure that looks authoritative down to the kilowatt-hour. It is tempting to treat that number as the answer. It is better understood as a well-informed first draft. Satellite estimates are genuinely useful and, for many roofs, close to right — but the belief that they settle the question is where homeowners get tripped up, usually when a later site visit revises the number in a direction they did not expect. Knowing which parts of that instant estimate to trust, and which to hold loosely, is the difference between a useful screening tool and a false sense of certainty.

## What the satellite view actually gets right

The data feeding these tools is better than most people assume. Aerial and satellite imagery, combined with elevation models that capture roof pitch and, increasingly, LiDAR that measures nearby structures and tree height, lets a good algorithm estimate three of the four things that matter: your roof's size, its orientation, and its tilt. Layer in decades of local irradiance data — the same peak-sun-hour records that underpin any honest production estimate, and that [a roof suitability assessment](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) leans on as well — and the model can produce a credible annual output figure without anyone leaving their desk.

For a simple roof, this works well. A large, rectangular, south-facing plane with no trees nearby and a common pitch is exactly the case these tools handle best, because it is the case with the fewest hidden variables. On that kind of roof, a satellite estimate of usable area and annual production often lands within roughly 10% of what a full survey concludes. The simpler and more open your roof, the more you can trust the screen. Orientation and tilt are geometric facts that imagery and elevation data capture cleanly, and those two variables, together with your location's sun hours, drive most of the production number for an unshaded roof. When there is nothing to hide behind — no dense canopy, no towering neighbor, no cut-up roofline — the software is working with almost everything it needs.

It also helps to know that "satellite estimate" covers a wide range of tools with different purposes and different accuracy, and treating them as interchangeable is part of how people get misled. A free consumer mapping estimator like Project Sunroof is designed to give a homeowner a fast, motivating ballpark. It is deliberately broad, and Project Sunroof accuracy is best read as "is my roof plausibly worth pursuing," not "here is my exact production." Installer-facing design platforms sit at the other end. They pull in higher-resolution imagery, let a human adjust the panel layout by hand, and often incorporate a modeled sun path, so their output is closer to a survey even though it is still built from imagery rather than a visit. Between those poles sit the estimating tools built into many installers' online quote flows, which vary widely in how much human judgment goes into them.

The precision a tool displays is not the same as the accuracy it has, and confusing the two is the single most common mistake. Any of these will happily report a figure to the nearest kilowatt-hour, but that last digit is false confidence dressed up as rigor. A number can be specific and wrong at the same time. Treat the tool's *category* as a guide to how much weight the number deserves, and remember that the free, instant estimate is the roughest of the bunch by design — it is meant to get you interested, not to size a contract.

## Where the estimate drifts

The gap opens up on the fourth variable — shading — and on the messy physical details that overhead imagery flattens into a clean rectangle.

Shading is the hardest thing to model from above and the thing that most affects real output, which is an unfortunate combination. An algorithm can see a tree's footprint and estimate its height, but it struggles with a tree's density: a bare winter canopy that lets light filter through behaves nothing like the same tree in full summer leaf. It struggles with how a shadow marches across the array hour by hour, clipping the east end in the morning and the west end in the afternoon. And it struggles with obstructions that barely register from directly overhead — a neighbor's two-story addition, a tall chimney, a vent stack, a power line — because from a satellite's straight-down vantage a vertical object casts almost no visible footprint even when it throws a long shadow at ground level.

Shading losses are non-linear, too, which is what makes them so easy to underestimate. A shadow across even part of one panel can drag down more production than its size suggests, depending on how the array is wired and whether it uses panel-level electronics. That is why a modeled figure that ignores a real obstruction can be optimistic by a wide margin rather than a small one. A proper [shading analysis](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/) exists precisely because this is the variable software handles worst, and it is the variable most likely to separate a rosy estimate from a disappointing meter reading a year later.

Several other things routinely separate the satellite number from the buildable one:

- **Usable area versus total area.** Fire-code setbacks, plumbing vents, skylights, and the walking paths crews need all shrink the roof. A tool may show more panels than code and equipment will actually allow, because it sees the roof as a shape rather than as a permitted work surface.
- **Roof condition and structure.** Imagery cannot tell whether your roof is near the end of its life or whether the framing can carry the array. Those are cost and feasibility questions a photo never answers, and both can turn a viable roof into one that needs work first.
- **Stale imagery.** The picture may predate a new dormer, a solar-blocking addition next door, or a tree that has grown ten feet since the flyover. You are estimating on yesterday's roof, not today's.
- **Electrical reality.** Panel location, main service capacity, and the run back to your service panel are invisible from space and can change both layout and cost, sometimes significantly if an electrical upgrade turns out to be necessary.

None of these are exotic edge cases. A complex roofline with several planes, a mature tree on the south side, and an older electrical panel is an ordinary American house, and it is exactly the kind of property where the instant estimate and the buildable reality can part ways by a meaningful margin.

## Why the number usually moves down, not up

When a site survey revises a satellite figure, it more often trims it than raises it, and the reason is structural rather than a matter of pessimism. The things the tools miss almost all subtract. Unmodeled shade removes production. Setbacks and obstructions remove panels. Imagery rarely reveals *more* usable roof than it showed, because it already showed the whole roof. The occasional upward surprise does happen — a plane the model treated as shaded turns out to clear earlier in the day than expected — but the asymmetry is real, and it is worth carrying into any quote built purely from an address.

This is also why installers who quote entirely from satellite data, without a survey, tend to produce the rosiest proposals. The number is not dishonest; it is just the best case before the physical constraints get counted. A proposal built from an address describes the roof the software can see, and the software cannot see the vent stack, the aging shingles, or the neighbor's new second story. Treating that first figure as a ceiling rather than an expectation keeps you from anchoring on a number that a ladder and a shade meter may later revise downward — and it keeps you from feeling misled when they do.

The practical hazard is anchoring. Once a homeowner has been shown a specific annual production figure and a specific dollar savings, that number becomes the mental baseline, and a later, lower survey figure feels like a bait and switch even when it is simply the truth catching up. Going in with the expectation that the honest number is at or below the instant estimate, not above it, inverts that dynamic. A survey that confirms the ballpark is good news; a survey that trims it is the system working as intended.

## Using a satellite estimate for what it is good at

The right move is not to distrust these tools but to use them for what they genuinely do well. A satellite estimate is an excellent screening step. It tells you quickly whether your roof is roughly in the right size and orientation range to be worth pursuing, and it lets you compare scenarios — a bigger array versus a smaller one, this roof plane versus that one — before anyone visits. That is real value, and it saves everyone time by weeding out roofs that were never going to work and building rough expectations for the ones that will.

Run your address through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) to get a defensible ballpark for system size and production, and use that figure to sanity-check the proposals installers hand you. If a quote's production estimate sits far above your own calculator-based figure with no obvious reason — no unusually favorable roof, no premium panels — that gap is worth a direct question. A number that looks too good on paper usually has an unmodeled obstruction or an optimistic assumption hiding inside it, and the time to surface that is before you sign, not after your first full year of production comes in low.

Then let the [site survey](/blog/solar-site-survey/) do the job it exists for: confirming shade, usable area, roof condition, and electrical capacity, and turning the first draft into a number you can build a contract around. A good surveyor climbs the roof, measures the real setbacks, reads the shade with an instrument rather than a guess, opens the electrical panel, and checks the age and condition of the shingles. The output is not a fancier estimate; it is a commitment. The satellite view narrows the field and gets you to the point of taking solar seriously. The visit settles the answer and gives you something to hold an installer to. Trouble only arrives when the two get confused for each other — when a screening tool is asked to do a surveyor's job, and a homeowner treats an instant ballpark as a promise the roof never actually made.
