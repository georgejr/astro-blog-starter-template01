Type your address into a solar mapping tool and, in seconds, it hands back a roof outline, a panel count, and an annual production figure that looks authoritative down to the kilowatt-hour. It is tempting to treat that number as the answer. It is better understood as a well-informed first draft. Satellite estimates are genuinely useful and, for many roofs, close to right — but the belief that they settle the question is where homeowners get tripped up, usually when a later site visit revises the number in a direction they did not expect.

## What the satellite view gets right

The data feeding these tools is better than most people assume. Aerial and satellite imagery, combined with elevation models that capture roof pitch and, increasingly, LiDAR that measures nearby structures and tree height, lets a good algorithm estimate three of the four things that matter: your roof's size, its orientation, and its tilt. Layer in decades of local irradiance data — the same [peak-sun-hour records](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) that underpin any honest production estimate — and the model can produce a credible annual output figure without anyone leaving their desk.

For a simple roof, this works well. A large, rectangular, south-facing plane with no trees nearby and a common pitch is exactly the case these tools handle best. On that kind of roof, a satellite estimate of usable area and annual production often lands within roughly 10% of what a full survey concludes. The simpler and more open your roof, the more you can trust the screen.

## Not every tool is built for the same job

It also helps to know that "satellite estimate" covers a wide range of tools with different purposes and different accuracy. A free consumer mapping estimator like Project Sunroof is designed to give a homeowner a fast, motivating ballpark — it is deliberately broad, and Project Sunroof accuracy is best read as "is my roof plausibly worth pursuing," not "here is my exact production." Installer-facing design platforms sit at the other end: they pull in higher-resolution imagery, let a human adjust the panel layout by hand, and often incorporate a modeled sun path, so their output is closer to a survey even though it is still built from imagery.

The precision a tool displays is not the same as the accuracy it has. Any of these will happily report a figure to the nearest kilowatt-hour, but that fourth decimal place is false confidence. Treat the tool's *category* as a guide to how much weight the number deserves, and remember that the free, instant estimate is the roughest of the bunch by design.

## Where the estimate drifts

The gap opens up on the fourth variable — shading — and on the messy physical details that imagery flattens.

Shading is the hardest thing to model from above and the thing that most affects real output. An algorithm can see a tree's footprint and estimate its height, but it struggles with a tree's density (a bare winter canopy versus full summer leaf), with how a shadow marches across the array hour by hour, and with obstructions that barely register from directly overhead: a neighbor's two-story addition, a tall chimney, a vent stack, a power line. Shading losses are non-linear, too. A shadow across part of one panel can drag down more production than its size suggests, which is why a modeled figure that ignores a real obstruction can be optimistic by a wide margin. A proper [shading analysis](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/) exists precisely because this is the variable software handles worst.

Several other things routinely separate the satellite number from the buildable one:

- **Usable area versus total area.** Fire-code setbacks, plumbing vents, skylights, and the walking paths crews need all shrink the roof. A tool may show more panels than code and equipment will actually allow.
- **Roof condition and structure.** Imagery cannot tell whether your roof is near the end of its life or whether the framing can carry the array. Those are cost and feasibility questions a photo never answers.
- **Stale imagery.** The picture may predate a new dormer, a solar-blocking addition next door, or a tree that has grown ten feet since the flyover.
- **Electrical reality.** Panel location, main service capacity, and the run back to your service panel are invisible from space and can change both layout and cost.

## Why the estimate usually moves down, not up

When a site survey revises a satellite figure, it more often trims it than raises it. That is not because the tools are pessimistic — it is because the things they miss almost all subtract. Unmodeled shade removes production. Setbacks and obstructions remove panels. Imagery rarely reveals *more* usable roof than it showed. The occasional upward surprise happens — a shaded plane turns out to clear earlier in the day than modeled — but the asymmetry is real, and it is worth carrying into any quote built purely from an address.

This is also why installers who quote entirely from satellite data, without a survey, tend to produce the rosiest proposals. The number is not dishonest; it is just the best case before the physical constraints get counted. Treating it as a ceiling rather than an expectation keeps you from anchoring on a figure that a ladder and a shade meter may later revise.

## How to use a satellite estimate well

The right move is not to distrust these tools but to use them for what they are good at. A satellite estimate is an excellent screening step: it tells you quickly whether your roof is roughly in the right size and orientation range to be worth pursuing, and it lets you compare scenarios before anyone visits. Run your address through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) to get a defensible ballpark for system size and production, and use that figure to sanity-check the proposals installers hand you.

Then let the [site survey](/blog/solar-site-survey/) do the job it exists for: confirming shade, usable area, roof condition, and electrical capacity, and turning the first draft into a number you can build a contract around. The satellite view narrows the field. The visit settles the answer. Trouble only arrives when the two get confused for each other.
