A new EV is the largest single electricity load most households will ever plug in, and the first power bill after it arrives can be a jolt if you have not run the numbers first. The worry that usually comes with it — that home charging will double your bill — is almost always wrong, but not for a comforting reason. It is wrong because the real number, while substantial, is knowable to within a few percent before the car ever shows up. Of all the things that draw power in a house, home charging turns out to be one of the most predictable, and that predictability is the whole reason you can plan for it instead of bracing for it.

## The arithmetic that predicts almost everything

Everything about EV charging usage flows from a single relationship: miles driven, divided by the car's efficiency, gives you the energy at the wheels. Get that one calculation right and you have accounted for the vast majority of what an electric car adds to your meter.

Efficiency is quoted in miles per kWh, and it is the mirror image of the miles-per-gallon figure you already know. Efficient sedans manage roughly 3.5 to 4.5 miles per kWh; heavier SUVs and pickups often land between 2 and 3. To turn energy at the wheels into energy off your wall, add roughly 10% for charging losses — energy that vanishes as heat in the cable, the car's onboard charger, and the battery itself as it fills. That uplift is real and unavoidable; no home charging setup is perfectly efficient, and the wall always has to supply a bit more than the battery ends up holding.

Assume a fairly typical driver: 12,000 miles a year in a car that averages 3.5 miles per kWh. That is about 3,430 kWh at the wheels, or close to 3,800 kWh drawn from the wall once charging losses are counted. Round it and you get the figure most single-EV households should plan around: roughly 3,000 to 4,000 kWh a year, or about 250 to 350 kWh a month. That is the number to anchor on before any of the finer adjustments.

That range shifts with how much you drive and what you drive:

| Annual miles | Efficient car (3.5 mi/kWh) | Truck/SUV (2.5 mi/kWh) |
|---|---|---|
| 6,000 | ~1,900 kWh/yr (~155/mo) | ~2,650 kWh/yr (~220/mo) |
| 12,000 | ~3,800 kWh/yr (~315/mo) | ~5,300 kWh/yr (~440/mo) |
| 18,000 | ~5,650 kWh/yr (~470/mo) | ~7,900 kWh/yr (~660/mo) |

The figures assume a 10% charging-loss uplift over energy at the wheels. Your own mileage and the efficiency on your car's window sticker will move them, but the arithmetic never changes: pick your annual miles, divide by the car's real-world miles per kWh, and add ten percent. You can sharpen the estimate with numbers you already have. If you know roughly how many miles you drive a week, multiply by 52. If you are unsure, a common commuter pattern of 30 to 40 miles a day, five days a week, plus weekend errands, lands most people near that 12,000-mile line without any guesswork.

## Why it feels like more than it is

The average US home uses somewhere around 10,000 to 11,000 kWh a year, though the spread by region and home size is enormous — [average home electricity use](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) breaks down where that lands. Drop a single typical EV onto that baseline and you add roughly a third: a 10,500 kWh household becomes about 14,000. That is a real increase worth planning for, but it is a long way from doubling, and the difference between "a third more" and "twice as much" is the difference between a manageable line item and a reason to hesitate.

The doubling myth persists for two understandable reasons. First, an EV is the rare load people can actually *see* on the meter. A car that visibly plugs in and draws several kilowatts for hours feels like it should cost a fortune, in a way that a water heater cycling invisibly at 2 a.m. never does — even though that water heater may use nearly as much over a year. Second, charging usually happens overnight in one concentrated block, so it shows up as a dramatic spike on any monitoring app. The shape of the load is alarming; the size of it is not. A 7 kW charger running for four hours looks like a wall on the graph, but four hours at 7 kW is 28 kWh, roughly what the rest of a modest home uses in a day. Spread across a month, that block is ordinary.

Four factors explain nearly all the variation between one EV household and the next, and knowing which ones you can influence keeps you from either over-worrying or over-optimizing.

### Vehicle efficiency

This is the biggest lever, and it is set the day you choose the car. A full-size electric pickup can use nearly twice the energy per mile of an efficient sedan, which means it also costs nearly twice as much to fuel at home and calls for nearly twice the solar capacity if you plan to cover it with panels. If you are weighing two vehicles and care about the running cost, the miles-per-kWh figure matters more than any charging decision you will make later. Nothing you do at the outlet recovers the gap between a 2-mile-per-kWh truck and a 4-mile-per-kWh hatchback.

### Climate and season

Cold weather is the quiet tax on EV charging. Batteries are less efficient when cold, and cabin heating draws directly from the pack rather than from the waste engine heat a gas car gives away for free. A car that sips 3.5 miles per kWh in spring may drop toward 2.5 in a hard winter, pushing that month's charging usage up noticeably. Preconditioning the cabin while the car is still plugged in helps, because it heats the interior on grid power rather than on the battery you are trying to preserve for driving. Summer air conditioning costs less than winter heating but is not free either. Expect your charging months to swing much the way the rest of your bill does, with the deepest cold and the fiercest heat showing up as the heaviest months.

### How and where you charge

The 10% loss uplift is an average, not a constant. Level 1 charging from a standard 120-volt wall outlet is slightly less efficient than Level 2 on a dedicated 240-volt circuit, mostly because the car's supporting systems stay awake longer to add the same energy slowly. The difference is a few percent, not a reason to lose sleep, but it is one reason a home Level 2 charger is marginally cheaper per mile as well as far faster. The larger point about charging is timing, not efficiency: if your utility offers a time-of-use rate, moving the overnight charging block into the off-peak window can cut the *cost* of those kWh substantially even though it does nothing to the *number* of kWh. The energy is the same; the price attached to it is not.

### How and where you drive

Highway driving at steady speed is often *less* efficient for an EV than stop-and-go city driving, the reverse of a gasoline car, because regenerative braking recovers energy in traffic that sustained high-speed cruising cannot. A commuter who lives on the interstate will use more kWh per mile than the same car running errands around town. Terrain matters too — a hilly route costs more than a flat one, though some of the climb comes back on the descent. None of this overturns your estimate, but it explains why two drivers with identical cars and identical mileage can land a few hundred kWh apart over a year.

## From kilowatt-hours to a monthly plan

Once you have your annual kWh estimate, two questions usually follow, and both have clean answers.

The first is cost. Multiply your added kWh by your electricity rate. Assume 3,800 kWh a year and a rate of $0.17 per kWh, and the car costs about $645 a year to fuel at home — a labeled assumption you should replace with your own rate. That works out to roughly $54 a month, which is where the comparison against gasoline usually becomes stark: the same miles in a gas car at typical fuel prices often cost two to three times as much. A time-of-use plan can widen that gap further by pricing the overnight kWh well below the daytime rate, so the effective cost per mile of a home-charged EV can undercut its headline rate. This is the calculation that turns "an EV will raise my electric bill" into "an EV will raise my electric bill and lower my total energy spending," which is the honest version of the story.

The second question is solar. If you are sizing or resizing an array to cover the car, the rule is refreshingly direct: add your EV's annual kWh to your household total and size for the combined figure. Do not think of it as a separate solar project bolted onto the first — think of it as one larger number. A single EV typically calls for something in the range of 8 to 12 extra panels depending on your sun and the panels' wattage, though a heavy-driving household or an inefficient truck can push past that. [How many extra panels an EV really requires](/blog/solar-panels-for-ev-charging/) works through that sizing case by case, and [charging an EV with solar](/blog/ev-charging-solar-savings/) covers what it does to your savings, including the wrinkle that most charging happens at night when the panels are dark, so the economics lean on net metering or a battery rather than on charging directly from the sun. To put real numbers behind your own roof, the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) and the [panel-count estimator](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) let you fold the car's load straight into the total instead of treating it as an afterthought.

One practical habit makes all of this easier: before the car arrives, read a year of your own electric bills and note the annual kWh total. Add your EV estimate to it, and you have both your new expected usage and the target any solar system would need to hit. That single number — old usage plus roughly 3,000 to 4,000 kWh for a typical EV — is the foundation for every decision that follows, from whether to install a Level 2 charger to how big an array to quote.

## Common questions about EV charging and your bill

**How many kWh does an EV add per month?**
For most single-car households, roughly 250 to 350 kWh a month, based on around 12,000 miles a year in a reasonably efficient car. Heavy drivers or large electric trucks can push past 500. Divide your annual mileage by the car's miles per kWh and add about ten percent to get your own figure.

**Will charging an EV double my electric bill?**
Almost never. A typical EV adds around a third to an average home's annual usage, not 100%. The load looks dramatic on a monitoring app because it arrives in one overnight block, but the monthly total is moderate, and on a time-of-use rate the cost of those kWh can be lower than the rest of your bill.

**Does an EV mean I need a much bigger solar system?**
Bigger, not dramatically so. Adding one EV's annual kWh usually means eight to twelve more panels. Size the array for your household total plus the car's estimated draw and treat it as one combined number rather than a second, separate system.

**Is Level 2 charging cheaper than a regular outlet?**
Slightly, and much faster. Level 2 wastes a few percent less energy because the car finishes sooner and its support systems run for less time. The bigger reason to install it is convenience and the ability to schedule charging into off-peak hours, with efficiency a small bonus.
