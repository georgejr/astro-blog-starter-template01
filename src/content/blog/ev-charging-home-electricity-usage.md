A new EV is the largest single electricity load most households will ever plug in, and the first power bill after it arrives can be a jolt if you have not run the numbers first. The worry that usually comes with it — that home charging will double your bill — is almost always wrong, but not for a comforting reason. It is wrong because the real number, while substantial, is knowable to within a few percent before the car ever shows up. Home charging turns out to be one of the most predictable loads in the house.

## The one equation that predicts almost everything

Everything about EV charging usage flows from a single relationship: miles driven, divided by the car's efficiency, gives you the energy at the wheels.

Efficiency is quoted in miles per kWh. Efficient sedans manage roughly 3.5 to 4.5 miles per kWh; heavier SUVs and pickups often land between 2 and 3. To turn energy at the wheels into energy off your wall, add roughly 10% for charging losses — energy lost as heat in the cable, onboard charger, and battery while it fills.

Assume a fairly typical driver: 12,000 miles a year in a car that averages 3.5 miles per kWh. That is about 3,430 kWh at the wheels, or close to 3,800 kWh drawn from the wall once charging losses are counted. Round it and you get the figure most single-EV households should plan around: roughly 3,000 to 4,000 kWh a year, or about 250 to 350 kWh a month.

That range shifts with how much you drive and what you drive:

| Annual miles | Efficient car (3.5 mi/kWh) | Truck/SUV (2.5 mi/kWh) |
|---|---|---|
| 6,000 | ~1,900 kWh/yr (~155/mo) | ~2,650 kWh/yr (~220/mo) |
| 12,000 | ~3,800 kWh/yr (~315/mo) | ~5,300 kWh/yr (~440/mo) |
| 18,000 | ~5,650 kWh/yr (~470/mo) | ~7,900 kWh/yr (~660/mo) |

The figures assume a 10% charging-loss uplift over energy at the wheels. Your own mileage and the efficiency on your car's window sticker will move them, but the arithmetic never changes.

## Why the number feels bigger than it is

The average US home uses somewhere around 10,000 to 11,000 kWh a year, though the spread by region and home size is enormous — [average home electricity use](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/) breaks down where that lands. Drop a single typical EV onto that baseline and you add roughly a third: a 10,500 kWh household becomes about 14,000. That is a real increase worth planning for, but it is a long way from doubling.

The doubling myth persists for two understandable reasons. First, an EV is the rare load people can actually *see* on the meter — a car that visibly plugs in and draws power for hours feels like it should cost a fortune, in a way that a water heater cycling invisibly at 2 a.m. never does. Second, charging often happens overnight in one concentrated block, so it shows up as a dramatic spike on any monitoring app even though the monthly total is moderate. The shape of the load is alarming; the size of it is not.

## What actually moves your number

Four factors explain nearly all the variation between one EV household and the next.

### Vehicle efficiency

This is the biggest lever, and it is set the day you choose the car. A full-size electric pickup can use nearly twice the energy per mile of an efficient sedan. If you are weighing two vehicles and care about the running cost, the miles-per-kWh figure matters more than any charging decision you will make later.

### Climate and season

Cold weather is the quiet tax on EV charging. Batteries are less efficient when cold, and cabin heating draws directly from the pack rather than from waste engine heat. A car that sips 3.5 miles per kWh in spring may drop toward 2.5 in a hard winter, pushing that month's charging usage up noticeably. Summer air conditioning costs less than winter heating but is not free either. Expect your charging months to swing much the way the rest of your bill does.

### Charging losses

The 10% uplift is an average, not a constant. Level 1 charging from a standard wall outlet is slightly less efficient than Level 2 on a dedicated circuit, mostly because the car's supporting systems stay awake longer to add the same energy slowly. The difference is a few percent, not a reason to lose sleep, but it is one reason a home Level 2 charger is marginally cheaper per mile as well as far faster.

### How and where you drive

Highway driving at steady speed is often *less* efficient for an EV than stop-and-go city driving, the reverse of a gasoline car, because regenerative braking recovers energy in traffic that sustained high-speed cruising cannot. A commuter who lives on the interstate will use more kWh per mile than the same car running errands around town.

## Turning kWh into a plan

Once you have your annual kWh estimate, two questions usually follow.

The first is cost. Multiply your added kWh by your electricity rate. Assume 3,800 kWh a year and a rate of $0.17 per kWh, and the car costs about $645 a year to fuel at home — a labeled assumption you should replace with your own rate, and one that time-of-use plans can cut substantially by shifting charging to off-peak hours. That comparison against gasoline is usually where an EV's running-cost advantage shows up.

The second question is solar. If you are sizing or resizing an array to cover the car, the rule is refreshingly direct: add your EV's annual kWh to your household total and size for the combined figure. A single EV typically calls for something in the range of 8 to 12 extra panels depending on your sun and the panels' wattage — [how many extra panels an EV really requires](/blog/solar-panels-for-ev-charging/) works through that sizing, and [charging an EV with solar](/blog/ev-charging-solar-savings/) covers what it does to your savings. To put real numbers behind your own roof, the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) and the [panel-count estimator](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) let you fold the car's load straight into the total.

## FAQ

**How many kWh does an EV add per month?**
For most single-car households, roughly 250 to 350 kWh a month, based on around 12,000 miles a year in a reasonably efficient car. Heavy drivers or large electric trucks can push past 500.

**Will charging an EV double my electric bill?**
Almost never. A typical EV adds around a third to an average home's annual usage, not 100%. The load looks dramatic on a monitoring app because it arrives in one overnight block, but the monthly total is moderate.

**Does an EV mean I need a much bigger solar system?**
Bigger, not dramatically so. Adding one EV's annual kWh usually means eight to twelve more panels. Size the array for your household total plus the car's estimated draw and treat it as one combined number.

**Is Level 2 charging cheaper than a regular outlet?**
Slightly, and much faster. Level 2 wastes a few percent less energy because the car finishes sooner and its support systems run for less time. The bigger reason to install it is convenience, with efficiency a small bonus.
