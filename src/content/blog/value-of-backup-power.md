Ask whether a home battery pays for itself and you'll usually get an answer framed entirely around bill savings — arbitrage between cheap and expensive rate periods, or exports avoided under a stingy net-billing scheme. On that scorecard, most batteries look like a poor investment, because the electricity they shift around is worth pennies and the battery costs thousands. But that scorecard leaves out the entire reason most people actually buy a battery, which is that they don't want to lose power. Backup value doesn't show up on an electric bill, so the standard payback math ignores it, and ignoring it makes the battery look worse than it is to the people who want one most.

The problem is that "peace of mind" is hard to put a number on, and a vague benefit tends to lose every argument against a concrete cost. So the goal here is to make backup value concrete — to build it up from the actual, itemizable losses a power outage inflicts, so that when you weigh a battery you're comparing a real cost against a real benefit rather than a real cost against a feeling. Some of what follows is genuinely quantifiable; some of it is a judgment call you have to make for yourself. Both belong in the decision.

## Adding up what an outage actually costs you

Outages come in two flavors, and they cost you in completely different ways, so it helps to price them separately. The common flavor is the short outage — a few minutes to a few hours, a handful of times a year, the flicker that resets your clocks and drops your Wi-Fi. The rare flavor is the long outage — the multi-day event after an ice storm, a hurricane, a wildfire-driven public safety shutoff, or a heat wave that overwhelms the grid. These are the ones that make the news and make people buy batteries. A backup system's value comes almost entirely from the long outages, with the short ones contributing convenience rather than real dollars, so weight your thinking toward the rare, severe events even though they're the hardest to predict.

Start with the losses you can actually itemize, because a stack of concrete numbers is far more persuasive than a general sense of dread. Spoiled food is the classic one and it's larger than people expect. A full refrigerator and freezer in a well-stocked household easily holds several hundred dollars of food, and a modern refrigerator keeps things safe only for about four hours once the power's out, with a full freezer stretching to a day or two if you keep the door shut. A multi-day outage in a stocked home routinely destroys $400 to $800 of food, and a family that had just done a big grocery run or keeps a chest freezer of meat can lose well over a thousand dollars in a single event. That's a real, receipt-backed number, and it recurs every time a long outage hits.

Then there's shelter. When an outage runs long enough that the house becomes genuinely uncomfortable or unsafe — no heat in a hard freeze, no air conditioning in a dangerous heat wave, no way to run a medical device — families leave, and leaving costs money. A few nights in a hotel for a family, plus meals out because the kitchen is dark, runs several hundred dollars easily and can pass a thousand for a longer displacement. Some households can't leave, or won't, and instead spend on stopgaps: a last-minute generator at storm-week prices, fuel hauled in cans, ice bought by the bag. The generator you buy in a panic the night before a hurricane costs more than the one you'd have bought calmly, and its fuel over a multi-day run adds up fast.

For a growing number of households, the outage also stops income. Remote work has quietly made home power a business-continuity issue: a knowledge worker who can't work without electricity and internet loses billable hours or burns vacation days during a multi-day outage, and a home-based business can lose customers, inventory, or a payroll deadline. Anyone running a home freezer full of a small business's product, a home medical practice, or a workshop feels this directly. Putting a number on lost income is a matter of your own hourly value times the hours you'd lose, and for some households it dwarfs the food and hotel lines combined.

Finally there's the category that resists pricing but is real: the health and safety stakes. A household member on oxygen, a CPAP machine, refrigerated medication like insulin, a sump pump that keeps a basement from flooding, a well pump that's the only source of water — these turn an outage from an inconvenience into a genuine hazard, and the "value" of backup power in those homes isn't measured in spoiled groceries but in avoided emergencies. You can't put a clean dollar figure on not having your basement flood or your father's oxygen concentrator go dark, and you shouldn't pretend to, but you also shouldn't leave it out of the decision because it doesn't fit on a spreadsheet. For these homes, backup power is closer to insurance than to an investment, and insurance is priced against the severity of what it prevents, not against an expected return.

To turn all of this into a working number, do the arithmetic honestly. Estimate the dollar cost of a typical long outage for your household — add the food, the shelter or stopgap spending, and the lost income you'd realistically face. Then estimate how often long outages hit where you live, which is a question your own memory and your utility's reliability record can answer better than any national average. Multiply the per-event cost by the annual frequency and you have an expected annual cost of outages, the honest denominator for a backup decision. A home that faces a genuine multi-day outage costing $1,500 all-in once every two years carries an expected annual outage cost around $750 — and that's the number a battery's backup benefit gets measured against, on top of whatever modest bill savings the battery also delivers. The [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/) can help you pin down the routine numbers like your usage and rate that feed the bill-savings side; the outage side you build yourself from the losses above.

## Weighing that value against what backup actually costs

Once you have an honest annual value for avoiding outages, the battery decision gets a lot clearer, because you're finally comparing like with like. The battery's total value to you is its bill savings plus its backup value, and only the sum has to clear the cost for the purchase to make sense. A battery that returns $300 a year in rate arbitrage and provides $750 a year in expected outage protection is a $1,050-a-year benefit, which is a very different proposition than the $300 the bill-only math would show. This is the core reason batteries look like bad investments in analyses that ignore backup and reasonable purchases to the people who value backup — both camps are right about the numbers they're looking at, and both are looking at only half the picture. The full framing of whether storage earns its price, including the bill-savings side this article deliberately underweights, is worked through in [is a solar battery worth it](/blog/solar-battery-worth-it/).

Two adjustments keep this honest rather than letting it become a license to justify any battery. The first is that a battery only delivers its full backup value if it's sized and configured for the outages you actually face, and most home batteries are not whole-home, multi-day machines. A single battery typically carries a home's essential loads — refrigerator, some lights, internet, a few outlets, maybe a well pump — for a matter of hours to a day or so before it's empty, and it recharges only if paired with solar and the sun is out. That's plenty for the common short outage and for the first day of a long one, but a battery alone won't carry a house through a four-day winter storm with no sun. How far a given battery actually stretches depends on what you ask it to run, and the arithmetic of that is laid out in [how long a home battery can actually power your house](/blog/how-long-can-battery-power-house/). If your real fear is the multi-day event, one battery captures the food and the first-night comfort but not the full displacement cost, and you should credit it for what it can actually do rather than for what you wish it could.

The second adjustment is that the battery isn't the only tool for the job, and backup value is only value *relative to your next-best option*. A standby generator provides backup power too, often for less money per hour of runtime during a long outage, because it makes power from fuel for as long as you can supply fuel rather than emptying and needing sun to refill. The honest comparison, laid out in [standby generator or home battery](/blog/generator-vs-home-battery/), is that a battery gives you silent, instant, maintenance-light backup that's superb for frequent short outages and the first day of a long one, while a generator gives you sustained multi-day power at the cost of noise, fuel, and upkeep. The backup value you calculated is real, but before assigning all of it to a battery, ask what the same protection would cost from a generator, because the battery's true backup premium is only the amount by which it beats the alternative on the dimensions you care about — silence, no fuel runs, automatic operation, and doubling as a bill-savings device the rest of the year.

With those adjustments made, the method comes down to a single clean comparison. Build your annual outage cost from real line items — food, shelter, lost income, and the safety stakes you can't fully price but shouldn't ignore. Credit the battery for the share of that cost it can actually prevent given its size and your climate. Add the modest bill savings it also earns. And weigh that combined annual value against the battery's cost spread over its life, remembering that the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit applies to storage that meets the capacity threshold and cuts the up-front cost meaningfully. Run the whole thing through the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) with your own numbers rather than a generic template, because the answer swings enormously on how often your lights actually go out and how much it costs you when they do. For a household in a reliable grid area with a full freezer and a laptop job, the backup value might be a few hundred dollars a year — real, but not decisive. For a household on a well, with medical needs, in a storm-prone or wildfire-shutoff region, the same battery's backup value can run into the thousands and make the purchase easy. The number is personal, which is exactly why the standard bill-only payback misleads so many people in both directions. Do the outage arithmetic for your own house, and the battery stops being a leap of faith and becomes a decision you can defend.
