Inverter datasheets lead with a single, flattering efficiency number — 97.5%, 98%, 98.6% — and it's tempting to treat it as a fixed property of the box, the way a light bulb has a wattage. It isn't. That headline figure is the *best* efficiency the inverter ever achieves, hit at one particular operating point, and the actual efficiency swings meaningfully depending on how hard the inverter is working relative to its rating at any given moment. An inverter converting a trickle of power at dawn is not operating at 98%; an inverter converting near its full capacity at noon might be. The relationship between how loaded the inverter is and how efficiently it converts is captured in its efficiency curve, and reading that curve — rather than just the peak number stamped on the spec sheet — is what tells you whether an inverter is well matched to your array or quietly bleeding energy at the loads it'll actually spend most of its time handling. This is one of those specifications where the headline can genuinely mislead, and where a little curve-reading changes which inverter you'd choose.

## Why efficiency changes with load, and where it sags

An inverter's efficiency curve plots conversion efficiency on the vertical axis against output power — usually shown as a percentage of the inverter's rated capacity — on the horizontal axis, and the shape is consistent across most units even as the exact numbers differ. At very low output, down near a few percent of the inverter's rating, efficiency is poor. It climbs steeply as load increases, reaches a broad peak somewhere in the middle of the range — often around 30% to 70% of rated capacity — and then holds high or dips very slightly as the inverter approaches full output. The headline efficiency number lives at that middle-to-upper peak; the poor efficiency at the bottom of the curve is the part the datasheet doesn't put on the front page. The reason for this shape comes down to how inverter losses divide into two kinds. Some losses are roughly fixed regardless of load — the power the inverter's own electronics, controls, and cooling consume just to be running, sometimes called the self-consumption or standby draw. Other losses scale with how much power is flowing through, rising as output rises. At very low output, those fixed losses are large *relative* to the small amount of power being converted, so they eat a big proportional bite and efficiency looks bad. As output climbs, the same fixed losses become a smaller and smaller fraction of a growing throughput, so efficiency rises quickly. Near the top, the load-dependent losses grow enough to flatten and slightly reduce efficiency again, producing the gentle roll-off at the high end. Understanding that a fixed overhead is being spread across a variable throughput is the whole intuition: efficiency is worst when there's little power to spread the overhead across, and best once there's enough throughput to make the overhead negligible.

This matters because your inverter spends a great deal of its life at partial load, not at the sunny-noon peak where the headline number applies. Morning and evening, cloudy stretches, and the shoulder hours around sunrise and sunset all put the inverter down in the lower, less efficient region of its curve, and a solar array's output is a long tail of these partial-load hours bracketing a relatively brief peak. If an inverter's curve sags steeply at low load, all those hours convert at a worse efficiency than the spec sheet implies, and the losses accumulate across the year. A well-designed inverter has a curve that rises steeply and reaches high efficiency early — meaning it's already converting efficiently even at modest fractions of its rating — so it handles those long partial-load hours well. A poorly matched one lingers in the inefficient zone longer. The datasheet peak tells you none of this; only the curve does. Reading it is part of the broader skill of interpreting an inverter spec sheet properly rather than trusting the marketing line, and [how to read an inverter datasheet like an installer](/blog/how-to-read-inverter-datasheet/) covers the other specs that hide similar subtleties. The efficiency curve is the one most directly tied to how many kilowatt-hours you actually harvest, because it governs the conversion of every watt across every hour, not just the best ones.

## The oversized-inverter trap at low output

Here's the practical consequence that trips people up: an inverter that's too large for its array spends more of its time down in the inefficient low-load region, so oversizing the inverter can quietly cost you efficiency at exactly the outputs where you produce a lot of your energy. Think about what "oversized" means in curve terms. If you pair a big inverter with a modest array, the array can never push the inverter anywhere near its rated capacity, so even at solar noon the inverter might only be operating at, say, a quarter or a third of its rating — and all the morning, evening, and cloudy hours sit even lower, down where efficiency is poorest. The inverter's high peak efficiency is simply never reached, because the array can't supply enough power to get there. You paid for a capable box and then operate it perpetually in the weak part of its curve. Undersizing has the opposite tendency: a smaller inverter paired with a larger array runs closer to its rated capacity more of the time, keeping it up in the efficient middle-to-high region of the curve for more hours, which is one of several reasons a modestly undersized inverter — a DC-to-AC ratio above one — is often the deliberate design choice rather than a compromise. The trade-off is clipping at the very top, when a large array occasionally produces more than a smaller inverter can pass through, but that clipped energy is usually a small annual loss and is frequently outweighed by the efficiency and cost benefits of running the inverter in its sweet spot more often.

The interplay between array size and inverter size is exactly the DC-to-AC ratio decision, and the efficiency curve is one of the strongest arguments for why that ratio typically lands above one rather than at a conservative one-to-one match. [Inverter sizing and the DC-to-AC ratio sweet spot](/blog/inverter-sizing-dc-ac-ratio/) works through that balance in full, and the efficiency curve is the piece that explains *why* a slightly undersized inverter often outperforms a perfectly matched or oversized one across a real year — it keeps the box operating where its curve is highest for more of the day. When you're evaluating a proposed system, the question to ask isn't just "what's the inverter's peak efficiency" but "given the array it's paired with, where on its efficiency curve will it actually spend its hours." An inverter that will operate mostly at 15% to 30% of its rating because it's oversized for the array is a worse real-world performer than a nominally identical inverter sized so it runs at 40% to 70% for much of the day, even though both share the same headline number. This is also why you can't compare inverters on the peak figure alone — two units with the same 98% peak can have differently shaped curves, one rising to high efficiency early and one lagging, and the difference shows up as real kilowatt-hours over a year of partial-load operation. Modeling how a given inverter-and-array pairing produces across the day is the practical way to see the effect; running your proposed configuration through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) helps you estimate the production that the pairing actually delivers rather than the idealized peak-efficiency number.

## Reading the curve when you compare inverters

To use an efficiency curve rather than just admire it, look at three things beyond the peak. First, how steeply does the curve rise at low load — how quickly does the inverter reach good efficiency as output climbs from near zero? A curve that's already at high efficiency by 10% or 15% of rated capacity is far kinder to your morning, evening, and cloudy hours than one that's still climbing at 30%. Second, how broad and flat is the high-efficiency plateau — over what range of loads does the inverter stay near its best? A broad plateau means the inverter performs well across the wide range of loads a real array delivers through the day, rather than only at one narrow operating point. Third, where does the curve start to roll off at the top, and by how much — a gentle high-end decline is fine, but it tells you what to expect when the array is pushing hard near noon. Reading those three features together gives you a far truer sense of an inverter's real-world performance than the single peak figure, because it tells you how the box behaves across the full spread of conditions it'll face rather than at the one flattering point the marketing chose to print.

The efficiency curve also connects to the different ways efficiency gets *rated*, which is its own source of confusion. Because efficiency varies with load, the industry uses weighted efficiency figures — like the CEC weighted efficiency — that blend the curve across a realistic mix of operating points to give a single number more representative of real use than the raw peak. That weighted figure is a better comparison tool than the headline peak precisely because it accounts for the curve rather than cherry-picking its highest point, and understanding the distinction keeps you from being swayed by a high peak that a unit rarely reaches. [CEC versus peak efficiency](/blog/inverter-cec-vs-peak-efficiency/) unpacks exactly how those two ratings differ and why the weighted one deserves more of your attention when you're comparing units. The through-line across all of this is that inverter efficiency is a curve, not a point, and the point they print is the best one, not the typical one. An inverter well matched to its array — running in the meaty, efficient middle of its curve for most of its hours — quietly delivers more of your array's energy than a nominally identical inverter that's oversized into the weak low-load region or chosen on a peak figure it seldom sees. Read the shape, not just the summit, and you'll pick the box that actually harvests more of what your panels make.
