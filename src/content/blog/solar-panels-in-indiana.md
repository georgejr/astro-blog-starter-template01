Indiana is one of the clearest examples in the country of a state that changed the rules on solar economics without changing the sunshine. The panels produce the same energy they always did — Indiana's solar resource is ordinary Midwestern, neither desert-bright nor hopeless — but the way homeowners get *paid* for that energy has shifted fundamentally, and that shift, not the weather, is what a prospective Indiana solar buyer has to understand first. The state legislated an end to traditional full-retail net metering, and in its place put a compensation structure for exported power that pays substantially less than retail. That single policy choice reorganizes the entire calculation: it demotes the value of surplus power sent to the grid and promotes the value of power you use in your own house, which means the winning strategy in Indiana looks different from the "just cover your usage and export the rest" playbook that works in full-net-metering states.

The mechanics are worth stating plainly because they drive everything. Under the older regime, an Indiana homeowner's meter effectively spun backward when the panels overproduced, and every exported kilowatt-hour offset a future imported one at the full retail rate. Under the current structure, exported energy — what the state calls excess distributed generation — is credited at a rate tied to the utility's wholesale cost of power rather than the retail price you pay. The statutory formula sets that credit at roughly 1.25 times the utility's average wholesale rate, a modest premium over pure wholesale but still far below the retail rate you'd avoid by using the power yourself. The gap between what a self-consumed kilowatt-hour is worth (full retail) and what an exported one earns (that discounted wholesale-plus figure) is the whole story of Indiana solar, and it's why the design of your system now matters more than it used to.

## Why exporting is worth so much less than using it yourself

To feel the weight of this, picture two kilowatt-hours your panels make at noon on a sunny day. The first one runs your refrigerator, your standby electronics, and whatever else is drawing power in the house right then — it's consumed on the spot, and it's worth the full retail rate to you because it's a kilowatt-hour you didn't have to buy. The second one has no home to go to because nothing in the house needs it, so it flows out to the grid and earns only the excess-generation credit, that wholesale-plus rate. Same kilowatt-hour, same panel, same instant — but the first is worth perhaps three or four times what the second is worth, purely because of where it went. When retail power costs meaningfully more than the export credit, the difference between self-consuming a unit and exporting it is enormous, and it compounds across every sunny hour of every day for the life of the system.

That asymmetry flips the old sizing wisdom on its head. In a full-retail net metering state, you size to cover 100% of your annual usage and don't much care whether any given kilowatt-hour is used on-site or banked to the grid, because both are worth the same. In Indiana, you care intensely, because banking to the grid is a losing trade compared to using the energy yourself. The practical consequence is that the *shape* of your usage — how much power your home naturally draws during daylight, when the panels are producing — becomes a central input. A household that runs significant daytime loads captures more of its own production at full retail value and exports less at the discounted rate, so its solar economics hold up well. A household that's empty and quiet all day, then lights up in the evening after the sun is down, exports most of its midday production cheaply and buys back its evening power at retail, which is the worst-case pattern under Indiana's rules.

There's a further wrinkle in how the netting is measured that sharpens all of this. The value of exports depends not just on the credit rate but on the interval over which production and consumption are netted against each other. Where netting happens on a short, near-instantaneous basis — matching production and use within each metering interval rather than summing over a whole month — you can't lean midday surplus against evening demand at retail value the way monthly net metering allowed. Any energy you produce in an interval beyond what you're using in that same interval is treated as export and credited at the lower rate, even if you'll need grid power an hour later. This structure, which distinguishes true net metering from the various net-billing arrangements replacing it, is exactly the distinction we lay out in [net billing versus net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/), and Indiana sits firmly on the net-billing side of that line. The upshot for a homeowner is that the closer your consumption tracks your production hour by hour, the better you do — and the further apart they drift, the more the discounted export rate erodes your savings.

None of this makes Indiana a bad solar state. It makes Indiana a state where solar still saves real money, but where the *design* of the system determines how much. Retail electricity in Indiana isn't cheap enough to make solar pointless nor expensive enough to make it a slam dunk regardless of design; it sits in a range where the savings are genuine but sensitive to how well you self-consume. The broader question of whether solar can still pay when the utility won't credit exports at retail is one plenty of states now face, and our piece on [whether solar still saves money without net metering](/blog/solar-savings-without-net-metering/) walks through the general answer that applies squarely to Indiana: yes, but you earn the savings by using your own power rather than by banking it.

## The self-consumption strategy, and why storage enters the picture

If self-consumed energy is worth several times more than exported energy, then the winning Indiana strategy is to maximize the share of your production you use yourself. There are two levers for that, and they work together. The first is behavioral and free: shift flexible loads into the sunny hours. Running the dishwasher, the laundry, the pool pump, or the EV charger during the day — when the panels are producing — converts what would have been cheap exports into full-value self-consumption. A household that deliberately moves its discretionary electricity use into daylight can lift its self-consumption rate substantially without spending a dime, and in Indiana's rate structure that behavioral shift is worth real money in a way it simply wouldn't be under full net metering. The second lever is right-sizing: because the last panels on an oversized system produce surplus that mostly exports at the discounted rate, an Indiana system often optimizes at a size that closely matches daytime self-consumption rather than one that overshoots annual usage. Bigger isn't automatically better here; a system tuned to what you actually use while the sun is up frequently returns more per dollar than one built to zero out the annual bill on paper.

The lever that changes the game most, though, is a battery. Storage lets you capture your midday surplus — the energy that would otherwise export at the wholesale-plus rate — and hold it for the evening, when you'd otherwise buy power at full retail. In effect, a battery converts a low-value export into a high-value avoided purchase, arbitraging the gap between the export credit and the retail rate that Indiana's rules opened up. In a full-retail net metering state, a battery buys you backup power and little economic advantage, because the grid already credits your exports at retail; there's nothing to arbitrage. In Indiana, that same battery has a genuine economic job to do, because the spread between what exports earn and what retail costs is exactly the spread storage monetizes. This is why Indiana is one of the states where pairing solar with storage makes more financial sense than it does in net-metering markets — the policy that lowered export value simultaneously raised the value of keeping your own energy. To see how much storage capacity your usage pattern would justify and what it would cost, running your numbers through the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) is the concrete next step, because the right battery size depends on how much surplus you generate at midday and how much of your load falls after dark.

That said, a battery is a substantial added cost, and it doesn't pay for itself automatically just because the arbitrage exists — the value of the avoided-retail-versus-export spread has to be large enough, across enough kilowatt-hours per day, to earn back the battery's price over its life. For some Indiana households the spread and the daily throughput justify storage handily; for others, especially those who can shift enough load into daylight behaviorally, a well-sized solar array without a battery already captures most of the available savings, and the battery's marginal benefit doesn't clear its cost. The honest way to resolve that is to model both scenarios rather than assume storage is mandatory. Feed your usage, your retail rate, and Indiana's export structure into the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) once for solar alone and once for solar plus storage, and compare the payback and lifetime savings of each. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit — worth 30% of qualifying costs, and applicable to a battery paired with solar as well as to the panels, per current IRS guidance — improves both cases and is worth factoring in, but it doesn't change the underlying question of whether the arbitrage justifies the battery for your specific pattern.

It's instructive to set Indiana beside its neighbor Ohio, because the two states are marginal for different reasons and the contrast clarifies both. Ohio's challenge is primarily low retail rates that thin out the value of everything solar produces, a dynamic we cover in [solar in Ohio's marginal economics](/blog/solar-panels-in-ohio/); the sun and the export rules are secondary to the simple fact that cheap power means cheap savings. Indiana's challenge is structural in a different way: retail rates are workable, but the export compensation was deliberately lowered, which shifts the whole optimization toward self-consumption and storage rather than toward simply covering usage and exporting the rest. An Ohio buyer's main question is whether their bill is big enough to justify solar at all; an Indiana buyer's main question is how to design a system that self-consumes efficiently so the discounted export rate does as little damage as possible. Both states reward homeowners who run the numbers for their own house rather than trusting a national rule of thumb, but they reward *different* design choices — and in Indiana, the choices that matter most are load timing, right-sized array capacity, and a clear-eyed decision about whether storage earns its place. Get those right and Indiana solar pays; treat it like a full-net-metering state and export half your production at wholesale, and you'll wonder why the savings fell short of the brochure.
