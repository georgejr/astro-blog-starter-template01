A dishwasher feels like it should be an energy hog. It runs for an hour or two, it gets hot, it makes noise, and it deals with water — all the sensory cues we associate with a power-hungry appliance. In reality, a modern dishwasher is one of the more modest electricity users in a typical kitchen, drawing somewhere in the neighborhood of one to two kilowatt-hours per cycle. The interesting part isn't the headline number, though; it's where inside that number the energy actually goes. Almost none of it powers the pump that sprays the water around. The overwhelming majority goes to heating — heating the wash water, and then, if you let it, heating the air to dry the dishes at the end. Understanding that split is what lets you shrink a dishwasher's cost meaningfully, because the levers that matter are the ones tied to heat, not the ones tied to running the machine at all.

## Why heating water is almost the whole bill

To see where a dishwasher's electricity goes, you have to separate the machine's own mechanical work from the heating it does, because they're wildly different in scale. The motor that drives the spray arms and the pump that circulates and drains the water are genuinely small loads — a few hundred watts running intermittently over the cycle, adding up to a modest slice of the total. What dominates is bringing water up to temperature. Dishwashers wash most effectively with hot water, typically in the range of 120 to 150 degrees Fahrenheit, and heating water takes a surprising amount of energy because water has a high heat capacity. Raising even a few gallons from tap-cold to wash-hot is where most of a cycle's kilowatt-hours disappear, and that single fact explains nearly everything about a dishwasher's energy profile.

There's a wrinkle here that changes the number depending on your home: some of that water heating may not show up on the dishwasher's own electricity draw at all. Many dishwashers connect to your home's hot water supply and rely partly on water your water heater already heated, then boost it the rest of the way with an internal electric heating element. If your water heater runs on natural gas, a chunk of the "cost" of a dishwasher cycle is actually gas consumed elsewhere, not electricity the dishwasher pulled. If your water heater is electric, or if the dishwasher does most of the heating internally with its own element, then more of that energy lands on the dishwasher's electric tally. This is why per-cycle estimates vary — a dishwasher fed by a gas water heater and set to a light cycle might use well under a kilowatt-hour of electricity, while the same machine heating cold water internally on a heavy sanitizing cycle can push past two. The one-to-two-kilowatt-hour range covers most real-world cases, but your spot inside it depends on your water heater, your water temperature, and the cycle you pick.

The cycle you choose turns out to be one of the biggest variables you actually control. A "normal" or "eco" cycle uses less water and lower temperatures than a "heavy," "pots and pans," or "sanitize" cycle, and since heating water is the main cost, the hotter, higher-volume cycles simply cost more. Sanitize cycles, which push water to higher temperatures to meet a disinfection standard, are the most energy-intensive of all, and running one out of habit when a normal cycle would do is quietly expensive. Eco cycles save energy in a way that feels counterintuitive: they often run longer, not shorter, because they use less hot water and compensate with more time soaking and circulating at lower temperature. The longer runtime looks like it should use more power, but it uses less, because runtime isn't the cost — heat is. Dishwashers, as it happens, don't crack the list of the biggest electricity consumers in a home, which is dominated by heating, cooling, and water heating writ large; where a dishwasher actually ranks against the real energy hogs is laid out in [the appliances quietly eating most of your electricity](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), and the answer is reassuringly low on the list.

## The heated-dry setting is the cost you don't notice

If wash-water heating is the big, largely unavoidable cost, heated drying is the sneaky, entirely avoidable one — and it's where a lot of homeowners leave money on the table without realizing it. At the end of a wash cycle, the dishes are wet and hot. To dry them, many dishwashers run an electric heating element that warms the air inside the tub, evaporating the water off the dishes over the final stretch of the cycle. That element is a real load, and heated drying can add roughly half a kilowatt-hour or more to a cycle — a meaningful bump on top of a wash that might only have used a kilowatt-hour or so on its own. In relative terms, turning on heated dry can increase a cycle's electricity use by something like a third or more, all to do a job that physics will do for free if you give it a little time.

The free alternative is air drying, and every modern dishwasher offers some version of it. If you skip the heated-dry option and instead let the dishes sit — or crack the door open at the end of the cycle so the residual heat and steam escape and the dishes air-dry — you avoid running the element entirely. The dishes take longer to dry and may need a few minutes more before they're fully ready to put away, but they get there on the leftover heat from the wash without spending another watt. Some dishwashers automate this with a "cool dry" or "energy saver dry" setting; others rely on you cracking the door. Either way, it's one of the highest-return habit changes in the kitchen, because it eliminates a recurring cost for zero loss of function beyond a little patience. A related trick, if your machine offers it, is using the rinse aid feature, which helps water sheet off the dishes so they dry faster and more completely on their own, reducing the need for heated drying in the first place.

Put the pieces together and a typical household's dishwasher usage lands in a modest but non-trivial place. Assume a home runs the dishwasher about four times a week — a reasonable average for a small family — at roughly 1.2 kilowatt-hours per cycle including a bit of internal water heating but with heated dry turned off. That's about 4.8 kilowatt-hours a week, or roughly 20 kilowatt-hours a month, or around 240 a year. At an assumed electricity rate of $0.17 per kilowatt-hour, that's about $3.40 a month, or roughly $40 a year, to run the dishwasher. Turn heated dry back on and add half a kilowatt-hour per cycle, and the annual figure climbs by around $18 — a small number in isolation, but a pure, recurring waste for a task air drying handles for nothing. To put your own dishwasher's cost in dollars rather than kilowatt-hours, plug your cycle estimate and your actual rate into the [electricity bill calculator](/electricity-bill-calculator/); seeing the annual figure in money often makes the case for air drying more persuasive than the abstract kilowatt-hours do. And if you're not sure what rate you're paying, that number is on your utility statement — reading it correctly, including the difference between the headline rate and the all-in cost per kilowatt-hour after fees, is worth learning from [reading your electric bill line by line](/blog/how-to-read-electric-bill/).

There's one more small consideration that rounds out the picture, and it's the mirror image of the wash-and-dry story: what the dishwasher draws when it's doing nothing. A dishwasher with a digital display, a clock, or a delay-start feature pulls a trickle of standby power around the clock to keep those functions alive, even between washes. It's a tiny load — a watt or two — but it runs every hour of every day, and across a whole house full of appliances doing the same thing, those trickles add up to a real line on the bill. The dishwasher's contribution is negligible on its own, but it belongs to the same family of always-on draws worth understanding, which is the subject of [phantom loads](/blog/phantom-loads-electricity/). Compared to the wash cycle itself, standby is a rounding error, but it's a reminder that appliance energy use isn't only about the moments the machine is obviously working.

Where all of this lands is a genuinely reassuring place: a dishwasher is not an appliance to feel guilty about running. At roughly a kilowatt-hour or two per cycle and something like $40 a year for a typical household, it's a small player against the heating, cooling, and water-heating loads that actually drive an electric bill, and running a full dishwasher is generally more water- and energy-efficient than washing the same dishes by hand under a running tap. The levers that matter are simple: pick the lightest cycle that gets the dishes clean, run the machine full rather than half-empty so each cycle does more work, and skip heated drying in favor of letting the dishes air-dry on the residual heat. Do those three things and you've captured essentially all the savings a dishwasher offers, without any sacrifice in clean dishes — and you can stop worrying about the one kitchen appliance that turned out to be a modest user all along. Understanding how it fits into your household's total consumption, rather than fixating on it in isolation, is the more useful frame, and that broader picture of what a typical home actually uses is drawn in [how much electricity the average American home uses](/blog/average-home-electricity-usage/).
