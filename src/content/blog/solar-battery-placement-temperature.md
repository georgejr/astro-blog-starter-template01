Where you mount a home battery is really a question about chemistry. A lithium battery stores and releases energy through reactions that are temperature-sensitive: run them too hot and the reactions that age the cells speed up; run them too cold and the battery can't safely accept a charge. The location decision — garage, basement, closet, or an outdoor wall — comes down to which spot keeps the cells inside the band where those reactions behave. Understanding that band first makes the rest of the choice straightforward.

## Why temperature drives the decision

Every lithium battery has a comfortable operating range and a wider survivable range. As a general planning band, the cells are happiest somewhere around 32–86°F (roughly 0–30°C). Inside that window they charge, discharge, and age normally. Push outside it and two distinct problems appear at the two ends.

At the hot end, heat is a catalyst. The same reactions that store your energy also slowly degrade the electrodes and electrolyte, and that degradation accelerates as temperature climbs. A battery that lives in a 110°F attic all summer will lose usable capacity faster than one kept near room temperature — the cells wear out sooner even if you never cycle them harder. [Solar battery lifespan](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/) covers how cycles and time combine to age a pack; heat is the thumb on that scale.

At the cold end, the problem is different and more immediate: charging a lithium cell below freezing can plate metallic lithium inside it, causing permanent damage. To prevent this, batteries rated for cold either refuse to charge until they warm up or spend some of their own stored energy on an internal heater. Both protect the cells, but both cost you — either lost availability or lost capacity to self-heating. Cold hurts performance now; heat hurts longevity later.

Chemistry matters here too. The two common home chemistries tolerate temperature differently, and [LFP vs NMC home batteries](/blog/lfp-vs-nmc-home-batteries/) explains why — worth reading before you commit a battery to a harsh location.

## Reading the three common locations against that band

With the temperature logic in hand, the candidate spots sort themselves out.

### The garage

An attached garage is the default for good reason: it's sheltered from sun and rain, stays milder than the outdoors, and usually has easy access to the electrical panel, which shortens the wiring run. Its weakness is that an uninsulated garage in an extreme climate still swings toward the edges of the comfort band — hot in a desert summer, near freezing in a northern winter. In moderate climates it's close to ideal; in harsh ones it's acceptable but not optimal.

### The basement

A conditioned or semi-conditioned basement is often the best thermal environment in the house. Underground spaces stay remarkably stable year-round, sitting near the middle of the ideal band without any active climate control. The trade-offs are practical rather than thermal: getting a heavy battery down the stairs, and, in flood-prone areas, keeping it above any water risk. Where those aren't concerns, a basement is hard to beat on temperature.

### Outdoors

Many home batteries are rated for outdoor wall mounting, with enclosures built to shed weather across a wide survivable range. "Can it go outside?" is usually yes — but "should it, here?" depends on your climate. An outdoor battery in a mild region is fine. In a place with brutal summers or hard freezes, the pack spends more of the year fighting its environment: self-heating in winter, aging faster in summer. Shade, a north-facing wall, or a simple sun screen meaningfully help by keeping direct sun off the enclosure.

## Clearances and code, not just climate

Temperature picks the general zone; electrical and fire code pick the exact spot. Installers work to national and local requirements that govern how batteries are mounted, and while the specifics vary by jurisdiction and change over time, the recurring themes are consistent:

- **Working clearance** in front of and around the unit so it can be serviced and safely disconnected.
- **Separation from doors, windows, and egress paths**, and limits on how much energy can be installed inside a dwelling versus in a garage or outdoors.
- **Ventilation and mounting surface** requirements — a solid wall, adequate airflow, and distance from ignition sources.

These rules are why a spot that looks convenient to you may be ruled out by your installer, and why the final location is a joint decision with whoever pulls the permit. Don't fight it; the clearances exist because a battery is a dense store of energy.

## A few practical questions

**Can a solar battery be installed outside?**
Usually yes — most home units carry an outdoor rating. The real question is climate: in a mild region it's a fine choice, while in extreme heat or cold you'll get better longevity and availability by moving it into a garage or basement, or at least shading it.

**Will a battery work in an unheated space in winter?**
It will function, but a cold-rated pack may divert stored energy to self-heating before it can charge, reducing what's available to you. A more temperate location avoids that tax.

**Does placement affect how much backup I actually get?**
Indirectly. A pack that's fighting temperature — self-heating in the cold or derating in the heat — delivers less usable energy than the same pack in the comfort band.
