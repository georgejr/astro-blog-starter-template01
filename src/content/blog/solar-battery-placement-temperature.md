Where you mount a home battery is, underneath everything, a question about chemistry. A lithium battery stores and releases energy through chemical reactions that are sensitive to temperature: run them too hot and the side reactions that age the cells speed up; run them too cold and the battery physically cannot accept a charge without damaging itself. So the location decision — garage, basement, closet, or an outdoor wall — is really the decision of which spot keeps the cells inside the temperature band where those reactions behave. Every other consideration, from wiring runs to how far you have to carry a heavy unit, sits on top of that chemical foundation. Get the temperature right and the battery delivers its rated capacity and reaches its rated lifespan; get it wrong and you quietly pay for it, either in capacity you cannot access on cold mornings or in a pack that wears out years earlier than the warranty implied. Understanding the band first is what makes the rest of the choice straightforward, so that is where any sensible placement decision starts.

## Why temperature drives the decision, and how the common spots compare

Every lithium battery has two ranges worth distinguishing: a comfortable operating range where it performs and ages normally, and a wider survivable range where it will function but not happily. As a general planning band, home battery cells are happiest somewhere around 32 to 86°F, which is roughly 0 to 30°C. Inside that window they charge, discharge, and age at the pace the manufacturer designed for. Push outside it in either direction and two distinct problems appear, and they are worth separating because they behave nothing alike. At the hot end, heat acts as a catalyst. The same chemistry that stores your energy also slowly degrades the electrodes and the electrolyte over time, and that degradation accelerates as temperature climbs. A battery that spends every summer baking in a 110°F attic or an unshaded outdoor enclosure loses usable capacity faster than an identical pack kept near room temperature — the cells simply wear out sooner, even if you never cycle them any harder. Heat does not announce itself; it just shortens the life you paid for, and by the time it shows up as reduced capacity the damage is already done.

The cold end is a different and more immediate hazard. Charging a lithium cell below freezing can cause metallic lithium to plate onto the anode, a process that permanently damages the cell and, in the worst cases, creates a safety risk. Because this failure mode is so serious, batteries rated for cold climates protect against it in one of two ways. Some refuse to charge at all until the pack has warmed itself above a safe threshold, which means on a frigid morning your battery may sit idle when you most want it working. Others spend a portion of their own stored energy running an internal heater to keep the cells warm enough to charge, which protects the chemistry but costs you capacity — energy you paid to store, burned to keep the pack alive. Either way, cold exacts a tax on availability or capacity in the moment, whereas heat exacts a tax on longevity down the road. Both are real, and they pull toward the same conclusion: the closer you can keep the pack to that comfortable middle band year-round, the more of its rated performance and life you actually receive.

Chemistry compounds the point, because not all home batteries tolerate temperature equally. The two dominant chemistries in residential storage respond differently to heat and cold, differ in how hard they self-protect, and differ in the margins they leave you before either problem bites. That difference is worth understanding before you commit a battery to a harsh location, because the same closet or exterior wall that is marginal for one chemistry may be entirely fine for another. [LFP vs NMC home batteries](/blog/lfp-vs-nmc-home-batteries/) walks through how the two compare, including their temperature behavior, and it is genuinely relevant to placement rather than an abstract spec comparison. The broader aging picture matters too: temperature is only one of the forces wearing a pack down, and it interacts with how often and how deeply you cycle it. [Solar battery lifespan](/blog/solar-battery-lifespan-cycles/) covers how cycles and calendar time combine to age a battery, and heat is best understood as a thumb pressing down on that whole process — it does not replace normal wear, it multiplies it.

With the temperature logic in hand, the candidate spots more or less sort themselves out, and the ranking depends heavily on your climate rather than on any universal rule. An attached garage is the default choice for good reasons that have little to do with chemistry and everything to do with practicality: it is sheltered from direct sun and rain, it runs milder than the outdoors in both summer and winter, and it usually sits close to the main electrical panel, which shortens the wiring run and trims installation cost. Its weakness is that an uninsulated garage in an extreme climate still drifts toward the edges of the comfort band — genuinely hot in a desert summer, near or below freezing in a northern winter — because it is only partially buffered from the outdoor air. In a moderate climate a garage is close to ideal and hard to argue against. In a harsh one it remains acceptable, but it is no longer optimal, and it is worth weighing against a more stable indoor option if one exists.

A conditioned or even semi-conditioned basement is frequently the best thermal environment anywhere in the house, and it is underrated for exactly that reason. Underground spaces stay remarkably stable across the year, buffered by the surrounding earth, so a basement tends to sit near the middle of the ideal band without any active heating or cooling at all — the ground does the climate control for free. The trade-offs a basement brings are practical rather than thermal: getting a heavy battery down a flight of stairs during installation is a real logistical problem, and in any area with flooding risk the pack has to be mounted well clear of where water could reach it. Where those two concerns do not apply, a basement is very hard to beat on temperature, and it is often the quiet best answer that homeowners overlook because a garage seemed more obvious.

Outdoors is the option people ask about most, and the honest answer is "usually yes, but it depends on where you live." Many home batteries are rated for outdoor wall mounting, with weatherproof enclosures built to shed rain and operate across a wide survivable range, so the question is rarely whether a battery can go outside. It is whether it should, in your specific climate. An outdoor battery in a mild region is a perfectly sound choice and frees up interior space. In a place with brutal summers or hard freezes, though, an exterior pack spends far more of the year fighting its environment — self-heating through winter mornings, aging faster through summer afternoons — and that constant struggle shows up as reduced availability now and shortened life later. Even outdoors, placement within the site matters: mounting on a shaded or north-facing wall, or adding a simple sun screen, keeps direct sunlight off the enclosure and can meaningfully lower the temperatures the cells actually see. A battery that would struggle on a west-facing wall baking in the afternoon sun may be entirely comfortable a few feet away in permanent shade.

## Clearances, code, and what placement really costs you

Temperature picks the general zone, but electrical and fire codes pick the exact spot, and the two do not always agree. Installers work to national and local requirements governing how batteries are mounted, and while the specifics vary by jurisdiction and are revised over time, the recurring themes are consistent enough to plan around. Codes generally require a defined working clearance in front of and around the unit, so it can be serviced and safely disconnected without contortion. They impose separation from doors, windows, and egress paths, and they frequently limit how much stored energy may be installed inside a dwelling as opposed to in a garage or outdoors — which is why homeowners planning a large, multi-unit bank sometimes find their preferred indoor location ruled out on energy-quantity grounds alone. And they specify ventilation and a suitable mounting surface: a solid wall, adequate airflow around the enclosure, and distance from ignition sources. These rules are the reason a spot that looks convenient to you may be quietly off the table before the tape measure comes out, and why the final location is always a joint decision with whoever pulls the permit. It is not worth fighting the clearances; they exist because a home battery is a dense store of energy, and the separations are there to keep a rare fault from becoming a serious event.

Temperature and code narrow the field, but a handful of ordinary practical factors usually pick the winner among the spots that remain. Distance to the electrical panel and the inverter matters more than people expect, because a long conductor run adds real cost and small losses, so a location a few feet from where the wiring already lives often beats a thermally perfect spot on the far side of the house. Noise is worth considering if the unit will sit near a bedroom or a living space, since some batteries and their associated hardware run cooling fans or emit a faint hum under load, and a garage or basement wall absorbs that better than a hallway closet. Accessibility cuts both ways: the location has to be reachable for the occasional service call, but a battery mounted where a firefighter would expect to find it, clearly labeled, is easier for emergency responders to account for, and some jurisdictions lean toward garage or exterior placement partly for that reason. If there is any chance you will expand storage later — adding a second unit for more backup or more capacity — leaving physical room and a sensible wiring path beside the first one now saves a much messier retrofit down the road. None of these overrides the temperature band or the code clearances, but among the spots that satisfy both, they are usually what tips the decision from acceptable to genuinely good.

A few practical questions tend to follow from all this, and they are worth answering plainly. Can a solar battery be installed outside? Usually yes — most home units carry an outdoor rating — but the real determinant is climate, and in a region of extreme heat or cold you will get better longevity and availability by bringing the pack into a garage or basement, or at minimum shading an exterior unit. Will a battery work in an unheated space through winter? It will function, but a cold-rated pack may divert some of its stored energy to self-heating before it can accept a charge, which reduces what is actually available to you when you need it; a more temperate location avoids that recurring tax. Does placement affect how much backup you actually get? Indirectly but genuinely — a pack fighting temperature, whether self-heating in the cold or derating its output in the heat, delivers less usable energy than the same pack sitting in the comfort band, so a poor location quietly shrinks the backup you sized for. That last point ties placement back to sizing, because the two are not independent decisions.
