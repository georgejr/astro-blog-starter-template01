The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit — the "solar tax credit" — is the largest single incentive for US home solar. It reduces the effective cost of a typical system by tens of thousands of dollars over the market, and it is also one of the most misunderstood parts of the purchase. Here is how it actually works.

> **Important:** tax rules change and individual eligibility varies. Treat this article as orientation, and confirm the current rules and your situation with a tax professional before relying on the credit.

## The basics

The credit lets you subtract **30% of your qualified solar installation cost** from the federal income tax you owe. On a $22,000 system, that is a $6,600 reduction in your tax bill — bringing the effective system cost to $15,400.

Key characteristics:

- It is a **credit**, not a deduction: it reduces tax owed dollar-for-dollar
- It is **not a rebate**: no check arrives at installation; you claim it when filing
- It is **nonrefundable**: it can reduce your tax to zero, but unused credit generally carries forward to future years rather than being paid out

## What qualifies

The credit typically covers the full turnkey project on a home you own and live in:

- Panels, inverters, racking, and wiring
- Labor for installation and permitting
- Energy storage (batteries over 3 kWh), including standalone storage
- Sales tax on the above

Roof repairs and general re-roofing generally do **not** qualify, even when done alongside the install — only solar-specific work counts.

## What trips people up

### You need tax liability

The credit offsets taxes you owe. If your federal tax bill is small — common for retirees on modest income — you may not be able to use the full credit in year one. Carryforward helps, but plan the timing with your tax preparer.

### Leases and PPAs don't give *you* the credit

If a company owns the system on your roof, the company claims the credit — it is priced into your lease rate. Ownership (cash or loan) is how the credit lands in your pocket.

### The credit applies to the price you actually paid

Financed systems sometimes carry inflated "dealer fee" pricing. You claim 30% of the contract price — but a padded price means you financed the padding too. Compare cash and loan pricing carefully; see [average solar panel cost](/blog/average-solar-panel-cost/) for benchmarks.

## How it changes your payback

The credit is the difference between marginal and compelling economics for most homes:

| | Without credit | With 30% credit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| System cost (8 kW @ $2.75/W) | $22,000 | $15,400 |
| Yearly savings | $2,000 | $2,000 |
| Payback | 11 years | ~7.7 years |

## How to claim it

1. Keep your signed contract and proof of payment.
2. File **IRS Form 5695** (Residential Energy Credits) with your federal return for the year the system was placed in service.
3. The credit flows onto your Form 1040, reducing tax owed.

Most tax software walks through Form 5695 directly; a preparer is worthwhile for carryforward situations.

## State and local incentives

Some states, municipalities, and utilities layer additional incentives on top — property tax exemptions, sales tax waivers, performance payments, or rebates. These vary enormously and change often, so we deliberately do not estimate them here. Your installer and state energy office are the current sources.

## FAQ

**Does a battery-only project qualify?**
Standalone storage above 3 kWh generally qualifies for the same 30% credit. Details in [is a solar battery worth it](/blog/solar-battery-worth-it/).

**Can I claim it on a rental property I own?**
The residential credit is for homes you use as a residence. Rental-only properties fall under different rules — ask a tax professional.

**What if my credit is bigger than my tax bill?**
The unused portion generally carries forward to the next tax year.
