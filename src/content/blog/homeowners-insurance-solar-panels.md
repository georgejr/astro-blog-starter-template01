A roof-mounted array is one of the more expensive things bolted to your house, so asking who pays when a hailstorm cracks a panel is a reasonable thing to settle before you sign an install contract. The short answer is that a standard homeowners policy usually treats rooftop solar as part of the dwelling and covers it the same way it covers your roof. The longer answer has enough exceptions that a five-minute call to your insurer is worth making before, not after, the panels go up.

## Where solar sits in a standard policy

Most home insurance policies in the US are structured around a few coverage buckets. The one that matters here is dwelling coverage, which protects the house and anything permanently attached to it. Roof-mounted panels, racking, conduit, and a wall-mounted inverter are generally considered permanent fixtures, so they fall under dwelling coverage without a separate rider in most cases.

That attachment test is why placement matters. A ground-mounted array sitting on posts in the yard is often classified as a detached structure or personal property instead, which some policies cover at a lower limit or not at all. Portable panels and anything you could unbolt and carry off tend to land in the personal-property bucket, with its own sublimits. If your system is anything other than a straightforward roof mount, that is the first thing to confirm in writing.

Coverage also follows the same list of "named perils" or open-perils protection that applies to the rest of the roof: fire, wind, hail, lightning, falling objects, and similar events. The exclusions that apply to your roof apply to your panels too. Flood and earthquake are the usual gaps, and neither is typically included in a base policy anywhere in the country.

## When a claim actually applies

A solar panel insurance claim works like any other property claim: a covered peril causes sudden, accidental damage, and you file for the cost to repair or replace. Hail shatters the glass, a windstorm tears a section of array off the racking, a falling branch punctures a module. Those are the clean cases, and they are handled much like roof damage from the same event.

The claims that get denied usually fall into two categories. The first is wear and tear. Panels degrade slowly and predictably over decades, and no policy pays for age-related output decline or a component that simply reached the end of its service life. That is what the manufacturer's warranty is for, not your insurer. If you want the distinction between the two spelled out, [how long solar panels last](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/) covers the warranty side of it.

The second is faulty installation or manufacturing defects. If a poorly torqued clamp lets a panel slide off in an ordinary breeze, the insurer may argue the loss came from workmanship rather than a covered peril, and point you back to the installer. This is one reason vetting your installer matters beyond the price, since sloppy work can quietly become an uncovered risk years later.

Deductibles apply the same way they do to any claim, and many policies carry a separate, higher deductible for wind and hail in storm-prone regions. On a smaller panel loss, the repair cost can land close to or below that deductible, which makes filing pointless. Storm resilience is worth understanding on its own terms; [how solar panels hold up to hail and wind](/blog/solar-panel-hail-storm-damage/) walks through how durable modern modules actually are.

## What ownership status changes

Whether you own the system outright reshapes the insurance picture more than any other single factor.

If you bought the system with cash or a solar loan, you own the equipment, and it is yours to insure as part of the dwelling. This is the simplest arrangement and the one most of this article describes.

If you signed a lease or a power purchase agreement, a third party owns the hardware on your roof. Their equipment is typically their responsibility to insure, and your policy may specifically exclude it. That sounds convenient until a claim involves both the panels and your roof deck underneath, at which point two insurers and a leasing company can end up in the same conversation. Read the lease language on insurance, damage, and who is liable for what before assuming your homeowners policy has you covered, because a leased system's coverage picture is genuinely different from an owned one's.

## How solar changes your premium and limits

Adding solar to home insurance can nudge your premium, and the direction is not always up in the way people expect. Because the panels raise the replacement cost of your dwelling, the amount of coverage you need rises too, and premiums scale loosely with that replacement value. A system that adds a meaningful sum to the rebuild cost of your home will usually add something to the annual premium, though the increment is often modest relative to the system's value.

The step that trips people up is coverage limits. If your dwelling coverage was set to rebuild the house as it was before solar, it may not be enough to replace the house plus the array after a total loss. Ask your insurer to increase the dwelling limit to reflect the system's replacement cost. Underinsuring here is the quiet failure mode: the panels are technically covered, but the policy limit runs out before the full rebuild is paid for.

To put the added replacement cost in perspective, it helps to know roughly what a comparable system runs today. The [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) gives a ballpark you can hand to your agent when you adjust the limit.

## What to confirm before the install

A short list of questions to your insurer settles most of the uncertainty:

- Does my current policy cover roof-mounted solar under dwelling coverage, or do I need an endorsement?
- Will my dwelling limit need to rise, and by how much, to reflect the system's replacement cost?
- Does the wind/hail deductible apply to panel damage, and what is it?
- If I lease, what does my policy exclude, and what does the lessor's insurance cover?

Get the answers in writing, keep the install paperwork and the system's specifications with your policy documents, and you have done the part that actually protects you. The coverage is usually there. The gap, when there is one, is almost always a limit that never got updated or a lease clause nobody read.

## FAQ

### Do I need to tell my insurer I installed solar?

Yes. Most policies expect you to report material changes to the dwelling, and adding a system that increases the rebuild cost qualifies. Notifying your insurer also gives them the chance to raise your dwelling limit so the array is fully covered rather than technically covered but underinsured.

### Will solar panels raise my home insurance premium?

Often modestly, because they increase the replacement value the policy has to cover. The increase is usually small relative to the system's cost, and some homeowners see little change. The bigger risk is leaving the dwelling limit too low, not the premium itself.

### Does homeowners insurance cover a solar panel that stops working?

Not if it simply failed or degraded with age. Manufacturing defects and normal output decline fall under the manufacturer's warranty, not your insurance. A policy pays when a covered peril, like hail or wind, causes sudden accidental damage.

### Are ground-mounted panels covered the same as roof panels?

Not always. Many policies classify a ground mount as a detached structure or personal property with a lower limit than dwelling coverage. If your system is ground-mounted, confirm exactly which bucket it falls into and whether the limit is adequate.
