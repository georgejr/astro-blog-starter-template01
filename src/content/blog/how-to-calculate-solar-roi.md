Ask most people what solar "returns" and they answer with a payback period — the number of years until savings equal cost. That is a useful number, but it is not a return. An investor does not evaluate a bond by asking how many years until it repays the principal; they ask what annual rate it yields. Solar deserves the same treatment, because framed as an annualized return it competes directly with the other places your money could go.

The two framings answer different questions. Payback asks "when am I whole again?" Return on investment asks "what rate is this money earning while it sits on my roof?" A system with a nine-year payback that then runs cleanly for another sixteen years is not a nine-year investment — it is a twenty-five-year one, and most of the return arrives after payback.

## The simple ROI, and why it flatters solar

The crudest version of solar ROI divides lifetime net savings by upfront cost:

Simple ROI = (total lifetime savings − net system cost) ÷ net system cost

Assume a system that costs $18,000 after the 30% federal credit and saves $1,800 in year one. Over 25 years, with electricity prices rising and output slowly declining, suppose it saves about $54,000 total. Simple ROI is ($54,000 − $18,000) ÷ $18,000 = 200%. Tripling your money sounds impressive, and it is real, but the figure is misleading as a comparison tool because it ignores time. A 200% return over 25 years is not remotely the same as 200% in five.

To compare against a savings account, a bond, or an index fund, you need the annualized rate — the constant yearly percentage that would grow your $18,000 into $18,000 plus the net savings over the same period.

## Annualized return: the number worth computing

The annualized ROI treats the system like an investment that pays a stream of yearly savings. A reasonable approximation for a rough figure is:

Annualized ROI ≈ (average annual net savings ÷ net system cost) × 100

Using the example, average annual savings of about $2,160 ($54,000 over 25 years) on an $18,000 net cost gives roughly a 12% simple annual yield. Because savings actually grow over time as rates rise while the upfront cost is fixed, the early years run below that average and later years above it, so treat 12% as an optimistic ceiling for this scenario. A more conservative reading of most residential systems, once you account for the timing of cash flows, lands in a rough 9–12% annualized range under favorable conditions — and lower where sun is weak or power is cheap.

That range is worth sitting with. A tax-advantaged, inflation-linked return in that neighborhood, backed by hardware with a 25-year production warranty, is competitive with long-run stock market averages and beats most fixed-income options — with the important caveat that it is illiquid and tied to your house. The figures here are labeled assumptions to show the method; your own inputs will move the result substantially.

## The four inputs that actually move the number

ROI is only as honest as its assumptions, and four of them do most of the work:

- **Net system cost.** The price after the federal credit and any local incentives, not the gross sticker. This is the denominator of every ROI formula, so an inflated price quietly wrecks the return — one more reason to benchmark the quote hard before signing.
- **Year-one savings.** Your consumption, your electricity rate, and how your utility credits exported power. A home paying a high rate under full net metering saves far more per kWh generated than one on cheap power with weak export credits.
- **Electricity rate inflation.** Solar's savings grow as grid prices climb, so rising utility rates quietly lift the return over time. Assume a modest, defensible escalation rather than a dramatic one.
- **Degradation and lifespan.** Panels lose a fraction of a percent of output per year, so late-life savings are slightly lower than year one — a small drag that a long lifespan more than offsets.

Get those four right and the formula almost writes itself. Get any one badly wrong and the resulting ROI is fiction. ## Where ROI and payback disagree

The two metrics can rank systems differently, and knowing when they diverge is what makes ROI worth the extra effort.

### The financing fork

Paying cash produces the cleanest, highest ROI because there is no interest drag. A loan lowers the annualized return — you are now sharing the yield with a lender — even though it can improve early cash flow. The relationship between how you pay and when you break even is worked through in [how paying cash or financing changes solar payback](/blog/cash-vs-loan-solar-payback/); the same fork changes ROI in a parallel way.

### System size and the "worth it" question

A larger system often has a slightly better ROI per dollar because fixed project costs are spread thinner, yet its absolute payback may look no faster. And whether solar is worth it at all in your situation is ultimately an ROI question dressed as a yes-or-no — the framing behind [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/), which turns this same math into a decision.

The reason to compute an annualized figure rather than stopping at payback is comparison. Payback tells you nothing about the sixteen productive years that often follow break-even, and those years are where solar earns most of its return. A system that pays back in nine years and runs for twenty-five is quietly one of the better real-asset investments a homeowner can make from their own roof.
