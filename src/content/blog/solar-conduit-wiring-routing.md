Most homeowners spend their solar research on panels, inverters, and price, and almost none on the metal pipe that ties the whole thing together — right up until the install is done and there is a gray conduit running down the side of the house that nobody warned them about. The wiring path is one of the least discussed and most visible parts of a solar installation, and where it goes is not a fixed law. It is a design decision, shaped by your roof, your electrical panel's location, local code, and how much you are willing to spend and disrupt to keep the run out of sight. Understanding the options before the crew arrives is the difference between accepting whatever is easiest for the installer and getting a routing you can live with looking at for the next twenty-five years.

The wiring has a job to do, and the job dictates the general path even if the details vary. Power leaves the panels as direct current, and depending on your system architecture it either gets converted to alternating current right up on the roof — by microinverters or optimizers mounted under the panels — or is carried down as DC to a central inverter mounted lower on the house or in the garage. From the inverter, AC power runs to your main electrical panel, usually through a dedicated breaker or a service-entrance connection, sometimes by way of a separate AC disconnect switch that code or the utility requires. Somewhere in that chain sits the metering and monitoring equipment, and on most modern systems a rapid-shutdown device that can de-energize the array for firefighter safety. Every one of those connections needs a protected wiring path between it and the next, and conduit — rigid or flexible metal or plastic tubing that shields the wires — is how that path gets built. The question is never whether there will be conduit, but where it runs and how visible it ends up.

## Exterior runs, interior runs, and why the cheap option is the default

The path of least resistance, and by far the most common, is an exterior run. The conduit leaves the array, travels down the roof to the edge, drops down the outside wall of the house, and continues along the exterior to wherever the inverter and main panel live. Installers favor this approach for the obvious reason that it is fast, straightforward, and cheap: the crew works entirely from outside, never opens a wall or crawls through an attic to fish wire, and can mount the conduit in an afternoon. That efficiency is real, and it is why an exterior run is the default assumption baked into most standard quotes. The trade-off is entirely aesthetic. You end up with visible conduit — typically metal EMT, often left in its raw gray galvanized finish or painted to match the wall — running down the side of your home. On a wall that faces the backyard or a side lot nobody sees, that is a non-issue. On the front of a house, or across a stucco or brick façade that the conduit visually cuts across, it can bother a homeowner every time they pull into the driveway.

There is a middle path that softens the exterior look without the full cost of hiding everything: routing the conduit thoughtfully so it drops down an inconspicuous corner, hugs a downspout, or runs behind a gutter rather than marching straight across a prominent wall. Painting the conduit to match the siding or trim helps a great deal and costs little. Good installers do this as a matter of craft; rushed ones run the pipe along the shortest line regardless of how it looks. It is entirely reasonable to walk the proposed path with your installer before the work starts and ask where the conduit will land, because moving a planned run is trivial on paper and expensive once the pipe is mounted. If appearance matters to you, raise it during the design conversation rather than after the crew has packed up. This is exactly the kind of detail worth settling in advance, and it belongs on the list of things to confirm before install day — our [walkthrough of what happens on installation day](/blog/solar-installation-day/) covers how these decisions get executed once the crew is on site, and by then the conduit path should already be agreed.

The other end of the spectrum is an interior run, where the conduit is concealed inside the house — dropped through the roof into the attic, routed across the attic space, and brought down inside a wall or a closet to reach the inverter and panel. Done well, an interior run makes the wiring nearly invisible from outside: the only thing on the exterior is where the conduit penetrates the roof under the array, and even that sits beneath the panels. Homeowners who care about their home's appearance often ask for this specifically, and it is frequently possible — but it is not free, and it is not always practical. An interior run requires suitable attic access and a clear path through the framing, adds labor because the crew now works inside the house fishing wire through concealed spaces, and may involve opening and patching drywall where the conduit has to cross a finished wall. It also lengthens the job. On a home with a walkable attic directly above the main panel, an interior run is a modest upcharge; on a home with a cramped or inaccessible attic, a finished ceiling below the roof, or a panel located far from any clean interior path, it can be substantially more or occasionally not feasible at all. The right answer depends on your specific house, which is why it is a conversation with the installer rather than a blanket rule.

The location of your main electrical panel quietly governs a great deal of this, and it is the first thing an installer looks at when planning the run. If your panel sits in a garage or on an exterior wall directly below the array, the wiring path is short and the routing decisions are easy — there simply is not much conduit to hide. If the panel is buried in a finished basement, tucked in an interior closet, or on the opposite side of the house from the best roof, the run grows long, and every extra foot is more conduit to route, more visible pipe if it goes outside, and more labor if it goes inside. This is why two homes with identical roofs and identical panels can end up with very different wiring situations: one has a conveniently placed panel and a ten-foot run, the other has a far-flung panel and a path that has to cross half the house. It is also why the site survey matters. A surveyor who actually traces the route from array to inverter to panel will spot the long or awkward runs early, when they can still be planned for, rather than leaving the crew to improvise on install day. Wherever the conduit goes, it will cross the roof at some point to leave the array, and that penetration gets sealed with flashing the same way any roof penetration does — a detail worth confirming is handled properly, since a poorly sealed conduit entry is a leak waiting to happen regardless of whether the rest of the run is pretty.

## Code, safety, and what the routing costs you

Conduit routing is not purely a matter of taste, because a good deal of it is governed by electrical code and the requirements of your local jurisdiction and utility. Rooftop wiring for solar has to meet rules that exist for real reasons, chiefly firefighter safety. Rapid-shutdown requirements mean the system must be able to de-energize the conductors on the roof quickly in an emergency, which is part of why module-level electronics have become so common — they place the shutdown capability at each panel. Conduit carrying live conductors has to be secured, protected from physical damage, and in many areas kept a certain distance from other systems. Where DC wiring runs long distances, code may push toward getting the conversion to AC done sooner, which interacts with your choice of inverter architecture. The wiring realities are one of the underappreciated practical differences between a string-inverter system, where DC travels from the array to a central inverter, and a microinverter system, where AC leaves the roof directly — a distinction worth understanding on its own terms in [string inverters versus microinverters](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/), because it quietly shapes what the conduit has to carry and how far.

The inspection process is the backstop that keeps all of this honest. After the physical install, a local building or electrical inspector reviews the work, and the conduit routing, securing, grounding, and labeling are all part of what they check. This is why cutting corners on the wiring path is a poor idea even when it is out of sight: work that does not meet code will fail inspection and have to be redone, delaying your permission to operate and your ability to actually turn the system on. A reputable installer builds to pass inspection the first time, and the routing they propose reflects both what looks acceptable and what the inspector will approve. If you are trying to understand where the wiring work falls in the overall sequence — design, permitting, install, inspection, utility sign-off — our [solar installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/) lays out the full order, and conduit routing is decided in the design phase, executed during install, and verified at inspection, so a change you want is cheapest to request at the very start.

On cost, the honest framing is that routing is one of the labor-driven variables inside your total price rather than a headline line item, and it moves the number more than most people expect. An exterior run is baked into a standard quote at essentially no premium because it is the assumed default. An interior or concealed run adds labor and sometimes drywall repair, and where that lands depends entirely on your house — a clean attic path above the panel is a small addition, while a difficult route through finished space can add a meaningful amount and extra time on the schedule. Because this is labor and job-specific rather than hardware, it is one of the places where quotes from different installers can diverge for the same house: one may assume a simple exterior drop while another has priced in a tidier concealed run. When you compare proposals, it is worth checking what each one assumes about the wiring path, since a lower number that hides an ugly exterior run is not really cheaper — it is a different product. To see how routing and other labor choices sit within the overall installed price, the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) gives a baseline for your size and region that you can hold quotes against, and a run that comes in far above it may reflect a difficult concealed routing that is worth confirming is really what you asked for. A short list of questions turns this from something that happens to you into something you decide. Ask to see the planned conduit path on the proposal or a site photo, marked out, before you sign — not described vaguely, but shown. Ask whether the run is exterior or interior and, if exterior, which walls it crosses and whether it will be painted to match. Ask where the conduit penetrates the roof and how that penetration is flashed. Ask whether your panel's location makes an interior run feasible and what it would add if you want one. None of these questions is confrontational; they are the ordinary due diligence of a project that will hang on your house for decades, and a good installer answers them readily because they have already thought it all through. The ones who get evasive about the wiring path are often the ones planning the fastest, least attractive run, and the questions flush that out while there is still time to change it.

The through-line is simple: decide how much the appearance matters to you, say so before the design is finalized, and price the routing you actually want rather than discovering the default after it is bolted to your wall.
