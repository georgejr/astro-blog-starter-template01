Picture it as year four. One panel is reading zero on the monitoring app for no reason you can see, or there's a faint water stain blooming on the ceiling in the room under the array. Your first instinct is entirely practical: who do I call, and who's going to pay for this? The answer turns out to depend almost completely on *what* failed — because your solar system isn't protected by a single warranty. It's protected by at least two of them, written by two different companies, covering two different categories of failure, and running for two different lengths of time.

Most homeowners carry a vague mental image of "the solar warranty" as one big blanket thrown over the whole installation. In reality there is an equipment warranty from whoever manufactured the hardware, and a separate workmanship warranty from whoever installed it on your roof. They cover different things, last different amounts of time, and are honored by different parties who may or may not still be in business when you need them. Confusing the two is how people end up phoning the wrong company in a mild panic, or discovering that the failure they assumed was covered was never anyone's responsibility to fix. Sorting the distinction out calmly, before you sign anything, is far easier than sorting it out mid-leak with a bucket on the floor.

## Two contracts, not one

The cleanest way to hold the whole distinction in your head is to ask a single question whenever something goes wrong: is the problem with the *thing*, or with the *installation of the thing*? Almost every solar failure sorts into one of those two bins, and which bin it lands in tells you which warranty applies and which company owns the fix.

The equipment warranty comes from the manufacturer of each individual component — the panel maker, the inverter maker, the battery maker if you have storage. It covers defects in the product itself: a panel that delaminates or develops internal cell failures, an inverter that quits from an internal fault, cells that lose capacity faster than the spec sheet promised. The manufacturer stands behind the box it built, and it does so regardless of who bought it, who sold it, or who bolted it to your roof. That warranty travels with the hardware. If the panel is defective, the panel's maker is on the hook, full stop.

The workmanship warranty comes from your installer, and it covers something entirely different: their labor and their craft. The quality of the mounting, the wiring, the roof penetrations and their flashing, the general workmanship of attaching a power plant to your particular house — all of that is the installer's domain. If a panel itself is perfectly fine but the racking that holds it works loose, or a flashing detail was done sloppily and water finds its way in, that's a workmanship problem. The manufacturer of a flawless panel has nothing whatsoever to do with a bad bolt or a poorly sealed penetration, and it will, quite reasonably, decline to help.

Those two warranties differ in more than what they cover — they differ in how long they last and in who you'd even call, which is exactly why folding them into a single mental "solar warranty" leads to real mistakes. Equipment warranties are long and fairly standardized: the panel maker's product coverage and performance guarantee run for decades, written into the module's spec sheet, and they look broadly similar from one reputable brand to the next. Workmanship warranties are the wild card. They come from your installer, carry no industry-standard length, and are the one term you can actually shop and compare between competing bids. So when someone assumes "my system is under warranty for 25 years" and pictures one company on the hook for everything, they've quietly merged a 25-year panel product warranty, a shorter inverter warranty, and a workmanship warranty that might be five years or might be twenty-five — three separate promises running on three separate clocks. Walk the year-four scenario through both lenses and the stakes come into focus. If that zero-reading panel turns out to have an internal defect, the panel manufacturer owes you a replacement module, but getting the dead one down and the new one up is labor, and labor is the installer's workmanship territory. If instead the panel is fine and the reason it reads zero is a connector crimped poorly during installation, there's no defective product at all — it's pure workmanship, and the manufacturer owes you nothing. Same symptom on the app, two completely different companies responsible, and the only way to tell them apart is to correctly identify whether the failure lives in the thing or in how the thing was installed.

None of this is obscure once you've seen it laid out, but almost nobody thinks about it while signing, because at signing everything works and both warranties feel like abstractions on a page. The cost of that inattention gets paid years later, in the moment something breaks and you have to work out — under mild stress, possibly with water coming through a ceiling — which of two documents applies and which of two companies to dial. Reading both while the panels are still boxed on the driveway is far cheaper than reading them in the middle of the emergency they were written for. The half-hour it takes to read them at signing is the cheapest work in the whole project, and it's the moment you hold the most leverage — before the check clears, while the installer still wants your business and will happily walk you through exactly who covers what. Wait until something fails and you've lost both the leverage and the calm. The distinction matters because both warranties can be in force at the same moment, and a single visible symptom — that zero-reading panel, that ceiling stain — can trace back to either one. You cannot know which company to call until you know which kind of failure you're actually looking at, and that is exactly why the split is worth understanding before you ever need it.

## What each warranty actually promises

Manufacturer coverage tends to arrive in layers, and the layers carry different lengths, which trips people up because they hear "25-year warranty" and assume one clean number covers everything. Solar panels typically come with two separate manufacturer warranties, not one. There's a *product* warranty against defects — commonly 25 years on current modules — which handles the panel physically failing. And there's a distinct *performance* warranty, which guarantees the panel will still produce at least some stated percentage of its rated output after a given number of years. That performance guarantee is the one that caps how fast a panel is allowed to fade over time; it's not about the panel breaking, it's about the panel quietly weakening beyond what the maker promised. Inverters carry their own terms entirely, and those vary sharply by inverter type for reasons rooted in how the different designs age and how likely each is to need replacing during the system's life — the inverter being the component most owners can expect to address first, a pattern worth understanding on its own in [why the inverter is the part you'll likely replace first](/blog/solar-inverter-lifespan-replacement/).

What equipment warranties generally do *not* include is the cost of the labor to swap the failed part. This is the gap that surprises people. A manufacturer will cheerfully ship you a replacement panel or inverter under warranty — and then getting the dead one off your roof and the new one wired in is a separate cost that lands on you, unless something else covers it. That "something else" is usually the workmanship warranty, which is precisely why the two documents have to be read together rather than one at a time.

The workmanship warranty is the one buyers consistently underrate, because it protects the part of the system that is unique to your house: the holes drilled in your roof and the wiring run through your walls. These warranties vary enormously from one installer to the next — anywhere from a couple of years to 25, with no industry-standard length to anchor expectations. A longer workmanship warranty is one of the more meaningful signals a company can send, because it's a bet on the quality of its own crew's work and, implicitly, a bet that the company will still be around to honor the promise. Weighing that promise is a core part of [vetting an installer](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/), not a detail to skim past on the way to comparing prices. A solid workmanship warranty covers the labor to correct installation faults, and — this is the clause worth hunting for — it often bundles in the labor to handle equipment failures too, so that a manufacturer-supplied replacement part gets installed at no cost to you. Read for that bundling specifically, because a system with a superb equipment warranty and a thin workmanship warranty can still leave you paying the service call every single time a covered part fails.

The workmanship fear that comes up more than any other is a roof leak, and it deserves a straight answer rather than either reassurance or alarm. A properly flashed solar mount is a well-understood, thoroughly weatherproof detail, and a competent installation rarely leaks — the evidence on this is genuinely reassuring and is worked through in depth in [whether solar actually damages your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/). But *rarely* is not *never*, and if a penetration does fail, the workmanship warranty is what pays to repair both the mount and the surrounding roof. That is why the length and the specific language of roof coverage inside a workmanship warranty matter so much. Look for explicit wording about roof penetrations and water intrusion, not just a vague line about "installation." An installer confident in their own flashing will put that confidence in writing; one who won't may be telling you something.

Two housekeeping points make either warranty easier to actually collect on. The first is registration: several manufacturers require you to register the product within a window after installation for the full term to apply, and an unregistered panel or inverter can quietly drop to a shorter default coverage. Ask your installer whether they handle registration or whether it falls to you. The second is documentation. A warranty claim years from now goes far more smoothly if you've kept the proposal, the equipment list with model and serial numbers, the permits, and the commissioning report that recorded your system's expected production — that last one matters especially for performance and workmanship claims, because proving a shortfall requires a baseline to measure against. If your system includes a battery, note that it carries its own manufacturer warranty on the same equipment-versus-workmanship logic: the cells and electronics are the maker's responsibility, and the installation of them is the installer's.

## When something breaks, whose phone rings

Once you're holding the distinction clearly, the routing of any given failure becomes almost mechanical. A panel or inverter that fails internally is an equipment matter: the manufacturer supplies the replacement part, and whether you pay for the labor to install it depends on your workmanship warranty. A mount that loosens, wiring that was done wrong, or a penetration that leaks is a workmanship matter, and the installer owns the repair. Output that slowly drifts below the guaranteed curve is a performance-warranty matter, handled by the panel manufacturer if the shortfall exceeds the guaranteed threshold. And a system that underproduces from day one because it was badly designed or wired is workmanship again — which is a good argument for documenting your production early, so you have a baseline to point to if the numbers never come up to where they should be.

In practice, though, almost all of that routing happens behind a single phone call, and the number you dial first is almost always your installer's. A good one acts as your single point of contact: they diagnose the problem, tell you honestly whether it's their labor or the manufacturer's hardware at fault, and file the manufacturer claim on your behalf if it turns out to be an equipment defect. That convenience — one call, one company managing the whole thing — is one of the quiet reasons a strong installer is worth paying for. But it is only worth something if the company is still in business years down the line, which is the uncomfortable truth sitting underneath both warranties at once.

A written warranty is only ever as strong as the company standing behind it. A 25-year workmanship promise from an installer who folds in year six is worth exactly nothing in year seven, and at that point the manufacturer equipment warranty — which survives your installer's bankruptcy because it was never the installer's promise to begin with — becomes your only recourse, labor costs and all. That's the quiet asymmetry worth internalizing: the manufacturer's coverage outlives your installer, while the installer's coverage dies with the company — so the more of your protection rides on workmanship, the more the installer's durability matters to you. That asymmetry is the real risk to weigh, and it's the reason installer stability belongs in your decision right alongside the price on the quote. Neither warranty is a figure you can type into the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) as a tidy dollar amount. But the replacement and repair costs these warranties either absorb or leave on your plate are exactly the kind of mid-life expense that quietly shapes your long-run return, so they belong in the back of your mind while you model it. Read both documents before you sign, and put the one blunt question to every installer that cuts straight through the paperwork: if this specific thing breaks in ten years, who fixes it, and who pays?

Protecting yourself against all of this is less about legal sophistication than about a few concrete habits. Get every warranty in writing and actually read the workmanship document, since it's the one that varies and the one your installer controls; a company confident in its craft will hand it over without hesitation and explain the roof-penetration language when you ask. Weigh the firm's staying power the way you'd weigh any long-term contractor's, because a warranty is fundamentally a bet on the company still answering the phone a decade out — how long they've been in business, whether they're local or national, and what their track record looks like all bear on that bet. Some larger installers now back their workmanship promises with a third-party administrator precisely so the coverage survives even if the original company doesn't; where that structure exists, it's worth understanding who actually stands behind the paper. Ask, too, how both warranties handle a home sale. A workmanship-and-equipment package that transfers cleanly to a buyer is a genuine selling point when you list the house, while one that evaporates or charges a fee to move is a small liability you'd rather know about in advance than discover at closing. And keep the paperwork somewhere you'll find it in ten years, because the best-written warranty in the world does you no good if you can't produce the documents when the moment finally comes. None of these steps is difficult — they're just the kind of thing that's easy to skip in the excitement of going solar and painful to have skipped when a panel quits in year eight. These two warranties are the entire safety net under a system you expect to run for decades, and a couple of hours spent understanding them at the outset is the cheapest insurance in the whole project.
