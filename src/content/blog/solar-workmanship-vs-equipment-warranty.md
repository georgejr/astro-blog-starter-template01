Imagine it's year four. You notice one panel reading zero on the monitoring app, or a water stain blooming on the ceiling below the array. Your first question is practical: who do I call, and who pays? The answer depends entirely on *what* failed — because your solar system isn't protected by one warranty. It's protected by at least two, written by two different companies, covering two different kinds of failure.

Homeowners tend to picture "the solar warranty" as a single blanket. In reality there's an equipment warranty from whoever manufactured the hardware, and a workmanship warranty from whoever installed it. They cover different things, run for different lengths, and are honored by different parties. Confusing the two is how people end up calling the wrong company, or assuming they're covered for something that nobody actually agreed to cover. Sorting them out before you sign is far easier than sorting them out during a leak.

## Two contracts, not one

The cleanest way to hold the distinction is by asking whether a problem is with the *thing* or with the *installation of the thing*.

The **equipment warranty** comes from the manufacturer of each component — the panel maker, the inverter maker, the battery maker. It covers defects in the product itself: a panel that delaminates, an inverter that fails internally, cells that lose capacity faster than promised. The manufacturer stands behind the box regardless of who bolted it down.

The **workmanship warranty** comes from your installer. It covers their labor — the quality of the mounting, the wiring, the roof penetrations, the general craft of putting the system on your house. If a panel is fine but the racking works loose, or a flashing detail was done poorly and water gets in, that's workmanship. The manufacturer of a perfectly good panel has nothing to do with a bad bolt.

Both can be in force at once, and a single visible symptom can trace to either. That's exactly why the split matters.

## What the equipment warranty actually promises

Manufacturer coverage usually comes in layers, and the layers have different lengths.

Solar panels typically carry two separate manufacturer warranties: a *product* warranty against defects (commonly 25 years on current modules) and a *performance* warranty guaranteeing the panel still produces some percentage of its rated output after a given period — the guarantee that limits how fast the panel is allowed to fade. Inverters carry their own terms, which vary sharply by type; the reasons behind those differences are worth understanding on their own, and they're laid out in the [comparison of inverter warranty lengths](/blog/solar-inverter-warranties/).

What equipment warranties generally *don't* include is the cost of the labor to swap the part. A manufacturer will ship you a replacement panel; getting the dead one off the roof and the new one wired in is a separate cost — and that's frequently where the workmanship warranty, or its absence, becomes the deciding factor.

## What the workmanship warranty actually promises

This is the one buyers underrate, because it's the one that protects the part of the system unique to your house: the holes in your roof and the wiring in your walls.

Workmanship warranties vary enormously between installers — anywhere from a couple of years to 25, with no industry-standard length. A longer workmanship warranty is one of the more meaningful signals a company sends, because it's a bet on their own crew's craft and, implicitly, on the company still existing to honor it. Weighing that promise is a core part of [vetting an installer](/blog/choosing-a-solar-installer/) rather than an afterthought.

A solid workmanship warranty typically covers the labor to correct installation faults and, importantly, often bundles the labor to handle equipment failures too — so that a manufacturer-supplied replacement part gets installed at no cost to you. Read for that bundling specifically. A system with a great equipment warranty and a thin workmanship warranty can still leave you paying the service call every time a covered part fails.

### The roof-penetration question everyone worries about

The single most common workmanship fear is a roof leak, and it deserves a straight answer. A properly flashed solar mount is a well-understood, weatherproof detail, and a competent install rarely leaks — the evidence on this is reassuring, and it's covered in depth in [whether solar actually damages your roof](/blog/does-solar-damage-your-roof/). But *rarely* isn't *never*, and if a penetration does fail, the workmanship warranty is what covers the repair to both the mount and the roof around it.

This is why the length and specificity of roof coverage inside a workmanship warranty matters. Look for explicit language about roof penetrations and water intrusion, not just a vague promise about "installation." An installer confident in their flashing will put that confidence in writing.

## When something breaks, whose phone rings

The practical routing, once you hold the distinction, becomes simple:

- **A panel or inverter fails internally** — the equipment warranty (manufacturer) supplies the part; whether you pay for the labor depends on your workmanship warranty.
- **A mount loosens, wiring was done wrong, or a penetration leaks** — the workmanship warranty (installer) covers it.
- **Output slowly drifts below the guaranteed curve** — the panel's performance warranty (manufacturer) applies, if the shortfall exceeds the guaranteed threshold.
- **The whole thing underproduces from day one because it was badly designed or wired** — that's workmanship, and a reason to document production early.

In every real case, though, your first call is almost always the installer. A good one acts as your single point of contact, diagnoses the problem, and files the manufacturer claim on your behalf if it turns out to be an equipment issue. That convenience is only worth anything if the company is still in business years later — which is the uncomfortable truth beneath both warranties.

A written warranty is only as strong as the company standing behind it. A 25-year workmanship promise from an installer who folds in year six is worth exactly nothing on year seven, and at that point a manufacturer equipment warranty — which survives your installer's bankruptcy — becomes your only recourse, labor cost and all. That's the risk to weigh, and it's why installer stability belongs in your decision alongside price. Neither warranty is a line item you can plug into the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) as a dollar figure, but the replacement costs they do or don't absorb are exactly the kind of mid-life expense that quietly shapes your long-run return. Read both documents before you sign, and ask the one question that cuts through the paperwork: if this specific thing breaks in ten years, who fixes it and who pays?
