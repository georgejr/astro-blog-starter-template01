The homeowners who end up disappointed with their solar usually aren't the ones who sized it wrong for the house they had. They're the ones who electrified something big a year later — a heat pump, most often — and discovered that their perfectly sized array was suddenly too small for the house they now have. The panels didn't change. The house did. A gas furnace came out, a heat pump went in, and a load that used to live entirely on the gas meter moved onto the electric meter that the solar was supposed to cover. If a heat pump is anywhere on your horizon, even a few years out, the time to account for it is while you're sizing the solar, not after the array is already on the roof and the interconnection paperwork is filed.

## A heat pump is a major new electrical load, and turning it into panels

Swapping a gas furnace for a heat pump doesn't merely change how you heat your home. It relocates a large chunk of your energy consumption from the gas side of the house to the electric side, and space heating and cooling are among the very largest loads most homes carry. A heat pump that takes over the heating a furnace used to handle can lift a home's annual electricity use substantially — not a rounding error, but a meaningful step change in kilowatt-hours. If you also swap a gas water heater for a heat pump water heater at the same time, as many electrifying households do, still more consumption shifts onto the electric meter, and the array you sized for your old all-gas-heating life falls further behind the home you actually have.

How big that jump is depends heavily on your climate and your specific home, and the range is wide enough that a single rule of thumb would mislead. A heat pump in a mild region sips power, running gently for a modest number of hours across a short, easy heating season. The same technology in a cold-winter climate works far harder, running for many more hours against a much bigger temperature difference, and consuming dramatically more energy to do it. A drafty older house asks more of a heat pump than a tight, well-insulated one at the same outdoor temperature. This is exactly why heat pumps and electric heating deserve their own careful look at usage rather than a generic estimate — the winter-heavy shape of that load, and how sharply it varies with climate and building, is what [electric heat and the winter usage spike](/blog/electric-heating-usage/) is about, and it's worth reading before you settle on a number.

To size solar for a heat pump, you need just two figures: how many kilowatt-hours the heat pump will add per year, and how much a kilowatt of solar produces annually where you live. Divide the first by the second and you have the extra array capacity the heat pump demands. A labeled example shows the arithmetic without pretending to be your quote. Assume a heat pump replacing gas heat adds roughly 3,500 kilowatt-hours a year in a moderate climate, and assume your location yields about 1,300 kilowatt-hours per kilowatt of solar annually. That works out to roughly 2.7 kilowatts of additional panels to cover the new load — call it two or three more panels' worth beyond a small buffer. Push into a colder climate where the heat pump adds 5,000 or more kilowatt-hours a year, and the extra requirement climbs toward the top of the range or past it.

| Scenario | Added heat-pump load (assumed) | Extra solar (at ~1,300 kWh/kW) |
|---|---|---|
| Mild climate, space heat | ~2,500 kWh/yr | ~2 kW |
| Moderate climate, space heat | ~3,500 kWh/yr | ~2.7 kW |
| Cold climate, space heat | ~5,000+ kWh/yr | ~4 kW or more |

For most households the answer lands somewhere in a 2–4 kW window of added capacity — a real increment, but a manageable one if you plan for it up front rather than discovering it after the fact. Every figure in that table is an assumption to be replaced with your own climate, home, and equipment; the value is in the method, not the specific numbers. A [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) will translate your particular added load and location into a concrete panel count, and because the heat pump is really just one input into a larger whole-house sizing question, [what size solar system do you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/) frames the full decision the heat pump feeds into rather than treating the heat pump in isolation.

Nailing down that added-load estimate is worth a little care, because it's the number everything else scales from. A heat pump's efficiency is described by its coefficient of performance — how many units of heat it delivers per unit of electricity — and that efficiency sags as the outdoor temperature falls, which is part of why cold-climate homes see such a large winter draw. If you can, base your estimate on your home's actual heating demand rather than a generic figure: your past gas usage, converted to the equivalent heat, gives a far more honest starting point than a rule of thumb, because it already reflects your insulation, your climate, and how warm you like to keep the house. Remember to fold in any other electrification happening around the same time, too. A heat pump water heater, an induction range, or an EV charger each adds its own block of new consumption, and households often stack several of these moves within a few years. Sizing solar for the heat pump alone, while ignoring the water heater you're about to add next winter, just recreates the original mistake one appliance later.

## The timing problem, and planning for the load you're about to have

There's a wrinkle that the tidy annual-kilowatt-hour math quietly hides, and it's the single most important thing to understand about pairing solar with a heat pump. A heat pump's heaviest demand falls in winter, and winter is precisely when solar production is at its weakest. Short days, low sun angles, and frequent cloud cover mean your array might produce a third or less of its summer output in December — the very month your heat pump is drawing the most power it will draw all year. So even a system sized to cover the heat pump's *annual* energy will be running a large deficit in the coldest months and a corresponding surplus in the sunniest ones. The annual figure balances, but the monthly reality is wildly out of phase: you generate the extra energy in July and need it in January, and those two facts have to be reconciled somehow.

Whether that seasonal mismatch actually costs you money depends entirely on your utility's compensation rules, and this is where two homes with identical arrays and identical heat pumps can end up in very different financial positions. Under full-retail net metering, the mismatch mostly washes out on its own. The summer surplus your array produces banks as credit that offsets the winter shortfall, so an array sized to annual usage — heat pump included — can still net close to even across the year. The grid, in effect, does the seasonal storage for you, holding your July overproduction and handing it back in January at nearly the same value. In that world, sizing to annual energy is a sound strategy, and the timing problem is real but financially absorbed.

Under net billing or low export credits, the mismatch bites hard, and sizing to annual energy becomes a trap. Your summer overproduction gets credited cheaply — often at a small fraction of retail — while your winter deficits are bought back at the full retail rate. Trying to cover a winter heat-pump load with cheap summer exports is a losing trade in that arrangement: you sell high-summer surplus for pennies and buy winter shortfall for dollars, and no amount of annual balancing rescues the economics. In that world you're generally better off sizing toward what you can actually use as you generate it, accepting that winter heating will draw meaningfully from the grid, and not overbuilding an array whose extra summer output will only ever be dumped cheaply. The broader reason usage and production swing out of phase like this — and why the swing is so much sharper once a heat pump is added to the winter side of the ledger — is worked through in [why your usage swings with the seasons](/blog/seasonal-electricity-usage/). Understanding your export rules before you size is the single factor that most separates a heat-pump solar system that pencils out from one that quietly underperforms for years, and it's the question to settle before you decide how many extra panels to buy.

The cheapest time to add capacity for a heat pump is when the array is first designed, and the cost difference is not small. Incremental panels added to a fresh install — a few more modules on a layout that's already being engineered, mounted, and interconnected — cost far less than a second, separate project a year later with its own permit, its own truck roll, its own design fee, and its own interconnection application. Doing it once is meaningfully cheaper than doing it twice, and the household that adds the heat pump load into the original design almost always spends less than the one that bolts a second phase on afterward. So if a heat pump is genuinely likely within a few years, the move is to tell your installer now and have them size for the future load, or at minimum design the array and inverter with the headroom to expand cleanly later.

If electrification isn't certain — you're considering a heat pump but haven't committed — there's a sensible middle path that avoids paying today for kilowatts you may never need. Rather than building the extra capacity now, leave physical and electrical room to grow: unused roof area where more panels could go, and an inverter that isn't already maxed out so it can accept additional modules without being replaced. That preserves your options at low cost. You're not buying the extra panels, but you're keeping the door open to add them in a single, cheaper expansion rather than a full second project if and when the heat pump actually happens. The worst outcome is the one where the array was built right up to the edge of the roof and the inverter's capacity, leaving no room to grow, so that adding the heat pump load means starting over.

Either way, the discipline is the same: start from an honest estimate of the new load rather than a guess or a salesperson's optimism. Work out how many panels your total future usage implies — current consumption plus the heat pump, and the water heater too if that's in the plan — with a [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool, decide how your export rules treat the winter mismatch, and size accordingly. A heat pump is one of the best things you can do for a home's comfort and efficiency, and none of this is an argument against installing one. It's an argument for putting it in the plan before the panels go up, so the array is built for the house you're about to have rather than sprung on one that was never designed to carry it.
