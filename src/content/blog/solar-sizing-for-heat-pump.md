The homeowners who end up disappointed with their solar aren't usually the ones who sized it wrong for the house they had. They're the ones who electrified something big a year later — a heat pump, most often — and discovered their perfectly sized array was suddenly too small for the house they now have. If a heat pump is anywhere on your horizon, the time to account for it is while you're sizing the solar, not after.

## A heat pump is a major new electrical load

Swapping a gas furnace for a heat pump doesn't just change how you heat — it moves a large chunk of your energy consumption from the gas meter to the electric meter. Space heating and cooling are among the biggest loads in most homes, and a heat pump that takes over heating can lift a home's annual electricity use substantially. If you also add a heat pump water heater, more consumption shifts onto the electric side.

The size of that jump depends heavily on your climate and your home. A heat pump in a mild region sips power; the same technology in a cold-winter climate works much harder for many more hours and consumes far more. This is why heat pumps and electric heating deserve their own careful look at usage — the winter-heavy shape of that load is exactly what [electric heat and the winter usage spike](/blog/electric-heating-usage/) is about.

## Turning added kWh into added panels

To size solar for a heat pump, you need two numbers: how many kilowatt-hours the heat pump will add per year, and how much a kilowatt of solar produces where you live. Divide the first by the second and you have the extra array capacity.

A labeled example shows the arithmetic. Assume a heat pump replacing gas heat adds roughly 3,500 kWh a year in a moderate climate, and assume your location yields about 1,300 kWh per kilowatt of solar annually. That's roughly 2.7 kW of additional panels to cover the new load — call it two or three more panels' worth beyond a small buffer. Push into a colder climate where the heat pump adds 5,000+ kWh a year, and the extra requirement climbs toward the top of the range or past it.

| Scenario | Added heat-pump load (assumed) | Extra solar (at ~1,300 kWh/kW) |
|---|---|---|
| Mild climate, space heat | ~2,500 kWh/yr | ~2 kW |
| Moderate climate, space heat | ~3,500 kWh/yr | ~2.7 kW |
| Cold climate, space heat | ~5,000+ kWh/yr | ~4 kW or more |

For most households the answer lands in a **2–4 kW** window of added capacity — a real increment, but a manageable one if you plan for it up front. These are assumptions to replace with your own; a [solar panel size calculator](/solar-panel-size-calculator/) will translate your specific added load and location into panels, and [what size solar system do you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/) frames the whole-house sizing decision the heat pump feeds into.

## The timing problem hiding in the numbers

Here's the wrinkle that annual-kWh math hides: a heat pump's heaviest demand falls in **winter**, and winter is precisely when solar production is at its weakest. Short days and low sun angles mean your array might produce a third or less of its summer output in December — the same month your heat pump is drawing the most. So even a system sized to cover the heat pump's *annual* energy will be running a large deficit in the coldest months and a surplus in the sunniest ones.

Whether that seasonal mismatch matters to your wallet depends entirely on your utility's rules:

- **Under full-retail net metering,** it mostly washes out. Summer surplus banks as credit that offsets winter shortfall, so an array sized to annual usage — heat pump included — can still net close to even across the year. The bank is doing the seasonal storage for you.
- **Under net billing or low export credits,** the mismatch bites. Summer overproduction gets credited cheaply while winter deficits are bought back at full retail, so covering the winter heat-pump load with cheap summer exports is a losing trade. In that world you're better off sizing toward what you can use, and accepting that winter heating will draw meaningfully from the grid.

The broader reason usage and production swing out of phase like this is worked through in [why your usage swings with the seasons](/blog/seasonal-electricity-usage/), and it's the single most important factor separating a heat-pump solar system that pencils out from one that quietly underperforms.

## Plan for the load you're about to have

The cheapest time to add capacity for a heat pump is when the array is first designed — incremental panels on a fresh install cost far less than a second project with its own permit, truck roll, and interconnection. If a heat pump is likely within a few years, tell your installer now and have them size for the future load, or at least design the array and inverter with headroom to expand.

If electrification isn't certain, a middle path is to leave physical and electrical room to grow — unused roof area, an inverter that isn't maxed out — rather than building the extra kilowatts today. Either way, start from an honest estimate of the new load. Work out how many panels your total future usage implies with a [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool, decide how your export rules treat the winter mismatch, and size accordingly. A heat pump is one of the best things you can do for a home's efficiency; it just deserves to be in the plan before the panels go up, not sprung on an array that was never built to carry it.
