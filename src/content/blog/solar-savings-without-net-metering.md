Ask homeowners in areas with weak or vanishing net metering what worries them, and the answer is usually some version of the same fear: that solar has quietly stopped working. It hasn't. What's changed is *where* the savings come from, and once you see that clearly, most of the anxiety drains out of the question. Under full retail net metering, the grid banks every surplus kilowatt-hour at full price and hands it back after dark, so you barely have to think about timing — the meter does the accounting for you. Strip that away and the value collapses onto a single, durable idea: the electricity you make and use yourself, in the same moment, is worth the full retail price you would otherwise have paid the utility. That kilowatt-hour never touches the grid, so no export rule, tariff change, or rate committee can discount it.

That's the pivot the whole question turns on. Solar without net metering is still solar. It's just solar where self-consumption does the heavy lifting instead of the utility's meter. The homes that thrive in this environment aren't the ones with the best panels or the sunniest roofs. They're the ones that happen to use power while the sun is up, or that add a way to store it for when they don't.

## Where the value actually comes from once exports stop paying

True zero compensation for exports is rare. Far more common is some version of [net billing or an export rate](/blog/solar-export-rates/), where the power you push back to the grid earns a reduced credit — often a fraction of the retail rate, sometimes pegged to a wholesale market price that shifts hour to hour. The distinction from [full net metering](/blog/net-metering-explained/) is the whole story, because it flips the incentive on its head. Under retail netting there's little reason to care whether a given kilowatt-hour is used at home or exported; both are worth the same. Under an export rate, every kilowatt-hour you keep on-site is worth several times more than one you send back. So the practical question isn't the abstract "is solar worth it without net metering." It's sharper and more answerable: what fraction of my production can I actually use myself, and what is the rest worth when it leaves?

That fraction has a name — self-consumption, the share of your solar production your home uses directly rather than exporting — and it's the single biggest lever on your savings once retail netting is gone. A worked example shows how much it moves the outcome. Assume a system produces 9,000 kWh a year, your retail rate is $0.18 per kWh, and your export rate is $0.05 per kWh; treat all three as illustrative figures rather than a quote for your utility. If you self-consume 30% of production, you avoid buying 2,700 kWh at $0.18, which saves $486, and you export the remaining 6,300 kWh at $0.05, which earns $315 — roughly $801 for the year. Now lift self-consumption to 55% by moving some usage into daylight hours. You avoid 4,950 kWh of imports at $0.18 for $891, and export 4,050 kWh at $0.05 for $203, totaling about $1,094. Same panels, same sun, same weather — roughly 37% more value, produced entirely by using more of your own power instead of selling it cheap and buying it back dear.

Those numbers are labeled assumptions, not a promise, but the direction they point is reliable across almost any export-rate regime: without net metering, raising self-consumption is the highest-return move available to you, and much of it costs nothing. This is also the reason two homes with identical hardware can post very different savings under the same tariff. The one whose daily rhythm happens to line up with its production keeps far more of the retail value; the one that exports most of its midday output hands that value to the utility at a discount. Before you conclude solar doesn't pencil in export-rate territory, it's worth running your own rates and a realistic self-consumption estimate through a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) to see where your break-even genuinely lands, because the generic "no net metering kills solar" verdict assumes a self-consumption fraction you may be able to beat.

Estimating your own self-consumption fraction is less mysterious than it sounds, and it's worth doing before you accept anyone's savings figure. It comes down to the overlap between two shapes: the curve of your household's demand across a typical day, and the arc of your panels' production, which rises through the morning, peaks near midday, and fades by evening. A retiree at home all day, a remote worker whose home office hums from nine to five, or a household running a pool pump and central air through summer afternoons naturally captures a large share of production without trying. A house that empties at eight and refills at six captures far less, because the panels are busiest at exactly the hours nobody's drawing power. The fraction also shifts by season in ways an annual average quietly hides: a mild spring day with long sun might see you export most of what you make, while a scorching July afternoon with the air conditioner running flat out could push self-consumption well above your yearly average. That's why a single self-consumption number — whether it comes from you or from an installer's spreadsheet — is only ever a reasoned estimate rather than a fixed property of your home. If you want to move past estimation entirely, most utilities now let you download interval usage data, often at hourly or fifteen-minute resolution, through an online account; laid against a modeled production curve for your system, that data shows the overlap directly, hour by hour, and turns the whole question from a guess into a measurement you can act on. Either way, the useful move is to treat self-consumption as a lever you can pull rather than a fact you inherit.

## Shifting loads into the sun before you spend a dollar

The cheapest way to lift self-consumption is also the least glamorous: move flexible loads into the middle of the day, when the panels are producing at full tilt. The candidates are the appliances that run for a long stretch and don't much care *when* they run. An electric water heater on a timer, a pool pump, a dishwasher, a clothes dryer, and EV charging can often be rescheduled from evening to late morning through mid-afternoon with nothing more than a timer or a phone app. A household that runs a pool pump six hours a day can simply run those six hours across the solar peak instead of after dinner. None of this requires new equipment. It requires paying attention to timing, and every kilowatt-hour you redirect from an evening grid draw to a midday self-supply is worth the full spread between the retail rate you'd have paid and the export rate you'd have earned.

The water heater deserves special mention because it's an accidental battery. A well-insulated tank holds heat for hours, so heating water during the solar peak and drawing on it through the evening effectively stores solar energy as hot water at no extra hardware cost. The same logic applies, in a smaller way, to running the dishwasher on a delay-start so its heating element fires at 1 p.m. rather than 7 p.m., or to precooling the house with the air conditioner during peak production on a hot afternoon so the system coasts through the expensive evening hours. Each of these moves nudges the self-consumption fraction upward, and because they compound, a handful of them together can shift a home from the low-30s into the 50s without a single dollar of capital spent.

There is a ceiling to this, and honesty requires marking it. You can shift the water heater and the pool pump, but you can't shift your family being at work and school through the peak production hours. A home that's empty from eight to five has a structural gap between what it naturally consumes at midday and what the panels are making, and no amount of clever scheduling closes it entirely. Some loads are simply fixed to the evening — cooking dinner, the bulk of the lighting, the television, the second load of laundry after the kids are down. That residual gap, the surplus your home can't absorb no matter how you arrange the timers, is exactly the gap a battery is built to close, which is where the arithmetic gets more interesting and considerably more expensive.

Most of these shifts are worth automating rather than white-knuckling by hand, because a schedule you have to remember every evening is a schedule you'll abandon within a month. A basic timer on the water heater, a delay-start on the dishwasher and washing machine, a smart thermostat set to precool on a schedule, and an EV charger programmed to begin at midday instead of the instant you plug in at six all turn a nightly decision into a background setting you configure once and forget. That automation is what separates a durable ten-point jump in self-consumption from a good intention that fades by the second month, and the payoff isn't only discipline — it's predictability. A home that reliably self-consumes 55% is a home whose solar savings you can actually forecast, rather than one whose numbers swing with how attentive you happened to feel that month. It's worth being honest about the friction, though, because not every household can move much. Shift workers, families with small children on rigid routines, and homes whose biggest loads are cooking dinner and running the evening simply don't have flexible daytime demand to redirect. And even a well-optimized home eventually hits a wall, because the flexible loads are finite: once the water heater, the pool pump, the dishwasher, and the car are all running on the sun, there's nothing left to move, and whatever midday surplus remains just exports at the low rate. Recognizing that wall matters, because it's the exact point where free behavioral changes stop paying and the only way to capture more value is to spend money storing energy rather than merely timing it.

## Where a battery changes the arithmetic — and where it doesn't

Under full retail net metering, a home battery is a genuinely tough financial sell, because exporting surplus already pays full price — there's no spread for storage to capture, and the battery mostly buys you backup and independence rather than savings. Take retail netting away and the logic inverts cleanly. When exports earn $0.05 and imports cost $0.18, storing your midday surplus and discharging it in the evening captures that difference instead of giving it away. The battery becomes a machine for converting cheap exports into avoided expensive imports: each kilowatt-hour you'd otherwise have sold for $0.05 and bought back for $0.18 is worth about $0.13 of recovered spread when you cycle it through storage instead — minus round-trip losses of roughly 10 to 15%, which you should keep in the calculation rather than wishing away.

Whether that spread justifies the hardware depends on three things you can actually estimate. The first is the battery's installed cost against its usable capacity, since you're effectively buying a fixed number of stored kilowatt-hours per day for the life of the unit. The second is how many days a year you genuinely have surplus to store — a battery that sits full and idle through a cloudy winter earns nothing in those months. The third is the size of the retail-to-export spread itself, because a $0.13 gap makes storage attractive while a $0.02 gap makes it nearly pointless. This is why storage economics look so different in export-rate territory than under net metering, a shift worked through in more depth in [what a battery does to solar payback](/blog/solar-battery-payback-impact/). A [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) can help you size the daily surplus you'd actually be storing and test whether the recovered spread covers the cost of the box. A battery also delivers something a spreadsheet tends to undervalue: backup power during outages, which many owners weigh as a separate benefit entirely apart from the export arbitrage, and which can tip a borderline decision.

There's a natural sequence to all of this that keeps you from overspending. Exhaust the free moves first — shift every load you reasonably can — and only then size a battery to the surplus that actually remains, rather than to your raw midday overproduction. A home that has already lifted self-consumption from 30% to 55% through scheduling has captured most of the easy value, so the battery only needs to mop up the smaller leftover surplus, which means a smaller and cheaper battery does the job. Sizing storage to your pre-shifting surplus instead buys capacity you'd never have needed had you set a few timers first, and the gap between a battery sized to your real residual and one sized to your gross overproduction is often several thousand dollars of hardware you either genuinely need or genuinely don't. It's also worth holding the whole decision a little loosely, because the rules can move. Export rates and net-billing structures are set by regulators and utilities, and they do change over time — sometimes in your favor, sometimes against it. A home wired and equipped to lean on self-consumption is well positioned either way, because it already keeps most of its value on-site regardless of what the export rate does next. That resilience is a quiet argument for the self-consumption approach beyond the immediate math: it leans less on a policy you don't control and more on habits and hardware you do.

Honesty cuts the other way as well. There are households where losing net metering genuinely undermines the case, and pretending otherwise helps no one. A home with very low daytime occupancy, few or no shiftable loads, an expensive installation, and an export rate close to zero may find that most of its production leaves the property at a steep discount while the payback stretches uncomfortably long. Adding a battery helps, but a battery is itself a large capital expense that has to earn its keep, and stacking it on top of an already-marginal solar investment doesn't automatically rescue the numbers. The clear-eyed takeaway is that solar without net metering rewards a specific kind of buyer — one who uses power during the day, or is willing to shift loads and possibly store energy, and who runs the math on their own rates rather than borrowing someone else's optimism. If that's not your household and your export rate is punishing, model it carefully before committing rather than assuming the savings survive the loss of full netting unchanged. The panels still work exactly as advertised. The open question is whether your usage pattern lets them work for you.
