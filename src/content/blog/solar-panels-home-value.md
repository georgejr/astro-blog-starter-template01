Start with a worked illustration to fix the scale. Assume a $350,000 home and a resale premium of 4% for an owned solar system — a figure in the low-single-digit range that research on home sales has generally landed near, used here only as an example, not a promise. That works out to about a $14,000 bump at sale. Set that against a typical system's net cost and you can see why the resale question matters: for many homeowners, the added value recovers a meaningful share of what the panels cost, sometimes most of it, occasionally more, depending on the market and the system.

The catch is that "4% premium" is an average pulled across many homes and markets, and your house is one data point, not the average. The premium is real and repeatedly observed, but it's conditional — on who owns the panels, how the appraiser handles them, and whether local buyers value solar at all. Understanding those conditions is the difference between counting on a number and hoping for one.

## What the research actually shows

Studies of home sales, including large analyses of transactions across multiple states, have consistently found that homes with owned solar sell for more than comparable homes without it. The premium tends to land in a modest percentage range rather than dollar-for-dollar with system cost, and it varies widely by region — larger in markets where electricity is expensive and solar is common and understood, smaller or negligible where power is cheap and buyers are unfamiliar with panels.

Two findings recur across this work and are worth carrying into your own expectations:

- The premium is a percentage of home value that's meaningful but not enormous, generally in the low single digits, and it's an average that hides a wide spread between markets.
- Buyer familiarity matters. In areas with lots of installed solar, buyers price it confidently; in areas with little, they discount or ignore it, so the same panels add more resale value in a solar-savvy market than a skeptical one.

The reason a premium exists at all is straightforward: an owned solar system is a paid-off appliance that lowers the home's operating cost for decades. A buyer inheriting lower electric bills is buying a house that's cheaper to live in, and that's worth paying for — the same underlying value that makes solar worth it for the original owner, framed in [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/), is what a future buyer is purchasing secondhand.

## Owned versus leased changes everything

The single biggest factor in whether solar helps or hurts a sale is who owns the panels. An owned system — bought with cash or a loan you pay off — is an asset that transfers cleanly and adds value. A leased system, or one under a power purchase agreement, is a different animal at resale, because the payments are an ongoing obligation the buyer has to assume.

With a lease or PPA, the sale can stall on paperwork: the buyer must qualify to take over the contract, or you must buy it out before closing, and some buyers simply walk rather than inherit a decades-long agreement they didn't choose. Rather than adding a premium, a transferable lease is at best neutral and at worst a friction point that complicates the deal. So the resale case for solar is really a case for *owned* solar. If lifting your home's value is part of why you're going solar, ownership isn't a detail — it's the whole mechanism.

## The appraisal gap that can strand your premium

There's a practical wrinkle between "solar adds value" and "you get that value at closing," and it lives with the appraiser. An appraisal drives what a lender will finance, and appraisers value a solar system properly only when they have the tools and comparable sales to do it. In markets with few solar home sales, an appraiser may have no comps to lean on and may undervalue or overlook the system entirely, leaving the market premium unrecognized on paper even when a buyer would happily pay it.

You can reduce that risk. Keep and hand over the documentation that lets an appraiser do the job: proof of ownership, the system size and age, warranty paperwork, and a record of actual production and bill savings. An appraiser trained to value solar — and there is a recognized methodology for it — can translate a documented, owned, producing system into appraised value. Walking in with organized records tilts the odds toward the premium showing up where it counts.

It also helps to separate the resale premium from a related benefit people conflate with it: in many states, adding solar doesn't raise your *property taxes* even as it raises your home's market value, thanks to a property tax exemption. That's a genuine plus, but it's a different mechanism from the sale-price premium — the distinction is worth understanding through [property tax exemptions for solar](/blog/solar-property-tax-exemption/), because one affects your annual tax bill and the other affects what a buyer pays.

## When resale value matters most to your decision

How much the home-value angle should sway you depends on your timeline. If you expect to stay in the house for many years, the resale premium is a distant bonus, and the day-to-day bill savings are the real return — the panels pay for themselves through the electricity they offset long before resale enters the picture. If you might sell sooner, the premium moves to center stage, because it's how you recover value you haven't yet collected in bill savings. That scenario has its own full treatment in [what happens to your solar payback if you move](/blog/solar-payback-if-you-move/), which is worth reading if a sale is anywhere on your horizon.

Either way, the cleanest way to weigh it is to model both streams together — the bill savings while you own and the estimated premium if you sell — rather than treating resale as the whole story. ## FAQ

### How much value do solar panels actually add?

Research on home sales generally finds a modest premium for owned solar, in the low-single-digit percentage of home value, though it varies widely by market and is larger where electricity is expensive and solar is common. It's an average, not a guarantee for any specific house.

### Do leased solar panels add value too?

Generally not, and they can complicate a sale. A lease or PPA is an ongoing obligation the buyer must assume or you must buy out before closing, which makes leased systems at best neutral and sometimes a deal-slowing friction point. The resale premium is really about owned systems.

### Will an appraiser count my solar system?

Only if they can. In markets with comparable solar sales and an appraiser trained to value solar, a documented owned system can be reflected in appraised value. Where solar comps are scarce, the system may be undervalued, which is why keeping ownership, production, and warranty records to hand matters.

### Do solar panels raise my property taxes?

In many states, no — a property tax exemption means the added home value from solar isn't taxed even though the market value rises. That's separate from the resale premium and applies to your annual tax bill rather than your sale price.
