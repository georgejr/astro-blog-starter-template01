Start with a worked illustration to fix the scale in your mind. Assume a $350,000 home and a resale premium of 4% for an owned solar system — a figure in the low-single-digit range that research on home sales has generally landed near, used here purely as an example rather than a promise about your house. That works out to roughly a $14,000 bump at the sale. Set that against a typical system's net cost and you can immediately see why the resale question matters so much to how people think about going solar: for many homeowners, the added value at sale recovers a meaningful share of what the panels cost, sometimes most of it, occasionally more, depending entirely on the market and the specific system.

The catch buried in that tidy example is that "4% premium" is an average pulled across many homes and many markets, and your particular house is a single data point rather than the average itself. The premium is real and has been observed repeatedly across large studies, but it is thoroughly conditional — on who actually owns the panels, on how the appraiser handles them at closing, and on whether local buyers in your market value solar at all or shrug at it. Understanding those conditions is the entire difference between counting on a number you can reasonably expect and hoping for one that may or may not materialize when you finally list. The rest of this is about which conditions turn that averaged premium into money you actually collect.

## What the research shows, and why a premium exists at all

Studies of home sales, including large analyses of many thousands of transactions across multiple states, have consistently found that homes with owned solar sell for more than otherwise-comparable homes without it. That basic finding is robust and repeated; it's not a marketing claim. The premium tends to land in a modest percentage range rather than matching system cost dollar-for-dollar, and it varies widely by region — larger in markets where electricity is expensive and solar is common and well understood, smaller or close to negligible where power is cheap and buyers have little familiarity with panels. So when people ask how much value solar actually adds, the honest answer is a modest low-single-digit percentage of home value on average, with the crucial caveat that it's an average hiding a very wide spread. Your market could sit well above it or well below it, and which side you land on is not random.

Two findings recur across this body of work and deserve to be carried straight into your own expectations. The first is that the premium is a percentage of home value that's meaningful but not enormous, generally in the low single digits, and it is an average that conceals a wide gap between hot solar markets and indifferent ones. The second is that buyer familiarity is doing a lot of the work. In areas with a great deal of installed solar, buyers price it confidently because they've seen it, understood the lower bills it brings, and internalized what it's worth; in areas with little installed solar, buyers tend to discount it or ignore it entirely because they don't know how to value an unfamiliar feature on the roof. The identical panels therefore add more resale value in a solar-savvy market than in a skeptical one, which means part of your premium was determined by your neighbors' collective familiarity with solar long before you decided to sell.

The reason a premium exists at all is refreshingly straightforward once you see it from the buyer's side. An owned solar system is a paid-off appliance that lowers the home's operating cost for decades to come. A buyer inheriting that system is buying a house that's simply cheaper to live in, month after month, with electric bills a fraction of what the house next door will run — and that lower cost of living is worth paying a premium to acquire. It's the exact same underlying value that made solar worth installing for the original owner in the first place. The logic that makes a system pay for itself through offset electricity, framed in [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/), is precisely what a future buyer is purchasing secondhand: not the panels as objects, but the decades of reduced bills those panels represent. That's why the premium attaches to owned systems and why it tracks so closely with how expensive local electricity is. Where power is costly, the future bill savings are large and buyers pay up for them; where power is cheap, the savings are small and the premium shrinks toward nothing, because there's less operating cost to inherit.

Two honest caveats keep this from hardening into a promise. The first is that the research describes averages across large samples, and averages are exactly the thing that doesn't apply cleanly to your single transaction, with your specific buyer, in your specific month. A premium that shows up reliably across thousands of sales can still fail to materialize on one particular house that happens to draw a buyer who shrugs at solar, or that sells in a slow market where every feature gets discounted. The second caveat is that the premium attaches to a system in good, documented, transferable condition — a recent, owned array with records and remaining warranty, not a fifteen-year-old system of uncertain provenance that a buyer views as a looming replacement cost rather than an asset. Age, condition, and paperwork move a specific house within that averaged range, and can push it below. This is why treating the research as a floor you're guaranteed is a mistake, while treating it as a well-supported central expectation you can either strengthen or undermine through ownership and documentation is the right frame. The studies tell you which direction solar pushes home value and roughly how hard; they don't hand you a number to deposit. What you actually collect depends on the conditions the next sections lay out — whether you own the system outright, and whether the appraiser and the buyer can even see and price what you're handing them.

## Owned versus leased, and the appraisal gap that can strand your premium

The single biggest factor in whether solar helps or actively hurts a sale is who owns the panels, and it's a larger factor than market or region. An owned system — bought with cash or a loan you've paid off — is an asset that transfers cleanly to the buyer and adds value the way any valuable improvement does. A leased system, or one under a power purchase agreement, is an entirely different animal at resale, because the payments are an ongoing obligation the buyer must agree to assume rather than a benefit they inherit for free. This is where the answer to "do leased panels add value too" turns firmly negative. With a lease or PPA, the sale can stall on the paperwork: the buyer has to qualify to take over the contract, or you have to buy it out before closing, and some buyers simply walk away rather than inherit a decades-long agreement they didn't choose and don't fully understand. Rather than adding a premium, a transferable lease is at best neutral and at worst a genuine friction point that complicates or even kills the deal. So the resale case for solar is really a case for owned solar specifically. If lifting your home's value is any part of why you're going solar, ownership isn't a detail to decide later — it's the entire mechanism by which the value gets created and transferred.

For an owned system, a little presentation work at listing time helps the premium survive contact with the market. A one-page summary a buyer's agent can hand over — the system size, its age, who made the panels and inverter, what the warranties cover and how long they run, plus a year or two of actual production and bill-savings figures — turns an abstract "has solar" bullet into a concrete, valued feature. Buyers and their agents discount what they can't easily understand, and solar remains unfamiliar enough in many markets that a clear explanation of exactly what the new owner inherits does real work on the price. It also preempts the two questions that otherwise stall a solar sale: is it owned or leased, and how old is it. Answer both up front and in writing, and you remove a chunk of the friction that quietly shaves the premium in markets where buyers are wary of anything they'd have to go research themselves.

Even with an owned system, there's a practical wrinkle between "solar adds value" and "you actually collect that value at closing," and it lives with the appraiser. An appraisal drives what a lender will finance, and appraisers can only value a solar system properly when they have both the training and the comparable sales to do it. In markets with few solar home sales to reference, an appraiser may have no comps to lean on and may undervalue the system or overlook it entirely, leaving the real market premium unrecognized on paper even in cases where a buyer would happily pay it. That's the answer to whether an appraiser will count your solar: only if they can, meaning only where there are comparable solar sales and an appraiser trained to value them. You can meaningfully reduce that risk with preparation. Keep and hand over the documentation that lets an appraiser do the job right — proof of ownership, the system size and age, warranty paperwork, and a record of actual production and bill savings over the years you've owned it. There is a recognized professional methodology for valuing solar, and an appraiser equipped to use it can translate a documented, owned, producing system into appraised value rather than guessing at it or ignoring it. Walking into the appraisal with organized records tilts the odds toward the premium showing up where it actually counts, on the paperwork that governs the sale price and the loan.

It helps, finally, to separate the resale premium from a related benefit that people constantly conflate with it. In many states, adding solar doesn't raise your property taxes even as it raises your home's market value, thanks to a property tax exemption written specifically to encourage solar. That's a genuine and valuable perk, but it operates through a completely different mechanism than the sale-price premium, and mixing them up leads to muddled expectations. The distinction is worth understanding clearly through [property tax exemptions for solar](/blog/solar-property-tax-exemption/), because one affects your annual tax bill every year you own the home, while the other affects only what a buyer pays when you eventually sell. So no, in many states solar won't raise your property taxes even though it raises your home's assessed market value — and that's a feature, not a loophole, though it's a separate thing from the resale premium and shouldn't be added to it as if it were the same benefit counted twice.

## When resale should actually sway your decision

How much weight the home-value angle deserves in your decision depends heavily on your timeline, and getting this right keeps you from either over- or under-valuing resale. If you expect to stay in the house for many years, the resale premium is a distant, uncertain bonus, and the day-to-day bill savings are the real return you should be underwriting the decision on. The panels pay for themselves through the electricity they offset, month after month, long before resale ever enters the picture, and by the time you do sell you'll have already collected most of the system's value in lower bills. In that case, leaning on the resale premium to justify the purchase is unnecessary and a little beside the point; the savings already carry it. If, on the other hand, you might sell sooner — a few years out rather than a few decades — then the premium moves to center stage, because it becomes the primary way you recover value you haven't yet collected in accumulated bill savings. Selling before you've broken even is exactly the scenario where a strong, well-documented resale premium turns a solar investment from a loss into a wash or a modest gain, and that specific situation has its own full treatment in [what happens to your solar payback if you move](/blog/solar-payback-if-you-move/), worth reading closely if a sale is anywhere on your horizon.

The trickiest version of this is selling while a solar loan is still outstanding, and it's worth thinking through before it becomes a scramble at closing. If you financed the panels and haven't paid the loan off, selling means the loan has to be settled, usually from the sale proceeds, and what you actually pocket is the home-value premium minus whatever balance remains. When the premium roughly matches or exceeds the payoff, the solar washes out cleanly or leaves you a little ahead. When the balance is larger than the premium the market will grant, you're covering the difference out of the rest of your equity, which is the resale version of not yet having broken even. This is precisely why ownership structure and timing interact so much: a paid-off owned system is pure upside at sale, a nearly-paid-off one is close to it, and a freshly financed one transfers both a premium and a debt that may not fully offset each other. It's also why the resale premium shouldn't be double-counted against the bill savings you've already banked, because the two are sequential rather than additive on the same dollars. The savings you collected while living there are real and already yours; the premium is what you recover of the value you hadn't yet collected in savings. Adding a full averaged premium on top of years of full bill savings, as though both applied to the entire system cost, quietly overstates the return. The clean model is that the system pays you in electricity savings for as long as you own it, and then, if you sell before it has fully paid for itself that way, the resale premium recovers some of the unrecouped remainder. Frame it like that and you neither ignore the premium nor lean on it harder than it can bear.

Either way, the cleanest approach is to model both value streams together rather than treating resale as the whole story or dismissing it entirely. Count the bill savings you'll collect while you own the home and the estimated premium you'd capture if you sell, and look at how they combine over your realistic ownership window. Leaning on a single averaged percentage to justify the whole decision is fragile, because that average might not hold in your market; leaning on the two streams together is far sturdier, because even a disappointing resale premium is backstopped by the bill savings you banked along the way, and a strong premium becomes upside on top of them. Treat the resale premium as real but conditional, size your confidence in it to how solar-savvy your local market is, and let the bill savings do the heavy lifting of justifying the system. Do that and the home-value question takes its proper place — an important factor if you're selling soon, a pleasant bonus if you're staying put, and in neither case the entire basis for the decision.
