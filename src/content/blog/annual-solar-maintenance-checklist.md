Solar gets sold as maintenance-free, and that is close enough to true that the phrase rarely gets challenged. A rooftop array has no moving parts, no fuel, and no scheduled service the way a furnace does. But close enough is not the same as nothing, and the small gap between them is exactly where an unnoticed problem can quietly cost you production for months. A system that has silently lost 15% of its output still looks fine from the driveway. An hour of deliberate attention once a year is what turns "probably fine" into "confirmed fine," and it is the cheapest insurance a solar owner has.

## Why a once-a-year rhythm is enough

The reason solar needs so little maintenance is the same reason it needs *some*. With nothing mechanical to wear out, most problems are slow and undramatic: a gradual drop in output, a connector loosening over years of thermal cycling, moss creeping along a panel edge, a critter nesting under the array. None of these announce themselves. They accumulate. An annual checkup matches that pace — frequent enough to catch a slow decline before it becomes a season of lost production, infrequent enough that it is a genuinely small chore.

Pick a consistent time of year so it actually happens. Late winter or early spring is a natural slot: you head into the high-production months with a clean bill of health, and any winter storm damage gets caught before summer.

## The checkup, from the ground up

The single most useful check requires no ladder at all.

### Start with your production numbers

Your monitoring app is the most sensitive diagnostic you own, and it sees problems your eyes never will. Compare this year's monthly production against last year's, month for month. A small decline — a percent or so a year — is normal [panel degradation](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/) and nothing to worry about. A sudden step down, one string or panel lagging its neighbors, or a month that is far below its counterpart a year ago is the signal worth chasing. Weather moves the whole curve up and down together; a real fault distorts its shape or drops one part of the array while the rest holds. Knowing which [monitoring numbers signal trouble](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) turns the app from a curiosity into an early-warning system.

If the numbers look healthy, the rest of the checkup is confirmation. If they do not, the rest tells you where to look.

### Then look at the array — from a safe vantage

You do not need to walk the roof. From the ground, an upstairs window, or a stable ladder at the eave, scan for the visible stuff:

- **Debris and shading:** leaves or branches piled at the lower edge, and — the slow one people miss — a tree that has grown enough over a year to start clipping the array in the afternoon.
- **Dirt, moss, and streaks:** a light, even film that rain handles is fine; heavy soiling, moss, lichen, or bird-dropping stains that rain has clearly not cleared are worth addressing. Whether that needs [actual cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/) depends on your climate and how bad it looks.
- **Physical damage:** cracked or clouded glass, a visibly lifted panel, or anything that shifted after a storm.
- **Pests:** droppings, nesting material, or chewed wiring under the array, most common where the gap between panel and roof makes an inviting sheltered space.

### Check the equipment you can reach

The inverter and the area around it, usually in a garage or on an exterior wall, deserve a look:

- Confirm the inverter shows a normal status light or display — green and quiet, not a fault code or a dark screen.
- Listen for the ordinary low hum during daylight, and note anything new: a buzz, rattle, or a fan that never stops.
- Glance at visible conduit and mounts for obvious corrosion, looseness, or weather damage.
- Make sure nothing is stacked against the inverter blocking its ventilation, and clear any leaves or nests from around it.

## Where the line is between you and a professional

The whole point of this routine is that almost all of it is safe from the ground. Keep it that way. Getting up onto a roof — especially a steep or high one, and especially to work near live electrical equipment — is genuinely dangerous, and it is not where an owner should be earning savings.

Call a qualified installer or electrician when your checkup turns up any of the following:

- Production that has dropped and cannot be explained by weather, season, or new shade.
- An inverter fault code, a dark display, or a system producing nothing in daylight.
- Visible damage to panels, wiring, conduit, or mounts.
- Anything that would require you to walk the roof, touch wiring, or open electrical equipment to inspect or fix.

Most years, the checkup ends with a clean bill and no phone call — which is the point. The value is not in the fixing, which is rare, but in the confidence that comes from having actually looked instead of assuming.

## FAQ

**Do solar panels really need maintenance?**
Very little, but not zero. There are no moving parts to service, yet output can drift, connections can loosen, and dirt or pests can accumulate. A once-a-year inspection, mostly from the ground, catches the slow problems before they cost real production.

**How often should I inspect my solar system?**
Once a year is enough for most homes, ideally at a consistent time such as late winter or early spring. Keep a looser eye on your monitoring app year-round, since it flags most issues long before an annual walk-around would.

**Can I do solar maintenance myself?**
The inspection, yes — checking production data, scanning the array from a safe vantage, and confirming the inverter's status are all owner-level tasks. Anything that involves climbing onto a steep or high roof, touching wiring, or opening electrical equipment should go to a professional.

**How do I know if my panels are underperforming?**
Compare monthly production against the same month in prior years and against what the array was projected to make. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you a reasonable production benchmark for your system, and a persistent shortfall against it — not explained by weather or new shade — is the cue to investigate.
