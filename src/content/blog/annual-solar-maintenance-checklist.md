Solar gets sold as maintenance-free, and that is close enough to true that the phrase rarely gets challenged. A rooftop array has no moving parts, no fuel to buy, and no scheduled service the way a furnace or a car does. But close enough is not the same as nothing, and the small gap between them is exactly where an unnoticed problem can quietly cost you production for months before you ever suspect it. A system that has silently lost 15% of its output still looks perfectly fine from the driveway — the panels sit there flat and blue, giving no visible sign that a connector has loosened or a string has dropped offline. An hour of deliberate attention once a year is what turns "probably fine" into "confirmed fine," and it is the cheapest insurance a solar owner has.

## Why a once-a-year rhythm is enough

The reason solar needs so little maintenance is the same reason it needs *some*. With nothing mechanical to wear out on a fixed schedule, most solar problems are slow and undramatic rather than sudden. Output drifts down a little. A connector works loose over years of daily heating and cooling. Moss creeps along the shaded edge of a panel. A squirrel decides the gap between the array and the roof makes a fine sheltered nest and starts chewing. None of these announce themselves with a bang or a warning light on day one. They accumulate quietly, and the cost of ignoring them is measured in kilowatt-hours you never notice you lost. An annual checkup matches that pace exactly — it is frequent enough to catch a slow decline before it becomes a whole season of lost production, and infrequent enough that it stays a genuinely small chore rather than a recurring burden.

Pick a consistent time of year so it actually happens, because a maintenance routine you keep meaning to get to is worth nothing. Late winter or early spring makes a natural slot for two reasons. You head into the high-production months of spring and summer with a clean bill of health, so any problem you find gets fixed before it costs you during the sunniest, most valuable stretch of the year. And any damage from winter storms, ice, or heavy snow gets caught while it is fresh rather than discovered by accident in July. Tie it to something you already do at that time of year — a tax-season habit, a spring cleaning weekend — and it becomes automatic instead of a task you have to remember to schedule.

## The checkup, from the ground up

The single most useful check in the entire routine requires no ladder, no roof, and no risk at all.

### Start with your production numbers

Your monitoring app is the most sensitive diagnostic instrument you own, and it sees problems your eyes never will. Open it and compare this year's monthly production against last year's, month for month, the way you would compare bank statements. A small, steady decline — a percent or so a year — is normal and expected [panel degradation](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/), the gradual fade every array experiences, and nothing to worry about. What you are hunting for is a different shape entirely: a sudden step down from one period to the next, one string or one panel visibly lagging its neighbors, or a month running far below the same month a year earlier with no weather explanation. The distinction that matters is between the whole curve moving and part of it dropping. Weather pushes the entire production curve up in a sunny stretch and down in a cloudy one, but it moves everything together. A real fault does something different — it distorts the shape, or it drops one part of the array while the rest holds steady. Learning to read that difference is what separates a homeowner who catches a dead string in April from one who discovers it in December. Knowing which [monitoring numbers signal trouble](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) turns the app from a curiosity you check out of idle interest into a genuine early-warning system.

If the numbers look healthy, the rest of the checkup is confirmation and takes only minutes. If they look off, the rest of the checkup tells you where to look for the cause.

### Then look at the array, from a safe vantage

You do not need to walk the roof for this, and you should not. From the ground, an upstairs window, or a stable ladder braced at the eave, scan the array for the visible stuff that a production number cannot tell you. Look for debris and shading first — leaves or branches piled at the lower edge of the array, and the slow one almost everyone misses, a tree that has grown just enough over a single year to start clipping the panels in the late afternoon. A branch that cleared the array last summer may catch it this one, and shade that arrives gradually is exactly the kind your production data will flag before your eyes do.

Look next at dirt, moss, and streaks. A light, even film of dust that the next good rain will rinse away is nothing to act on. Heavy soiling, visible moss or lichen creeping in from the edges, or bird-dropping stains that rain has clearly failed to clear are worth addressing, because unlike a thin film they do not self-clean and they sit in one place blocking light. Whether that grime actually needs [a real cleaning](/blog/do-solar-panels-need-cleaning/) rather than patience depends heavily on your climate and how bad it looks — a damp, rainy region rarely needs intervention, while a dusty or pollen-heavy one sometimes does. Then look for physical damage: cracked or clouded glass, a panel that appears lifted or shifted at one corner, or anything that visibly moved after a storm. And look for pests — droppings, nesting material, or chewed wiring under the array, most common in that sheltered gap between the panels and the roof surface that critters find so inviting.

### Check the equipment you can reach

The inverter and the space around it, usually mounted in a garage or on an exterior wall, deserve a quick look while you are at it. Confirm the inverter shows a normal status indicator — a green light or an ordinary display, not a fault code or a dark, dead screen. Listen for the ordinary low hum it makes during daylight, and note anything new: a buzz, a rattle, or a cooling fan that runs constantly and never seems to rest. Glance at the visible conduit and the mounting points for obvious corrosion, looseness, or weather damage where cables enter the wall or the roof. And make sure nothing has been stacked against the inverter that would block its ventilation — a garage accumulates clutter, and an inverter buried behind stored boxes runs hotter than it should. Clear away any leaves or nesting material that have gathered around it while you are there.

## Where the line is between you and a professional

The whole design of this routine is that almost all of it is safe to do from the ground, and the discipline that makes it safe is refusing to cross the line into work that is not. Getting up onto a roof — especially a steep or high one, and especially to work near live electrical equipment carrying meaningful voltage — is genuinely dangerous, and it is emphatically not where a homeowner should be trying to earn a few dollars of savings. The value of the annual checkup is in the looking, which is safe, not in the fixing, which usually is not.

Call a qualified installer or electrician when your checkup turns up any of a short list of findings. Production that has dropped and cannot be explained by weather, season, or new shade is the clearest signal, because it points to a fault the data has caught but the eye cannot see. An inverter fault code, a dark display, or a system producing nothing at all in broad daylight is another — the inverter is the component most likely to need attention over a system's life, and a dead one stops the whole array. Visible damage to panels, wiring, conduit, or mounts belongs to a professional too, as does anything at all that would require you to walk the roof, touch wiring, or open electrical equipment to inspect or repair. If diagnosing a problem means going somewhere unsafe, the diagnosis itself is a professional's job.

Most years, the checkup ends the way it should — with a clean bill of health and no phone call to anyone. That quiet outcome is not the routine failing to find anything; it is the routine working. The point was never to generate repairs. It was to replace the vague assumption that everything is probably fine with the earned confidence that comes from having actually looked, using the one diagnostic tool that reliably catches trouble before it compounds.

## Common questions about solar maintenance

**Do solar panels really need maintenance?**
Very little, but not zero. There are no moving parts to service, yet output can drift down, connections can loosen over years of thermal cycling, and dirt or pests can accumulate unseen. A once-a-year inspection, done mostly from the ground, catches the slow problems before they cost you real production.

**How often should I inspect my solar system?**
Once a year is enough for most homes, ideally at a consistent time such as late winter or early spring so it actually gets done. Keep a looser eye on your monitoring app year-round, since the app flags most issues long before an annual walk-around would notice them.

**Can I do solar maintenance myself?**
The inspection, yes — checking your production data, scanning the array from a safe vantage point, and confirming the inverter's status are all owner-level tasks that require no special training. Anything that involves climbing onto a steep or high roof, touching wiring, or opening electrical equipment should go to a professional.

**How do I know if my panels are underperforming?**
Compare your monthly production against the same month in prior years, and against what the array was originally projected to make. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives you a reasonable production benchmark for a system of your size in your location, and a persistent shortfall against that benchmark — one that weather and new shade do not explain — is the cue to have someone investigate.
