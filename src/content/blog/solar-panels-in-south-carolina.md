South Carolina gives a rooftop almost everything it needs from the sky. The sun is strong and consistent across most of the state, the summers are long, and a well-placed array produces on the same generous scale as neighbors that get far more attention for their solar programs. What the sky offers freely, though, the regulators have spent a decade adjusting. The thing that decides whether a South Carolina system is a good deal isn't the weather. It's whichever version of the export rules happens to be in force when you sign — and that framework has been rewritten more than once.

That pairing of reliable physics and unsettled policy is what sets the state apart from a market where the rules have stopped moving. In a settled state you can price a system against a stable tariff and trust the number for years. In South Carolina you have to price it against a policy that is still, in a real sense, under construction. The homeowners who do well here are the ones who treat the compensation question as the central variable rather than an afterthought, and who get the specifics in writing before a crew shows up.

## The sun is the easy part

Set the compensation debate aside for a moment and South Carolina looks like a straightforwardly good solar state. The solar resource across the Midlands and the Lowcountry is strong enough that a modestly sized array can cover a large share of a typical home's usage, and the long cooling season means production peaks at exactly the moment air-conditioning demand — and bills — peak too. On the generation side alone, the state rewards a well-oriented roof about as well as anywhere in the Southeast. A south-facing plane with a decent pitch and clear sky will earn its keep here.

Heat is the one physical caveat worth naming honestly. South Carolina summers push panel temperatures well above their rating, and hot cells produce slightly less than the nameplate suggests. Solar modules carry a negative temperature coefficient, which is a technical way of saying they lose a small percentage of output for every degree above their test conditions. On a 95-degree July afternoon, an array can run meaningfully hotter than the air around it, and its real output will sit a notch below the sticker figure. It is a genuine deduction, but a modest and predictable one, and any honest production estimate already accounts for it. The practical response is to favor good roof ventilation and a mounting gap that lets air move behind the panels, and to work from realistic annual figures rather than best-case peak numbers. The [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) gives a grounded annual kilowatt-hour estimate for a South Carolina roof once you enter your location and array details, and that yearly total — not a sunny-day snapshot — is the right number to build every other decision on.

## Why the rules keep moving and what a successor tariff changes

Here is the part that makes South Carolina genuinely distinctive. For years the state offered retail-rate net metering, the simplest and most generous way to credit exported power. A kilowatt-hour sent to the grid at noon offset a kilowatt-hour drawn back at night, one for one, at the same price you paid to buy it. Under that arrangement your meter effectively ran backward, and the timing of your usage was irrelevant to the math. But the state legislature twice stepped in to redesign the framework, directing regulators to build successor programs rather than leave the original terms in place indefinitely.

The 2014 and 2019 energy laws set that redesign in motion, instructing the utilities and the Public Service Commission to develop new tariffs as the earlier caps and terms expired. The practical result is that South Carolina has been migrating, in stages, away from pure retail net metering and toward successor structures that credit exports on different terms. The details differ by utility and have shifted with each proceeding, which is exactly why a number you read in an older article — or hear from a salesperson quoting last year's deal — cannot be trusted for this state. The framework is a moving target by design, not by accident, and that is the single most important thing a South Carolina buyer needs to understand.

The direction of travel is the same one many states are heading: from net metering toward something closer to net billing, where exported power earns less than the retail rate you pay to buy electricity back. The full mechanics of that shift, and why it reshapes the economics, are worth reading in [net billing vs net metering](/blog/net-billing-vs-net-metering/), but the short version is that the timing of your usage suddenly starts to matter a great deal. Under one-for-one net metering, it made no difference whether you consumed a kilowatt-hour as it was produced or exported it and bought it back later — the credit was identical either way. Under a successor tariff that pays less for exports than it charges for imports, every kilowatt-hour you consume while the panels are producing is worth full retail, while every kilowatt-hour you export is worth noticeably less. That one change rewards self-consumption and quietly penalizes a system designed simply to run the meter backward.

The design response is to size a South Carolina system around the power you can actually use during daylight, rather than around your annual kilowatt-hour total. A slightly smaller array with high self-consumption can outperform a larger one that dumps a big midday surplus onto the grid at a reduced export rate. Shifting flexible loads into sunlit hours — running the pool pump at midday, doing laundry in the afternoon, charging an EV while the sun is up, pre-cooling the house before the evening — converts low-value exports into full-value avoided purchases. A battery enters the same conversation for the same reason: it stores your own midday surplus for the evening instead of selling it cheaply, which is a far stronger argument under net billing than it ever was under one-for-one net metering. None of this makes solar a bad bet in South Carolina. It just means the winning design here looks different from the meter-spinning array that made sense a decade ago.

## The grandfathering question is worth asking in writing

When a state is actively rewriting its rules, one question outranks all the others: which version applies to you, and for how long? Utilities transitioning between tariffs often let customers who interconnect under an existing program keep those terms for a defined period — the mechanism explained in [net metering grandfathering](/blog/net-metering-grandfathering/). That protection can be worth years of stronger credits, and in a state that has already changed its rules more than once, it is not a minor footnote. It can be the difference between a system that pays back comfortably and one that limps.

But grandfathering is specific, dated, and never guaranteed to survive the next proceeding. So before signing anything in South Carolina, get two things in writing from the installer or the utility. First, exactly which tariff your system will be interconnected under. Second, what — if anything — protects that arrangement if the rules change again, and for how many years. A production and savings projection built on a tariff you might not actually receive is worth nothing, no matter how polished the spreadsheet looks. This is the same caution that applies next door, where [North Carolina](/blog/solar-panels-in-north-carolina/) has navigated its own transition away from retail net metering, but South Carolina's repeated redesigns make the paperwork question sharper here than in most states. Treat any verbal assurance about export rates as a starting point for a written question, not as a fact you can bank on.

## Turning uncertainty into a decision

One part of the math, at least, does not move with South Carolina's proceedings. The federal residential clean energy credit — worth 30% of an eligible system's cost, per current IRS guidance — applies the same way in every state and sits entirely outside the tug-of-war over export rates. That stability is worth leaning on when so much else about the in-state picture is provisional: whatever the utility decides about your exports, the federal credit does its work at tax time regardless. It does not rescue a system whose export economics turn out weak, but it does mean the least certain part of a South Carolina decision is the state-level compensation, not the incentive sitting on top of it.

Policy churn does not mean solar fails to pay in South Carolina — the sun is simply too good for that. What it means is that you evaluate the state defensively, the way you would price any investment whose terms could shift. Run the numbers twice. Once assuming the strongest export terms you might qualify for, and once assuming a weaker successor tariff paired with high self-consumption. If the pessimistic case still clears the bar you would need to feel comfortable, the decision is robust to whatever the next proceeding decides, and you can sign with confidence. If only the optimistic case works, you are betting on a policy that has already proven willing to change on you — a bet worth recognizing as a bet.

In a settled state, you can trust a single number and move on. Here, trust the range — and make sure the low end of it is a number you would still be glad you signed for.
