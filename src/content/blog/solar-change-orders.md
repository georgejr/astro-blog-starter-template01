You signed a contract for a fixed price, the crew opened up the roof or the electrical panel, and now there's a call: an extra charge before the job can finish. Maybe it's a few hundred dollars for a conduit reroute, maybe a couple thousand for a main-panel upgrade nobody flagged. That mid-project price change is a change order, and the reflex to feel cheated is understandable — but not every change order is a shakedown. Some reflect real conditions no one could see through a roof; others are the predictable result of a rushed quote the salesperson should have caught.

Telling the two apart comes down to one question: could this have been known before work started? If a competent site survey would have caught it, the surprise is on the installer. If it was genuinely hidden inside a wall or under the decking, a fair charge is defensible. That test does most of the work.

## The charges that are usually legitimate

Some conditions really do stay hidden until a crew is on-site with the roof or panel open. When one of these turns up, a reasonable change order isn't a red flag — it's the system working as intended, provided the price is fair and documented.

### Genuinely concealed conditions

Rotted or delaminated roof decking under the shingles, undersized or unsafe existing wiring discovered when the panel is opened, or a rafter layout that doesn't match the plans — these can be invisible during a normal survey. Fixing them is real labor and real material, and doing solar over a compromised roof or panel would be worse than paying to correct it. A fair change order here itemizes the problem, the fix, and the cost, ideally with a photo.

### Utility or jurisdiction requirements

Occasionally the utility or the permitting office imposes a requirement that couldn't be fully priced up front — a specific disconnect, a service upgrade to meet interconnection rules, or a structural detail the plan reviewer demands. These are legitimate in origin, though a seasoned installer in your area should anticipate most of them. When one appears, ask to see the requirement in writing from the utility or building department, not just the installer's summary of it.

### Changes you requested

If you added panels, moved the array, upgraded the equipment, or asked for a battery after signing, the resulting change order is simply the cost of your own new scope. That's the least controversial category, and the math is worth checking yourself; the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) helps you sanity-check whether the added capacity is priced in line with the rest of the system.

## The charges that deserve pushback

The change orders worth resisting are the ones that describe conditions a proper survey should have caught. A main-panel upgrade is the classic example: your panel's amperage and available breaker space are visible during any competent site visit, so "your panel can't handle solar" arriving as a mid-project surprise suggests the survey was skipped or rushed. The same goes for a roof that was always going to need reinforcement for the array's weight, or a conduit run whose length was obvious from the start.

A thorough [solar site survey](/blog/solar-site-survey/) exists precisely to surface these costs before you sign, which is why a change order for something that should have been on the survey is really a billing problem dressed up as a discovery. This overlaps with the broader category of [hidden solar costs](/blog/hidden-costs-of-solar-panels/) — items that are entirely foreseeable but conveniently left off the initial quote to keep the headline price attractive, then reintroduced once you're committed and the crew is on your roof.

Watch for the pattern where the "surprise" conveniently appears only after demolition has started and stopping would be costly and disruptive. Leverage, not physics, is doing the work in that situation, and a change order timed for maximum pressure deserves the most scrutiny.

## How to respond without stalling the job

When a change order lands, a few moves protect you without turning the project adversarial:

- Ask for it in writing, itemized. A legitimate charge can be described: the condition found, the specific fix, the labor and materials, and the new total. Vague lump sums are a warning sign.
- Ask for evidence. Photos of the rotted decking or the full panel, a copy of the utility's written requirement — real conditions have real documentation.
- Check it against your contract. A well-written agreement already spells out how change orders are priced and approved, and often lists which conditions are the installer's responsibility versus the homeowner's. This is exactly why [reading the contract terms twice](/blog/solar-contract-terms/) before signing pays off.
- Get your written approval into the loop. Reputable installers won't proceed on extra scope without your sign-off; if a charge simply appears on the final invoice with no prior approval, dispute it.
- Compare against a walk-away. Knowing your deposit terms and cancellation rights before the job removes the installer's ability to use "the crew is already here" as a lever.

The goal isn't to fight every change order — some are the honest cost of what the roof revealed. The goal is to make sure each one passes the same test: it describes a genuinely unforeseeable condition, it's documented, it's priced fairly, and you approved it before the work happened. Charges that clear that bar are part of building on a real house. Charges that don't are worth a hard conversation before another dollar moves.
