You signed a contract for a fixed price, the crew opened up the roof or the electrical panel, and now there's a phone call: an extra charge before the job can finish. Maybe it's a few hundred dollars for a conduit reroute nobody anticipated, maybe a couple thousand for a main-panel upgrade that never came up during the sale. That mid-project price change is a change order, and the reflex to feel cheated is completely understandable — you agreed to a number, and now the number is moving. But not every change order is a shakedown. Some reflect real conditions no one could have seen through a closed-up roof; others are the predictable result of a rushed quote that a competent salesperson should have caught before you ever signed.

Telling the two apart comes down to a single question you can apply to any charge that lands: could this reasonably have been known before work started? If a competent site survey would have caught it, the surprise is on the installer, and you have grounds to push back. If it was genuinely hidden inside a wall or under the roof decking where no inspection could reach it, a fair charge is defensible and arguably the system working as intended. That one test does most of the work of sorting legitimate change orders from padded ones, and the rest of this comes down to documentation, timing, and knowing what your contract already promised.

## The change orders that are usually legitimate

Some conditions really do stay hidden until a crew is physically on-site with the roof or the electrical panel open, and when one of these turns up, a reasonable change order isn't a red flag. It's the honest cost of building on a real house that turned out to be different from the plans, provided the price is fair and the problem is documented rather than just asserted over the phone.

Genuinely concealed conditions are the clearest case. Rotted or delaminated roof decking hiding under sound-looking shingles, undersized or unsafe existing wiring discovered only when the panel cover comes off, or a rafter layout that doesn't match the drawings the designer worked from — these can be invisible during a normal survey, because a surveyor can't see through shingles or inside walls any better than you can. Fixing them is real labor and real material, and mounting a solar array over compromised decking or tying it into unsafe wiring would be far worse than paying to correct the problem first. A fair change order in this category itemizes the specific problem found, the specific fix, and the cost, ideally backed by a photo of the rot or the bad wiring so you're not taking it on faith. Utility or jurisdiction requirements form a second legitimate category. Occasionally the utility or the permitting office imposes something that couldn't be fully priced up front — a particular disconnect they insist on, a service upgrade demanded to meet interconnection rules, or a structural detail the plan reviewer requires before approving the permit. These are legitimate in origin, though a seasoned installer who works in your area regularly should anticipate most of them from experience. When one appears, ask to see the requirement in writing from the utility or the building department, not merely the installer's paraphrase of a conversation, because the documentation is what separates a real jurisdictional demand from a convenient story.

Even a legitimate change order deserves a sanity check on price, because "the condition is real" and "the charge is fair" are two different questions that a padded change order deliberately blurs together. Rotted decking genuinely needs replacing, but the going rate to swap a few sheets of plywood is knowable, and a charge that bills it at three times what a roofer would ask for the same work is a real problem wearing an honest costume. The same holds for a service upgrade or an added disconnect: these are standard jobs with reasonably standard costs, and you're entitled to a price that reflects what the work actually takes rather than what an installer thinks they can attach now that the roof is open and you're committed. If a number feels high, it's entirely reasonable to ask how it breaks down into labor hours and materials, and to compare it against what the same repair would run as a standalone job from an independent trade. A fair installer won't bristle at that question, because their figure can survive it. The goal isn't to nickel-and-dime an honest contractor over a genuine repair — that sours a relationship you need for the rest of the project — but to make sure the mid-project timing isn't quietly being used to attach a premium to work that has a well-known market price.

The third legitimate category is the least controversial: changes you asked for. If you added panels after signing, moved the array to a different roof plane, upgraded to better equipment, or decided you wanted a battery after all, the resulting change order is simply the cost of your own new scope. Nobody's padding anything; you expanded the job and the price expanded with it. Even so, the math is worth checking yourself rather than accepting whatever number comes back. Running the revised system through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) helps you sanity-check whether the added capacity is priced roughly in line with the rest of the system, so an extra two panels cost about what two panels should and not a premium the installer tacked on because the contract was already signed and you seemed committed. Legitimate scope changes still deserve a fair per-unit price, and the calculator gives you a baseline to hold the number against.

## The change orders that deserve pushback

The change orders worth resisting are the ones that describe conditions a proper survey should have caught, dressed up as discoveries. A main-panel upgrade is the classic example, and it's worth understanding why it fails the test so cleanly. Your panel's amperage rating and its available breaker space are plainly visible to anyone who opens the cover during a competent site visit — there's nothing hidden about it. So "your panel can't handle solar" arriving as a mid-project surprise strongly suggests the survey was skipped, rushed, or done by someone who didn't look inside the panel at all. The same logic applies to a roof that was always going to need structural reinforcement for the array's weight, or a conduit run whose length was obvious from the layout before a single panel was ordered. None of those are discoveries. They're things a thorough surveyor would have written down.

A thorough [solar site survey](/blog/solar-site-survey/) exists precisely to surface these costs before you sign, which is exactly why a change order for something that should have appeared on the survey is really a billing problem wearing the costume of a discovery. The installer had the chance to price it correctly and either didn't look or chose not to mention it. This overlaps heavily with the broader category of [hidden solar costs](/blog/hidden-costs-of-solar-panels/) — items that are entirely foreseeable but get conveniently left off the initial quote to keep the headline price attractive, then reintroduced once you're committed, the deposit is paid, and the crew is standing on your roof. A quote that wins on price by omitting foreseeable costs isn't actually cheaper; it's just back-loaded, and the change order is where the rest of the real price finally arrives.

The main-panel upgrade deserves a second look, because it's both the most common surprise and the most avoidable one. Your panel's rating is stamped on the main breaker, and the number of open slots is visible the moment the cover comes off — there is nothing genuinely hidden about either. So the way to inoculate yourself against this particular change order is to raise it before you sign rather than after: ask the salesperson directly whether your existing panel can accommodate the system as designed, and get the answer in writing. If they've done a real survey, they can tell you on the spot. If they haven't looked, that itself is the warning sign, because it means the quote you're weighing against others was built without checking a fact that could add thousands to the final bill. The pattern repeats across the foreseeable-cost categories. A conduit run's length, a roof plane's need for structural reinforcement, the trenching distance for a ground mount — all of these are knowable up front by anyone who actually inspected the site. When one surfaces mid-project instead, the honest interpretation is rarely that the house surprised anyone; it's that the quote was made to look competitive by leaving foreseeable costs off the page, to be reintroduced once you'd stopped comparing. A thorough quote that comes in slightly higher often proves cheaper than a thin one that later blossoms with change orders.

It helps to keep the original signed quote and its scope description somewhere you can find it quickly, because a change order is, by definition, a change from that baseline — and you can only judge whether something is a genuine addition or a foreseeable cost being reintroduced if you can see what the original scope actually claimed to cover. A quote that listed a possible main-panel upgrade as a contingent extra is being honest about a known uncertainty; one that showed a firm all-in price and then "discovers" a panel problem it should have caught during the survey is a different situation entirely. The paperwork you signed is the reference point that turns a vague sense of being shaken down into a specific, answerable question about what you were actually promised — and that shift, from feeling wronged to pointing at a line in the contract, is what gives you standing in the conversation.

The pattern to watch most closely is timing. Be especially skeptical when the "surprise" conveniently appears only after demolition has started, panels are staged in your driveway, and stopping the job would be costly and disruptive for you specifically. In that moment it's leverage, not physics, doing the work. The condition may be entirely real, but a change order timed to land when your ability to walk away is at its weakest deserves the hardest scrutiny, because the pressure isn't coming from the roof — it's coming from the calendar and your own understandable desire to just get the project finished. A charge that could have been raised during the survey but instead surfaces at the moment of maximum inconvenience is telling you something about how the company operates.

## Responding without stalling the whole job

When a change order lands, a few disciplined moves protect you without turning the project adversarial or grinding it to a halt. The goal isn't to fight every extra charge, because some of them are the genuine cost of what the roof revealed. The goal is to make sure each one passes the same test before you approve it.

Ask for the change order in writing, itemized. A legitimate charge can always be described in detail: the condition found, the specific fix, the labor and the materials, and the resulting new total. A vague lump sum with no breakdown is a warning sign in itself, because real work has real components and an honest contractor can list them. Ask for evidence to go with the description — photos of the rotted decking or the full electrical panel, a copy of the utility's written requirement — since real conditions come with real documentation and invented ones tend not to. Then check the charge against your contract, because a well-written agreement already spells out how change orders are to be priced and approved, and often lists which categories of condition are the installer's responsibility versus the homeowner's. This is one of the clearest reasons that [reading the contract terms twice](/blog/solar-contract-terms/) before signing pays off later: the contract you signed may already answer whether this particular surprise is yours to pay for or theirs to absorb, and you want to know that before you argue rather than after.

There's a calmer way to run the actual conversation than most people manage under pressure. When the call comes, resist the reflex to approve on the spot just to keep the crew moving; a few hours to review a written, itemized change order rarely derails a project and often surfaces something worth questioning. Say plainly that you want it in writing before you approve it and that you'll respond quickly once you've seen the detail — a reasonable installer treats that as normal, because it is. If the answer is that the work can't pause and you must decide this minute, that pressure is itself information about how the company operates. Keep the exchange in writing wherever you can, so approvals and refusals are documented rather than recalled differently by each side later. And know before the project starts what your contract says about deposits, cancellation, and any lien rights the contractor holds, because those terms define your real leverage in a dispute. A homeowner who understands exactly what walking away would cost negotiates a questionable charge from a very different footing than one who assumes they're trapped the moment the first shingle lifts. None of this needs to turn adversarial, and most projects never hit a disputed charge at all — but the homeowner who's quietly prepared handles the one that does without either caving or torching the job.

For a genuinely large surprise — a several-thousand-dollar structural or electrical change — it's entirely reasonable to pause and get an independent opinion before approving, especially when the condition can be photographed and described. A roofer or electrician with no stake in the solar company can often tell you in a single phone call whether the described problem is real and whether the price is sane, and that outside read is cheap insurance against both a fabricated condition and an honestly-found but badly-overpriced fix. A reputable installer won't object to you verifying a major charge before you pay it; the ones who lean on you to decide immediately, before anyone else can look, are precisely the ones a second opinion is designed to catch. The bigger the number, the more the short delay to check it is worth.

Two more moves finish the job. Make sure your written approval is genuinely in the loop before any extra work happens. Reputable installers won't proceed on added scope without your sign-off, so if a charge simply materializes on the final invoice for work already done, with no prior approval you agreed to, you have solid grounds to dispute it — you never authorized it. And know your walk-away position before the project starts. Understanding your deposit terms and your cancellation rights up front removes the installer's ability to use "the crew is already here and the roof is open" as a lever against you, because you'll know exactly what stopping costs and what your rights are. Put those habits together and the picture is simple. Charges that describe a genuinely unforeseeable condition, that come documented, that are priced fairly, and that you approved before the work happened are part of building on a real house, and you pay them without resentment. Charges that fail that test — foreseeable, undocumented, vague, or presented as a done deal — are worth a hard conversation before another dollar moves.
