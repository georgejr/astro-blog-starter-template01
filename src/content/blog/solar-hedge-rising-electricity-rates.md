Buying solar changes what kind of cost your electricity is. Before solar, power is an operating expense: a variable price per kilowatt-hour that you pay forever and that the utility can raise. After solar, a large chunk of that power becomes a capital expense — money spent once, up front, to own the production. The rate on the kWh you now generate yourself is fixed at zero for the life of the panels. That conversion, from a price you keep paying to an asset you already bought, is the whole mechanism behind calling solar a hedge.

A hedge is just a position that gains value when the thing you're worried about gets worse. Homeowners worry that electricity keeps getting more expensive. Solar is a hedge because the more utility rates climb, the more each self-generated kilowatt-hour is worth — and you locked in that production at today's price.

## Why utility rates tend to drift upward

The hedge only matters if the underlying price actually rises, so it's worth understanding the machinery that pushes it up. Retail electricity is built from a few stacked costs, and most of them face steady upward pressure.

- **Fuel.** The natural gas, coal, and other inputs that fire power plants are commodities with volatile, generally rising long-run prices.
- **Grid investment.** Utilities recover the cost of poles, wires, substations, and increasingly the hardening of the grid against storms and wildfire through your rates. Those capital programs are large and ongoing.
- **General inflation.** Labor, equipment, and financing costs rise with the broader economy, and regulated utilities are allowed to pass much of that through.

Averaged across the country and across decades, residential electricity prices have climbed roughly 2–4% a year, though any single year and any single utility can swing well outside that. The point isn't a precise forecast — it's that the long-run direction has been up, and the forces behind it haven't reversed. Some regions have seen much sharper jumps in individual years.

## What a rate increase does to power you already own

Here's the arithmetic that makes the hedge concrete. Assume a system that produces 9,000 kWh a year, and assume your utility rate is $0.18/kWh today. That production offsets purchases worth 9,000 × $0.18 = $1,620 in the first year.

Now let the rate rise. At 3% annual escalation, that same 9,000 kWh offsets:

- Year 1: $1,620
- Year 5: about $1,824
- Year 10: about $2,115
- Year 20: about $2,842

Nothing about the panels changed. The array produces the same electrons; you simply avoid buying power that got more expensive. The value of your fixed asset rose because the alternative got worse — which is exactly what a hedge is supposed to do. Over a 25-year life, the difference between a flat-rate assumption and a modestly rising one adds up to thousands of dollars in avoided cost, and it accrues entirely on the strength of production you already paid for.

This is also why rate increases quietly shorten payback. [Rising electricity rates](/blog/electricity-rate-increases-solar-payback/) works through that effect in detail: the break-even point arrives sooner because each year's savings is larger than the last. And because the savings compound in the direction of rising rates, the full [lifetime savings from solar](/blog/how-much-do-solar-panels-save/) depend heavily on which escalation rate you assume — a lever worth testing rather than guessing.

## Where the hedge is weak

The hedge is strongest when your solar production directly replaces retail purchases at the full retail rate. That's the condition under classic net metering: every kWh you generate is worth the same as a kWh you'd have bought, so a rising retail rate lifts the value of all of it.

The hedge weakens in a few situations, and it's honest to name them:

- **Weak export credits.** If your utility pays far less for exported power than it charges for imported power, only the production you consume on-site is hedged at the full retail rate. Exported surplus is hedged at the lower export rate, which may rise more slowly.
- **Fixed charges.** Utilities are shifting more of the bill into fixed monthly charges that solar can't offset. A rising fixed charge is a cost the hedge doesn't touch.
- **Very low starting rates.** In a cheap-power region, there's simply less price to hedge against, and the dollar value of protection is smaller even if the percentage increases match the national trend.

None of these erase the hedge; they just cap how much of your bill it protects. The practical move is to size the system so that as much of your production as possible displaces full-rate purchases, then treat exports as a bonus rather than the core of the case.

There's a second, softer benefit worth stating plainly: predictability. Even setting aside the dollars, converting a variable, utility-controlled cost into a fixed, owned one removes a line item you can't budget for. A homeowner on a fixed income who no longer has to guess what next summer's rates will do has bought something real, even before the savings are tallied.
