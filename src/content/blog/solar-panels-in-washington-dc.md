Washington, D.C. is one of the strangest and most rewarding places in the country to put solar on a roof, and the reason has almost nothing to do with sunshine. The District gets ordinary Mid-Atlantic sun — decent, not spectacular, comparable to Maryland and Virginia around it. What sets it apart is a policy market that turns the electricity your panels generate into a tradable commodity worth, at times, more than the power itself. For most homeowners across America, solar economics come down to how much electricity you make and what your utility pays you for the surplus. In D.C., a third and often dominant factor enters: the sale of solar renewable energy certificates, the SRECs your system earns simply for producing clean power. Understanding that market is the whole game in the District, because it is the difference between D.C. solar being a good investment and being one of the most lucrative residential solar deals in the nation — even on the small, awkward rooftops that define the city.

The catch that pulls the other way is the roofs themselves. D.C. is dense, old, and architecturally protected in ways that complicate a solar install far more than a suburban ranch house ever would. Rowhouses with tiny footprints, flat roofs hemmed in by neighbors, mature street trees, and swaths of the city under historic-preservation rules all conspire to shrink and complicate the array you can actually build. So the District presents an unusual split: unusually strong economics for every kilowatt you can install, paired with unusually hard constraints on how many kilowatts you can fit. Getting D.C. solar right means understanding both halves — the market that makes each panel valuable and the urban realities that limit how many panels you get.

## The SREC market that drives D.C. solar economics

The mechanism behind D.C.'s solar value is a Renewable Portfolio Standard with an aggressive solar carve-out — a legal requirement that electricity suppliers serving the District source a rising share of their power specifically from solar. To prove compliance, suppliers must acquire SRECs, and here is the piece that matters to you: your solar system earns one SREC for every 1,000 kilowatt-hours — one megawatt-hour — of electricity it generates. Those certificates are separate from the electricity itself. You still get the power to use or export as usual, and on top of that the act of generating it mints certificates you can sell into the compliance market. When suppliers cannot buy enough SRECs to meet their obligation, they pay a penalty called an alternative compliance payment, and that penalty effectively sets a ceiling that props up SREC prices. D.C. has historically set that penalty high and its solar requirement steep, which is precisely why District SRECs have long ranked among the most valuable in the country. The general mechanics of how these certificates are created, tracked, and sold apply anywhere they exist, and our explainer on [how SRECs work](/blog/srecs-explained/) covers that machinery in full — D.C. is simply the place where the numbers have historically been largest.

A caution belongs right here, because SREC values are a market, not a fixed rate, and treating them as guaranteed income is the most common way D.C. homeowners get the math wrong. Certificate prices move with supply and demand: as more solar gets built and more certificates flood the market, prices can soften, and the policy that underpins the whole thing — the size of the solar requirement, the level of the compliance payment, the rules for how long a system can earn — is set by law and can be revised over time. What has made D.C. exceptional is a policy design that has kept the solar requirement climbing and the penalty high, but no one should build a twenty-five-year financial projection assuming today's SREC price holds flat for the life of the system. The honest way to treat SRECs is as a substantial but variable revenue stream that is likely to be strong in the near term and uncertain in the far term. Model your investment with a conservative assumption about certificate income and let the upside be a bonus rather than the foundation. That discipline is what separates a D.C. homeowner who is genuinely ahead from one who counted on a number that a shifting market quietly walked back.

Turning certificates into money involves a few practical steps that are worth knowing before you count on the income, because SRECs do not simply appear as a check in the mail. Your system has to be registered and certified as eligible to generate certificates in the District's market, its production has to be metered and reported so the certificates can actually be minted for the energy you make, and then the certificates have to be sold — either directly, through a broker, or through an aggregator who bundles many small residential systems together and handles the transactions for a cut. Most homeowners use an aggregator, because trading certificates on your own is impractical at a single home's scale, and the aggregator's fee is the price of not having to become an energy trader. Some solar companies fold this registration and sale into their offering; others leave it to you to arrange. It is a question worth asking any D.C. installer directly: who registers the system, who sells the certificates, and what does that service cost, because the SREC income is only real once someone has actually set up the machinery to capture it. None of this is difficult, but it is a step that a homeowner expecting certificate revenue needs to see through rather than assume happens automatically.

Stacked on top of the SREC income are the more conventional pieces of D.C. solar economics, and they are solid. The District offers full retail net metering, meaning the surplus your panels export is credited at the same rate you pay for power — the most favorable export arrangement there is, and increasingly rare as other jurisdictions move away from it. That makes sizing simpler and every exported kilowatt-hour worth its full retail value, so a well-sized D.C. array genuinely zeroes out much of the electricity bill on the energy side, before SRECs even enter. The 30 percent federal Residential Clean Energy Credit applies here as everywhere, reducing your qualifying system cost by nearly a third, with your personal eligibility and the details spelled out in IRS guidance rather than a sales pitch; the [federal tax credit calculator](/federal-tax-credit-calculator/) helps estimate what that credit is worth against your specific system price. The District has also historically run additional local incentives aimed at making solar accessible, including programs targeted at lower-income residents, though the specifics of any such program change over time and should be verified as currently open rather than assumed. Put the pieces together — high-value SRECs, full retail net metering, the federal credit, and local support — and D.C. is a place where multiple incentives genuinely combine, which is a topic worth understanding in its own right, since [stacking solar incentives](/blog/stacking-solar-incentives/) is exactly the exercise that makes the District's numbers work. The payback on a D.C. system, driven hard by the SREC income layered on top of ordinary bill savings, can be among the fastest in the country, and modeling it with the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) — setting a deliberately conservative SREC assumption — is the sober way to see just how much of that speed survives a cautious forecast.

## Building an array on a real D.C. roof

The other half of the District's story is the roof, and it is where the enthusiasm has to meet the physical city. D.C. housing skews toward attached rowhouses with small, often flat roofs, and those rooftops present a very different design problem than the open, pitched, south-facing expanses that solar sizing rules of thumb assume. A rowhouse roof may offer only a few hundred square feet of usable space once you account for existing rooftop equipment, access requirements, and setbacks, which caps the system size well below what a detached suburban home could hold. The upside, and it is a real one, is that D.C.'s economics reward even a small array so generously that a modest rowhouse system still earns meaningful SREC income and still offsets a solid share of a city dweller's typically moderate electric usage. In a state with weak incentives, a tiny roof might not be worth the trouble; in D.C., a tiny roof still pencils, because every kilowatt-hour it makes carries the full weight of the SREC market behind it. That inversion — small systems being worthwhile specifically because of policy — is one of the things that makes the District distinctive, and it is why solar spreads across rowhouse blocks where you would not expect to find it.

Flat roofs, which dominate much of the city's housing stock, change the installation approach in ways worth knowing before you get a quote. Panels on a flat roof are typically mounted on tilted racking to angle them toward the sun and let rain wash them, and that racking is often ballasted — weighted down rather than bolted through the membrane — to avoid penetrating and potentially compromising a flat roof's waterproofing. Ballasted arrays add weight the roof structure has to carry and require spacing between rows so the panels do not shade each other, which further limits how many fit. Shading is a particular urban headache: on a dense block, a neighbor's taller building, a mature street tree, or a rooftop bulkhead can throw shade across your array for hours, and the tight quarters mean there is often nowhere better to move the panels. A careful shading assessment is not optional in the city; it is central, because a rowhouse roof has little margin to work around an obstruction. These constraints do not disqualify D.C. roofs — thousands of them host solar — but they mean the gap between a roof's raw square footage and the system it can actually support is wider in the District than almost anywhere else.

Then there is the layer that is genuinely unique to a historic city: preservation review. Large parts of D.C. fall within historic districts or involve historically designated properties, and installing solar on or visible from those buildings can trigger review by the relevant historic-preservation authorities. The concern is usually visibility — whether panels can be seen from the public street and whether they alter the character of a protected façade or roofline — and the practical effect is that homeowners in these areas may need to site panels out of public view, on rear-facing slopes or set back from the street edge, or navigate an approval process before installing. This is not a blanket prohibition; the District has worked to accommodate solar even in historic contexts, and many homeowners in protected areas do install successfully. But it is a real step that can constrain your layout and add time, and it is worth determining early whether your property sits in a historic district, because the answer shapes both what you can build and where. An installer experienced specifically in D.C. work will know the local preservation landscape and the utility's interconnection process, and that local fluency is worth more in the District than in a place where every roof is a blank suburban slate. The broader lesson D.C. illustrates so vividly — that a state or district's policy design can matter far more to solar's value than its sunshine — runs through the whole country, and [why solar pays off in some places and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/) puts the District in that national context. Few places make the point as sharply: ordinary sun, difficult roofs, and yet some of the best residential solar economics in America, built almost entirely on the strength of a certificate market and a policy choice.
