The image is appealing: a sleek turbine spinning on a pole in the backyard, quietly making power day and night while the neighbors' solar panels sit dark after sunset. Wind blows at night, the pitch goes, so why not capture it? It's a reasonable question, and it leads a surprising number of homeowners down a research rabbit hole that almost always ends in the same place — a rooftop full of solar panels and no turbine. Understanding why that happens, and the narrow set of circumstances where it doesn't, saves a lot of wasted enthusiasm.

The short version is that small residential wind is a real technology that works well under conditions most homes simply don't have, while rooftop solar works acceptably almost everywhere. That asymmetry decides the comparison for the overwhelming majority of houses before any cost calculation even begins. But the reasons behind it are worth understanding in detail, because they explain both why the turbine looks so tempting on paper and why it disappoints so reliably in a typical suburban yard.

## Why the wind at your house is not the wind on the brochure

The physics of wind power are unforgiving in a way that solar physics are not, and it comes down to two multipliers that punish anything close to the ground or anything small.

The first is height. Wind speed increases dramatically with elevation above the ground, because the friction of the earth's surface — trees, buildings, hills, the ground itself — drags on the air and creates turbulence in the lowest layer of the atmosphere. The clean, fast, steady wind that makes utility-scale turbines profitable lives a hundred feet up and higher, out in open country. A turbine mounted on a short pole in a residential backyard, surrounded by houses and trees, sits in exactly the churned, slowed, gusty air that wind engineers spend their careers avoiding. Manufacturers and installers of small wind consistently stress that a residential turbine needs to be mounted well above every obstacle within a few hundred feet — often meaning a tower of 60, 80, or 100-plus feet — to see usable wind at all. A roof-mounted or short-pole turbine, the kind most homeowners picture, generally produces a fraction of its rated output and can transmit vibration into the structure it's bolted to.

The second multiplier is the brutal one: the power available in wind scales with the cube of wind speed. Double the wind speed and you get eight times the power; halve it and you get one-eighth. This cubic relationship is why siting matters so overwhelmingly for wind and so much less for solar. A solar panel in a mediocre location still makes maybe 70% of what the same panel makes in a great location — sunlight varies, but it varies gently. A wind turbine in a mediocre location might make 10% of what it makes in a great one, because a modest drop in average wind speed gets cubed into a collapse in output. Marketing materials quote a turbine's output at a specific wind speed, often a brisk 24 or 28 mph, that a backyard site may hit only during storms. At the 8-to-12 mph average that a real suburban yard might see, the same turbine limps along at a small share of its headline number.

Put height and the cubic law together and you get the core problem with home wind: it demands a tall tower on open land with a strong, steady wind resource, and almost no residential lot offers all three. A house on a quarter-acre suburban lot, ringed by other houses and mature trees, has none of them. The trees and buildings that make a neighborhood pleasant are precisely the obstacles that destroy its wind resource, and you can't fix that by buying a bigger turbine — you'd have to move the turbine up and away from the obstacles, which means a tall tower, which means acreage, permits, and money most homeowners don't have or can't get approved. This is why credible small-wind guidance almost universally specifies a minimum lot size measured in acres, not fractions of an acre, and a genuinely windy site to begin with.

Solar carries none of this baggage. Sunlight arrives at your roof at effectively the same strength it arrives at your neighbor's, unshaded by anything but nearby trees and buildings — and even then the penalty is a manageable percentage, not a cliff. A panel doesn't need a tower, doesn't need acreage, doesn't need a permit for a 100-foot structure, and doesn't vibrate the house or make noise or need annual mechanical servicing of moving parts. It sits flat on the roof you already own and quietly converts whatever light falls on it. Even the classic wind talking point — that panels do nothing at night while the wind keeps blowing — turns out to be far weaker than it sounds, because at a residential site the nighttime wind is usually the same slow, obstructed, near-ground air that produces almost nothing anyway. You're not trading day-only solar for round-the-clock wind; you're trading reliable daytime solar for wind that's feeble whenever it blows at all near the ground. The related myth that panels shut down in anything less than full sun is worth correcting too, since it feeds the "solar only works in the desert" instinct that pushes people toward wind — [what solar panels really do without direct sun](/blog/do-solar-panels-work-on-cloudy-days/) walks through how much a panel still makes under clouds.

## Where a turbine actually earns its keep

None of this means small wind is a scam or that it never works. It means it's a specialist tool for a specific site, and the sites where it shines are genuinely out there — just rare in the residential market. The honest version of the comparison identifies who should keep the turbine on the table rather than dismissing the technology outright.

The candidate site has real acreage, on the order of several acres at minimum and ideally more, with open, unobstructed exposure in the direction the wind predominantly comes from. Think a rural property on the Great Plains, a coastal parcel, a ridgeline, or a homestead in one of the genuinely windy corridors that stretch through the middle of the country. The site has a documented average wind speed in a range the turbine is actually built for — this is knowable, not a guess, from wind resource maps and, for a serious buyer, a season of on-site measurement with an anemometer at hub height. And the owner is willing and legally able to erect a tall tower, which means the setbacks, the permits, the neighbors, and the local zoning all have to line up. Where all of that is true, a modern small turbine can be a strong producer, and in the very best wind sites it can even out-produce solar per dollar spent, particularly in winter and at night when a well-sited turbine keeps working and panels are dark or snow-covered.

The most compelling case for home wind, though, isn't wind versus solar at all — it's wind plus solar, on an off-grid property. Off-grid systems live or die by their worst stretch, and their worst stretch is usually a run of short, dark, still winter days when the panels make little and the batteries drain. In many climates, winter is also the windiest season, so a turbine's production is negatively correlated with solar's — it makes the most power in exactly the months solar makes the least. For a remote cabin or homestead running entirely on its own generation, that complementary seasonal pattern can shrink the battery bank and the generator run-time you'd otherwise need to survive the dark months. This is the one configuration where a wind turbine routinely earns a place in a residential-scale energy system, and notably it's on properties that already have the acreage and the wind by virtue of being remote. Even there, the turbine supplements a solar-and-battery core rather than replacing it, because solar remains the cheaper, lower-maintenance, more predictable workhorse for most of the year.

For everyone else — which is to say, essentially every grid-connected house on a normal residential lot — the comparison isn't close, and the reasons are structural rather than a matter of opinion. Solar has a mature, competitive installer market that has driven per-watt prices down for years, a standardized permitting and interconnection path, a well-understood warranty and financing landscape, and the 30% federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, which applies to solar and to small residential wind alike but is far easier to actually capture on a routine rooftop solar job than on a bespoke tower installation. Small wind, by contrast, is a thin market with few installers, custom engineering, higher maintenance from the moving parts that wear and eventually fail, moving-machinery noise close to the house, and output that's hostage to a wind resource most lots don't have. The maintenance point deserves weight: a turbine is a machine with bearings, a generator, and often a yaw mechanism, all exposed to weather at the top of a tower, and it needs periodic service and eventual overhaul. A solar array has no moving parts and asks for little beyond the occasional cleaning.

The cleanest way to settle the question for your own property is to price the option that almost certainly applies. Run your roof and your usage through the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) to see what an array would produce and cost, then test whether that system clears your financial bar with the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If solar pays back within your ownership horizon — and in most of the country [it does](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/) — the turbine has to beat a proven, low-risk baseline on a site that probably can't support it. The state-by-state variation that makes solar a strong deal in some places and a marginal one in others, laid out in [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/), is real, but even in solar's weaker markets the answer for a normal lot is rarely "put up a turbine instead." It's usually "solar still wins, just with a longer payback."

So the practical verdict is easy to state and hard to argue with. If you live on a normal residential lot connected to the grid, put solar on your roof and don't think about a turbine — the wind at your house isn't the wind on the brochure, and no amount of enthusiasm changes the physics. If you own several open, windy acres and especially if you're building an off-grid system that has to survive dark northern winters, a turbine is worth a serious look, ideally alongside solar rather than instead of it, and worth the season of real wind measurement that a good decision requires. The romantic backyard turbine spinning next to a suburban house, sadly, belongs to neither group. It's the one configuration that looks best in a rendering and performs worst in real life.
