A simple roof — one big rectangle facing more or less south, unshaded — is the easy case for solar, and on that kind of roof the cheapest inverter setup usually works fine. A complex roof is the interesting case. Dormers, hips, valleys, a chimney throwing a moving shadow, several small faces pointing in different directions, and shade from a neighbor's tree that creeps across the array through the afternoon all conspire to make a cut-up roof behave very differently from a clean rectangle. And it's precisely on those roofs that the choice of inverter technology stops being a minor line item and starts determining how much energy the system actually produces. The short version is that complex roofs push hard toward per-panel electronics — microinverters or optimizers — because the alternative, a traditional string inverter, is built on an assumption that a cut-up roof violates.

That assumption is that all the panels in a series string see roughly the same conditions. When they do, a string inverter is efficient, reliable, and inexpensive. When they don't — when some panels face east and others west, when one is shaded while its neighbors are in full sun, when a dormer forces the array into small clusters that catch light at different times of day — the string design starts leaving energy on the table, sometimes a lot of it. Microinverters attack that problem by giving every single panel its own inverter and its own maximum power point tracking, so each panel produces independently of the others. On a complex roof, that independence is worth real money and real peace of mind, and understanding why turns the inverter decision from a guess into a clear call. The broader head-to-head between the two technologies is covered in [string inverters versus microinverters](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/); what follows is why the complex roof, specifically, tilts the balance.

## Why a single string struggles on a cut-up roof

To see the problem, you have to understand how a string inverter wires panels together. It connects a group of panels in series — electrically end to end, like links in a chain — and the whole string feeds one inverter that runs a single maximum power point tracker, the circuit that constantly adjusts the electrical operating point to extract the most power available. The catch with a series string is that the panels share a current, so the string tends to be governed by its weakest performer. If one panel in the chain is shaded, dirty, or simply facing a less favorable direction and producing less, it drags the operating point of the entire string toward its own diminished output. The inverter can only pick one optimal point for the whole string, and that compromise point is worse for the strong panels than it would be if they could run on their own. A single underperforming panel doesn't just lose its own share — it pulls down its healthy neighbors too.

On a simple roof this rarely bites, because all the panels are on the same plane, at the same angle, seeing the same sun and the same shade at the same time, so there's no weak link and the single MPP point suits them all. A complex roof breaks that harmony in several ways at once. Panels on different roof faces point in different directions, so their output peaks at different times of day — the east-facing group is strong in the morning while the west-facing group lags, and vice versa in the afternoon — and no single operating point serves both well. If those differently-oriented panels are forced into the same string, the inverter is perpetually compromising between groups that want to run differently. Worse, if a string has to span more than one orientation because the roof faces are too small to hold a full string each, the mismatch is baked in and permanent, costing production every hour of every day, not just when something is shaded.

Shading turns the weakest-link problem from a nuisance into a serious loss. A complex roof usually comes with more shading obstacles — the dormers and chimneys that make it complex also cast shadows, and cut-up roofs are often on older homes surrounded by mature trees. When a shadow falls across even part of one panel in a string, that panel's output can collapse, and because of the series connection, it can pull a disproportionate share of the string's total production down with it. A shadow covering a small fraction of the array's area can cost far more than that fraction of its output on a string system, because the loss propagates through the chain. Bypass diodes built into panels limit the damage somewhat by letting current route around the worst-shaded sections, but they're a coarse tool, and on a roof where shading moves across different panels through the day, a string inverter is fighting a losing battle. The array spends much of the day with at least one panel compromised, and the single-MPP design has no way to isolate the healthy panels from the affected ones.

Then there's the layout problem, which is subtler but just as real on a genuinely complex roof. String inverters impose design constraints — a string needs a minimum number of panels to reach the inverter's operating voltage, and there are limits on how strings can be arranged. On a roof carved into several small faces, you may not have room for a proper full-length string on any single face, which forces awkward compromises: combining faces into mismatched strings, leaving otherwise-usable roof area unpopulated because it can't hold enough panels to form a valid string, or adding hardware to make the geometry work. The physical puzzle of fitting panels around dormers, hips, and valleys while respecting string rules is one of the genuinely hard parts of designing solar for these homes, and it's explored in depth in [fitting solar around dormers, hips, and valleys](/blog/solar-complex-roof-dormers/). The more the roof fights the string's requirements, the more attractive an approach becomes that has no string requirements at all.

## What per-panel electronics change, and where the trade-offs land

Microinverters dissolve most of these problems by changing the fundamental architecture: instead of wiring panels in series to one shared inverter, each panel gets its own tiny inverter mounted right beneath it, converting that single panel's output to household AC on the spot. The consequence is that every panel operates independently, with its own per-panel maximum power point tracking, so each one always runs at its own optimal point regardless of what its neighbors are doing. The weakest-link dynamic simply disappears — a shaded or east-facing panel produces less, but only its own less, and it can't drag down the panels around it. On a complex roof this is transformative, because the whole reason string inverters struggle there is the shared-fate wiring that microinverters eliminate. Panels facing four different directions can all coexist happily, each harvesting its own peak at its own time of day, and a shadow crossing the array costs only the production of the panels it actually touches while everything else keeps running full-out.

That independence cascades into practical advantages that matter specifically on cut-up roofs. Because there are no string-length or voltage constraints, panels can be placed wherever they fit — a pair here on a small dormer face, three there on a hip, one tucked into a spot a string could never use — which lets a complex roof hold more total panels and capture roof area that string rules would have wasted. Mixing orientations becomes a non-issue, so a design can chase the sun across every usable face rather than being forced onto one plane. And the granular, per-panel monitoring that comes with microinverters means you can see each panel's production individually, so if one underperforms or fails you know exactly which one and where, rather than watching a whole string's output sag with no idea which panel is the culprit. On a roof with many panels in many places, that panel-level visibility turns troubleshooting from guesswork into a pinpoint, which is a real ongoing benefit over the decades a system runs. If the basic job an inverter does still feels fuzzy, [what a solar inverter actually does](/blog/what-does-a-solar-inverter-do/) lays out the conversion role that microinverters simply distribute across every panel instead of centralizing in one box.

None of this makes microinverters automatically the right answer even on a complex roof, and the honest trade-offs deserve a fair hearing. Microinverters cost more than a string inverter for the same system, because you're buying many small inverters instead of one large one, so on a roof where the complexity is mild the extra cost may not be justified by the production it recovers — a slightly irregular roof with no shading and modest orientation differences might do nearly as well on a well-designed string system for less money. There's also a middle path worth knowing about: power optimizers, which pair per-panel electronics for tracking and shade tolerance with a central string inverter, capturing much of the panel-level benefit at a different cost and reliability profile. Optimizers occupy the ground between the two extremes and are a legitimate option for many complex roofs, sometimes the sweet spot. The reliability conversation cuts both ways too — putting many electronic units on the roof means many components exposed to heat and weather, though microinverters are designed and warrantied for that environment, while a central inverter concentrates the failure risk in one accessible box. These are the kinds of trade-offs a good installer weighs against your specific roof rather than applying a blanket rule.

The practical decision comes down to how complex your roof genuinely is and how much shading it carries. If you have multiple roof faces at real angle differences, meaningful shading that moves through the day, small faces that can't hold clean strings, or some combination of all three, the case for per-panel electronics is strong, and microinverters or optimizers will very likely produce more energy over the system's life than a string inverter fighting the geometry — enough more to justify the added cost. If your roof is only mildly irregular, unshaded, and mostly one orientation, a string inverter may serve you well and save money.
