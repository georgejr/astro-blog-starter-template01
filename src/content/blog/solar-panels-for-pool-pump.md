A pool pump is one of the quiet giants of a home's electricity bill. An older single-speed pump running long hours through a swim season can pull on the order of 3,000 kWh a year — enough, in many homes, to rival the refrigerator, the dryer, and a chunk of the air conditioning combined. So it's natural to ask how much solar it takes to cover it. The surprising part of the answer is that the panels are rarely the hard part. The pump is.

There's also a framing to clear up first, because it changes everything about how you approach the question.

## You don't wire panels to the pump

The mental image many people start with is a set of panels feeding the pump directly, like a solar fountain in a garden — sun hits the panels, pump runs, clouds roll in, pump slows. For a grid-tied home system, that's not how it works, and the difference matters.

A grid-tied solar array doesn't power any single appliance. It feeds your home's electrical panel and, over the course of a year, offsets your total consumption. The pump draws from the same panel as everything else. "Running the pool pump on solar" really means *sizing your system large enough that its annual production covers the pump's annual consumption on top of everything else you use.* You're not dedicating panels to the pump; you're adding the pump's kilowatt-hours to the total the array has to cover. (True direct-drive solar pumps exist for off-grid and DC applications, but that's a separate product category from a grid-tied home array.)

That reframing turns a wiring question into an arithmetic one, and the arithmetic is dominated by the pump, not the panels.

## How much energy a pool pump really uses

Pool pump consumption swings wildly depending on the type of pump and how long it runs. Treat the figures below as illustrative assumptions — your pump's motor rating, your pool's size, and your runtime will move them:

| Pump type | Typical power draw | Illustrative seasonal energy |
|---|---|---|
| Single-speed (older standard) | ~1.5–2 kW | ~2,500–3,500 kWh/yr |
| Two-speed | Varies by speed | ~1,500–2,500 kWh/yr |
| Variable-speed | ~0.1–1.5 kW by setting | ~800–1,500 kWh/yr |

The spread is enormous — the biggest single-speed pump can use several times what an efficiently run variable-speed pump does. That's why "how many panels for a pool pump" has no single answer until you know which pump you have. A pool pump is frequently one of [the appliances quietly eating the most electricity in a home](/blog/what-uses-most-electricity-home/), and it's often the largest load a pool owner has never actually measured.

### Runtime is the other half of the equation

Energy is power multiplied by time, so the hours matter as much as the wattage. A pump sized to turn the water over once or twice a day might run 6 to 12 hours in swim season. Cutting runtime — or shifting it to a lower speed for more of the day — cuts energy directly. Many owners run their pumps longer than water chemistry actually requires, simply out of habit, which means some of the load a solar array would have to cover is discretionary. Trimming runtime is the cheapest kilowatt-hour you'll ever "generate."

## Turning that load into panels

Once you have a realistic annual figure for the pump, converting it to panels is straightforward. Assume, as a rough planning number, that one modern panel produces somewhere around 500 to 700 kWh a year in a decent US location — the per-panel logic is worked through in [what one panel really produces in a day](/blog/solar-panel-output-per-day/). Dividing the pump's annual draw by that per-panel figure gives the incremental panel count.

Take the single-speed example at 3,000 kWh a year against 600 kWh per panel: roughly 5 additional panels to cover the pump alone. A variable-speed pump at 1,000 kWh a year needs closer to 2. That's the whole range of the answer, and it lands squarely on the pump type rather than on anything exotic about pools.

Because the pump is an *addition* to your baseline usage, the cleanest way to size for it is to fold it into your total rather than treat it separately. Add the pump's annual kWh to the rest of your consumption and run the combined figure through the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool, or start from your whole-home load and let the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) work out the system size. Sizing the array around your real total, pool included, is the same discipline described in [figuring out what size system you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/) — the pump is just a large, and unusually seasonal, line in that total.

That seasonality is worth a note. A pool pump's load concentrates in the warm months, which happen to be your highest-production months too. That alignment is convenient: the extra generation you're sizing for arrives in the same season the pump demands it, so the pump load and the solar surplus rise and fall together rather than fighting each other.

## The variable-speed pump changes the whole question

Here's the lever most pool owners overlook. Because pump power scales steeply with speed, running a variable-speed pump slower for longer moves far less water per watt and slashes energy use dramatically compared to a single-speed unit blasting at full power. The reduction is large enough that upgrading the pump can be cheaper than adding the solar panels to feed the old one.

Run the comparison before you size anything. If covering an old single-speed pump would require 5 extra panels, and a variable-speed replacement would drop that to 2, the panels you *don't* have to buy may more than offset the pump's price — and you shrink the load permanently rather than generating your way around it. In many US jurisdictions, efficiency rules have already pushed new pool pumps toward variable-speed designs for exactly this reason.

The honest bottom line for a pool owner considering solar: measure or estimate your pump's actual annual kilowatt-hours first, ask whether a more efficient pump would shrink that number before you size the array, and then treat whatever load remains as one more entry in your home's total. The panels are the easy, predictable part. The pump is where the real decision lives.
