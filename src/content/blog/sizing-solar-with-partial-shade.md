A shaded roof rarely means no solar. It usually means a smaller, smarter array than the one a salesperson first sketches by counting every rectangle of shingle. The mistake homeowners make is treating the roof as a flat canvas of square footage, when what actually matters is how many hours of clean, direct sun each part of that canvas receives across the year. A chimney, a tall neighbor's oak, a second-story gable that throws a shadow every winter afternoon — these don't just cost you the panels directly under the shadow. They change which panels are worth installing at all, and that changes the size of the system you should be paying for.

The goal is not to cram the largest possible array onto the roof and accept whatever it produces. It's to find the sweet spot where you're only buying panels that will earn their keep, then size the rest of the decision — your offset target, your budget, your expectations — around that honest number.

## Reading which parts of your roof actually perform

Shade is not binary, and that's the first thing to get straight. A panel isn't either "shaded" or "clear." It sits somewhere on a spectrum from full sun to deep, all-day shadow, and where it lands depends on the season and the hour. The sun rides high in June and low in December, so a shadow that clips the bottom row of panels for twenty minutes on a summer morning can swallow that same row for three hours on a January afternoon. Trees make it worse by growing; the sapling that clears your roofline today may not in eight years. So the useful question is never "is this spot shaded?" but "how many hours of direct sun does this spot get, averaged across the year, and when do those hours fall?"

That's what a proper shading analysis answers. Installers use tools — a Solar Pathfinder, a drone with modeling software, or satellite-and-lidar services like the ones behind many online design platforms — to map the sun access of each roof plane and often each individual panel location. The output is usually a percentage, sometimes called a solar access value or a shade factor, that tells you what fraction of the ideal annual sunlight a given spot receives. A panel position at 95% is essentially unshaded. One at 75% is losing a meaningful quarter of its potential. One at 50% is a genuine question mark, and anything below that is usually not worth the hardware, the roof penetration, and the warranty exposure it represents. If you want to understand how these numbers get produced and what to ask for, our walkthrough of [how a solar shading analysis works](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/) covers the methods in detail.

Here's why the distinction matters so much for sizing. Say your roof has room for 24 panels if you counted every square foot. A shading report might show that 16 of those positions sit above 90% sun access, four sit in the 70–80% range, and four are dragged down to 40–55% by a neighbor's tree line. The naive move is to install all 24 and enjoy a big number on the contract. The honest move is to recognize that the last four positions will produce so little — and in a poorly designed system, may even drag down their neighbors — that they're closer to decoration than generation. A panel that costs you the same $600 to $900 installed as any other but produces half as much energy roughly doubles your effective cost per usable kilowatt-hour for that slice of the array. You'd be paying premium prices for discount output.

There's a hardware wrinkle that decides how badly shade hurts, and it's worth understanding before you size anything. In a traditional string-inverter system, panels are wired in series like old Christmas lights, and the string's output is pulled down toward the weakest panel on it. Shade one module and you don't just lose that module's share; you can drag the whole string. This is the origin of the old warning that "one shaded panel kills the array" — it was true, or nearly so, for early string systems. Module-level electronics changed the picture. Microinverters give each panel its own inverter, and DC optimizers let each panel operate at its own maximum, so a shaded module only loses its own output while its neighbors carry on at full tilt. On a shaded roof, that architecture isn't a luxury; it's often the difference between a system worth building and one that isn't. The trade-offs between the two approaches are laid out in our comparison of [string inverters and microinverters](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/), and if any part of your roof sees regular shade, that decision moves to the center of the design.

## Sizing the array around the panels that earn their keep

Once you know which roof positions actually perform, sizing becomes a cleaner exercise than it looks. You start not with the roof but with your consumption. Pull twelve months of electricity bills and total the kilowatt-hours — a full year, because summer air conditioning and winter heating pull usage in opposite directions and one season lies to you. If you're not sure how to translate a target offset into a panel count, [what size solar system you actually need](/blog/what-size-solar-system-do-i-need/) walks through the usage-first method that every good design starts from. Suppose the total comes to 11,000 kWh a year, and you'd like solar to cover most of it.

Now bring in the shade. In a full-sun location with roughly 4.5 average daily sun hours — a reasonable middle-of-the-country assumption you should replace with your own region's figure — a 1 kW of panels produces on the order of 1,400 to 1,600 kWh a year before shading losses. To cover 11,000 kWh, you'd want somewhere around 7 to 7.5 kW of unshaded capacity. The question a shaded roof forces is whether you have room for that much capacity in the positions that actually get sun. Using the earlier example — 16 strong positions plus four middling ones — and assuming modern 400-watt panels, 16 clean panels give you 6.4 kW at nearly full production, and the four middling positions at 75% access add perhaps another 1.2 kW of nameplate but only about 0.9 kW of effective, sun-adjusted output. The four deeply shaded positions you leave off entirely.

Do the arithmetic and you land near 7.3 kW of nameplate capacity producing roughly what a 6.7 kW unshaded system would — enough to cover the bulk of that 11,000 kWh, but not the last slice. And that's the realistic outcome of a shaded roof: a system a bit smaller and a bit less productive per installed watt than the brochure case, covering perhaps 80 to 90 percent of usage rather than 100. Whether that's disappointing or fine depends entirely on your expectations going in, which is exactly why sizing around real sun access beats sizing around square footage. You can sanity-check these panel-count and production numbers against your own roof with the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/), which lets you dial in system size and see estimated output, and cross-check the panel count itself with the [how many solar panels do I need](/how-many-solar-panels-do-i-need/) tool.

Two design levers can recover some of what shade takes. The first is higher-efficiency panels. If your usable roof is the binding constraint — you have the sun access but not the surface area — stepping from 400-watt to 430- or 440-watt modules in the same physical footprint squeezes more capacity out of every clear position. You pay more per panel, but on a space-limited roof that premium buys real kilowatt-hours rather than just a nicer spec sheet. The second lever is layout. A thoughtful installer will orient panels to keep shadows from marching across a shared string, group the clear positions onto their own circuits, and place the module-level electronics where a growing tree line is heading, not just where it stands today. This is design work, not a commodity, and it's a good reason to weight your installer choice toward whoever produces the most credible shading report rather than whoever quotes the lowest number of dollars per watt on paper.

It's worth being blunt about the temptation on the other side. Some sales models are paid on system size, which creates pressure to keep the four shaded panels in the design so the contract reads bigger. If your quote includes panels in positions your own shading report flags below 60% sun access, ask directly what those panels are projected to produce and what they add to the price. A panel that returns a fraction of its cost isn't helping your payback; it's padding someone's commission. The right array is the one where every panel you pay for is a panel that pulls its weight.

## When a smaller system is the right answer, and when shade is a deal-breaker

Accepting a smaller array than your roof's raw dimensions suggest is not settling. A tightly designed 7 kW system that produces reliably beats a sprawling 9 kW system where a fifth of the panels underperform, both on cost per real kilowatt-hour and on the cleanliness of the payback math. A system built only from strong positions also ages more gracefully — there's no marginal panel whose output collapses the first time a neighbor's tree has a good growth year. If shade caps you at, say, an 85% offset instead of 100%, you still eliminate the large majority of your bill, and you can often close the remaining gap later through efficiency: an LED and insulation pass, a smarter thermostat, or shifting flexible loads into midday can shrink the usage that your smaller array has to cover.

There's also the question of what to do about the shade itself, and it's a real fork in the road. If a single deciduous tree is the culprit, selective trimming or removal can convert several dead positions into strong ones, and the value of the extra lifetime production sometimes exceeds the cost of the arborist. If the shade comes from a neighbor's mature trees, your own chimney, or a permanent structure, that option narrows or closes, and you design around what you can't change. The honest accounting is to compare the cost of removing the obstruction against the extra energy the freed-up panels would generate over 25 years; sometimes the tree stays and the array shrinks, and that's a perfectly rational outcome.

The line where shade becomes a genuine deal-breaker is narrower than the fear around it suggests. Light-to-moderate partial shade — a few positions in the 60–80% range, the rest clear — is a design problem, not a disqualifier, and module-level electronics handle it well. The array simply comes out somewhat smaller and the payback somewhat longer than a pristine south-facing roof would deliver. Where the economics actually break is heavy, all-day, unavoidable shade across most of the usable roof: a lot below dense canopy, a house hemmed in by taller buildings, a north face that never sees the winter sun. In those cases the most valuable service a good installer provides is telling you the truth early, before you've paid for a design. If more than half your roof sits below 60% annual sun access and there's no obstruction you can remove, the responsible answer may be to wait, to explore community solar if it's available in your area, or to invest first in the efficiency measures that shrink the problem. Solar rewards honest measurement more than optimism, and a shaded roof is where that rule earns its keep.
