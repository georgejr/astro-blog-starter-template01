A chimney is one of the most common obstacles a solar designer has to work around, and it is trickier than it looks because it does not sit still. A tree can be trimmed and a vent pipe can sometimes be relocated, but a masonry chimney is a fixed, tall object planted in the middle of the very roof you want to fill with panels, and the shadow it throws sweeps across that roof through the day and shifts dramatically with the seasons. Homeowners tend to picture the shadow as a small fixed patch right next to the chimney, and if that were true it would be easy to design around. The reality is a moving finger of shade that reaches farthest exactly when your panels most need every scrap of the low winter sun, and getting the layout right means understanding how that shadow travels before a single panel is placed.

The geometry is not complicated once you see it. As the sun moves from east to west across the sky, the chimney's shadow swings the opposite way, from west in the morning through north at midday to east in the late afternoon. Its length changes with the sun's height: high summer sun casts a short shadow, while the low winter sun stretches it out. At midday around the winter solstice in much of the US, a chimney's shadow can reach more than twice the chimney's own height across the roof — a four-foot chimney throwing an eight-foot-plus shadow at noon, and considerably longer in the hours on either side. That is the case designers plan for, because winter is when days are short, the sun is weak, and every hour of unshaded production counts most. A layout that looks clean in a July site photo can be quietly bleeding output every December morning if nobody accounted for where the shadow actually falls in the depth of winter.

## Why a little shade does more damage than you'd expect

The instinct is to assume that shading one corner of one panel costs you one corner of one panel's worth of power. For older-style systems, that instinct is badly wrong, and the reason is how the panels are wired together. In a traditional string inverter setup, panels are connected in series, like links in a chain, and the current that flows through the whole string is limited by its weakest link. When part of one panel falls into the chimney's shadow, that shaded panel's output drops, and because the string can only carry as much current as its most restricted member, it drags down the panels wired alongside it. A single shaded module can pull down the production of an entire string it belongs to, so a shadow touching two or three panels in the wrong configuration can cost far more than those panels' individual share. This is why installers treat shade so seriously and why "it's just a small shadow" is a dangerous thing to wave away — the loss propagates well beyond the shaded glass itself.

Module-level electronics change this calculation substantially, and they are the main reason chimney shading is a more manageable problem today than it was a decade ago. With microinverters — one small inverter per panel — or DC power optimizers attached to each module, every panel operates independently rather than being chained to its neighbors. When the chimney shades one panel, only that panel's output falls; the rest of the array keeps producing at full tilt. You still lose the energy the shaded panel would have made while it sits in shadow, but you no longer lose the healthy panels' production along with it. On a roof with an unavoidable obstruction like a centrally placed chimney, that architecture is often worth the modest added cost precisely because it contains the damage. It also brings panel-level monitoring, so if a particular module near the chimney is underperforming you can actually see it in the app rather than guessing. The trade-offs between string inverters, optimizers, and microinverters run deeper than shade alone, but on a shaded roof the case for module-level electronics is at its strongest, and a good designer will raise it unprompted when a chimney sits in the array's path.

Even with per-panel electronics, though, the goal is to avoid the shade in the first place rather than merely survive it, because a panel that spends the winter mornings in shadow is a panel you paid for that earns little in the season when you need it most. That is where thoughtful layout does its real work. A designer maps the chimney's shadow path across the worst-case day and leaves a keep-out zone on the roof — an area, generally to the north and swinging east and west through the day, where panels would spend too many productive hours in shade to be worth placing. The size and shape of that zone depend on the chimney's height and position and on your latitude, since the same chimney throws a longer shadow the farther north you live. Panels get concentrated in the areas that stay clear, and the roof's total capacity comes down accordingly. Understanding the full picture of how obstructions carve up a roof, from chimneys to vent stacks to neighboring rooflines, is the subject of [how a solar shading analysis works](/blog/solar-shading-analysis/), and it is the tool that turns a hand-wave about "some shade" into a specific, mapped keep-out zone you can design around.

The height and position of the chimney relative to the roof do most of the work in deciding how much of a problem it really is, and it is worth thinking about your own case in those terms. A short chimney that pokes up just above the ridge on the north edge of the roof may throw its winter shadow harmlessly off the back of the house, never touching the panels at all. A tall chimney standing proud in the middle of the south-facing slope is the worst case, because its shadow sweeps directly across the roof's best solar real estate through the productive middle of the day. Most situations fall between those extremes, and the only way to know which you have is to look at the geometry specifically rather than assume. A designer does this by modeling the sun's path on the shortest days of the year and tracing where the chimney's shadow lands hour by hour, because a shadow that clips a panel at 9 a.m. in June — when the array is drowning in surplus light anyway — matters far less than one that sits on the same panel at 10 a.m. in December, when every scarce winter watt counts. Judging shade by when it falls, not merely whether it falls, is the distinction that separates a careful layout from a lazy one.

## Laying out the array, and deciding what a shaded spot is worth

Working around a chimney usually comes down to a handful of practical moves that a good installer applies in combination. The simplest is to keep the array on the sunny side of the chimney — typically south of it — where the obstruction sits behind the panels relative to the midday sun rather than in front of them. On many roofs the chimney is near a ridge or an edge, and there is a clear expanse below or beside it that never sees the shadow at productive hours; that becomes the home for the panels. Where the chimney sits more centrally and splits the usable roof, the array may be divided into two groups on either side of the keep-out zone, wired so that the shading of one group cannot affect the other. Raising panels on taller racking to clear a low shadow is occasionally floated but rarely done on residential roofs, both because it looks awkward and because it invites wind and permitting complications; leaving the shaded area empty is almost always the cleaner answer. And in some cases the honest conclusion is simply that a few panels' worth of capacity has to be given up, because forcing modules into a chronically shaded patch produces so little that they are not worth their cost.

That last point is worth dwelling on, because homeowners often push to fill every square foot of roof and resist a design that leaves a bare patch near the chimney. The math usually favors the designer's restraint. A panel that spends several hours a day in shadow through the winter might produce only a fraction of what an unshaded panel makes, yet it costs exactly the same to buy and install. You are far better served concentrating your budget on panels that will run clear all day than scattering a few into a shadow where they will limp along. If leaving that patch empty means your roof holds, say, twenty-eight good panels instead of thirty-one compromised ones, the twenty-eight-panel system may well out-produce the crowded one over a year while costing less. The right question is never "how many panels can we physically fit" but "how many panels can we fit that will actually produce," and a chimney is one of the clearest cases where those two numbers diverge. There is one option homeowners occasionally raise that deserves an honest answer: relocating or removing the chimney itself. Unlike a tree, a chimney is not casually taken down — it may be structural, it may serve a still-active furnace, water heater, or fireplace, and altering it involves masonry work, permits, and often re-routing whatever it vents. For most households the chimney stays, and the array is designed around it. But if the chimney is already disused — a relic of a fireplace nobody lights or a furnace long since converted to a direct-vent unit that no longer needs it — then removing it can occasionally be worth pricing, because it frees the roof's best area and eliminates the shadow permanently rather than merely coping with it. That is a decision to make on its own merits, weighing the cost of the masonry work against the extra panels the cleared roof would hold, and it is only ever on the table for a chimney that has genuinely outlived its purpose. For a working chimney, the answer is simpler: design around it, and treat the keep-out zone as a fixed feature of the roof rather than a problem to be argued with.

None of this means a chimney disqualifies a roof from solar — far from it. The vast majority of homes with chimneys go solar without issue, because the shadow, however inconvenient, affects a defined and predictable slice of the roof that a competent designer plans around as a matter of routine. What it means is that the chimney belongs in the conversation early, during the design and site assessment, not discovered as a surprise after panels are already placed. When you review a proposed layout, look specifically at where the panels sit relative to the chimney and ask how the design handles the winter shadow, because that is the season the site photos rarely capture. A chimney is one item on the longer list of things that determine whether a roof is a good candidate in the first place, alongside orientation, pitch, structural condition, and other obstructions — the full checklist is laid out in [whether your roof is a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/). And if a nearby tree compounds the chimney's shade, the two problems interact in ways worth thinking through together, since [trees and solar panels](/blog/trees-and-solar-panels/) can sometimes be trimmed or removed while a chimney cannot — which occasionally tips a marginal layout back into workable territory. The practical goal is modest and achievable: put the panels where the sun reliably reaches them, keep them out of the chimney's winter path, and use per-panel electronics to blunt whatever shade proves unavoidable. Do that, and a chimney becomes a design detail rather than a dealbreaker.
