You have found the house. It ticks the boxes, the price works, and there is an array of solar panels on the roof that the listing proudly describes as a feature. Before you let that array quietly tip you toward the offer, it is worth understanding that "the house has solar" is not one fact at all — it is a set of questions, and the answers range across an enormous span. At one end sits "you just inherited a valuable, paid-off asset that will trim your power bill for twenty years." At the other sits "you are about to assume a stranger's twenty-year contract, complete with an annual price increase you did not negotiate." Same panels on the roof, wildly different deals, and nothing in the listing photo tells you which one you are looking at.

The panels themselves are rarely the issue. Modern modules are durable and largely maintenance-free, and a competent installation will keep producing for decades. The paperwork underneath the panels is where the real story lives, and sorting out who owns that system and on what terms is the single most important piece of diligence you will do on a solar home. It needs to happen early, before you are emotionally committed to the house, because the answer can swing the effective value of the property by tens of thousands of dollars and can, in the wrong case, complicate the entire transaction.

## The first and most important question: who owns the system?

Every solar home falls into one of a few ownership buckets, and they are not remotely equivalent, so establishing which bucket you are in comes before everything else. The best case, and the simplest, is a system owned outright. The previous owner either paid cash or has fully paid off a solar loan, and the system conveys with the house at no additional cost or obligation to you. You inherit the production and the savings, free and clear, with nothing to assume and no third party in the picture. This is the version that genuinely adds value to the home, and studies of buyer behavior consistently find that owned systems help a property's appeal and price. The mechanics of how that value shows up are worth understanding from the seller's side too, in [selling a house with solar panels](/blog/selling-house-with-solar-panels/), because the same forces that let a seller command a premium are the ones that justify your paying it.

The second bucket is a system under a loan. The panels are owned rather than rented, but there is still a balance owed on them, and now the pressing question is who pays that balance off. Sometimes the seller clears the loan at closing out of the sale proceeds, which is the clean outcome and the one you should push for. Sometimes the seller expects the buyer to assume the remaining payments, which is a very different proposition. That balance has to be reconciled explicitly in the transaction, and a solar loan secured by a lien against the property can complicate the title and your mortgage if it is not handled cleanly before closing. Insist on knowing the exact payoff amount and who is responsible for it, in writing, as a term of the deal rather than a detail to sort out later. An unresolved lien discovered late can stall or sink a closing.

The third bucket is the one that catches unprepared buyers: a system that is leased or covered by a power purchase agreement. Here the panels bolted to the roof are not the home's property at all — they belong to a solar company, and the homeowner pays either a fixed monthly lease or a per-kilowatt-hour rate under a PPA for the electricity those panels produce. To close on the house, you will typically have to qualify for and assume that contract, which can run many years into the future and very often includes an annual price escalator that ratchets the payment up over time. You are not inheriting free electricity; you are stepping into someone else's utility-like bill, and the contract may carry buyout terms, transfer fees, and end-of-term obligations that you need to read closely and price honestly. A lease is not automatically a dealbreaker, but it is emphatically not the same thing as owning the panels, and it should never be valued as though it were. The distinction between an owned system and a leased one is the difference between acquiring an asset and agreeing to carry a liability, so establish which one you are dealing with first, and let every other question follow from that answer.

## What to verify once ownership is clear

Ownership tells you what kind of deal you are getting into; the next round of questions tells you whether the system itself is any good. Start with age and warranty status. Find out when the system was installed and what warranties actually remain, because the panels, the inverter, and the workmanship each run on their own separate clock. The inverter is the component most likely to need replacement within the panels' lifetime, so a ten-year-old system with an out-of-warranty inverter carries a foreseeable future cost that belongs in your calculations now, not as an unpleasant surprise in year two.

Ask next for the actual production history, not the nameplate rating. The monitoring records or a stack of old utility bills will tell you what the system genuinely produced over the last year, which is worth far more than the theoretical output printed on a spec sheet. A system's real numbers reflect its true orientation, any shading that has crept in as trees grew, and any quiet underperformance from a failing component. If the seller cannot produce any production data at all, treat that absence as a genuine question mark rather than a formality to wave through — a well-run system generates a record, and the lack of one usually means either poor monitoring or output nobody wanted to look at.

Then confirm the net metering arrangement, because this single item can be worth thousands of dollars over the years you own the home. In many areas a favorable older net metering rate is tied to the system and can transfer to you when you buy, preserving a genuinely valuable deal — but in others it resets to a less generous current tariff the moment ownership changes hands. Whether you keep the good rate or lose it is the subject of [net metering grandfathering](/blog/net-metering-grandfathering/), and it is worth confirming directly with the utility rather than trusting the listing or the seller's recollection. Two more checks round out the picture. First, documentation and permits: a properly permitted, inspected, and interconnected system comes with a paper trail, and missing permits can resurface later as an insurance complication or a resale problem, while an unpermitted array is a red flag about how carefully the rest of the work was done. Second, the roof condition under and around the array: panels last decades, but the roof beneath them may not, and if that roof is near the end of its life, someone will eventually pay to remove and reinstall the entire array in order to replace it — a real, predictable cost you would much rather discover during diligence than five years into ownership.

## Putting a defensible value on it

A solar home should be worth more when the system is owned and productive, but "should be" is doing quiet work in that sentence, and it is worth being clear-eyed about the gap. The premium a buyer pays needs to be justified by real, quantifiable savings, and the way appraisers and buyers actually credit solar tends to be more conservative than owners expect it to be. The reasoning behind that conservatism, and how much value solar realistically adds, is laid out in [do solar panels raise your home's value](/blog/solar-panels-home-value/) — reading it before you make an offer keeps you from either overpaying for an owned system or dismissing a genuinely valuable one.

The practical move is to translate the system into money you can actually see, rather than accepting a vague "it has solar so it is worth more." Take the real production history, your own electricity rate, and the net metering terms you will actually receive after the transfer, and run all three through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If the array reliably knocks a meaningful amount off the power bill and it is owned free and clear, a modest price premium over a comparable non-solar home is defensible and worth paying. If the system is leased, the "savings" belong to the contract rather than to you, and the panels are closer to neutral — a thing to manage and budget for rather than a feature to pay extra for. The number the calculator gives you is the anchor for that judgment: it turns a roof full of panels from an emotional selling point into a line item you can weigh against the asking price like any other.

## FAQ

**Is a leased solar system a dealbreaker?**
Not necessarily, but it changes the math entirely and demands close reading. You are agreeing to take over a contract, so read it in full: the monthly cost, the escalator, the remaining term, any transfer fees, and the end-of-term options. If the payments run lower than the electricity the panels offset, it can still work in your favor. If the escalator has pushed the payment near or above what your utility bill would otherwise be, the "feature" is actually a cost you are inheriting.

**Can I remove or replace panels I inherit?**
If the system is owned, yes, though removal and reinstallation cost real money and may affect any remaining warranties, so it is rarely worth doing casually. If the system is leased, no — the equipment is not yours to touch, and the contract governs what can happen to it and who decides.

**Does the seller's low net metering rate automatically come with the house?**
Sometimes, sometimes not. Grandfathered rates are often tied to the system and its original interconnection, but the transfer rules vary by utility and program, and some reset on change of ownership. Never assume — confirm the specific terms with the utility before you count those savings in your offer.

**What if there is no production data available?**
Ask again, and then treat persistent silence as a warning rather than a shrug. A system whose owner cannot show what it produced is either poorly monitored or quietly underperforming, and neither is good news. At minimum, factor the uncertainty into your offer rather than paying full value for output nobody can document.
