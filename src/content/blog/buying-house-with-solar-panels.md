You have found the house. It ticks the boxes, the price works, and there is an array of solar panels on the roof that the listing describes as a feature. Before you let that array tip you toward the offer, understand that "the house has solar" is not one fact — it is a set of questions, and the answers range from "you just inherited a valuable, paid-off asset" to "you are about to assume a stranger's twenty-year contract."

The panels themselves are rarely the issue. The paperwork underneath them is. Sorting out who owns that system, and on what terms, is the single most important piece of diligence you will do on a solar home, and it needs to happen before you are emotionally committed.

## The first question: who owns the system?

Every solar home falls into one of a few ownership buckets, and they are not remotely equivalent.

### Owned outright

The best case. The previous owner paid cash or has fully paid off a solar loan, and the system conveys with the house at no additional cost or obligation. You inherit the production and the savings, free and clear. This is the version that genuinely adds value, and studies of buyer behavior consistently find that owned systems help a home's appeal — the mechanics of which are worth understanding from the seller's side too, in [selling a house with solar panels](/blog/selling-house-with-solar-panels/).

### Under a loan

The panels are owned, but there is still a balance owed. Now the question is who pays it off. Sometimes the seller clears the loan at closing out of the proceeds; sometimes they expect the buyer to assume the remaining payments. That balance has to be reconciled in the transaction, and a solar loan secured by a lien on the property can complicate the title and the mortgage if it is not handled cleanly. Insist on knowing the payoff amount and who is responsible for it, in writing.

### Leased or under a power purchase agreement

Here the panels on the roof are not the home's property at all — they belong to a solar company, and the homeowner pays either a fixed monthly lease or a per-kilowatt-hour rate under a power purchase agreement. To close, you will typically have to assume that contract, which can run many years and often includes an annual price escalator. You are not inheriting free electricity; you are stepping into someone else's utility-like bill, and the contract may include buyout terms, transfer fees, and end-of-term obligations you need to read closely.

The distinction between an owned system and a leased one is the difference between an asset and a liability you agreed to carry, so establish it first and let everything else follow.

## What to verify once ownership is clear

Ownership tells you what you are getting into; the following tells you whether the system is any good.

- **Age and warranty status.** Find out when the system was installed and what warranties remain — panels, inverter, and workmanship each have their own clock, and the inverter is the component most likely to need replacement within the panels' lifetime. A ten-year-old system with an out-of-warranty inverter carries a foreseeable future cost.
- **Actual production history.** Ask for the monitoring records or utility bills. A system's real output over the last year tells you far more than its nameplate rating. If the seller cannot produce any data, treat that as a question mark, not a formality.
- **The net metering arrangement.** This one can be worth thousands over time. In many areas, a favorable older net metering rate is tied to the system and can transfer to you — or it can reset to a worse current tariff on change of ownership. Whether you keep the good deal is the subject of [net metering grandfathering](/blog/net-metering-grandfathering/), and it is worth confirming with the utility directly rather than trusting the listing.
- **Documentation and permits.** A properly permitted, inspected, and interconnected system comes with a paper trail. Missing permits can surface later as an insurance or resale problem, and an unpermitted array is a genuine red flag about how the rest of the work was done.
- **Roof condition under and around the array.** Panels last decades; the roof beneath them may not. If the roof is near the end of its life, someone will eventually pay to remove and reinstall the array to replace it — a real cost you would rather discover now than in five years.

## Putting a value on it

A solar home should be worth more when the system is owned and productive, but "should be" is doing work in that sentence. The premium a buyer pays needs to be justified by real savings, and the way appraisers and buyers actually credit solar is more conservative than owners expect, as [do solar panels raise your home's value](/blog/solar-panels-home-value/) explains.

The practical move is to translate the system into money you can see. Take the production history, your own electricity rate, and the net metering terms you will actually receive, and run them through the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). If the array reliably knocks a meaningful amount off the power bill and it is owned free and clear, a modest price premium is defensible. If it is leased, the "savings" belong to the contract, not to you, and the panels are closer to neutral — a thing to manage rather than a thing to pay extra for.

## FAQ

**Is a leased solar system a dealbreaker?**
Not necessarily, but it changes the math entirely. You are agreeing to take over a contract, so read it in full: the monthly cost, the escalator, the remaining term, transfer fees, and end-of-term options. If the payments are lower than the electricity the panels offset, it can still work in your favor. If the escalator has pushed the payment near or above your would-be utility bill, it is a cost, not a benefit.

**Can I remove or replace panels I inherit?**
If the system is owned, yes, though removal and reinstallation cost money and may affect any remaining warranties. If it is leased, no — the equipment is not yours, and the contract governs what can happen to it.

**Does the seller's low net metering rate automatically come with the house?**
Sometimes, sometimes not. Grandfathered rates are often tied to the system and the original interconnection, but transfer rules vary by utility and program. Never assume — confirm the specific terms with the utility before you count those savings in your offer.

**What if there is no production data available?**
Ask again, then treat persistent silence as a warning. A system whose owner cannot show what it produced is either poorly monitored or underperforming. At minimum, factor the uncertainty into your offer rather than paying full value for an unknown.
