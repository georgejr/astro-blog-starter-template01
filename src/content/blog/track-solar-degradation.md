Solar panels lose a little of their output every year, slowly and predictably, and the honest expectation for modern modules is a decline of somewhere around half a percent annually — which is why a panel warrantied to still produce roughly 85 to 90 percent of its original output after 25 years is a reasonable and common promise. That much you can read on any spec sheet. What the spec sheet cannot tell you is what your particular array is actually doing on your roof, and the gap between the manufacturer's tidy curve and your real system is where tracking degradation earns its keep. A panel quietly fading at the expected rate needs no attention. A panel or a string dropping faster than that is trying to tell you something — a fault, a failure, a creeping shade problem — and the only way to hear it is to measure. The trouble is that measuring degradation from home production data is genuinely harder than it sounds, because the signal you are looking for is small and the noise sitting on top of it is large.

The core difficulty is weather. Your panels' output in any given month or year is dominated not by their slow aging but by how sunny that period happened to be, and sunshine varies enormously from one year to the next. A cloudy, wet year can knock several percent off your annual production for reasons that have nothing to do with the panels, while a bright, dry year can flatter them. Against that backdrop, a half-percent annual degradation is a whisper. If you naively compare this year's total to last year's and see a 4 percent drop, you have learned almost nothing, because that 4 percent could be entirely weather. To find the real degradation trend you have to either average across enough years that the weather noise starts to cancel out, or normalize your production against how much sun actually fell — and both approaches take some patience and the right data.

## Building a baseline you can actually compare against

Tracking degradation starts with capturing your production faithfully, and nearly every modern system already does this for you through its monitoring platform. The inverter or a separate monitoring device logs how much energy the array produces, usually down to the day and often the hour, and stores it in an app or web portal you can log into. The single most valuable habit you can build as a solar owner is to record your total annual production each year and keep a simple running list — year one, year two, year three, and so on. That yearly total is the cleanest number to work with because it averages over all four seasons, smoothing out the huge swing between a July of long sunny days and a December of short gray ones. Comparing month-to-month is nearly useless for degradation because the seasonal variation dwarfs everything; comparing full calendar years is where a trend can actually emerge. The catch is that you need several years before that trend is trustworthy, since a single year-over-year change is buried in weather noise. By year four or five, though, a line drawn through your annual totals starts to reveal the underlying slope, and if that slope is a gentle downward drift of roughly half a percent a year, your panels are aging exactly as they should.

The faster and more rigorous approach is to normalize your production against the sunlight your location actually received, which strips the weather out of the comparison rather than waiting years for it to average away. The idea is to compare what your system produced to what it should have produced given that period's real sun, and the ratio between the two — sometimes called a performance ratio — is what you track over time. A performance ratio that holds steady year after year means your system is keeping pace with the sun it is given; one that drifts downward faster than expected degradation accounts for means something is genuinely losing ground. You do not need to be an engineer to approximate this. A modeling tool that estimates expected output from your system's size, location, tilt, and orientation gives you the "should have produced" figure to compare against, and the [solar panel calculator](/solar-panel-calculator/) produces exactly that kind of location-specific baseline. Compare your real annual production to the modeled expectation, watch the ratio between them across years, and you have a weather-adjusted view of how your panels are holding up that is far more sensitive than raw totals alone. It will never be laboratory-precise from home data, but it is more than good enough to catch the difference between normal aging and a real problem.

Whatever method you use, a few practical disciplines make the numbers mean more. Note the state of the panels when you read production — a dusty array in a dry season is underproducing for a reason that a rinse will fix, not because it has aged, and mistaking soiling for degradation is one of the most common errors homeowners make. Keep an eye on whether anything has changed around the array: a tree that has grown taller and now clips the panels in late afternoon, a new neighboring structure, a vent added to the roof. Creeping shade masquerades as accelerating degradation, and it climbs year over year in a way that looks alarmingly like the panels themselves are failing when the real culprit is a maple that needs trimming. And record your data at consistent times and conditions where you can, so you are comparing like with like. The point of all this is not to turn your evenings into a physics project but to build a record clean enough that a genuine deviation stands out against it. Understanding what the normal, expected curve even looks like is the essential context for all of this, and our [explainer on solar panel degradation rates](/blog/solar-panel-degradation-rate/) lays out what a healthy array should lose each year so you have something legitimate to measure your own numbers against.

One practical anchor makes all of this easier: treat your first full year of production as the benchmark everything else is measured against. The first twelve months, assuming the system was healthy and unshaded from day one, capture the array near its peak output across a complete cycle of seasons, and that figure becomes your reference point. Every subsequent year gets compared back to it, adjusted for how sunny that year was, and the slow erosion of degradation shows up as a gentle, consistent gap that widens by a fraction of a percent annually. Write that first-year number down somewhere you will not lose it, along with the modeled expectation from your proposal, because in year eight you will be very glad to have both. A surprising number of homeowners never establish this baseline — they let the monitoring app accumulate data they never look at, and when a panel finally fails years later they have nothing clean to compare against and no way to prove the system used to do better. The discipline costs nothing: a note of the first-year total, a note of each following year's total, and a rough sense of whether each year was sunny or gray. That thin record is the entire foundation on which every degradation judgment rests, and it is far easier to start it now than to reconstruct it later from patchy memory.

## Telling normal aging from a fault worth chasing

The whole reason to track degradation is to catch the moment your system stops behaving like a slowly aging array and starts behaving like one with a problem, and the two look different once you know what to watch for. Normal degradation is gradual and smooth: output eases down by a fraction of a percent a year, a slope so gentle it is invisible from one month to the next and only shows up across years of data. A fault, by contrast, usually announces itself as a step change or an accelerating decline. A single panel that fails, a string that goes offline, a connection that corrodes, or an inverter that begins to struggle tends to produce a sudden drop or a curve that bends downward far too fast — not the placid half-percent-a-year drift but a lurch of several percent, or a persistent shortfall that no amount of weather explains. If your weather-adjusted performance holds steady for years and then falls off a cliff, that is not degradation; that is a component asking to be looked at. The value of your baseline is precisely that it lets you distinguish the two, because without a record of what "normal" looked like, you have no way to know whether this year's number is fine or a warning.

The distinction matters financially as much as technically, because degradation is already baked into the economics you signed up for while a fault is unplanned lost production you are paying for in missed savings. The expected slow fade was assumed when you ran the payback numbers — a system modeled honestly already accounts for producing a little less each year, which is why serious return calculations apply a degradation factor rather than assuming flat output forever. You can see how that assumption flows through the lifetime numbers using the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/), which lets you model production declining over the system's life rather than pretending it stays constant. A genuine fault, though, is a different animal: every month a failed panel or a struggling inverter underproduces is money you expected to save and did not, and unlike degradation it is often fixable, sometimes under warranty. Catching it early is the entire payoff of tracking, because a fault left undiagnosed for a year is a year of lost production you will never recover. This is where your monitoring app graduates from a curiosity to a genuinely useful tool — learning to read it for the specific patterns that signal trouble is worth doing deliberately, and our guide to the [warning signs in your solar monitoring data](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) walks through what an unhealthy production curve actually looks like on screen.

Finding an abnormal decline is only useful if you act on it, and here the record you have been keeping becomes leverage. Most panels carry a performance warranty — a manufacturer's promise that the modules will still produce at least a stated percentage of their original output after a given number of years — and most systems carry separate warranties on the inverter and on the installation workmanship. If your tracking shows production falling faster than the expected curve and you can rule out weather, soiling, and shade, that documented shortfall is exactly what a warranty claim rests on. The manufacturer or installer will want evidence that the system is genuinely underperforming, and a homeowner who can show a clean, weather-adjusted record of declining output is in a far stronger position than one who simply feels the bill has crept up. This is the quiet payoff of measurement: it converts a vague suspicion into a documented case. Before assuming the panels themselves are at fault, though, walk the cheaper suspects first — a dirty array, a tripped or failing inverter, a shading change, a tripped breaker on one string — because those are common, fixable, and far more likely than a manufacturing defect in the modules. The tracking tells you something is wrong; the diagnosis tells you what, and the warranty is there for the cases where the answer is the hardware.

Set against the full arc of a system's life, degradation tracking is really about protecting the long tail of value that makes solar worth owning. Panels are built to run for decades, and the reason a 25-year-plus service life pencils out is that the slow, predictable fade leaves them still producing the large majority of their original output at the end — the whole premise explored in [how long solar panels really last](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/). That premise only holds if the array actually ages the way it is supposed to, and the only way you will know is if you have been watching. You do not need to obsess; a yearly check-in with your production data, a rough normalization against the sun you received, and a habit of asking whether a soft number is weather, dirt, shade, or something real is plenty. Keep that record, and you turn an invisible, gradual process into something you can see clearly enough to act on when it matters and ignore when it does not.
