A home battery mounted on the garage wall typically holds somewhere around 10 to 13 kilowatt-hours. The battery sitting in your driveway holds five or six times that. A typical EV pack runs roughly 60 to 80 kilowatt-hours — enough, on paper, to carry a house through days of an outage rather than hours. Vehicle-to-home, or V2H, is the technology that tries to unlock that battery for the house, turning the car from a thing you charge into a thing that can power your home when the grid goes down.

The appeal is obvious once you see the capacity gap. The complication is that a car battery wasn't built to back up a house, and bridging the two takes specific hardware, a compatible vehicle, and some electrical work. Set V2H against the dedicated home battery it competes with and the trade-offs come into focus: enormous capacity you already paid for, versus reliability and readiness you have to buy separately.

## The battery you already own

An EV is, functionally, the largest battery most households will ever own. That capacity normally does one job — moving the car — and sits idle the roughly 90% of the time the vehicle is parked. V2H aims at that idle time. Instead of drawing power only to charge, the car's battery discharges back out through the same connection, feeding your home's circuits during an outage or, in some setups, during expensive grid hours.

It helps to separate three terms that get tangled. Vehicle-to-load (V2L) is the simplest: an outlet on the car itself that powers a few plug-in devices, no house wiring involved. Vehicle-to-home (V2H) is the one this article is about — the car powers your home's actual circuits through your electrical panel. Vehicle-to-grid (V2G) goes further still, sending power back onto the utility grid, and brings in a layer of utility approval that puts it beyond most homeowners today. For backup power, V2H is the target.

## What it actually takes

Making a car power a house is not as simple as running the charge cable backward. Several pieces have to be in place, and missing any one of them stops the whole thing.

- **A bidirectional charger.** An ordinary EV charger only pushes power into the car. V2H needs a charger that can also pull power out and convert the car's DC battery into the AC your home uses. This unit is the heart of the system and the main added expense.
- **A vehicle that supports it.** The car's battery management has to permit discharge for V2H, and only a growing — but still limited — set of models and charging standards allow it. A vehicle that isn't designed to export power can't be made to, regardless of the charger.
- **A way to isolate from the grid.** Just like any battery backup, a V2H system must disconnect your home from the utility before it energizes your circuits, so it never back-feeds a line a utility worker might be handling. That's the same anti-islanding safety logic that governs [how home solar batteries work](/blog/how-home-solar-batteries-work/), handled by a transfer switch or an equivalent automatic disconnect.
- **A defined set of backed-up circuits.** Because you rarely want — or can afford — to run the entire house, most setups route backup power to a critical-load subpanel that feeds only the essentials. That's the same whole-home-versus-essentials decision every backup system faces, weighed in [whole-home vs partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/).

Put together, a V2H installation looks a lot like installing a home battery — because electrically, that's what you're doing. The car just supplies the storage.

## V2H versus a wall battery

The honest way to judge V2H is against the product it replaces: a fixed home battery. They solve the same problem from opposite directions.

On **capacity**, V2H wins decisively. A single EV pack dwarfs one or even two wall batteries, so for a long outage the car can outlast dedicated storage that would cost far more to match. You've already bought that capacity for transportation; V2H is an attempt to get a second job out of it.

On **readiness**, the wall battery wins. It's bolted to the house, always charged, always present, and it engages the instant the grid drops without anyone doing anything. Its behavior in an outage is predictable — the experience described in [what happens when the grid goes down with a battery](/blog/solar-battery-during-power-outage/). A car can't promise any of that, for one simple reason.

## The catch is that the car has to be home

The defining limitation of V2H is mobility. A home battery is always where you need it. A car is a car — it might be at work, on a trip, or sitting at 15% charge after a long day when the outage hits. The capacity advantage only exists if the vehicle is parked, plugged in, and holding enough charge to spare. Plan to drive the next morning and the calculus shifts again: power drawn to run the fridge overnight is range you don't have at dawn.

That tension — backup versus mobility — is the real cost of V2H, and it's not one a wall battery carries. It makes the car excellent for backup precisely when you're home and staying put, which is often exactly when a storm knocks the power out, and unavailable when your routine has the car elsewhere. Whether that fits depends entirely on your household's driving patterns.

## Where V2H makes sense today

V2H is most compelling for a household that already owns — or is about to buy — a capable EV, wants outage protection, and would rather not pay again for a separate wall of storage. In that situation the car's battery is a resource already on the balance sheet, and V2H is a way to draw a second dividend from it. For essential-load backup, sizing the loads you'd actually run matters more than the raw pack size; the [solar battery calculator](/solar-battery-calculator/) helps translate a fridge, some lights, and a furnace fan into the kilowatt-hours a night of backup really needs, whether that comes from a wall unit or a driveway.

For a household that wants guaranteed, hands-off backup regardless of where the car is, a fixed battery remains the more dependable answer, and the two aren't mutually exclusive — some owners keep a modest wall battery for instant, always-present coverage and lean on the car for the long tail of a multi-day outage. As bidirectional hardware and compatible vehicles become more common, V2H shifts from a novelty toward a genuine third option alongside a home battery and a generator. The battery in the driveway is real capacity; the question is only whether it's reliably there when you need it.
