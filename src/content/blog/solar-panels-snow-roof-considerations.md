Two claims circulate in snowy markets, and both are half-wrong. A salesperson eager to close will wave off snow entirely — "the panels warm up, it slides right off, you'll never think about it." A skeptical neighbor leaning on the fence insists solar is pointless anywhere it snows and you'd be throwing money onto the roof. The honest version sits between them. Snow costs you real production in the deep of winter, but how much it costs depends far more on choices made during system design than on the weather itself. A roof laid out with shedding in mind loses a fraction of what a carelessly planned array loses on the identical street, under the identical storm.

Snow country rewards planning in two directions at once, and they pull design slightly apart. One direction is production: keeping the array clear enough, often enough, that it earns through the cold months instead of sitting buried. The other is structural: making sure the roof and the racking carry the weight of accumulated snow safely, which is not a small load. A good installer balances the two deliberately. A rushed one designs for a mild climate and hands you a system that fights the winter it lives in. Knowing what each choice actually buys you is what lets you tell the difference before you sign.

## What snow actually costs you in production

The winter loss is real, but it's smaller than it looks from the driveway when your whole roof is white. Panels sit dark against a bright roof and absorb sunlight even through a thin cover, so a light layer often melts at the glass surface and slides off before the same snow would let go of the shingles beside it. That means the losses aren't spread evenly across the season — they concentrate in a handful of genuinely heavy-snow weeks, not the whole of winter. And those weeks fall on the shortest, lowest-sun days of the year, hours that contribute little to your annual production total even under a clear sky. Losing some output in late December stings less than it would in June, because December was never going to be a big producer anyway.

Put the winter months in proportion and the fear shrinks further. In most of the country the deep-winter stretch — the weeks when snow cover is even possible — produces only a small slice of the year's total energy, because the sun is low, the days are short, and production is naturally at its floor regardless of snow. The bulk of your annual generation comes from spring through fall, when snow is a non-issue. So even a bad snow year, one that knocks out a meaningful chunk of December and January, dents an already-small part of the annual pie. People instinctively weight the winter loss heavily because it's the part they can see out the window, but the calendar quietly protects the annual number: the season most affected by snow is also the season that mattered least to your total in the first place.

This is why a snowy climate is not the same thing as a poor solar climate, a confusion that costs people good projects. Cold air actually [improves panel efficiency on the clear days that bracket the storms](/blog/solar-panels-in-cold-climates/), and there are a lot of those days — the crisp, blue-sky cold that follows a front is close to ideal operating weather for a panel. That efficiency bump partly offsets the buried days, so the annual math in snow country is usually far better than ground-level pessimism suggests. The panels that spend a week under snow in January are the same panels producing strongly through a bright, freezing February.

It's worth being precise about which snow actually costs you, because not all of it behaves the same way. A light, dry dusting after a cold clear night often burns off the dark glass within hours of the sun hitting it, sometimes before it ever really settles. A heavy, wet, sticky snow that falls at just above freezing and then gets locked in place by a hard overnight freeze is the stubborn kind — it bonds to the surface, and if it builds a ridge of ice at the bottom edge of each panel, even the snow above it can't slide until the whole mass releases. Those wet-then-frozen events are where most of your real winter losses come from, and they're relatively few in a season. The powdery cover that photos make look catastrophic is usually gone fast. Reading a white roof from the driveway, you can't tell the two apart, which is exactly why ground-level impressions overstate the damage.

There's even a small bonus hiding in a snowy landscape. Fresh snow on the ground around the house is bright and reflective, and some of that reflected light reaches the panels and adds a little production on clear days, an effect that's modest on standard panels but real. It won't rescue a buried array, but it's one more reason the winter picture is less bleak than it looks. The practical upshot is to trust the annual estimate and not the worst-case week. Regional production models from a reputable tool already fold in typical snow losses for your area — the modeled output for a snowy zip code has the buried weeks baked in, so you don't need to subtract for snow a second time on top of an estimate that already did. What you should not do is let a single winter of white panels convince you the system is broken and start climbing on an icy roof to fix a problem that will melt on its own in a few days. The array is doing exactly what a snow-country array does: giving up a little in the depths of winter and making it back across the other nine months. ## The design levers that shed snow

If one thing on a snowy roof matters more than any other, it's pitch. Snow slides when gravity finally beats the friction holding it against the slick panel glass, and a steeper roof crosses that threshold sooner and more reliably. On a pitch around 35 degrees or steeper, snow tends to release off the panels within a day or two of a storm, helped along by the dark glass warming underneath it. On a low-slope roof near 10 degrees, the same snow can sit for a week and then freeze into a stubborn slab at the base of each panel, right where meltwater refreezes overnight. You rarely rebuild a roof for solar, and nobody's suggesting you should. But when a house offers more than one roof plane, favoring the steeper one for the array is close to a free win — you're choosing the surface that clears itself instead of the one that holds snow like a shelf.

A ground mount deserves a mention here, because it hands you the one thing a roof usually can't: control over the tilt. Where a roof forces the array to whatever pitch the builder chose, a ground-mounted array can be set at a steep, snow-shedding angle regardless of any roof, and some owners in heavy-snow country deliberately run a steeper winter tilt precisely so the panels clear themselves. A ground mount is also reachable — you can sweep the bottom row with a soft roof rake from solid footing rather than climbing onto an icy roof — which turns manual clearing from a genuine hazard into a two-minute chore on the rare day it's worth doing. The trade is that a ground mount needs the land and enough height under the lowest panel that a winter's accumulation on the ground doesn't drift up and bury it. Where the space exists, though, it sidesteps the roof-pitch lottery entirely, which is why it's a common choice on rural snow-country properties that have the room for it.

Orientation compounds what pitch starts. A south-facing array in the northern hemisphere catches the most direct winter sun, and that does double duty in the cold: it produces more energy on short days, and it warms the panel's contact layer faster so the snow above it releases sooner. This is one of the cases where the general advice about [roof pitch and panel output](/blog/roof-pitch-solar-panels/) matters more than it does in a mild climate, because the same steep south face that surrenders a sliver of peak summer production buys back reliability all winter. In a snowy market that trade is usually worth making — a slightly lower July number in exchange for panels that don't disappear for weeks at a time is a good deal for most homeowners.

The panels themselves play a quieter role in shedding than most people realize. A smooth, framed module with a slick glass surface sheds better than one where a protruding frame at the lower edge creates a lip for snow and ice to catch on. It's not a reason to choose your entire system around it, but where a design leaves the choice open, mounting orientation and the panel's edge profile both nudge shedding in the right direction, and a thoughtful installer in snow country factors that in rather than treating every panel as identical. The same goes for row spacing: packing panels tight to maximize capacity can create continuous surfaces where snow bridges from one panel to the next and sits as a single slab, while a little breathing room can help each panel clear on its own. These are second-order effects next to pitch and orientation, but in a marginal winter climate the second-order effects are the difference between an array that clears itself by Tuesday and one that's still white on Friday.

Placement is the third lever, and it's where a tempting shortcut turns into a hazard. Some homeowners ask to leave a bare gap at the eave so snow can slide off freely. That does help shedding, but it costs you production area, and done carelessly it drops heavy slabs of snow and ice onto walkways, driveways, gutters, and whatever — or whoever — is standing below. Panel placement near roof edges is a safety decision as much as an energy one, and a good designer treats it that way, keeping the shed zone clear of doors and paths. The same caution applies to the urge to climb up and clear the array by hand after every storm; whether that's ever worth the risk, and how to do it without cracking a panel or falling, is its own question worth thinking through before you buy a roof rake, and one that [snow on solar panels](/blog/snow-on-solar-panels/) gets into directly. For most well-pitched arrays the honest answer is to let physics do the work and leave the ladder in the garage.

## Structural load and the sites design can't rescue

Snow is heavy, and an array changes how a roof handles it in ways you can't see from below. Panels add their own dead load — modest, but not nothing — and more importantly they change how snow drifts and where it piles. Rows of panels can act as small dams, holding snow that would otherwise blow clear of a bare roof, and the sheltered space beneath a tilted panel can trap accumulation that then sits and compacts. A roof that shrugged off snow for years can behave differently once it's carrying an array, which is exactly why this part of the design has to be conservative rather than clever.

A competent installer confirms that your roof structure and the racking are rated for the local ground snow load your building code specifies, with the array's added weight and its effect on drifting included in the calculation. That ground snow load isn't a guess; building codes publish it by location, and it's the design snowfall the whole roof is already supposed to carry. The array's job is to not make that harder — which is why the calculation looks at more than just the panels' own weight. It has to account for where snow now piles that used to blow clear, and for the load paths through the mounting feet into the rafters below. In heavy-snow regions this occasionally means extra attachment points to spread the load, upgraded racking rated for the conditions, or — rarely — a structural review before the project goes ahead. None of that is exotic or alarming; it's ordinary engineering for a place that gets serious snow. But it is the reason a snow-country quote can look a little different from an identical system priced in a mild climate, and a quote that ignores snow load entirely in a region that routinely gets four feet of it is a quote worth questioning. You want to see that someone actually did the load math, not that they assumed the roof would be fine.

The sliding-snow hazard deserves its own line in that conversation, because it's the part that can hurt someone rather than just cost production. A steep, panel-covered roof that sheds beautifully is also a roof that periodically launches a heavy slab of snow and ice off its edge with real force. Where that slab lands matters: over a walkway, a driveway where cars park, a doorway people use, or a gas meter or heat pump below, it's a genuine safety and property concern, not a theoretical one. The fix is design, not luck — orienting the shed zone away from where people and things sit, or adding snow guards where the layout leaves no better option, accepting that guards slow shedding in exchange for controlling where it happens. Raising it with your installer before the array is placed is far easier than relocating a walkway afterward.

It's fair to ask what a good snow-country quote should actually show you on this front, since the load work happens out of sight and you're taking it partly on trust. Look for evidence that the installer knew and used your jurisdiction's design snow load rather than a generic number, that the racking specified is rated for those conditions, and that the layout accounts for where snow will now pile and slide rather than treating the roof as if it were in a mild climate. You don't need to check the engineer's arithmetic, but you should see that the arithmetic was done — a quote that mentions snow load, shed zones, and attachment spacing is one where someone thought about winter, and a quote that reads identically to one you'd get in a snow-free state is one where nobody did. The difference rarely shows up in the glossy summary; it shows up when you ask, and how readily the installer can speak to it tells you whether the design engaged with the climate or ignored it.

Honesty also means naming the sites where no amount of thoughtful design rescues the numbers, because those cases exist. A low-slope roof in a heavy-snowfall region, with no steeper plane available to use instead, will lose meaningful winter production no matter how the array is arranged — the project can still pencil out on the strength of the other three seasons, but you should insist on seeing a production estimate that honestly reflects those losses rather than a rosy one that quietly pretends the snow away. A shaded, north-facing roof in snow country is close to a non-starter, combining the two worst factors a panel can face. And a ground mount, which otherwise solves pitch and access beautifully, needs enough height that a winter's accumulation doesn't bury the bottom row and choke the whole string. The workable path for most snowy homes is unglamorous and reliable: put a well-pitched, south-leaning array on a roof engineered for the local load, keep the shed zone clear of where people walk, size the system against your real annual usage rather than a summer-only fantasy, and accept a few dark weeks in January as the price of doing business with winter. Done that way, snow country runs a great deal of solar, and it runs it well — because the roof was designed for the climate instead of against it.
