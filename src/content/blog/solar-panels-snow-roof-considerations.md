Two claims circulate in snowy markets, and both are half-wrong. Salespeople sometimes wave off snow entirely — "the panels warm up and it slides right off." Skeptical neighbors insist solar is pointless anywhere it snows. The honest version sits in between: snow costs you real production in the deep of winter, but how much depends far more on choices you make during design than on the weather itself. A roof laid out with shedding in mind loses a fraction of what a poorly planned one does on the same street.

Snow country rewards planning in two directions at once. One is production — keeping the array clear enough, often enough, to earn through the cold months. The other is structural — making sure the roof and racking carry the load of accumulated snow safely. They pull design in slightly different directions, and a good installer balances them deliberately rather than by default.

## What snow actually costs you in production

The winter loss is real but smaller than it looks from the ground. Panels sit dark against a bright roof and absorb sunlight, so a thin layer often melts at the glass surface and slides before it would on the shingles beside it. Losses concentrate in a handful of heavy-snow weeks, not across the whole season, and those weeks are already the shortest, lowest-sun days of the year — hours that contribute little to your annual total even in perfect conditions.

Regional production estimates from a reputable modeling tool already fold in typical snow losses for your area, so you don't need to subtract again. What you should not do is let a winter of covered panels convince you the system is broken. A snowy climate is not a poor solar climate; cold air actually [improves panel efficiency on the clear days that surround the storms](/blog/solar-panels-in-cold-climates/), which partly offsets the buried days.

## Pitch and orientation do most of the shedding work

If there's one design lever that matters most in snow country, it's roof pitch. Snow slides when gravity beats the friction holding it to the slick panel glass, and steeper roofs cross that threshold sooner. On a pitch around 35 degrees or steeper, snow tends to shed off panels within a day or two of a storm; on a low-slope roof near 10 degrees, it can sit for a week and turn to ice at the base. You rarely rebuild a roof for solar, but when a home has multiple roof planes, favoring the steeper one for the array is close to a free win.

Orientation compounds the effect. A south-facing array in the northern hemisphere catches the most direct winter sun, which does double duty: it produces more and it melts the contact layer faster, so snow releases sooner. This is one of the cases where the general advice about [pitch and panel output](/blog/roof-pitch-solar-panels/) matters more than usual, because the same steep south face that gives up a little summer production buys back reliability all winter.

A caution against a tempting fix: some homeowners ask to leave a gap at the eave so snow can slide freely. That helps shedding but sacrifices production area and, done carelessly, drops heavy slabs of snow onto walkways below. Panel placement near roof edges is a safety decision as much as an energy one.

## Structural load: the part you can't see

Snow is heavy, and a solar array changes how a roof handles it. Panels add their own dead load — modest, but not zero — and they change how snow drifts and where it piles. Rows can create small dams that hold snow that would otherwise blow clear, and the space beneath a tilted panel can trap it.

This is where design has to be conservative rather than clever. A competent installer checks that your roof structure and the racking are rated for the local ground snow load your building code specifies, with the array's added weight included. In heavy-snow regions this occasionally means additional attachment points, upgraded racking, or, rarely, a structural review before the project proceeds. None of it is exotic, but it is the reason a snow-country quote may look different from an identical system in a mild climate — and a quote that ignores snow load entirely in a place that gets four feet of it is a quote worth questioning.

## Where design can't rescue a site

Honesty requires naming the cases design can't fix. A low-slope roof with heavy annual snowfall and no steeper plane to use will lose meaningful winter production no matter how the array is laid out; the numbers can still work on the strength of the other three seasons, but you should see a production estimate that reflects the losses rather than a rosy one that pretends them away. A shaded north face in snow country is close to a non-starter. And ground mounts, while they solve pitch and access beautifully, need enough clearance that a winter's accumulation doesn't bury the bottom row.

The workable path in most snowy homes is unglamorous: put a well-pitched, south-leaning array on a roof engineered for the local load, size it against your real annual usage rather than a summer-only fantasy, and accept a few dark weeks in January as part of the deal. The result is usually better than the ground-level pessimism suggests — snow country runs a great deal of solar, and it runs it well when the roof is designed for the climate instead of against it.
