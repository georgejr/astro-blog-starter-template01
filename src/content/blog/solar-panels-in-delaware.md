Delaware is a small state with an outsized reason to look closely at solar, and it isn't the sunshine. The Mid-Atlantic gets respectable but unremarkable sun — figure something in the range of 4 to 4.5 peak sun hours averaged across the year, roughly comparable to New Jersey and Maryland next door. What sets Delaware apart is the way a home solar system can earn money two separate ways at once. The first is the obvious one: the electricity you generate offsets electricity you'd otherwise buy, and Delaware's retail rates are high enough to make that offset worthwhile. The second is less obvious and easy to overlook if you've only shopped solar in states that don't have it — a market for Solar Renewable Energy Certificates, or SRECs, that pays you for the mere fact of generating clean power, independent of the electricity's value on your bill. Understanding how those two revenue streams stack is the key to reading a Delaware solar quote honestly.

## What the grid and the rates look like before you factor in certificates

Delaware sits inside the PJM Interconnection, the large regional grid that stretches across the Mid-Atlantic and into the Midwest. For a homeowner, the practical consequence of being in PJM is less about grid mechanics and more about the regulatory environment that comes with it — a functioning renewable energy market, a state renewable portfolio standard that creates demand for solar certificates, and utilities that operate under state commission rules for how they compensate rooftop generation. The two main utility territories most residential customers fall into are Delmarva Power, the investor-owned utility serving much of the state, and the Delaware Electric Cooperative, which serves a large rural swath. Which one you're in matters, because net metering terms and any utility-specific credits are set at that level, not statewide in perfect uniformity. As with everywhere, your utility's current tariff is the controlling document, and it's worth reading rather than assuming.

On the bill-savings side, the logic is the same as any state: the higher your retail rate, the more each self-generated kilowatt-hour is worth. Delaware's residential electricity rates have generally run above the national average, which tilts the basic economics in solar's favor before any certificates enter the picture. Net metering has historically applied to residential systems, meaning exported power earns bill credits that offset later consumption, though the precise structure — whether credits roll indefinitely, how any annual reconciliation works, and what the export credit is worth relative to retail — is the kind of detail that changes and should be confirmed with your utility rather than taken from a blog. What's durable to say is that Delaware has treated rooftop solar reasonably on the billing side, and that the retail rate environment gives you a solid foundation of ordinary savings.

To get a feel for the scale, it helps to run a stripped-down example and label every assumption. Suppose a Delaware home uses 10,000 kWh a year and pays an average all-in retail rate of $0.16 per kWh — a figure you should replace with your own, since rates move and vary by utility and season. A system sized to produce roughly that same 10,000 kWh, offsetting most of the bill, would be avoiding on the order of $1,600 a year in electricity purchases if it offset essentially all of that usage at retail value. That's the baseline before certificates. It's a respectable number, driven mostly by Delaware's above-average rates rather than by heroic amounts of sun, and it's the number most homeowners in most states would stop at. Delaware gives you a second layer on top of it, which is where the state gets genuinely interesting. If you want to see how Delaware stacks against neighbors and against the national spread, the state-by-state overview in [why solar pays off in some states and barely breaks even in others](/blog/solar-panels-by-state/) puts the rate-and-incentive picture in context, and it's a useful reminder that two states with identical sunshine can produce very different returns.

## How SRECs turn your generation into a second, tradable revenue stream

The mechanism that distinguishes Delaware — shared with a handful of Mid-Atlantic neighbors and not with most of the country — is the SREC. A Solar Renewable Energy Certificate is created for every megawatt-hour, that is, every 1,000 kilowatt-hours, of electricity your system generates. It represents the environmental attribute of that generation, and crucially it's separate from the electricity itself. You consume or export the actual power and get bill value for it; you also earn a certificate for having generated it, and that certificate can be sold. The full mechanics of how certificates are created, tracked, and sold are laid out in [SRECs, explained](/blog/srecs-explained/), and the concept is worth reading in general terms because it behaves unlike any other solar incentive. It isn't a rebate off your install cost and it isn't a tax credit against what you owe the IRS. It's a commodity your roof produces year after year, on top of the electricity.

The reason these certificates have value comes down to a policy called a renewable portfolio standard. Delaware, like other states with an RPS, requires electricity suppliers to source a rising share of their power from renewables, including a specific carve-out for solar. Suppliers demonstrate compliance by holding enough SRECs — and rather than build their own solar, many buy certificates from homeowners and other generators to meet the requirement. That obligation on the supply side creates demand for the certificates your panels mint, and the trading of certificates between generators and obligated suppliers is what gives an SREC a market price. It's genuinely a second income stream: your system generates power, and separately it generates certificates that someone is required by policy to buy.

The honest and important caveat is that SREC prices are not fixed, not guaranteed, and not something to model as a stable annual figure. They're set by supply and demand in a market that can swing considerably from year to year. When lots of solar gets built and certificates are plentiful, prices soften; when the standard tightens or supply is scarce, prices firm up. States typically build a ceiling into the system — a compliance payment suppliers can make instead of buying a certificate, which effectively caps the price — but there's no comparable hard floor, so the downside is real. Some homeowners choose to sell their certificates through an aggregator that handles registration and trading for a cut, and some programs offer contracts that lock in a price for a period in exchange for a lower rate. Because of that volatility, the right way to treat SREC income in a Delaware solar decision is as an upside that improves an already-sound investment, not as the load-bearing assumption that makes a shaky one pencil out. If the bill savings alone don't justify the system at a conservative electricity rate, certificate revenue is a fragile thing to lean the whole case on. If the bill savings make it a reasonable buy on their own, SRECs are the sweetener that can meaningfully shorten the payback — and modeling them at a deliberately cautious price, rather than a peak one, keeps the decision honest. The [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) lets you test how sensitive your payback is to the certificate price you plug in, which is exactly the sensitivity worth checking before you sign.

## Stacking the federal credit and reading a Delaware quote

Sitting above both the bill savings and the SREC income is the federal Residential Clean Energy Credit, which applies in Delaware exactly as it does everywhere else in the country. It's a credit worth 30% of the qualifying cost of your system, claimed on your federal return, and it's the single largest incentive most homeowners will receive — larger, usually, than several years of combined bill savings and certificate income. Because it's a credit against your federal tax liability rather than a refund, whether you can use the full amount in the year you install depends on your tax situation, with unused credit generally carrying forward. The details of who qualifies and how the credit interacts with your liability are covered in [the solar tax credit, explained](/blog/solar-tax-credit-explained/), and the arithmetic for your specific system cost is what the [federal tax credit calculator](/federal-tax-credit-calculator/) is for. The one discipline worth keeping is to treat it as 30% of qualifying cost with a pointer to current IRS guidance, rather than assuming any particular dollar figure — the structure is stable, but you should confirm the specifics for your own return.

Put the three layers together and a Delaware system looks like this: the federal credit knocks roughly a third off the upfront cost, the retail bill savings provide a steady annual return driven by above-average rates, and the SREC market adds a variable but potentially meaningful third stream for years. That's a genuinely favorable stack, and it's why Delaware punches above what its middling sunshine alone would suggest. The catch — and it's the thing to watch hardest when reading a quote — is that some installers fold an optimistic SREC price into their savings projection to make the payback look shorter, sometimes using a certificate price near the top of the historical range as if it were guaranteed for the life of the system. When you see a Delaware proposal, ask specifically how the SREC revenue is modeled: what price per certificate, whether that price is assumed flat for years, and what the payback looks like if certificate income is stripped out entirely. A quote that still makes sense without SREC revenue is one built on solid ground, with the certificates as upside. A quote that only pencils out because it assumes years of top-dollar certificate prices is leaning on the least predictable part of the whole equation. Delaware genuinely gives homeowners a second way to get paid for their panels — the trick is valuing it conservatively enough that it stays a pleasant surprise rather than a disappointment.
