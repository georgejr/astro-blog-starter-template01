When homeowners get a battery quote, they usually focus on the battery and the inverter and miss a line item that can quietly add a meaningful chunk to the bill: the critical-load panel, sometimes called an essential-loads subpanel or a backup panel. It's the electrical box that makes partial backup work. If you're not backing up your entire house — and most battery owners aren't, because whole-home backup demands far more storage and often more expensive equipment — then the circuits you *do* want to keep alive during an outage have to be physically separated from the rest, and that separation is a real piece of electrical work with real cost. It's not glamorous, it doesn't show up in the glossy battery brochure, and it's frequently underexplained in the sales conversation, but it's the difference between a battery that seamlessly powers your fridge and lights when the grid fails and one that's wired to do nothing useful. Understanding what this panel is, why it costs what it does, and how the design choices move the number keeps it from being an unwelcome surprise deep in a contract.

## What the panel does and why it costs what it does

A critical-load panel is a second, smaller electrical panel that houses only the circuits you want the battery to keep running during an outage — typically the refrigerator, some lighting, the furnace or boiler controls, a few outlets for phones and medical devices, maybe the internet equipment and a well pump. During normal operation, grid power feeds this subpanel just like any other; when the grid drops, the battery system isolates from the utility and powers only this subpanel, ignoring the big loads left on your main panel. That selective isolation is the whole point of partial backup, and it's what lets a modestly sized battery provide meaningful resilience without having to carry your entire house, including the air conditioner and electric dryer it could never sustain for long. The reason it costs money is that creating this separation is genuine electrician's labor. Someone has to install the new subpanel enclosure, then identify each circuit you want backed up and physically move those circuits from the main panel to the subpanel — re-landing wires, extending conductors where they don't reach, and in some cases running new wire. Then the whole arrangement has to be connected to the battery system's backup output and integrated with the transfer equipment that switches between grid and battery, all of it inspected and permitted like any electrical work. It's careful, code-governed labor, and labor is the bulk of the cost.

As a planning figure — and this is an assumption to sanity-check against your own quotes, not a fixed quote — the subpanel hardware and the labor to populate it commonly land somewhere in the range of roughly $1,000 to $3,000, with plenty of installations falling outside that band in either direction depending on how much rewiring is involved. A clean job where the circuits you want are few, already conveniently located near where the new panel will go, and easy to move, sits toward the low end. A job that requires relocating many circuits, running long wire home-runs across the house, or working in a finished space where walls have to be opened and patched climbs toward the high end and beyond. The variability is almost entirely in the labor and the wiring difficulty, not the box itself, which is why two homes with identical batteries can get very different subpanel prices. This is the same dynamic that governs solar labor costs generally — the hardware is relatively predictable, and the crew's time is where the real variation lives, shaped by access, complexity, and how much has to be moved or run. [What you're actually paying the crew for on a solar install](/blog/solar-installation-labor-cost/) breaks down that labor logic, and the critical-load panel is a textbook example of it: a small box whose installed price is dominated by how hard it is to rewire your particular house, not by the cost of the panel on the shelf.

## The design choices that move the number

The single biggest lever on critical-load panel cost is how many circuits you back up and which ones, because every circuit added to the subpanel is another set of wires an electrician has to relocate, and some circuits are far more disruptive to move than others. A short, honest essential-loads list — fridge, a lighting circuit or two, furnace controls, a few outlets — keeps the rewiring modest and the cost down. A wish list that creeps toward "well, back up the microwave, and the office, and the bedroom AC, and the garage" not only balloons the subpanel labor but also demands a bigger battery to sustain those loads, so scope creep on the panel and scope creep on the battery compound each other. This is why the essential-loads exercise is worth doing carefully before you finalize anything: deciding deliberately what genuinely needs to run in an outage, rather than defaulting to "as much as possible," controls both the panel cost and the storage cost at once. [Sizing a battery for essential loads only](/blog/battery-sizing-essential-loads/) is the companion decision, and it pays to make it in tandem with the subpanel scope, because the list of circuits you route into the backup panel is the same list that determines how much battery you need. Trim one and you trim both.

The physical layout of your existing electrical system is the other major cost driver, and it's largely outside your control but very much worth understanding before you're quoted. If your main panel is in an accessible garage or basement with room beside it for a subpanel, and the circuits you want are on nearby breakers with enough wire slack to reach, the job is straightforward. If your panel is in a cramped or finished location, if the essential circuits originate far from where the subpanel needs to go, or if the wiring is old and short on slack, the electrician faces extra work — running new conductors, fishing wire through finished walls, patching afterward — that pushes the price up. There's also the question of the backup architecture itself. Some newer battery systems use a smart electrical panel or an integrated backup gateway that can select which circuits to back up without a separate physical subpanel and all its rewiring, which changes the cost structure — you may pay more for the smart hardware but less in rewiring labor, or vice versa, depending on the product and your house. It's worth asking your installer whether your battery system needs a traditional critical-load subpanel or offers an integrated alternative, because the answer materially affects both the price and how flexible your backup will be. The broader decision of what to back up at all — a curated set of circuits versus the whole house — sits upstream of all these cost questions, and [whole-home versus partial backup](/blog/whole-home-vs-partial-backup/) lays out that trade-off, since choosing partial backup is precisely what creates the need for the critical-load panel in the first place. Whole-home backup skips the subpanel but demands far more storage and often costlier equipment; partial backup adds the subpanel but shrinks the battery. The critical-load panel is the price of the partial-backup path, and it usually buys you a much cheaper overall system than backing up everything.

## Budgeting for it without getting surprised

The way this line item catches people is that it's often folded into a battery proposal in a way that obscures it, or left vague until the electrical work is scoped after signing. The defense is to ask directly, early, and in writing: does this quote include a critical-load panel, what circuits will it back up, and what's the separately itemized cost of the panel and the associated rewiring labor? A reputable installer can answer that, and a quote that lumps everything into one round number without breaking out the backup panel is worth pushing on, because it's exactly the kind of cost that can shift once the electrician actually looks at your main panel. Treat the roughly $1,000-to-$3,000 range as a planning placeholder to hold in your head while you gather real quotes, expecting your actual figure to depend on your circuit count and your home's wiring difficulty, and don't be alarmed if a complex home comes in higher — that's the wiring reality, not padding. When you're comparing battery proposals from different installers, make sure they're scoping the same set of backup circuits, because one company quoting a lean three-circuit subpanel against another quoting a generous eight-circuit one aren't offering the same thing, and the price difference may be scope rather than value.

Sizing the whole partial-backup package sensibly is what keeps the subpanel cost in proportion to what it buys you, and that starts with the battery-and-loads side rather than the panel in isolation. The point to carry away is that the critical-load panel is not an optional extra or a markup — it's the necessary plumbing that makes partial backup function, and partial backup is usually the cost-effective choice precisely because the subpanel it requires is far cheaper than the oversized battery whole-home backup would demand. Budget for it deliberately, keep the circuit list honest, and it becomes a modest, well-understood line item rather than the surprise at the bottom of the contract that it too often is.
