Ask around online whether leasing solar is a good idea and you'll get a lot of strong opinions delivered as if they were settled facts. "Never lease" is a popular one. So is "leasing is the only way to go solar with no money." Both are too blunt to be useful, because a solar lease isn't good or bad in the abstract — it's a specific financial trade, and whether it works for you depends on your tax situation, your appetite for upfront cost, how long you plan to stay in the house, and how much you value simplicity over maximum return. The honest framing is that a lease usually gives up the best possible savings in exchange for eliminating the biggest barrier to going solar: the upfront cost and the responsibility that come with owning the equipment.

To evaluate it fairly you have to understand what a lease actually is and, just as importantly, what it isn't. When you lease solar, or sign the close cousin of a lease known as a power purchase agreement, a third-party company owns the panels on your roof. You don't buy the system; you pay the company a monthly amount — a fixed lease payment for a lease, or a per-kilowatt-hour rate for the power under a PPA — and in return you get to use the electricity the panels produce. That single fact, that someone else owns the hardware, is the root of every advantage and every drawback that follows, so it's the place to start.

## What you give up, and what you get, when someone else owns the panels

The biggest thing you surrender by leasing is the federal tax credit, and this is the point that turns a lot of people against leases once they understand it. The federal Residential Clean Energy Credit lets the owner of a solar system claim 30% of qualified costs against their federal income tax, under current IRS guidance. But the operative word is owner. In a lease or PPA, the third-party company owns the system, so the company claims that 30% credit, not you. On a typical residential system that credit is worth thousands of dollars, and when you lease, that money goes to the leasing company. A good lease deal passes some of that value back to you in the form of lower payments — the company can afford to charge you less precisely because it collected the credit — but you never touch the credit directly, and you're trusting that the pricing genuinely reflects it. When you buy, that 30% comes straight off your own cost. That difference alone accounts for much of the savings gap between leasing and buying.

The second thing you give up is the larger long-term savings that come with ownership. When you buy a system — with cash or a loan — you eventually own an asset that produces free electricity for decades after it's paid off, and your savings grow over the system's life as electricity rates rise while your production cost stays flat. With a lease, you're making payments for the entire term, often 20 to 25 years, and many leases include an escalator clause that raises your payment a small percentage every year. That escalator is the detail that quietly erodes the deal: if your lease payment climbs 2 to 3% annually, your "savings" versus the utility can shrink over time, and in an unlucky case where utility rates rise slowly and your escalator marches on, the gap can narrow uncomfortably. The savings from a lease are real but capped and front-loaded in appeal, whereas ownership's savings start smaller (you had to pay for the thing) and grow. The full accounting of that difference, dollar for dollar, is worked through in [leasing vs buying solar panels: the cost difference](/blog/solar-lease-vs-buy-cost/), and it's the single most useful comparison to read before deciding.

There's also the matter of what happens to the payback concept itself, which trips people up. When you buy solar, you have a payback period — a point where cumulative savings equal what you spent, after which you're ahead. A lease has no payback period in that sense, because you never made an upfront investment to recoup; you're simply paying monthly for power, ideally at a rate below the utility's, for as long as the agreement runs. That's not automatically bad — a smaller, guaranteed monthly discount with nothing down is a legitimate value to some people — but it means you can't evaluate a lease with the same break-even math you'd use for a purchase. Why a leased system has no break-even point, and why that changes how you should judge it, is the subject of [why leased solar has no payback period](/blog/solar-lease-payback/), which is worth reading if you keep trying to force lease numbers into a buyer's framework and finding they don't fit.

Now the other side of the ledger, because a lease genuinely does buy you things. The first is zero or near-zero upfront cost. Owning solar, even with a loan, typically involves either a large cash outlay or a financed balance you're responsible for; a lease can put panels on your roof for little or nothing down and a predictable monthly payment from day one. For a household that can't or won't tie up cash and doesn't want to take on solar debt, that's a real door-opener. The second is that you offload responsibility for the equipment. Because the leasing company owns the system, they're generally on the hook for maintenance, repairs, and monitoring — if the inverter fails or a panel underperforms, that's their problem to fix, not a bill that lands on you. For an owner who doesn't want to think about hardware, warranties, or an eventual inverter replacement, that hands-off arrangement has genuine appeal. You're trading the upside of ownership for the simplicity of being a customer rather than an operator.

## Who each path actually suits

Because the trade is real in both directions, the useful question isn't "is leasing bad" but "which situation are you in." The clearest case for buying — with cash or a loan — is a homeowner with enough federal tax liability to actually use the 30% credit, the means to cover the upfront cost or qualify for a reasonable loan, and the intention to stay in the home long enough to enjoy the growing savings and eventual free power. For that person, ownership almost always produces more total savings, because they capture the tax credit themselves, avoid the escalator, and end up owning a productive asset. If that describes you, a lease is usually the weaker financial choice, and the online "just buy it" advice is aimed squarely at you. The broader question of whether solar makes sense at all for your situation — before you even get to how to pay for it — is worth settling first, and [is solar worth it](/blog/is-solar-worth-it/) frames that underlying decision.

The case for leasing is stronger than the internet's blanket dismissals admit, and it centers on a few specific situations. The most important is tax liability: the federal credit is nonrefundable, meaning it can only offset federal income tax you actually owe. A retiree on a modest fixed income, or anyone whose tax bill is too small to absorb a several-thousand-dollar credit, may not be able to use the full credit if they buy — and for that person, leasing can be the better deal precisely because the leasing company can use the credit and pass some of the benefit through. If the credit would be stranded in your hands, handing it to someone who can monetize it isn't a loss in the same way. A lease also suits a homeowner who wants the electricity-bill relief without any upfront cost, doesn't want to manage equipment or plan for an eventual inverter replacement, and values a predictable monthly payment over maximizing lifetime return. And it can appeal to someone who's uncertain about staying in the home very long, though that cuts both ways because a lease can complicate a future home sale.

That home-sale wrinkle deserves a flag, because it's the lease drawback most likely to cause a real headache. When you sell a house with a leased system, the buyer generally has to either assume the remaining lease — agreeing to take over the payments — or you have to buy out the lease before closing. Some buyers are happy to inherit a below-market power deal; others balk at taking on a long-term obligation attached to equipment they didn't choose, and that reluctance can slow a sale or force a buyout you didn't budget for. Owned systems don't carry this friction in the same way; they simply convey with the house as an asset. So if there's a real chance you'll move within the lease term, factor in the possibility of a buyout or a complicated closing, and weigh it against the appeal of zero upfront cost.

If you do lean toward a lease, the contract is where a fair deal and a regrettable one separate, and it rewards slow, careful reading. The escalator clause is the first thing to find: a lease with no annual escalator, or a low one, keeps your payment predictable, while an aggressive escalator can erode your savings year after year until the deal you signed looks very different a decade in. Look next at the term length and at the buyout provisions — what it would cost to end the agreement early or purchase the system outright, and at what points in the term those options open up — because those terms govern your flexibility if your circumstances change. Check how the contract handles a home sale, since the ease or difficulty of transferring the lease to a buyer is exactly the friction that can complicate a future closing. Many leases also include a production guarantee, promising the system will generate a certain amount and compensating you if it falls short; understand what that guarantee actually covers and how a shortfall gets remedied. And confirm who is responsible for maintenance, repairs, monitoring, and insurance on the equipment, since the whole appeal of leasing is offloading that responsibility, and the contract is where that promise is either real or hollow. These aren't reasons to avoid a lease; they're the difference between a lease that does what it should and one whose fine print quietly works against you, and reading them before signing is the cheapest protection you have.

The way to cut through all of this for your own situation is to compare the actual paths side by side rather than arguing from principle. Line up cash purchase, loan purchase, and lease or PPA for the same system, using your real numbers — your tax liability, the quoted lease payment and its escalator, the loan terms you'd qualify for — and see what each does to your costs over time. A [cash vs. loan vs. lease comparator](/solar-payment-comparison/) is built for exactly this, laying the three payment structures against each other so the trade-offs show up in dollars instead of opinions. And if you want to test how the ownership paths pay back specifically, running the purchase scenarios through a [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/) shows where buying pulls ahead and by how much, which is the number the "never lease" crowd is really pointing at. Do that comparison honestly and the answer usually stops being a slogan and becomes a fit: for most buyers who can use the tax credit and stay put, ownership wins; for a household that can't use the credit, won't front the cost, or wants nothing to do with owning equipment, a fairly priced lease is a reasonable way to put the sun to work without the deal being anything close to a mistake.
