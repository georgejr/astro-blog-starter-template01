Picture a homeowner with a north-sloping, tree-shaded roof and half an acre of open, sunny lawn behind the house. The roof is the obvious place to put panels only out of habit. The lawn is where the sun actually is. That gap — between where panels are convenient and where they perform — is the whole reason ground-mounted solar exists, and it is why the question of roof versus ground is rarely about which is better in the abstract. It is about which one fits the specific patch of dirt and shingles you happen to own.

A ground mount is exactly what it sounds like: an array set on a steel or aluminum frame anchored into the yard, wired back to the house, and otherwise electrically identical to a rooftop system. The panels, the inverter, the interconnection to your main panel — all the same. What changes is the structure holding everything up, and with it, two things that matter a great deal: how much the project costs, and how freely you get to aim the panels at the sun.

## Why anyone leaves a perfectly good roof empty

Roofs constrain solar in ways a yard simply does not, and a ground mount removes several of those constraints at once. The most valuable freedom is orientation and tilt. On a roof, you inherit whatever direction and pitch the builder chose decades ago, for reasons that had nothing to do with solar. A ground mount lets you face the array due south at the ideal tilt for your latitude, and that freedom alone can lift annual production noticeably compared with a compromised roof plane. A home whose only good roof faces east and west, splitting the array away from the midday sun, may recover a real chunk of that lost output just by standing the panels up in the yard and pointing them where they belong.

Shading is the second constraint a yard escapes. On a roof, you are stuck with the shadows the roof lives under — the neighbor's oak, your own chimney, the dormer that clips the afternoon sun. In a yard, you place the array where the light is, not where the rafters are. If the house sits under mature trees but the back of the lot is open, the decision very nearly makes itself. And because you control the siting, you can plan around the low winter sun deliberately instead of discovering a shading problem after the panels are already bolted down.

Then there is the roof itself, which a ground mount leaves entirely alone. No penetrations through the shingles, no flashing to seal and re-seal, no awkward coordination when the roof eventually needs replacing and the array has to come off and go back on. For an older roof, that avoided hassle can matter as much as any production gain. A roof with ten years left in it is a poor host for a system meant to run for twenty-five, and putting the panels in the yard sidesteps the whole timing conflict. Maintenance follows the same logic: cleaning, inspecting, and clearing snow all happen at ground level, with no ladder and no walking a pitched surface. For homeowners who would never feel comfortable on the roof, that accessibility is not a small thing.

None of this comes free, which is the catch that sends most people back to the roof. But the list is worth taking seriously before you default to shingles out of habit, because the situations where a ground mount wins are real and more common than the roof-first instinct suggests.

## What the extra cost buys, and why it exists

A ground mount usually carries a per-watt premium over an equivalent rooftop system, and it helps to understand where that money goes rather than treating it as an arbitrary surcharge. Treat a range of roughly **$0.20–0.50/W** in added cost as a planning assumption rather than a quote; the real figure depends on your soil, the distance the wire has to travel, and local labor rates. On an 8 kW system, that premium alone can add a few thousand dollars before you have compared anything else about the two approaches.

The reason the premium exists is straightforward: a roof already exists as a mounting surface, and a ground mount has to build one from scratch. That construction shows up as three distinct line items. The first is the foundation and racking. The frame needs footings — driven piles, ground screws, or concrete piers — engineered to resist wind uplift and, in cold regions, the frost heave that can shove an underbuilt foundation out of the ground over a few winters. That is real steel, real excavation, and often an engineered drawing that a roof system never required. The full anatomy of these added line items is broken down in [why ground-mounted solar costs more than rooftop](/blog/ground-mount-solar-cost/), and it is worth reading before you assume the premium is padding rather than genuine construction.

The second line item is trenching and the wire run. Power has to travel from the array to your main panel, almost always through buried conduit. The longer that trench, the more you pay in labor and copper, and the larger the conductors you need to hold voltage drop within limits over the distance. A mount fifteen feet from the house is cheap to connect; one at the far corner of a large lot is not, and the cost climbs with every additional foot of trench your crew has to dig and backfill. If you have a choice of where to site the array, siting it close to the electrical service can meaningfully trim the bill without hurting production, provided the near spot is unshaded.

The third is permitting and setbacks. Ground mounts are often treated as a structure in their own right, which can pull in property setback rules, a separate structural permit, and occasionally a conversation with a homeowners association about how a yard-level array looks from the street or a neighbor's window. Roof systems rarely trigger the same scrutiny, because they sit on an already-approved structure. The paperwork is your installer's job to handle, but it can add weeks to the schedule, and in a restrictive jurisdiction it can add cost. Knowing this going in keeps a permitting delay from feeling like something went wrong when it is simply how ground mounts are reviewed.

Laid out side by side, the trade-offs sort into a fairly clean picture.

| Factor | Roof mount | Ground mount |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Base cost per watt | Lower | Higher (added racking + trenching) |
| Orientation & tilt | Fixed by the roof | Chosen for best output |
| Shading control | Limited to the roof planes | Sited wherever sun is best |
| Roof penetrations | Yes | None |
| Cleaning & maintenance | Ladder, pitched surface | Ground level |
| Land required | None | Open, unshaded space |
| Permitting | Usually straightforward | Sometimes a separate structure permit |

The honest read is this. If your roof is south-facing, unshaded, and structurally sound with plenty of life left, the roof almost always wins on cost per delivered kilowatt-hour, and you should not talk yourself into a ground mount for the novelty of it. The case for going to the ground gets stronger the worse your roof is — the more it is shaded, poorly oriented, aging, or simply too small for the system you want. A roof-suitability check like [is your roof a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/), together with the sizing constraints laid out in [how roof size affects your system](/blog/how-roof-size-affects-solar/), is the right place to start before you rule the roof out. Often the roof is fine and the ground-mount question answers itself. When it is not fine, the yard stops looking like an extravagance and starts looking like the sensible option it is.

Because the two paths can differ by thousands of dollars, and can differ again in how much they produce, the only way to compare them fairly is to run both. Put each configuration through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) to see the all-in price, then check how each one changes your break-even in the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). The result is sometimes counterintuitive. A ground mount that produces 15% more but also costs 15% more can land in almost exactly the same place on payback as the roof — or noticeably ahead of it, if the roof alternative was genuinely compromised by shade or orientation. The right answer depends on numbers specific to your property, which is precisely why running them beats trusting a rule of thumb.

## FAQ

**Does a ground mount produce more electricity than a rooftop array?**
It can, when the roof is poorly oriented, shaded, or shallow-pitched. Given a free choice of tilt and direction, a ground mount removes the compromises a roof imposes, and on a bad roof that difference can be substantial. On a good south-facing roof, the production gap is usually small and does not justify the added cost. The advantage is real only to the extent your roof was holding the array back.

**How much land do I need?**
Plan on roughly the same footprint as the panels themselves, plus clearance for maintenance and enough row spacing to keep the panels from shading each other. A typical residential array fits comfortably in a portion of an average suburban backyard, but the low winter sun angle means rows must be spaced generously so a front row does not throw shade on the one behind it during the shortest days.

**Do ground mounts need more permitting?**
Often yes. Because the array is a freestanding structure, jurisdictions may require a structural permit, engineered footings, and adherence to property setbacks that a roof mount never triggers. Your installer handles the paperwork, but it can add time to the schedule, so build that into your expectations rather than treating a review period as a sign something has gone wrong.
