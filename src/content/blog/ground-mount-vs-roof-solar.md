Picture a homeowner with a north-sloping, tree-shaded roof and half an acre of open, sunny lawn behind the house. The roof is the obvious place to put panels only out of habit. The lawn is where the sun actually is. That gap — between where panels are convenient and where they perform — is the whole reason ground-mounted solar exists.

A ground mount is exactly what it sounds like: an array set on a steel or aluminum frame anchored into the yard, wired back to the house, and otherwise electrically identical to a rooftop system. The panels, inverters, and interconnection are the same. What changes is the structure holding them up and, with it, the cost and the freedom to aim.

## Why anyone leaves a perfectly good roof empty

Roofs constrain solar in ways a yard does not. A ground mount removes several of those constraints at once.

- **Orientation and tilt are yours to choose.** Instead of accepting whatever direction and pitch the builder gave you, you can face the array due south at the ideal tilt for your latitude. That freedom alone can lift annual production noticeably compared with a compromised roof plane.
- **Shading is easier to escape.** You place the array where the sun is, not where the rafters are. If the roof sits under trees but the back forty is open, the choice makes itself.
- **The roof stays untouched.** No penetrations, no flashing, no coordinating a future re-roof around an existing array. For an older roof, that can matter as much as the production.
- **Maintenance and cleaning happen at ground level.** No ladder, no walking a pitched surface. Snow clears more easily and inspections are trivial.

None of this is free, which is the catch that sends most homeowners back to the roof.

## What the extra cost buys, and why it exists

A ground mount usually carries a per-watt premium over an equivalent rooftop system. Treat a range of roughly **$0.20–0.50/W** in added cost as a planning assumption rather than a quote; the exact figure depends on soil, trenching distance, and local labor. On a 8 kW system that premium alone can add a few thousand dollars before you compare anything else.

The money goes somewhere specific. A roof already exists as a mounting surface; a ground mount has to build one. That means:

### Foundations and racking

The frame needs footings — driven piles, ground screws, or concrete piers — engineered to resist wind uplift and, in cold regions, frost heave. That is real steel, real digging, and often an engineered drawing the roof never required. The full anatomy of these added line items is broken down in [why ground-mounted solar costs more than rooftop](/blog/ground-mount-solar-cost/).

### Trenching and the wire run

Power has to travel from the array to your main panel, usually through a buried conduit. The longer that trench, the more you pay in labor and wire, and the more voltage drop you design around. A mount fifteen feet from the house is cheap; one at the back of a large lot is not.

### Permitting and setbacks

Ground mounts are often treated as a structure in their own right, which can pull in setback rules, a separate structural permit, and occasionally a conversation with a homeowners association about a yard-level array's appearance. Roof systems rarely trigger the same scrutiny.

## Weighing the two side by side

The decision is rarely about which is better in the abstract; it is about which fits your specific roof, yard, and budget.

| Factor | Roof mount | Ground mount |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Base cost per watt | Lower | Higher (added racking + trenching) |
| Orientation & tilt | Fixed by the roof | Chosen for best output |
| Shading control | Limited to the roof planes | Sited wherever sun is best |
| Roof penetrations | Yes | None |
| Cleaning & maintenance | Ladder, pitched surface | Ground level |
| Land required | None | Open, unshaded space |
| Permitting | Usually straightforward | Sometimes a separate structure permit |

The honest read: if your roof is south-facing, unshaded, and structurally sound, the roof almost always wins on cost per delivered kilowatt-hour. The case for a ground mount gets stronger the worse your roof is — and a roof-suitability check like [is your roof a good fit for solar](/blog/is-your-roof-good-for-solar/) or the sizing constraints in [how roof size affects your system](/blog/how-roof-size-affects-solar/) is the right place to start before you rule the roof out.

Because the two paths can differ by thousands, it is worth running both through the [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) and then checking how each changes your break-even in the [solar ROI calculator](/solar-roi-calculator/). A ground mount that produces 15% more but costs 15% more may land in almost the same place on payback — or noticeably better, if the roof alternative was genuinely compromised.

## FAQ

**Does a ground mount produce more electricity than a rooftop array?**
It can, when the roof is poorly oriented, shaded, or shallow-pitched. Given a free choice of tilt and direction, a ground mount removes the compromises a roof imposes. On a good south-facing roof, the production difference is usually small and does not justify the added cost.

**How much land do I need?**
Plan on roughly the same footprint as the panels themselves plus clearance for maintenance and to avoid self-shading between rows. A typical residential array fits comfortably in a portion of an average suburban backyard, but a low winter sun angle means rows must be spaced so they do not shade each other.

**Do ground mounts need more permitting?**
Often yes. Because the array is a freestanding structure, jurisdictions may require a structural permit, engineered footings, and adherence to property setbacks. Your installer handles the paperwork, but it can add time to the schedule.
