Water heating is one of the largest electricity loads in a typical home, often trailing only heating and cooling, so the choice between the two kinds of electric water heater — a plain resistance tank and a heat pump model — has an outsized effect on the bill. The headline is genuinely striking: a heat pump water heater uses roughly a third of the electricity a standard electric-resistance tank uses to deliver the same hot water. That's not a marginal efficiency tweak; it's the difference between a water heater that quietly eats several thousand kilowatt-hours a year and one that sips a fraction of that. Understanding why the gap is so large, and where the real-world catches lie, is what separates a smart replacement decision from an expensive disappointment.

The physics behind the difference is the same idea that makes a refrigerator or a home heat pump work. An electric-resistance water heater makes heat the brute-force way: it runs current through a metal element, and the element's resistance turns electricity directly into heat, roughly one unit of heat for each unit of electricity. It's simple and cheap to build, but it's a one-to-one converter, and there's no getting around that ceiling. A heat pump water heater doesn't *make* heat at all — it *moves* it. A compressor and refrigerant loop pull warmth out of the surrounding air and pump it into the water tank, the same way an air conditioner pulls heat out of your house and dumps it outside, just run in reverse and aimed at the water. Because it's moving existing heat rather than generating it from scratch, it can deliver several units of heat energy for each unit of electricity it consumes. That ratio is the whole ballgame.

## The kWh math, and why the ratio is so favorable

The efficiency of a heat pump is expressed as its coefficient of performance, or COP — the ratio of heat delivered to electricity consumed. A resistance element has a COP of about 1.0: one kilowatt-hour in, one kilowatt-hour of heat out. A heat pump water heater typically operates with a COP somewhere in the range of 3 to 4 under favorable conditions, meaning it delivers three to four units of heat for every unit of electricity it draws. Flip that around and the electricity story becomes obvious: to deliver the same amount of hot water, the heat pump needs only a quarter to a third of the electricity the resistance tank would burn. That's where the "about a third of the power" figure comes from, and it's why the annual kWh totals for the two technologies aren't close.

Put rough annual numbers on it, with clearly labeled assumptions. A household whose hot water demand would drive a resistance tank to consume somewhere around 3,500 to 4,500 kWh a year — a reasonable band for a family of a few people — would see a heat pump water heater cover the same demand on something closer to 1,000 to 1,500 kWh a year, thanks to that three-to-fourfold efficiency. The exact figures depend on how much hot water your household actually uses, the incoming water temperature, and how warm the air is around the unit, but the shape holds across the range: the heat pump's annual consumption is a small fraction of the resistance tank's. At an assumed rate of $0.17 per kWh, that difference between, say, 4,000 kWh and 1,300 kWh is roughly a $450 gap in annual operating cost — real money, repeated every year the unit runs. The savings, though, come with a set of conditions that the glossy COP number quietly assumes, and honest planning means accounting for them. The first is that a heat pump water heater pulls its heat from the surrounding air, which means it *cools and dehumidifies the space it sits in*. In a warm garage or a basement in a hot, humid climate, that's a bonus — you get free-ish dehumidification alongside your hot water. But in a small, enclosed, or already-cold space, the unit can chill the room it's in, and if that room is inside your heated living envelope, your home heating system ends up making up some of the heat the water heater stole, which claws back part of the efficiency. Placement matters enormously: a heat pump water heater wants a reasonably large, reasonably warm space with decent air volume — a garage, a utility room, a basement — not a tight closet. The manufacturers specify a minimum air volume or clearances for exactly this reason, and ignoring it hurts both performance and the COP you'll actually see.

The second condition is temperature. A heat pump extracts less heat from cold air, so its efficiency drops as the surrounding air gets colder. In a chilly climate or an unheated space during winter, the COP falls from its warm-weather peak, and the unit works harder for the same hot water. This is why most residential heat pump water heaters are actually *hybrid* units: they pair the heat pump with backup resistance elements and switch to resistance heating when demand outpaces what the heat pump can supply, or when the surrounding air is too cold for the heat pump to keep up. That hybrid design is a safety net that guarantees you never run out of hot water, but it has an efficiency cost — every hour the unit spends in resistance-backup mode, it's consuming electricity at that unfavorable one-to-one ratio rather than the efficient three-or-four-to-one. A household that regularly drains the tank faster than the heat pump can recover it, or that keeps the unit in a cold space, will see more resistance-mode operation and a lower real-world efficiency than the rated COP promises.

## Sizing the decision: recovery, cost, and where it fits

Beyond raw efficiency, the two technologies differ in a way that shapes daily life: recovery speed. A resistance tank reheats water quickly because its elements pour heat in at full power. A heat pump reheats more slowly, because moving heat is inherently gentler than blasting it in with a resistance coil. For most households this is invisible — the tank holds enough hot water to buffer normal demand, and the heat pump recovers steadily between draws. But a household with heavy, clustered hot water use, like several back-to-back showers on a winter morning, may push the tank into its resistance-backup mode to keep up, which is the hybrid design doing its job at the cost of some efficiency. Sizing the tank appropriately for your household's peak demand is the way to minimize that backup-mode operation, and it's one reason the "right" heat pump water heater for a big family is a larger-capacity model than a couple would need.

The upfront cost is the other half of the trade, and it's where the decision gets personal. A heat pump water heater costs meaningfully more to buy and install than a plain resistance tank — the compressor, refrigerant loop, and controls are more complex hardware, and installation can involve considerations like condensate drainage and adequate air space that a simple resistance swap doesn't. The premium is real. What justifies it is the annual operating saving compounding over the unit's life: if the heat pump saves several hundred dollars a year in electricity, it recovers its price premium over the resistance model within a handful of years and then keeps saving for the rest of its service life. The steeper your electricity rate, the faster that payback, because the value of every kilowatt-hour you *don't* burn is higher. There may also be utility rebates or a federal tax credit associated with efficient heat pump water heaters that shorten the payback further; those programs change over time, so it's worth checking current federal and utility guidance rather than assuming a specific figure, but they can meaningfully sweeten the math when available.
