The first sign is usually a sound: scratching or scrabbling overhead, coming from the roof, most often at dawn or dusk when the animals are moving. What you're hearing is a creature that has discovered the sheltered, weather-protected gap between your solar panels and your roof deck — one of the more appealing pieces of real estate you unintentionally created the day the array went up. Squirrels, rats, and pigeons all find that space attractive for exactly the same reasons: it's dry, shaded, hidden from predators, and gently warmed by the panels above. Left alone, an animal will turn it into a nest, and in the process do real, sometimes costly damage to a system that was supposed to run untouched for decades.

This is one of the more common maintenance issues solar owners genuinely face, and it's almost entirely preventable, which is what makes ignoring it so frustrating in hindsight. The damage falls into two broad categories — chewed wiring and fouled, nested-in spaces — and both have straightforward physical fixes that cost far less than the repairs they head off. The mistake people make is nearly always the same one: hearing the early scratching, deciding it's probably nothing, and doing nothing until a nest is well established and the wiring underneath has already been gnawed. By then the cheap fix has become an expensive one.

## Why the gap under your panels is prime real estate

A rooftop array sits a few inches above the roof surface on its mounting rails, which leaves an open, sheltered channel running beneath every panel. To a squirrel looking for a den or a pigeon looking for a nesting ledge, that channel is close to ideal habitat. It offers overhead cover from rain and blazing sun, protection from the hawks and cats that would otherwise pick them off in the open, and radiant warmth from the panels that soak up heat all day and release it slowly. Nothing about a solar array was designed to attract animals, but everything about the geometry happens to suit them, and once a neighborhood's squirrels or pigeons learn that these gaps exist, they treat them the way they'd treat any hollow tree or sheltered eave. Trees whose branches overhang the roof, and utility lines that run to the house, only make the problem worse, because they hand animals an easy on-ramp to a roof they might otherwise struggle to reach.

The trouble begins the moment an animal moves in, because of what already shares that space with it: the system's wiring. The DC cables running between panels, along with the grounding wires and the connectors that tie the array together, are strung through precisely the gap the animal now regards as home. Installers route those cables up under the panels deliberately, to keep them shaded, secured, and out of the weather — which is good practice for the wiring and, unfortunately, convenient for a rodent. So the animal and the electrical system end up occupying the same few inches of sheltered space, and that overlap is where both of the real problems start. Understanding that the wiring lives exactly where the pests want to be is the key to taking the early scratching seriously rather than filing it under harmless roof noise.

It helps to know which animals you're most likely dealing with, because the culprit shapes both the damage and the fix. Squirrels are the classic offender across much of the country — agile, relentless chewers who treat the under-panel gap as a den and the wiring as a chew toy. Rats and mice exploit the same space in warmer and more urban areas, slipping in through gaps far smaller than you'd expect. Pigeons are the dominant bird problem, drawn to the flat, sheltered ledge to roost and nest, though plenty of other birds will use it too. The season matters as much as the species. Spring and fall are peak move-in times, as animals look for somewhere protected to raise young or wait out the cold, so the scratching you hear in March or October is rarely a one-off visitor and more often a tenant settling in. What makes solar uniquely attractive compared with other spots on the roof is the combination the array happens to create: a roof already offers height and relative safety, but the panels add a continuous roofed corridor — evenly warmed, shaded, and running the whole length of the array — better shelter than an exposed eave or the gap under a single vent, and roomy enough to build in. Animals aren't drawn to solar because it's solar; they're drawn to the microhabitat the mounting geometry accidentally builds. The early behavioral signs are worth learning to read, because they run ahead of the expensive damage: scratching at dawn and dusk, droppings appearing on the ground below the array's edge, a sudden interest from neighborhood cats staring up at your roof, tufts of nesting material poking out from under a panel, or fresh gnaw marks on nearby fascia and cables. None of these signs is subtle once you know to watch for them, which is why the mistake is almost never a failure to notice and almost always a decision to wait. The scratching gets rationalized as the house settling or a branch on the roof, the first droppings get hosed away without a second thought, and by the time the evidence is undeniable the animal has had weeks or months to nest and gnaw in a space you can't easily see into. Treating the first clear sign as a prompt to look, rather than a nuisance to ignore, is the single most valuable habit an array owner can build around pests.

## What chewed wires and nests actually do

Rodents — squirrels and rats in particular — have incisors that grow continuously throughout their lives, so they gnaw compulsively to keep them worn down, and they're not fussy about what they gnaw on. Cable insulation is a convenient, satisfying target. When a squirrel chews through the insulation on a DC wire, several bad things can follow in sequence. The exposed conductor can create a ground fault that trips protective equipment and shuts a whole section of the array offline. Production drops as that string or group of panels stops contributing. And in the worst case, a damaged conductor becomes a source of arcing, which carries a genuine fire risk on a rooftop full of energized wiring. Because all of this happens out of sight beneath the panels, chewed wiring is usually invisible from the ground and often invisible from the roof, too — it tends to announce itself not as visible damage but as a mysterious, unexplained drop in output that shows up in your data before you'd ever spot the cause by looking.

That's exactly why [your monitoring app](/blog/solar-monitoring-warning-signs/) is your real early-warning system for this kind of damage. A single string, or a group of microinverters, suddenly producing noticeably less than its neighbors, or a ground-fault alert appearing where there wasn't one before, can be the first hard evidence of gnawed wiring — frequently well before you'd have any reason to suspect an animal. Watching for an unexplained production dip between physical checks is one of the most useful habits an owner can build, precisely because wiring damage is one of the more disruptive entries in the whole catalog of [common panel problems](/blog/common-solar-panel-problems/) specifically because it hides so well.

It's worth being precise about why chewed wiring is more than a nuisance, because the stakes are what separate it from cosmetic problems. Solar wiring carries direct current at meaningful voltage, and DC arcs don't self-extinguish the way household AC arcs tend to. When a rodent bares a conductor and it faults to the grounded racking, the best case is that the system's ground-fault protection trips and takes that section offline; the worst case is a damaged conductor arcing against the frame or the roof, and on a surface surrounded by dry debris and sometimes an old nest, that's a genuine ignition risk rather than a theoretical one. That's the reason installers route and secure the cabling so carefully in the first place, and the reason gnaw damage deserves prompt attention rather than a wait-and-see. The repair itself is rarely a five-minute fix, either. A technician usually has to lift panels to reach the damaged run, trace the fault to its source, replace the chewed cable and any compromised connectors, and verify the string is sound before buttoning everything back up — labor that climbs quickly precisely because the wiring lives in that awkward, sheltered gap. When they open it up, they frequently find the damage is worse than the monitoring suggested, with several nicked cables rather than one, because a rodent that chewed once has usually chewed repeatedly over the weeks it's been living there. All of which is why the unglamorous prevention is so much cheaper than the cure: keeping the animal out entirely sidesteps a repair that is fiddly, labor-heavy, and occasionally dangerous.

Birds, and pigeons above all, cause a different but equally real kind of harm. They build nests in the under-panel gap, packing it with twigs, feathers, and debris. That nesting material blocks the airflow beneath the panels, which matters more than it sounds: panels rely on that ventilation gap to shed heat, and a panel running hotter than it should produces a little less and ages a little faster. The debris also traps moisture against the roof surface, where it doesn't belong. Droppings compound everything, because they're acidic, they accumulate steadily under an established roost, and over time they can corrode mounting hardware and stain or foul the roofing beneath. A well-settled pigeon colony will also clog nearby gutters and leave a persistent, unpleasant mess streaking down the side of the house — the kind of problem that grows quietly for a season and then presents a large cleanup bill all at once. Left unaddressed, both forms of damage feed on neglect, which is why the animals under the panels can indirectly cost you production even when no single dramatic failure ever occurs.

## Guards, deterrents, and catching it before it's expensive

The standard fix is a critter guard, sometimes sold as panel skirting or a pigeon guard, and it addresses the problem at its root by denying access. It's a mesh — usually galvanized or coated metal for durability — clipped around the entire perimeter of the array, closing off the gap between the panels and the roof so animals simply can't get underneath in the first place. The important detail is how it attaches: a properly installed guard clips to the panel frames and the mounting rails rather than being fastened through the roof, so it adds no new penetrations and creates no new leak paths, and its mesh is specifically designed to keep the ventilation gap open for airflow while still blocking anything larger than an insect from squeezing through. Because good guards clip to the frames and rails rather than piercing the roof or the panels, they generally don't affect your roof or panel warranties either — though it's always worth confirming with your installer that the specific product and the specific installation are warranty-safe for your system before the work is done.

Not all guards are equal, and it's worth a word on choosing one. The mesh should be a rustproof metal — galvanized or stainless, or a quality coated steel — because a plastic mesh that turns brittle under UV and cracks within a few seasons simply hands the space back to the animals. The clips should be designed for solar frames rather than improvised from whatever's on hand, and the spacing should leave no gap a determined squirrel can widen. It's a small, unglamorous product, but a cheap or poorly fitted one fails quietly, and you won't know it failed until something is nesting behind it again. Once a good guard is on, it's close to maintenance-free, needing little more than an occasional glance to confirm it's still seated and no section has been pried loose — a fair trade for permanently closing off the one part of the system animals actually want.

Guards work best when they're installed before anything moves in, on a clean, empty gap. If wildlife is already nesting under there, the area has to be cleared and cleaned first — ideally by someone equipped to handle droppings and nesting debris safely, since bird droppings in particular carry health hazards — and only then sealed, because fitting a guard over an occupied space risks trapping animals inside, which is both cruel and a worse mess than the one you started with. So if there's already a nest, the sequence is clear: clear and clean it out properly, then fit the guard, then keep it from happening again. Beyond the guard itself, reducing roof access pays off: trimming back tree branches that overhang or touch the roof removes the highway animals use to reach it in the first place, a bit of maintenance that helps in several ways at once. Because so much of this damage happens where you can't easily see it, a periodic look matters more than for most parts of the system. A couple of times a year, and any time you notice fresh scratching, it's worth having someone check the array perimeter and the wiring beneath — either yourself from a safe vantage point or as part of a scheduled maintenance visit — and watching your monitoring for unexplained dips in between those checks.

On cost, the math strongly favors acting early. Minor deterrence is inexpensive relative to the repair it prevents. Clearing an established nest, replacing chewed wiring, and retrofitting a critter guard after the damage is done costs considerably more than simply fitting a guard proactively on a clean array, and a badly damaged section can mean re-running cable or replacing connectors, which pushes the bill higher still. Whether these costs land on you or on an insurer varies more than people expect, so it's worth checking your homeowners policy directly before you assume you're covered — some policies cover sudden animal damage while others specifically exclude rodents or vermin, treating that damage as a maintenance failure rather than a covered peril, and if you ever do have damage, documenting it with photos before repairs helps any claim. If you're budgeting for a system and its upkeep over its full [25-to-30-year lifespan](/blog/how-long-do-solar-panels-last/), pest prevention is a genuinely small line item, and a [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) can help you keep that maintenance spending in proportion to the overall investment rather than letting it feel like an afterthought. The system is built to run for decades with very little attention, and protecting the sheltered space beneath it is one of the few upkeep tasks that meaningfully helps it get there — a small guard and an occasional look, weighed against a hidden repair you won't see coming until the numbers on your monitoring app start slipping.
