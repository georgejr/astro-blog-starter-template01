The panels are on the roof, the inverter's on the wall, and then a notice arrives that the system failed inspection. It sounds alarming, like something is broken or dangerous, and for a homeowner who just spent five figures it can feel like the project has gone off the rails. In almost every case, it hasn't. A failed solar inspection is usually a paperwork or detail problem, a missing label, a fitting that isn't quite to code, a breaker in the wrong slot, not a sign that your system is defective or unsafe. Failures are common enough that experienced installers treat them as a routine hiccup rather than a crisis. What actually matters is understanding what a failure is, who's responsible for fixing it, how the re-inspection works, and how much time it adds before you can legally flip the system on and start saving. Once you see the mechanics, the word "failed" loses most of its sting.

The first thing to get straight is that your solar project typically faces not one inspection but two different gatekeepers, and they check for different things. Knowing which one flagged you tells you a lot about what went wrong and what happens next.

## The two inspections and why installs get flagged

The first gatekeeper is your local jurisdiction, the city or county building department, sometimes called the authority having jurisdiction. After your installer finishes the physical work, a local inspector visits to verify the installation meets the building and electrical codes your permit was issued under. They're checking that the array is mechanically sound, that the wiring and grounding are done correctly, that the required safety labels are present, and that the electrical connections meet code. The second gatekeeper is your utility, which does its own inspection or verification before it grants permission to operate, the formal green light that lets you legally energize the system and begin exporting power. These two are separate steps run by separate organizations, and both usually have to pass before your system can go live. If this whole sequence of permits, inspections, and approvals is new to you, the fuller map of who handles what and when is laid out in [solar permits and inspections](/blog/solar-permits-and-inspections/).

When the local inspector fails an install, it's almost always for one of a handful of recurring reasons, and they cluster around details rather than fundamental design flaws. Labeling and signage is one of the most common. Code requires a specific set of placards and warning labels, marking the disconnects, warning of the rapid-shutdown system, identifying the power sources, and installers occasionally miss one, use an outdated wording, or place it wrong. It's trivial to fix and has nothing to do with whether the system works, but an inspector will fail an install over a missing required label because that label is a safety requirement for the firefighters and technicians who might work on the system later. Grounding and bonding issues are another frequent flag: a loose lug, a bonding jumper that wasn't installed where the plans called for it, or a grounding connection that doesn't meet the inspector's read of the code. Conduit and wiring workmanship comes up often too, conduit not adequately secured, the wrong fitting used, wire management that leaves conductors exposed to damage, or a penetration that wasn't sealed properly.

A more technical but common failure involves the electrical panel where the solar ties in. There are code rules governing how much backfed solar current a given electrical panel can accept, based on the panel's busbar rating and the size of the main breaker, and if the installer's connection would exceed what the panel can safely handle, the inspector fails it until it's corrected, sometimes requiring a different connection method or a panel upgrade. Rapid shutdown is another: the system that de-energizes the roof conductors for firefighter safety has to be present and functioning, and if the inspector can't verify it operates, that's a failure that draws real scrutiny because it exists to protect first responders. Occasionally an inspector raises a structural concern, questioning the attachment spacing or the roof's ability to carry the load, which is more serious and may require an engineer's sign-off. And working-clearance problems, equipment installed where there isn't enough required space around it to service safely, round out the usual list.

The utility's inspection tends to fail for a narrower set of reasons, usually tied to the meter, the interconnection equipment, and whether the physical installation matches what the utility approved in the interconnection application. A mismatch between the approved plans and what's actually on the wall, an incorrect disconnect, or a metering issue can hold up the utility's sign-off even after the local jurisdiction has passed the system. Because the utility step is really the gateway to earning credit for your power, it's worth understanding on its own terms, which we cover in [the grid interconnection process](/blog/solar-interconnection-process/). The common thread across both inspections is that failures are overwhelmingly about correctable details, not about a system that's fundamentally wrong. An inspector's job is to catch exactly these things, and a flag is the process working as intended, not evidence you hired a bad company, though a pattern of many failures on one job is a fair reason to ask questions.

## Who fixes it, what re-inspection looks like, and what it costs you

Here's the part that should ease the anxiety: fixing a failed inspection is your installer's responsibility, not yours. Under a standard residential solar contract, the installer is obligated to deliver a system that passes inspection and reaches permission to operate. When an inspector writes up a correction, the installer schedules the fix, sends a crew or technician back to address the specific items, and then arranges the re-inspection. You generally don't lift a finger beyond granting access. This is a good reason to read your contract before signing to confirm that passing inspection and achieving permission to operate are explicitly the installer's duty, and that any re-inspection fees fall on them rather than on you. Reputable companies build the possibility of a correction into their process and don't pass those costs along, but the contract is where you verify that rather than assume it.

The re-inspection itself is usually straightforward. The installer corrects the flagged items, often a quick visit for something like adding a label or re-securing conduit, and requests that the inspector return to verify the fix. Many jurisdictions charge a re-inspection fee, and some limit their review to the items that failed rather than re-examining the whole system, which speeds things up. If the correction was minor, the second inspection frequently passes without issue, and the project moves forward. If the failure was more substantial, a structural question requiring engineering, or an electrical-panel limitation requiring a hardware change, the fix takes longer and may involve additional work, but it's still the installer's job to resolve. In the rare case where fixing the problem genuinely adds cost, say a panel upgrade nobody anticipated, that's a conversation to have against your contract's terms, and the general shape of solar pricing that a change like that plugs into is worth understanding with a [solar panel cost calculator](/solar-panel-cost-calculator/) so you can judge whether a proposed add-on is reasonable.

The real cost of a failed inspection to you is usually time, not money. Your system is physically built and sitting on your roof, but until it passes inspection and the utility grants permission to operate, you can't legally turn it on. Every week the correction and re-inspection take is a week you're still paying full electric bills while your paid-for panels sit idle. Depending on how quickly your installer moves and how backed up the local inspector and utility are, a failure can add anywhere from a few days to several weeks to the timeline. That's frustrating but rarely financially painful in itself, since you haven't lost the system, only delayed its start. It does underline why the whole approval sequence often takes longer than homeowners expect, a reality we set expectations for in [the solar installation timeline](/blog/solar-installation-timeline/). The system you're about to switch on is the same one you paid for; the delay is administrative, and once permission to operate arrives, the meter starts working for you and the failed inspection becomes a footnote.

## Lowering the odds before the inspector ever arrives

While a first-pass failure is common and usually harmless, you are not powerless to reduce the chance of one, and the levers mostly come down to who you hire. An experienced, local installer who does volume in your jurisdiction knows that particular inspector's expectations, the labeling conventions they insist on, the connection methods they prefer, the details they always check, and builds to those expectations the first time. A company that rarely works in your area, or a crew subcontracted in from elsewhere, is more likely to miss a local quirk. When you are vetting installers, asking how familiar they are with your specific building department and utility is a fair and revealing question; the ones who work there constantly answer it easily.

The permit and plan set matter too. A clean, code-compliant plan reviewed and approved before work begins is the foundation for a clean inspection, because the inspector is largely checking that the finished install matches the approved design. Reputable installers pull the permit, get the plans approved, and then build exactly to them, which is the single best predictor of a first-pass approval. If a company is cavalier about permitting, or suggests skipping it, that is a serious red flag not just for inspection but for the safety and insurability of the whole system.

There is also a quiet quality signal in how a company handles the inevitable. Even the best installers occasionally get flagged, so the real question is not whether they ever fail an inspection but how they respond when they do. A good outfit treats a correction as routine, fixes it quickly at its own expense, and reschedules the re-inspection without drama. When you are comparing bids, it is worth asking directly what happens if the system does not pass the first time, who bears the cost, and who handles the scheduling, then confirming the answer appears in the contract. The gap between a company that shrugs and handles it and one that goes quiet or tries to bill you for the fix tells you a great deal about how it will treat you across the next twenty-five years. Building that clarity in before you sign is how you keep a routine inspection hiccup from turning into a real headache.

So if you get that notice, take a breath. Ask your installer three questions: exactly which items failed, what the fix is, and when the re-inspection is scheduled. The answers will almost always describe something small and correctable, handled at the installer's expense, adding a short delay rather than a real problem. A first-pass failure is common, it's usually about details an inspector is right to insist on, and it says very little about the quality of your system. The homeowners who get burned aren't the ones whose install failed once; they're the ones who didn't confirm in their contract that fixing it was the installer's job, or who let a company drag its feet on the correction while the bills kept coming. Know that fixing it is on them, keep the pressure on to schedule the re-inspection promptly, and a failed inspection stays what it usually is: a routine bump on the way to switching your system on.
