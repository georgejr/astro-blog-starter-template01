If your system uses a string inverter, at some point in the design your installer decides how many panels to wire together in a series chain, and that number is not a guess or a matter of convenience. It is bounded on both ends by the inverter's voltage window, a range printed on the inverter's datasheet that the string must stay inside across every temperature your roof will ever see. Get too few panels in the chain and the inverter starves; get too many and, on the coldest morning of the year, the string can push a voltage high enough to damage or shut down the inverter. Understanding this one relationship, panels in series add up their voltages, and the inverter can only accept voltages within a set band, explains most of why arrays are laid out the way they are.

## Why panels get wired in a chain, and what the voltage window is

Start with what happens when you connect panels in series, because it is the whole basis for string sizing. Wire two panels in a series string, positive to negative, and their voltages add together while the current stays the same, exactly like stacking batteries end to end. Add a third and the voltage climbs again. A single modern panel might produce somewhere around 40 volts at its maximum power point, so a string of ten panels sits near 400 volts, and a string of fifteen approaches 600. A string inverter is designed to take a fairly high DC voltage like that and convert it to the AC your house uses, and doing the conversion at higher voltage is efficient, which is the whole reason panels are ganged into series strings in the first place rather than each running its own low-voltage line. That series behavior is a defining trait of string systems, and it is one of the central differences from the alternative approach, where each panel gets its own tiny inverter; if you are still weighing the two architectures, our comparison of [string inverters versus microinverters](/blog/string-vs-microinverters/) lays out why the wiring works so differently between them.

Every string inverter publishes an operating voltage window, a minimum and a maximum DC input voltage between which it will work. Below the minimum, the inverter cannot do its job; the voltage is too low for it to operate efficiently or, at the bottom, to turn on at all, so a string that is too short will underperform or fail to start on weak-light mornings. Above the maximum, you are in dangerous territory: exceed the inverter's absolute maximum input voltage and you can trip its protection, force it to shut down, or in the worst case damage it. There is also a narrower "MPPT window" inside the absolute limits, the range where the inverter can actually track the string's maximum power point and harvest efficiently, and good design keeps the string inside that tighter band as much of the time as possible rather than merely inside the absolute survival limits. The inverter, in other words, is a somewhat picky eater, and string sizing is the job of building chains whose voltage stays on its plate through everything the seasons throw at it. If the inverter's role in all this is still fuzzy, our explainer on [what a solar inverter actually does](/blog/what-does-a-solar-inverter-do/) covers the conversion job that the voltage window exists to protect.

The complication that makes string sizing an actual calculation rather than simple division is that panel voltage is not fixed. It moves with temperature, and it moves in the direction people find counterintuitive. Solar panels produce higher voltage when they are cold and lower voltage when they are hot. This is a physical property of the cells, and it is substantial, not a rounding error: on a frigid winter morning a panel can put out noticeably more voltage than its rated figure, and on a blazing summer afternoon noticeably less. That temperature sensitivity is the crux of the whole exercise, because it means a string that sits comfortably in the middle of the inverter's window on a mild day can swing toward the top of the window when it is very cold and toward the bottom when it is very hot. The installer is not sizing for a typical day. They are sizing for the extremes, and specifically for the two worst-case temperatures your location produces.

## Cold sets the ceiling, heat sets the floor

The design rule that falls out of temperature sensitivity is elegant once you see it. The coldest expected temperature at your location determines the maximum number of panels you can put in a string, because cold pushes voltage up, and the string has to stay under the inverter's maximum voltage even on the single coldest morning of the year, when panel voltage peaks. The hottest expected temperature determines the practical minimum, because heat pulls voltage down, and the string has to stay above the inverter's minimum operating voltage even on the hottest afternoon, when voltage sags. So the usable range of string lengths is squeezed from both ends by climate: cold weather sets the ceiling, heat sets the floor, and your string length has to live in the gap between them. In a cold climate, the cold-weather voltage spike is the binding constraint, and it often forces shorter strings than a mild climate would allow with the very same panels and inverter.

A worked version of the cold-weather check shows how few panels the math can allow, and how the numbers actually flow. Assume a panel with an open-circuit voltage (Voc) of 40 volts at the standard 25°C rating and a temperature coefficient of Voc of about -0.30% per degree Celsius, a typical value you would read off a datasheet. Assume the inverter's absolute maximum DC input is 600 volts, and assume your area's record low is -25°C, which is 50 degrees below the rating temperature. Voltage rises as it gets colder, so the panel's Voc at that record low climbs by 0.30% times 50 degrees, or 15%, from 40 volts to about 46 volts per panel. Divide the 600-volt ceiling by 46 volts and you get about 13, so the cold-weather rule caps this string at 13 panels, even though at the mild 25°C rating those same 13 panels sit at only around 520 volts and look like they have plenty of headroom. That gap between the mild-day number and the record-cold number is exactly the margin a careless design burns through, and it is why the calculation uses your record low rather than an average winter day. Push the string to 14 panels and the record-cold voltage lands around 644 volts, over the inverter's limit, and you have built the overvoltage fault right into the array.

This is why an installer does not simply take your total panel count and divide by some fixed number. They pull the record low temperature for your area, look up the panel's voltage-temperature coefficient on its datasheet, calculate what the string voltage climbs to at that record cold, and confirm it stays safely under the inverter's maximum. Then they check the hot end the same way to make sure the string still clears the minimum on the worst summer day. Only a string length that passes both tests, at both extremes, is valid. Skip the cold-weather check and you get the classic failure: a system that works fine all summer and then, on the first genuinely cold clear morning, throws an overvoltage fault and shuts down exactly when the cold, bright conditions should be producing beautifully. That is not a defect; it is a sizing mistake, a string built one or two panels too long for the local climate. It is uncommon from careful installers precisely because the calculation is standard practice, but it is a real failure mode, and it is the concrete reason the cold-weather math is not optional.

The too-short failure is quieter than the too-long one, which is part of why it gets less attention. A string that dips below the inverter's minimum on the hottest afternoons does not throw an alarm and shut the house down; it simply drops out of its efficient tracking band and harvests less than it should during those hours, or on a marginal design fails to wake up promptly on dim mornings. You lose a slice of production with no obvious symptom, which makes it the kind of mistake that can hide for years behind a system that otherwise looks healthy. That asymmetry, a dramatic and visible fault on the cold end but a silent efficiency leak on the hot end, is why careful installers check both extremes even though only one of them announces itself when it goes wrong.

Total array size then falls out of combining string length with the number of strings and MPPT inputs the inverter offers. Say the cold-weather rule allows up to fourteen panels per string for your climate, panel, and inverter, and the hot-weather rule requires at least eight to keep the inverter operating. Any string length from eight to fourteen is electrically valid, and the installer picks within that band to match your total panel count and to balance the strings against the inverter's inputs. If your design needs twenty-four panels, they might run two strings of twelve. If it needs twenty-eight, two strings of fourteen. The panels also have to physically fit your roof and be grouped sensibly by orientation, since panels facing different directions or under different shade generally belong on different strings or MPPT inputs so one shaded chain does not drag on a sunny one. This is where string sizing meets the rest of system design, and where the array's electrical layout and its physical layout have to agree. One more design lever sits alongside string sizing and often gets tuned together with it: the relationship between how much panel wattage you connect and the inverter's power rating, the DC-to-AC ratio. Installers commonly connect somewhat more panel capacity than the inverter's rating, for reasons that are entirely deliberate and covered in our piece on [inverter sizing and the DC-to-AC ratio sweet spot](/blog/inverter-sizing-dc-ac-ratio/). That ratio interacts with string sizing because both are about matching the array's electrical output to what the inverter can handle, one on voltage and the other on power. For a homeowner, the practical takeaway is not to memorize the arithmetic but to know the right questions. If you have a string inverter, it is fair to ask your installer to confirm they sized the strings for your area's record low temperature, not just an average, and to ask whether panels on different roof faces are split across separate MPPT inputs. Those two questions catch the mistakes that actually happen. The voltage window is the inverter's comfort zone, cold weather is the constraint that quietly rules the ceiling, and a string sized to respect both is one you will never think about again, which is exactly the goal.
